Leader's introduction

\n \n\n\n

This study guide follows, chapter by chapter, the book, Walking In Victory, by\nDennis McCallum. I have referred to myself in the third person in order to keep the focus\non the text. We have seen excellent results using this book as a basis for both small\ngroup and classroom study. The book challenges young and mature Christians alike to seek\nmaturity in Christ at all costs, and to do so through the power of God, not self-effort.\nRomans 5-8 is the most complete and exciting explanation of Christian growth in any of the\nletters of Paul. I hope you are challenged and edified by this wonderful passage that\nnever grows old. \n\n

How to lead a study based on this book:

\n\n\n

First discuss the idea of going through the book with the group, and proceed only if\nthey agree to do the work. Walking in Victory is not a typical Sunday School\nquarterly, and will require careful reading, thought, and prayer on the part of your group\nmembers. If you, the leader(s), have read the book and benefited from it, you should be\nable to convince the members of the group they, too, will benefit. People usually respond\nto a challenge, and may like the idea that they are not just going to go over simple\nideas.

\n\n

Obtain a copy of Walking in Victory for each member of the group. If you cannot\nget quantity pricing from your local bookstore, come to the Xenos Home Page\n(http://www.xenos.org) and order at substantial discount.

\n\n

This study guide is designed for leader-facilitated discussion. The leader will not be\nlecturing, unless for short five minute introductions. Participation is a preferable way\nto learn. You will be reading in the group, so at least most of the members need their own\ncopy to follow along. Walking in Victory is written very tight, so members won't\nhave to read long passages before you have material to discuss. Unlike most books, this\nbook can be read in the group without boring your members.

\n\n

The best results come when the members answer the questions before coming to the group,\nand then compare their work. But you may also answer them together in the group after\nreading. An average study will include about fifteen or twenty minutes of reading, and twenty to\nthirty or more minutes of discussion based on the questions in this guide.

\n

Does the group understand the difference between an external finite reference\n point and an external infinite reference point?

\n \n

External finite reference points are points outside ourselves that we\n can refer to in order to come to conclusions about ourselves. They are like ourselves in\n that they, too, are finite and do not possess any inherent position of authority to make\n these determinations. Our parents, teachers, friends, relatives, the media, and even our\n own observations all give input to the formation of our identity. What we think is\n important and what we think of ourselves is primarily determined through these external\n finite reference points. The conclusions we draw based on these might be true (consistent\n with what is actually true about us) or might not be. Most likely we simply view things\n the way the important people in our lives viewed them. Obviously if our conclusions are\n inaccurate, and our actions and attitudes are derived from our sense of who we are, then\n we have a miserable life ahead of us to one degree or another.

\n

An external infinite reference point would be a point that stands\n above all of creation and is inherently in a position of authority. This would be an\n absolute point of reference, not a relative one. God doesn't need to compare himself to\n anyone or anything to determine who he is, rather, he is the standard. What he says is\n true about the identity or significance of anything would be the final word, regardless of\n what all the finite reference points believed.

\n \n \n

· If we compared other's view of us to God's view of\n us, what would we discover? We get the sense that God's view might be different than\n others', but how?

\n

· How might his view be different than our own view of\n ourselves?

\n

· If there is a difference between the way God views me and\n the way I view myself, would it matter? What impact would such a difference have on my\n life?

Possible answers:

\n

Our attitudes and behaviors grow out of who we are. We must have a\n certain identity and we must know what that identity is if we expect to see change from\n the inside out.

\n

God is an absolute and infinite reference point. Therefore, His view of\n us is the real view of us. Any difference between his view and our own indicates that we\n are under some level of deception about ourselves.

\n \n \n

This chapter mentions two modern (and non-biblical) solutions to self-image\n problems. See if the group members can summarize the limitations of both methods.

\n \n

1) Self-Affirmation:

\n

Some schools of counseling advise daily self-affirmation,\n perhaps while looking in a mirror. The client repeats in confident tones that he or she is\n a good person, is worthwhile, has a lot to offer, etc., etc. The problem with this view is\n that the reference point for changing how a person thinks and feels about himself, is\n himself, and is therefore completely arbitrary. We could decide we are superhuman or that\n we are worthless, but either conclusion would have no necessary correspondence with\n reality.

\n

2) Others-Affirmation:

\n

Some counselors urge the client to hang around with people who\n are affirming and willing to remind us why we are significant. The problem with this\n approach is two-fold. First, the people upon whom we are depending establish our sense of\n identity are as finite as we, ourselves. Second, their opinion of us is subject to change\n based on how we've treated them. Therefore we may well become the slaves of those we hope\n will establish our sense of identity. The result is that we live for the approval of\n others -- we become man-pleasers: a position that is incompatible with pleasing God\n (Galatians 1:10; 2 Corinthians 10:12).

\n \n \n \n \n\n\n

Our sense of identity is our knowledge of who we are, and why we matter. We either make\nup an identity based on nothing more than our hunch about who we are, or we find out who\nwe really are by consulting the God who created us. In the next chapter, we begin to\nconsider how to appropriate God's view of us. \n\n

Believing

\n \n

Remember, unlike New Age religion, where the power comes from my\n determination to believe something until it is true (imaging, or mind power), in\n Christianity, power comes from the object of our faith. Our beliefs must be based\n on what is true. We cannot believe what is true about us without first knowing\n what is true.

\n

Knowing can only happen as we turn to, study, and learn these truths from God's\n word through the illuminating power of the Holy Spirit. Therefore, knowing is the\n first and necessary step to believing what God says is true about us.

\n \n

After discussing this in the area of sins of commission, ask the\n group about sins of omission.

\n \n \n

On Walking in Victory page 180, we have a list of three reasons to avoid sin\n under the grace paradigm. Can you think of any more?

\n \n \n

Condition 3: No Illegitimate Pain Reducers

\n \n

Make a list of potentially illegitimate pain reducers. Include things that\n may not be sinful in themselves, but could become illegitimate under certain conditions.

\n

When is it right to flee a situation rather than stay and suffer? McCallum claims there\n is no exact way to know. But what are some principles people might use in making this\n decision?

\n \n \n

Condition 4: A Position-Oriented Perspective

\n \n

Paul repeatedly talks about setting his eyes on the things above, or on the\n unseen things. McCallum claims this includes even looking away from the breaking process\n itself. He says, \"If we constantly take our spiritual temperature, we become like\n spiritual hypochondriacs.\" A hypochondriac is one who worries constantly about his\n health, even when there are no symptoms suggesting illness. Studies have shown that\n hypochondriacs actually worsen their own health through their worry and fussing. Can you\n think of times in your life where this has happened on a spiritual plane? Has worry about\n your own growth ever drawn your attention away from Christ himself?

\n

McCallum says, \"Only those who know where they are headed eternally can be\n expected to welcome suffering.\" How can reflecting on our future with God give us\n courage to face trial?
