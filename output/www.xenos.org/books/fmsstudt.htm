Introduction

\n\n\n

Christianity: The Faith That Makes Sense has been used by God to lead many to\npersonal faith in Jesus Christ. This study guide is intended to help Christians use Christianity:\nThe Faith That Makes Sense more effectively as a witnessing tool by discussing the\nissues in greater depth. The guide can be used either for individual study or for group\nstudy. We will address a group facilitator throughout and individuals can simply ignore\nthe group discussion suggestions.

\n\n

For each chapter, read the section in Christianity: The Faith That Makes Sense\nfirst. If you are leading a group you must consider whether or not your members will do\nthe reading outside the group. If you suspect members have not read the chapter, you can\nread either the chapter or relevant sections in the group by going around taking turns\nreading aloud. Then compare answers to selected study questions.

Chapter 1 The Worst Party Ever\n\n

\n
  • Christianity: The Faith That Makes Sense
    •  begins with a metaphor of a party where\n everyone dies within three hours. How do you see this party fitting into the communication\n strategy of the book?
    \n
    \n The party is intended to emphasize the need to consider spiritual things. In an age of\n apathy and cynicism, our first task is to persuade non Christians to engage with us. A\n story or metaphor like this may prick a sense of need that could lead to further talk.\n     \n
  • How would you react to the suggestion by a non Christian reader that being\n ushered into such a party is unfair, and any God who would create such a situation should\n not be acknowledged?
    \n
    \n Ask, \"How do you define fair?\" and \"Where does your sense of fairness come\n from?\" Point out that, in reality, God doesn’t actually inject each person with\n a virus—the virus is a result of our own autonomy. The amazing thing is that God gets\n involved in our bad situation and rescues us.
    • \n \n
  • Aside from the duration of the party being different from the duration of our\n lives, can you think of any other differences or discontinuities between the metaphor and\n real life?
    \n
    \n To be completely fair, we should imagine, not one witnesser with a door he claims leads to\n health, but a few hundred all claiming that their door is it. The individual would have to\n try to discern which one(s) are more credible. However, we also must stress that just\n because there are many false claimants doesn’t mean none of them are true.
    • \n \n
  • For postmodern or relativistic readers, consider a person who refuses to\n believe in the reality of the deadly virus and insists that because his belief's are\n different, his fate will be different also.
    \n
    \n His belief's are irrelevant. He will die like the rest.
    • \n \n
  • Do your friends have a strong sense of purpose in their lives? If so, what is\n it? If not, are they bothered by this lack? Should they be?
    \n
    • \n
  • As Christians, what is the purpose of our lives?
    \n
    • \n
  • A non Christian says, \"A life doesn’t have to be eternal to be\n meaningful.\" How would you respond?
    \n
    \n We should ask, what then is that meaning? If all are here momentarily, and our world is\n also momentary in the history of the universe, why would any thing be meaningful in the\n ultimate sense?
    • \n \n
  • If you were really in such a party and found a door leading to life, would you\n go back to lead others out?

    Ground Rule 1: Reason Works\n\n

      \n \n
    1. Many today, including Christians, doubt that reason can tell us much in the spiritual\n realm. What do you think?
      \n
      \n Answers to this question will help reveal postmodern attitudes in the group.
      2. \n
    2. Can we deny the validity or the importance of reason but still stick up for\n truth?
      \n
      \n No, we cannot. Truth is only meaningful when juxtaposed to falsehood. Apart from reason,\n the true and the false can both be right. Only when we exclude the possibility that a\n thesis and its antithesis cannot both be right at the same time is truth objective. If\n both a thesis and its antithesis can be right at the same time, what is a lie anyway?
      3. \n \n
    3. Can you think of any other area of knowledge that requires both reason and\n experience?
      \n
      \n Learning to drive; playing music; speaking a language; appreciating the northern lights.
      4. \n \n
    4. Do you think some Christian doctrines such as the trinity are beyond or above\n reason? Can something be beyond human comprehension but still compatible with rationality?
      \n
      \n Explain the difference between something that is irrational and something that is\n incomprehensible. You may may not comprehend how your copying machine works, but that\n doesn’t mean it’s irrational. Likewise, we may not understand some aspects of\n the infinite God, but we err if we claim he is irrational. The trinity is one in essence,\n three in person. This is not irrational, even though we have never experienced such a\n thing and cannot comprehend it. God says he cannot tell a lie.
      5. \n \n
    5. Consider the concept of a square triangle. Can such a thing exist? How do you\n know?
      \n
      \n A contradiction in terms is irrational and self-refuting. The universe is real and created\n by a rational God, not a dream state where anything can happen. Eastern gods contradict\n themselves, not the God of the Bible.

      \n \n
    1. Can you think of any world view that can prove itself?

      2. \n
    2. Does naturalism (e.g. Atheism or agnosticism) require faith? Why or why not?

      3. \n
    3. If McCallum is right that no world view can be proved, can we really know\n anything? How would you differentiate between Christianity and any other opinion?

Interestingly, all three come from a biblical tradition. Do you agree that\n the passages cited show that these religions teach salvation by works? Are you aware of\n any claims to the contrary?
      \n
      \n Many liberal American Jewish congregations are universalistic (See below ch. 9). So are\n individual Catholics. But Catholic dogma is clear that not only grace, but works are\n essential for eternal life.

    Chapter 4

    \n\n\n
      \n
    1. This chapter focuses on arguments, not for Christianity specifically, but for the\n existence of God. Surveys show that over 90% of Americans believe in a god or gods. What,\n then is the usefulness of this material?
      \n
      \n Many are not formal atheists, but are practical atheists. Also, of those claiming to\n believe in God, probably half or more to not accept an infinite, personal God like the God\n of the Bible. These arguments will not support pantheistic ideas of God.
      2. \n \n
    2. What is another good illustration (besides the Taj Mahal) for the argument\n from design?
      \n
      3. \n
    3. The genius of the presuppositional argument is that it points out, not just\n what is likely or unlikely (like the argument from design) but what is consistent or\n inconsistent with one’s own presuppositions. Why is this relevant for witnessing\n Christians?
      \n
      \n Presuppositional arguments can refute individual world views, which is often necessary.\n Likewise, by a process of elimination, it can point the way to biblical Christianity,\n which alone is able to be completely self-consistent.
      \n Facilitators should be aware that the form of pressuppositional argument given here is not\n the classical pressupositionalism of Cornelius Van Til, but a modified pressuppositional\n argument more like that advanced by Francis Schaeffer.
      4. \n \n
    4. Why is the man’s suggestion that they change their currency incompatible\n with his view of the rocks spelling Canadian Railways. . .?
      \n
      \n Just checking to make sure they can articulate how this argument works.
      5. \n \n
    5. Postmodern thinkers claim that it doesn’t matter if they are inconsistent\n with their own presuppositions because everyone is inconsistent. Is this true?
      \n
      \n No. Christians may not be consistent, but this is because of personal failure, not because\n our faith makes it impossible to be consistent. Another world view is able to be\n self-consistent, namely deism.
      6. \n \n
    6. Postmodernists also claim that consistency is nothing but a western test\n unknown in other cultures. Give some examples of inconsistency between truth claims and\n actions or views. Follow these examples:
      \n
      \n -A man claims he doesn’t believe gravity has any claim on him, but he wears a\n parachute when skydiving.
      \n
      \n -Your wife claims she loves you but she also wants to have affairs with other men.
      \n
      \n -A postmodern mother claims there is no such thing as objective truth, but chides her\n daughter for lying.
      \n
      \n
      7. \n
    7. Why, in your own words, is it impossible to devise moral norms based only on a\n material world with material creatures?
      \n
      \n Focus on the illustration involving vinager and soda.
      8. \n \n
    8. Is there a difference between relative morality (morals relative to the\n individual or group) and no morality at all?
      \n
      \n No, there is no difference. A moral code that dictates I do whatever I think is right is\n exactly the same as no moral code at all. Moral norms must be able to call us to behavior\n different from what we want.
      9. \n \n
    9. As the author points out, the arguments in this chapter point to the existence\n of God, not exclusively to the Bible or to Jesus Christ. At the same time, we could argue\n that they to not point at all to eastern mystical, or pantheistic concepts of God. Why is\n this true?
      \n
      \n An impersonal, pantheistic force would not be able to plan or think in a way that would\n account for what we see in nature.

    Chapter 5 God’s Chosen Method of Self-authentication

    \n\n\n
      \n
    1. What are the problems and the possible strengths for each of the following means of\n authentication for Christianity or any other truth claim?
      \n l
      \n - The evidence of miracles
      \n
      \n Weakness: Must be authenticated—hard to tell if real. Hard to repeat for every\n person. Might be duplicated by occult religion.
      \n Strength: points directly to the supernatural.
      \n
      \n       - The evidence of others’ testimony
      \n
      \n Weakness: People make mistakes and they lie. How do we know who to believe among\n conflicting testimonies?
      \n Strength: If we know or trust the person, it can be very convincing.
      \n
      \n       - The evidence of historical reliability of the Bible
      \n
      \n Weakness: Can be complicated and hard to explain. Does not point to inspiration\n necessarily. Only convincing if people accept our methodology.
      \n Strength: Depending on one’s presuppositions, can tip the scale in favor of\n belief.
      \n
      \n       - Rational consistency with its own propositions
      \n
      \n Weakness: Only shows consistency with a given paradigm, therefore not necessarily\n binding on other paradigms. Some don’t value consistency.
      \n Strength: Can set Christianity apart from other self-refuting views.
      \n
      \n       - Correlation between claims made in a religious text and provable things outside that\n text (eg. in nature)
      \n
      \n Weakness: Only demonstrates that some parts of a text are valid, not all parts.\n Requires interpretation of the text.
      \n Strength: Can point directly to the reliability of the Bible if handled well.
      \n
      \n       - The historical argument for the resurrection of Christ
      \n
      \n Weakness: Can be hard to explain or defend. Relies on convincing non Christians that\n it’s more likely that someone rose from the dead than that religious people would\n lie.
      \n Strength: Depending on one’s starting point, it can move someone from general\n respect for Christ and the Bible to personal faith. When handled by a master rhetorician\n it can and has led many to Christ (e.g. C.S. Lewis’ Mere Christianity).
      \n
      \n       The argument that Jesus must be true because I have experienced him in my heart
      \n
      \n Weakness: Same argument is advanced by Buddhists, New Agers, and Cultists.
      \n Strength: Supremely convincing to the one having the experience. Also convincing to\n others if they trust the one with the experience.
      \n
      \n       - The argument that God alone can tell the future course of human history, and has\n done so in the Bible.
      \n
      \n Weakness: Can be difficult to explain, requires study. Requires interpretation of\n biblical prophecies. Must verify the dates of texts and that fulfillment actually\n occurred. Finally must be able to rule out deliberate fulfillment (e.g. Jesus riding into\n Jerusalem on a colt of a donkey could have been deliberate fulfillment of Zech 9:9).
      \n Strength: Can point directly and exclusively to Jesus Christ and the Bible. If handled\n well, the few predictions that meet all these conditions can be very convincing, even to\n skeptics if they come to believe our interpretations of the passages are fair.
      \n
      2. \n \n
    2. A non Christian says, \"The Bible is a written document, and has been\n translated many times. Therefore, it probably contains unknown thousands of errors and we\n have no idea what was in the original.\" Respond.
      \n
      \n Point to a good text on this issue, like Geisler and Nix, From God to Us, or J.W.\n Montgomery, History and Christianity, or McDowell, Evidence that Demands a Verdict.

    Chapter 7 The Time of Christ’s Coming Predicted

    \n\n\n

    Daniel 9 is a remarkable prophecy, but some people steer away from it because it is\ncomplicated. Yet, as you can see from this chapter of Christianity: The Faith That\nMakes Sense, it is possible to summarize and simplify the passage enough to use\neffectively in evangelism.

    \n\n

    Let’s look at Daniel chapter 9 and gain a better understanding of the background\nand basic interpretive issues:\n\n

      \n
    1. Daniel 9:1 dates this vision as being in \"In the first year of Darius son of\n Xerxes.\" We have no record of Darius, but we know Xerxes well from history. Since\n Daniel says Darius \"was made ruler\" we can assume he was a viceroy serving under\n Xerxes, the new Persian ruler. Look in a Bible dictionary and find out when Xerxes\n conquered Babylon.
      \n
      \n For most of these, just try to ascertain that members are doing the work and see what\n their answers are.
      2. \n \n
    2. Daniel says his reflection began when he read in Jeremiah the number of years\n for the Jewish exile in Babylon. (See Jeremiah 25:11) Considering that Daniel was taken\n into exile with the first group of captives in about 605 BC, (and remembering that years\n go down instead of up in the BC period) about how many years had passed since his capture?
      \n
      \n About 70 years, especially when including the Hebrew tendency to count any part of a year\n as a whole.
      3. \n \n
    3. As Daniel prays and confesses the sins of the people, he also mentions the\n curse that Moses warned them about. Read Leviticus 26:33-35 in context. Their\n unfaithfulness had resulted in their exile, but somehow the \"sabbatical year\"\n was involved as well (see chapter 24:1-6). This link is important, because the Sabbaths of\n years in the last part of the chapter have this as their context. Read also IIChronicles\n 36:20-22. Here again, we see reference to the Jeremiah prediction, to Xerxes, and to the\n sabbatical years, of which 70 had apparently been ignored.
      \n
      \n Some translations merely says \"70 weeks\" instead of \"70 sevens\" thus\n making it harder to understand. See margin of NASB.
      4. \n \n
    4. In verse 24, Gabriel says God will allow the Jewish people 70\n \"sevens\" or shabuim (from which we get the word \"Sabbath\"). Now\n you can see why the context dictates that these be Sabbaths of years, not of days. Now\n read the list of things the Jewish people must do to complete their commission. For each\n item, write one or more possibilities for what it might refer to.
      \n
      \n finish transgression
      \n Bring in the millenium?
      \n
      \n put an end to sin
      \n Bring in the millenium?
      \n
      \n atone for wickedness
      \n The work of Christ
      \n
      \n bring in everlasting righteousness
      \n Could refer to the church or to the millenium
      \n
      \n to seal up vision and prophecy
      \n Complete the Old Testament, and perhaps the whole Bible
      \n
      \n anoint the most holy
      \n Either re-consecrating the rebuilt temple, or annointing Christ (his baptism?)
      5. \n \n
    5. In verse 25 some versions refer to the coming of Messiah, and others refer to\n \"The Anointed One. In Hebrew this word is Meshiach, from which we get Messiah.\n Anointing the \"most holy\" (verse 24, NIV) could be a reference to the Messiah as\n well.
      6. \n
    6. We will not attempt to dissect the chronology of years here, although this has\n already been done in fms. Go over that section again, including the footnotes to make sure\n you understand it. Since the rebuilding of Jerusalem occurred in the 400’s BC, it\n should be clear that the coming of Messiah is about 483 years from some time in the fifth\n century BC. Without going into detail, this alone should make it clear that the\n fulfillment falls in the early first century. See the works referenced in the notes for\n more detail on the two main ways to interpret the passage.

      \n Help members to overcome intimidation from the complicated nature of this text.

    Chapter 8 Modern Day Predictions

    \n\n\n

    Although the fulfillment of modern day predictions is rarely as objective as those\nconcerning Christ, there are a few key exceptions.

    \n\n

    Read Luke 21:20-25. In this passage Jesus lists a series of events in consecutive\norder. Write here the order of events in that passage:
    \n
    \nThe surrounding and descruction of Jerusalem (AD 70)
    \nThe deportation of the Jews into all nations (After AD 70)
    \nThe re-occupation of Jerusalem by the Jews (1967)
    \nThe end times (?)
    \n\n

      \n
    1. Compare this prediction to the one in Isaiah 11:1-12. This passage describes the coming\n of Messiah as the Root of Jesse (King David’s father). Isaiah says God will then\n \"reach out his hand a second time to reclaim the remnant that is left of his people\n from Assyria, from Lower Egypt, from Upper Egypt, from Cush, from Elam, from Babylonia,\n from Hamath and from the islands of the sea. He will raise a banner for the nations and\n gather the exiles of Israel; he will assemble the scattered people of Judah from the four\n quarters of the earth.\" (vs. 11,12) Notice the following about this prediction:
      \n
      \n This is the second time God regathered Israel. When was the first?
      \n After the Babylonian captivity in the 6th C. BC.
      \n
      \n He gathers them from a number of nations, including \"the islands of the sea.\"\n Look in a Bible dictionary or study Bible and record what they say this expression means.
      \n
      \n Usually refers to unknown lands beyond the (Mediterranean) Sea
      \n
      \n He also gathers them from \"the four quarters of the Earth.\" Would this be a good\n description of the regathering that occurred under Ezra and Nehemaiah?
      \n
      \n No, they were only regathered from one nation--Babylon
      2. \n \n
    2. Read Ezekiel 37:1-14. According to verse 11, what do the dry bones stand for?
      \n
      \n The Nation of Israel, scattered around the earth.
      \n
      \n The bones are said to be very dry and dead. What does this suggest about Israel prior to\n the miracle of regathering?

    Chapter 9 Objections to Christianity

    \n\n\n

    This chapter is easily the longest in the book, so if you are using this guide for\ngroup study, consider dividing this chapter into more than one week.

    \n\n

    Remember that responding to objections to Christianity is not the heart of evangelism.\nIn fact, we should only answer these common complaints, in my opinion, after we have\nalready clearly shared the gospel itself. We may find it all too easy to become\nside-tracked onto these issues and never actually come to grips with the gospel message of\ngrace. On the other hand, after we have declared the gospel, we are obligated to give an\naccount for our belief—that is, to answer honest questions to the best of our\nability. This is why the book has been arranged with the objections at the end.

Note also, that the rejection of Christianity (even though\n based on its’ absolutism) also constitutes crossing the threshold.
