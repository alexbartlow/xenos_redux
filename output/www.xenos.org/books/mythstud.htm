Leader's introduction

\n\n

This study guide follows, chapter by chapter, the book, The Myth of Romance, by\nDennis McCallum and Gary DeLashmutt. We refer to ourselves in the third person in order to\nkeep the focus on the text. The guide is arranged for use in any one of four venues: \n\n

    \n
  • Individual enrichment
    • \n
  • Couples premarital study
    • \n
  • Married couples' enrichment
    • \n
  • Group study
    • \n
\n\n

We have seen excellent results using this book as a basis for small group as \n well as in premarital counseling. If using The Myth of Romance as a premarital \n or marital counseling aid, you may want to read the chapter together before \n going over the questions. For group leaders, the facilitator's guide provides \n suggested answers or tips to key questions. The other three venues will have \n no use for the facilitator's guide. Hundreds of couples planning their marriage \n have reported that this study brought to light the issues God wanted to call \n to their attention. May God use the material to richly bless your present or \n future marriage as well as the other relationships in your life.

Chapter 1
\nMarriage Success Today:
\nWhat Are Your Chances?

\n\n
    \n
  1. Are you from an intact, or a broken family? How do you think this may have affected you,\n and how have you adjusted?
    2. \n
  2. How could you imagine divorce possibly impacting children and their future marriages?
      \n
    • Single parent families offer decreased nurture to kids creating adjustment problems
      • \n
    • Kids who see marriage fail may become cynical about marriage or even fearful
      • \n
    • Unless divorcees area cooperative with each other, kids may get a negative view of the\n opposite sex
      • \n
    • Separation anxiety may cause kids to become insecure, depending on others for their\n sense of security, leading to hyper-dependence
      • \n
    \n
    3. \n
  3. The authors suggest it's possible to \"prepare\" for marriage. Do you find this\n suggestion plausible? Or does it seem contrived?
      \n
    • Think of other things we prepare for--job, parenting, sports
      • \n
    • Those who think we shouldn't have to prepare for marriage betray an underlying belief\n that marriage should happen automatically--probably because of the eros myth\n
      • \n
    • The authors discuss the disturbing case of Esau. Record your reaction to the following:
      \n
      \n God loves us and if we mess up he will fix our lives.
      \n
      \n Agree Somewhat agree Doubtful Disagree
      \n
      \n If we ignore God's will we may suffer disaster in this life.
      \n Agree Somewhat agree Doubtful Disagree
      \n
      • \n
    \n
    4. \n
  4. Marriage is harder than shining a pair of shoes. Before God, how far are you prepared to\n go in making your marriage all it can be?
      \n
    • You could share what you put on this question
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n

Chapter 2
\nIs \"Love\" Enough?

\n\n
    \n
  1. Have you ever had the experience of believing you were in love with someone, only to\n find later that you no longer had any interest in that person? How do you interpret such\n an experience?
      \n
    • At the very least, this must suggest the unreliability of eros love as a guide to\n the \"will of God\" or as a guide to finding everlasting love.
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. If a couple are \"in love,\" shouldn't they get married? Why, or why not?
      \n
    • This question reveals people's reasons for getting married.
      • \n
    • What would be a good reason for getting married if not being in love?
      • \n
    • Ask the group, \"What does being in love mean?\"
      • \n
    \n
    3. \n
  3. Have you ever known a couple who believed they were in love and got married, only to\n fail? If so, what do you think accounts for the failure?
      \n
    • Answers to this question could reveal more about how people interpret eros mythology.\n Try to summarize the principle their answers imply.
      • \n
    \n
    4. \n
  4. McCallum and DeLashmutt think Christian love has to do with serving others more than it\n does with feelings. What do you think of the passages they cite on p. (The good Samaritan,\n 1John 3:17) to back this up?
      \n
    • Observe that the Samaritan both felt compassion and did something about it.
      • \n
    • For those who think love is mainly a feeling, ask how this can be reconciled with the\n biblical material. Isn't our modern definition of love more focused on feeling and\n sentiment than the biblical view which focuses more on serving and giving?
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n
  5. The authors mention married couples whose \"love seems to desert them\" after\n marriage. Are you aware of any cases where this occurred? If so, what does it mean?
      \n
    • Under the eros myth, it might suggest that they married by mistake and that their love\n was not \"the real thing.\"
      • \n
    • To biblical Christians, it should suggest the inadequacy of eros feelings for\n discovering eternal love.
      • \n
    \n
    6. \n
  6. Are you aware of any cases where a married person came to believe that he or she was in\n love with another person other than their spouse? If so, what lesson(s) can be drawn from\n such episodes?
      \n
    • Again, eros feelings for another woman or man are impossible to distinguish from eros\n feelings we originally felt for our spouse.
      • \n
    \n
    7. \n
  7. Your friend says, \"I wouldn't buy a car without driving it around the block, and I\n wouldn't marry someone without a trial marriage.\" How would you assess such a\n statement?
      \n
    • That which is reasonable when buying a machine is hardly appropriate for a personal\n being.
      • \n
    • Note the \"self-first\" ethos in this thought: \"I have to check to make\n sure I'm getting all I can get.\" This direction is seeking to take, not to give.
      • \n
    \n
    8. \n

Chapter 4
\nLearning Christian Love

\n\n
    \n
  1. Do you agree with the authors that people need the experience of building one or more\n successful intimate relationships (non-romantic) before they can feel confident they will\n succeed in marriage? Why or why not?
      \n
    • For those who answer no, why do they think they will be able to do something in marriage\n that they have not been able to do before marriage? (see #3 below)
      • \n
    • For those who feel intimate relationships with dating partners is adequate, ask whether\n they believe the feelings that now buttress that relationship will continue to do so\n within marriage? Do they feel they are different than other married couples?
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. Can you suggest an alternative capability that would point toward likely marriage\n success?
    3. \n
  3. Respond to the authors' question: \"If we are not successfully loving people at the\n intimate level before marriage, what makes us think we will be able to do so after\n marriage?\"
    4. \n
  4. The authors also say: \"Likewise, if we are married, but find no examples of\n intimate relationship outside our marriage, perhaps the problem is not with our spouse\n after all!\" What are they suggesting? Do you agree?
      \n
    • Married people having relational problems tend to blame their spouses. But if none of\n their other relationships are successful at an intimate level, perhaps they themselves are\n unable to relate at an intimate level.
      • \n
    • Help your married members turn their eyes away from blaming their spouses and onto\n themselves.
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n
  5. Do your own relational inventory. What areas do you see as areas of strength? What areas\n need improvement?
    6. \n

Where do you see the boundaries between the privacy of the marital relationship\n and the shared life of the Christian community?

    \n Try to imagine things that might be inappropriate in the direction of encroachment by the\n community into marriage:
    \n
    \n
    \n Try to imagine things that might be inappropriate in the direction of unqualified\n individualism and privacy:

      \n
    • The purpose of these questions are to make members wrestle with the issue of privacy\n versus community.
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. Would you ask counsel of older Christians on your readiness for marriage? Or do you feel\n this is an area that is nobody else's business?
      \n
    • Why should marriage be the only major decision in life for which we ask no advice?\n Probably, those who feel no one can advise them on this question are betraying a belief in\n the eros myth-- \"Others cannot help because they can't possibly tell how deep our\n love feelings are.\"
      • \n
    \n
    3. \n
  3. Why do the authors think parents and older Christians are a source of counsel but not a\n \"chain of authority?\" What is the difference? Do you agree?
      \n
    • Be aware of the desire of some to offload responsibility for tough, \n dangerous and scary decisions. Stress the union of responsibility and \n decision-making authority: Who will live with the outcome of the decision? \n
      • \n
    • Would orthodox Muslim parents ever agree to a Christian marriage?
      • \n
    \n
    4. \n
  4. Are you aware of any time when a troubled marriage was helped by the Christian\n community?
      \n
    • Many married couples report being helped by other Christian married couples. If this is\n not the case in your church, it may be a sad comment on the level of intimacy in that\n community. Perhaps your group will be different!
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n
  5. Is your own involvement in body life superficial? Do you merely go to meetings? Or are\n you vitally involved in relationships and ministry? What are some steps you could take to\n upgrade your level of involvement?
      \n
    • Begin with theological and moral convictions. Find like-minded people, or convince\n others. Begin devoting time and energy to one another in meetings and other venues to\n develop body life.

Chapter 8
\nSexual Love or Exploitation?

\n\n
    \n
  1. The authors say, \"Married sexuality will never conform to the standards of modern\n pornography.\" Name some ways in which married sex is different from pornography and\n different from the sex relationships portrayed in porno-type media.
      \n
    • The stimulation of immorality is absent
      • \n
    • Our physical appearance can rarely measure up to the models in movies and magazines
      • \n
    • Sex with someone you live with and relate to in an ongoing way involves resolving\n conflicts and nurturing good feelings in the relationship, unlike casual sex, where you\n walk away from each other after the act.
      • \n
    • In real, marital sexuality, the insecurities and emotional needs of the other must be\n constantly considered, whereas in the make-believe sex of porno, others have no emotional\n needs other than sexual desire.
      • \n
    • Think of others. . .
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. The authors also list some factors that successful married couples say are key to sexual\n success. For each feature, try to imagine (without getting graphic) what sort of\n problem(s) might be generated if this feature is missing:
    \n
    \n mutual trust [distrust can lead to frigidity or impotence]
    \n
    \n vulnerability [if we sense our partner only wants to relate sexually, not personally, we\n feel used]
    \n
    \n security based on a lifelong commitment [we have difficulty responding freely \n unless we know we are secure]
    \n
    \n ongoing personal sharing [personal closeness leads to sexual responsiveness]
    \n
    \n sexual self-control [inability to control one's sexual desires may lead to harmful\n aggression, masturbation, or lack of sensitivity, all of which may crush sexual\n responsiveness]

  3. Is it carnal to want your spouse to have a pleasant looking body?

In other words, when someone calls on you for \n premarital sex, he is really saying, 'I want to use your body to meet my sexual \n appetite, but I want to remain free to reject you afterward.'\" Can you \n suggest a different motive for premarital sex?

Chapter 10
\nOvercoming Obstacles from the Past: Relationships

\n\n
    \n
  1. DeLashmutt and McCallum say that romantic love is blind. What do they mean by this?\n Drawing from your own experience in this area, do you agree with this assertion?
      \n
    • Eros love feelings are so strong that they tend to create an idealized picture of\n the loved one, filtering out character flaws and other personal problems. Couples who are\n \"in love\" also tend to put their best foot forward and relate to each other in\n very positive settings.
      • \n
    • Perhaps those in the group have experienced sudden and distressing changes in their\n feelings about their romantic partners. Ask them to share this along with how they\n responded to it and what they learned from this experience.
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. How can you gain a more realistic assessment of the other person before marriage?
      \n
    • Delay serious decisions (engagement, marriage) until the eros feelings die down\n enough to get a more objective picture. Sometimes you can do this by taking the dating\n relationship slowly. If the relationship has become too \"hot and heavy\" it may\n be wise to take a break from the relationship in order to get some objectivity.
      • \n
    • Focus on what others say about the person. Ask friends and mature people who know you\n both for some honest feedback.
      • \n
    • Probe more deeply when you see something problematic instead of ignoring it.
      • \n
    \n
    3. \n
  3. Take an honest inventory of the sexual damage you have incurred. What kinds of sexual\n sin have you been involved in? To what extent and over how long a period of time? What\n negative effects can you identify? How much healing has occurred in this area? What\n negative effects should you look out for in marriage?
      \n
    • The key here is to be honest about your problems in this area, while also affirming the\n power of God to change you.
      • \n
    • Close friends are a big help in considering this issue. They will often have insight on\n these questions, and they can also encourage you.
      • \n
    \n
    4. \n
  4. When would be the best time to frankly discuss each other's past sexual involvement?
      \n
    • Shortly before engagement seems best. We should resist lengthy and detailed sharing in\n this area until we know (on the basis of things discussed in this book) we are both ready\n for marriage. This kind of intimate confession tends to deepen couples' commitment to one\n another, whereas the decision to become committed should be made on other bases.
      • \n
    • In the meantime, we should share this area with our close Christian friends.
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n
  5. Can you clearly articulate how you contributed to the failure of your marriage or\n serious romantic relationships? How have you made progress in these areas?
      \n
    • Inability to answer this question may indicate that we need to do some praying and\n careful thinking about this area.
      • \n
    \n
    6. \n
  6. For engaged and married couples, discuss your relationships with both sets of parents.\n Do you foresee or are you experiencing problems with them? If so, have you discussed them?\n Devise a plan for how you can address these problems and still initiate genuine love\n toward them.
      \n
    • Check these areas:
      • \n
    • unresolved resentments that should be forgiven
      • \n
    • lack of loving investment on their part or yours
      • \n
    • failure to stand against parental manipulation
      • \n
    • failure to discipline in love
      • \n

Chapter 12

  1. If you are single, have you ever spent a significant time as a Christian without\n romantic involvement? If so, what has God taught you about trusting him and about your own\n character during this time?

    • Those who have responded to the trial of loneliness properly will be thankful for that\n trial because they have learned such valuable lessons. The lessons may include: increased\n confidence that God will truly meet their needs, a lifestyle built around love-giving\n rather than love-taking, sexual self-control, and transformation of character issues that\n affect relational ability (forgiveness; loving confrontation; emotional sensitivity;\n active listening).
