Chapter 1 The Stakes are High

\r\n

  1. Discovering God begins with a metaphor of a party where\r\n everyone dies within three hours. How do you see this party fitting into the communication\r\n strategy of the book?

    Leader: The party is intended to emphasize the need to consider spiritual things. In an age of\r\n apathy and cynicism, our first task is to persuade non-Christians to engage with us. A\r\n story or metaphor like this may prick a sense of need that could lead to further talk.

    2. \r\n \r\n
  2. How would you react to the suggestion by a non Christian reader that being\r\n ushered into such a party is unfair, and any God who would create such a situation should\r\n not be acknowledged?

    \r\n Ask, \"How do you define fair?\" and \"Where does your sense of fairness come\r\n from?\" Point out that, in reality, God doesn’t actually inject each person with\r\n a virus—the virus is a result of our own autonomy. The amazing thing is that God gets\r\n involved in our bad situation and rescues us.
    3. \r\n \r\n
  3. Aside from the duration of the party being different from the duration of our\r\n lives, can you think of any other differences or discontinuities between the metaphor and\r\n real life?

    \r\n \r\n

    \r\n To be completely fair, we should imagine, not one person with a door he claims leads to\r\n health, but a few hundred all claiming that their door is it. The individual would have to\r\n try to discern which one(s) are more credible. However, we also must stress that just\r\n because there are many false claimants doesn’t mean none of them are true.

  4. For postmodern or relativistic readers, consider a person who refuses to\r\n believe in the reality of the deadly virus and insists that because his belief's are\r\n different, his fate will be different also.

    \r\n His belief's are irrelevant. He will die like like everyone else.
    5. \r\n \r\n
  5. Do your friends have a strong sense of purpose in their lives? If so, what is\r\n it? If not, are they bothered by this lack? Should they be?
    6. \r\n
  6. As Christians, what is the purpose of our lives?
    7. \r\n

  7. A non Christian says, \"A life doesn’t have to be eternal to be\r\n meaningful.\" How would you respond?

    \r\n We should ask, what then is that meaning? If all are here momentarily, and our world is\r\n also momentary in the history of the universe, why would any thing be meaningful in the\r\n ultimate sense?

Making sense

\r\n
\r\n
  1. Many today, including Christians, doubt that reason can tell us much in the spiritual\r\n realm. What do you think?

    Answers to this question will help reveal postmodern attitudes in the group. Consider the illustration with prescription drugs, vs. unknown drugs.

    2. \r\n
  2. Can we deny the validity or the importance of reason but still stick up for\r\n truth?

    \r\n No, we cannot. Truth is only meaningful when juxtaposed to falsehood. Apart from reason,\r\n the true and the false can both be right. Truth only becomes objective when we exclude the possibility that a\r\n thesis and its antithesis cannot both be right at the same time. If\r\n both a truth and its antithesis can be right at the same time, what is a lie anyway?

    Experience

    3. \r\n \r\n
  3. Can you think of any other area of knowledge that requires both reason and\r\n experience?

    \r\n Learning to drive; playing music; speaking a language; appreciating the northern lights.
    4. \r\n \r\n
  4. Do you think some Christian doctrines such as the trinity are beyond or above\r\n reason? Can something be beyond human comprehension but still compatible with rationality?

    \r\n Explain the difference between something that is irrational and something that is\r\n incomprehensible. You may may not comprehend how your copying machine works, but that\r\n doesn’t mean it’s irrational. Likewise, we may not understand some aspects of\r\n the infinite God, but we err if we claim he is irrational. The trinity teaches that God is one in essence,\r\n three in person. This is not irrational, even though we have never experienced such a\r\n thing and cannot comprehend it. The Bible says God cannot tell a lie.
    5. \r\n \r\n
  5. Consider the concept of a square triangle. Can such a thing exist? How do you\r\n know?

    \r\n A contradiction in terms is irrational and self-refuting. The universe is real and created\r\n by a rational God, not a dream state where anything can happen. Eastern gods contradict\r\n themselves, not the God of the Bible.
    6. \r\n
  6. Can you think of any illustration (other than the lost peanut butter jar or drug prescription)\r\n where reasonable faith is used?
  7. Does naturalism (e.g. atheism or agnosticism) require faith? Why or why not?
    8. \r\n
  8. If McCallum is right that no world view can be proved, can we really know\r\n anything? How would you differentiate between Christianity and any other opinion?

    \r\n Faith is required, but reasonable faith is plausible faith—faith that doesn’t\r\n deny itself; faith that answers the main questions facing us. We don’t need to know\r\n with absolute certainty, we have relative certainty.
    9. \r\n \r\n
  9. Is the person who claims to believe in nothing at all actually believing in\r\n something? What about the person who says there are no universal truths?

    \r\n Yes. This person believes that no point of view merits belief. But this is itself a point\r\n of view. To say there are no universal truths is a universal truth claim. This statement\r\n can’t possibly be right!

    \r\n What the color blue is...

    \r\n Love; beauty

God’s Chosen Method of Self-authentication

\r\n
\r\n\r\n
  1. What are the problems and the possible strengths for each of the following means of\r\n authentication for Christianity or any other truth claim?

    \r\n - The evidence of miracles:


    Weakness: Must be authenticated—hard to tell if real. Hard to repeat for every\r\n person. Might be duplicated by occult religion.
    \r\n Strength: points directly to the supernatural.

    \r\n - The evidence of others’ testimony

    Weakness: People make mistakes and they lie. How do we know who to believe among\r\n conflicting testimonies?
    Strength: If we know or trust the person, it can be very convincing.

    - The evidence of historical reliability of the Bible

    Weakness: Can be complicated and hard to explain. Does not point to inspiration\r\n necessarily. Only convincing if people accept our methodology.
    Strength: Depending on one’s presuppositions, can tip the scale in favor of\r\n belief.

    - Rational consistency with its own propositions

    Weakness: This only shows consistency with a given paradigm, therefore not necessarily\r\n binding on other paradigms. Some don’t value consistency.
    Strength: Can set Christianity apart from other self-refuting views.

    - Correlation between the claims made in a religious text and provable things outside that\r\n text (eg. in nature)

    Weakness: Only demonstrates that some parts of a text are valid, not all parts.\r\n Requires interpretation of the text.
    Strength: Can point directly to the reliability of the Bible if handled well.

    - The historical argument for the resurrection of Christ

    Weakness: Can be hard to explain or defend. Relies on convincing non Christians that\r\n it’s more likely that someone rose from the dead than that religious people would\r\n lie.
    Strength: Depending on one’s starting point, it can move someone from general\r\n respect for Christ and the Bible to personal faith. When handled by a master rhetorician\r\n it can and has led many to Christ (e.g. Lee Strobel, The Case for Christ).

    - The argument that Jesus must be true because I have experienced him in my heart

    Weakness: The same argument could be advanced by Buddhists, New Agers, and Cultists.
    Strength: Supremely convincing to the one having the experience. Also convincing to\r\n others if they trust the one with the experience.

    - The argument that God alone can tell the future course of human history, and has\r\n done so in the Bible.

    Weakness: Can be difficult to explain, and requires study. It also requires interpretation of\r\n biblical texts. Must independently verify the dates of the texts and that fulfillment actually\r\n occurred. Finally must be able to rule out deliberate fulfillment (e.g. Jesus riding into\r\n Jerusalem on a colt of a donkey could have been deliberate fulfillment of Zech 9:9).
    Strength: Can point directly and exclusively to Jesus Christ and the Bible as unique in the world. If handled\r\n well, the few predictions that meet all these conditions can be very convincing, even to\r\n skeptics if they come to believe that our interpretations of the passages are fair.

    2. \r\n \r\n

    \r\n Nature- leaves too much up to human interpretation
    \r\n Verbal testimony- unreliable \r\n etc. . .

    5. \r\n

    \r\n No, you don’t    . There are no others.

Chapter 4 The Time of Christ’s Coming Predicted

\r\n

Daniel 9 is a remarkable prophecy, but some people steer away from it because it is\r\n complicated. Yet, as you can see from this chapter of Christianity: The Faith That\r\n Makes Sense, it is possible to summarize and simplify the passage enough to use\r\neffectively in evangelism.

\r\n

Let’s look at Daniel chapter 9 and gain a better understanding of the background\r\nand basic interpretive issues:

\r\n
  1. Daniel 9:1 dates this vision as being in \"In the first year of Darius son of\r\n Xerxes.\" We have no record of Darius, but we know Xerxes well from history. Since\r\n Daniel says Darius \"was made ruler\" we can assume he was a viceroy serving under\r\n Xerxes, the new Persian ruler. Look in a Bible dictionary and find out when Xerxes\r\n conquered Babylon.

    \r\n For most of these, just try to ascertain that members are doing the work and see what\r\n their answers are.
    2. \r\n \r\n
  2. Daniel says his reflection began when he read in Jeremiah the number of years\r\n for the Jewish exile in Babylon. (See Jeremiah 25:11) Considering that Daniel was taken\r\n into exile with the first group of captives in about 605 BC, (and remembering that years\r\n go down instead of up in the BC period) about how many years had passed since his capture?

    \r\n About 70 years, especially when including the Hebrew tendency to count any part of a year\r\n as a whole.
    3. \r\n \r\n
  3. As Daniel prays and confesses the sins of the people, he also mentions the\r\n curse that Moses warned them about. Read Leviticus 26:33-35 in context. Their\r\n unfaithfulness had resulted in their exile, but somehow the \"sabbatical year\"\r\n was involved as well (see chapter 24:1-6). This link is important, because the Sabbaths of\r\n years in the last part of the chapter have this as their context. Read also IIChronicles\r\n 36:20-22. Here again, we see reference to the Jeremiah prediction, to Xerxes, and to the\r\n sabbatical years, of which 70 had apparently been ignored.

    \r\n Some translations merely says \"70 weeks\" instead of \"70 sevens\" thus\r\n making it harder to understand. See margin of NASB.
    4. \r\n \r\n
  4. In verse 24, Gabriel says God will allow the Jewish people 70\r\n \"sevens\" or shabuim (from which we get the word \"Sabbath\"). Now\r\n you can see why the context dictates that these be Sabbaths of years, not of days. Now\r\n read the list of things the Jewish people must do to complete their commission. For each\r\n item, write one or more possibilities for what it might refer to.

    \r\n finish transgression
    Bring in the millenium?

    \r\n put an end to sin
    Bring in the millenium?

    \r\n atone for wickedness
    The work of Christ

    \r\n bring in everlasting righteousness
    Could refer to the church or to the millenium

    \r\n to seal up vision and prophecy
    Complete the Old Testament, and perhaps the whole Bible

    \r\n anoint the most holy
    Either re-consecrating the rebuilt temple, or annointing Christ (his baptism?)

    5. \r\n \r\n
  5. In verse 25 some versions refer to the coming of Messiah, and others refer to\r\n \"The Anointed One. In Hebrew this word is Meshiach, from which we get Messiah.\r\n Anointing the \"most holy\" (verse 24, NIV) could be a reference to the Messiah as\r\n well.
    6. \r\n
  6. We will not attempt to dissect the chronology of years here, although this has\r\n already been done in fms. Go over that section again, including the footnotes to make sure\r\n you understand it. Since the rebuilding of Jerusalem occurred in the 400’s BC, it\r\n should be clear that the coming of Messiah is about 483 years from some time in the fifth\r\n century BC. Without going into detail, this alone should make it clear that the\r\n fulfillment falls in the early first century. See the works referenced in the notes for\r\n more detail on the two main ways to interpret the passage.

Chapter 11 For Further Reading: Objections to the Biblical Worldview

\r\n

This chapter is easily the longest in the book, so if you are using this guide for\r\ngroup study, consider dividing this chapter into more than one week, or be selective about which questions you cover.

\r\n\r\n

Remember that responding to objections to Christianity is not the heart of evangelism.\r\nIn fact, we should only answer these common complaints after we have\r\nalready clearly shared the gospel itself. We may find it all too easy to become\r\nside-tracked onto these issues and never actually come to grips with the gospel message of\r\ngrace. On the other hand, after we have declared the gospel, we are obligated to give an\r\naccount for our belief—that is, to answer honest questions to the best of our\r\nability. This is why the book has been arranged with the objections at the end.

\r\n\r\n\r\n

What about other religions?

\r\n
    \r\n \r\n
  1. Your non Christian friend at work says, \"It really kills me that you Christians\r\n think you are right and everyone else in the world is wrong. What arrogance!\" How\r\n would you respond?

    \"Well, someone must be wrong. . .\"
    \r\n \"Are you saying you are right and I am wrong?\"

    2. \r\n
  2. A friend of yours is about to walk off the top floor of a 10 story building\r\n because he thinks he can fly. Would it be arrogant of you to intervene to try to prevent\r\n him from walking off? Why or why not?
    3. \r\n
  3. If I claim to have knowledge, am I being arrogant? What about Jesus, Paul, or\r\n Moses?

    Ignorance is no guarantee of humility. Knowlege doesn't necessarily mean arrogance.

    4. \r\n \r\n
  4. While you may not think you are being arrogant when you claim to know\r\n universal truths, you may be perceived that way by postmodern hearers. What do you plan to\r\n do to minimize this problem?

    Admit areas where you don’t know. Admit good parts of other views. Other\r\n ideas. . .

    5. \r\n \r\n
  5. Again, when your postmodern friend hears you point out that Christianity and\r\n other religions contradict one another, she suggests that such contradiction is only on\r\n the rational level—the level of dogma. What really matters is an experience of the\r\n divine or a sense of the sacred. How would you respond?

    The David Koresh example speaks to this. Think of others. \"Heaven's gate\" \"Jonestown\" etc.

    6. \r\n

Otherwise,\r\n why would we reject some but not others?\" This could be a key insight. If true, it\r\n means that once our hearers denounce any religion as false, they have crossed a threshold\r\n where critical issues matter. We can now call on them to be consistent by going the next\r\n step and re-assessing all religion. How does McCallum attempt to bring people this\r\n threshold? How will you do so?

    \r\n McCallum cites several of the most extreme and outlandish examples of religion first\r\n (head-hunting, etc.) and gradually moves to more common religions (Hindu teaching that\r\n women cannot go to heaven). Note that the rejection of even one extreme religion is\r\n enough to cross the threshold. Note also, that the rejection of Christianity (even though\r\n based on its’ absolutism) also constitutes crossing the threshold.
