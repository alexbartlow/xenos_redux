By Dennis McCallum and Gary DeLashmutt

Leader's introduction

\n

This study guide follows chapter by chapter, the book, Spiritual Relationships \n That Last, by Gary DeLashmutt and Dennis McCallum. We refer to ourselves \n in the third person in order to keep the focus on the text. The guide \n is arranged for use in any one of four venues: \n

    \n
  • Individual enrichment
    • \n
  • Couples premarital study
    • \n
  • Married couples' enrichment
    • \n
  • Group study
    • \n
\n

We have seen excellent results using this book as a basis for small group \n as well as in premarital counseling. If using Spiritual Relationships \n That Last as a premarital or marital counseling aid, you may want \n to read the chapter together before going over the questions. For group \n leaders, the facilitator's guide provides suggested answers or tips to \n key questions. The other three venues will have no use for the facilitator's \n guide.

\n

Hundreds of couples planning their marriage have reported this study \n brought to light issues God wanted to call to their attention. May God \n use the material to richly bless your present or future marriage as well \n as the other relationships in your life.

\n

How to lead a group study based on this book:

\n

First read the chapter from the book. Then come to this guide to deepen \n and apply the principles you learn in the book. You can obtain copies \n of Spiritual Relationships That Last for each member of the group \n either at your bookstore, or you get quantity pricing from the Xenos webstore. \n Also, get both this facilitator's guide and the discussion guide. Copy \n the pages from the discussion guide and pass them out, preferably a week \n before the discussion on a given chapter. The facilitator may need to \n select which questions to cover on a given chapter because covering all \n questions may take too long.

\n

You can download for free either the leader's version or the group member \n or counselee version (missing suggested answers) from the Web site mentioned \n above, or call 800-698-7884 and request they be sent to your address. \n Hard copies are not free like soft copies, because paper, reproduction, \n mailing, and handling all cost. We charge our cost only for hard copies. \n

\n

Please feel free to send us feedback about problems or positive results \n by e-mail.

Chapter 1
\n Marriage Success Today:
\n What Are Your Chances?

  1. Are you from an intact, or a broken family? How do you think this \n may have affected you, and how have you adjusted?

  2. How could you imagine divorce possibly impacting children and their \n future marriages? \n

    • Single parent families offer decreased nurture to kids creating \n adjustment problems.

      • \n
    • Unless divorcees area cooperative with each other, kids may get \n a negative view of the opposite sex.

Chapter 2
\n Is \"Love\" Enough?

\n
    \n
  1. Have you ever had the experience of believing you were in love with \n someone, only to find later that you no longer had any interest in that \n person? How do you interpret such an experience? \n
      \n
    • At the very least, this must suggest the unreliability of eros \n love as a guide to the \"will of God\" or as a guide \n to finding everlasting love.
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. If a couple are \"in love,\" shouldn't they get married? Why, \n or why not? \n
      \n
    • This question reveals people's reasons for getting married.
      • \n
    • What would be a good reason for getting married if not being in \n love?
      • \n
    • Ask the group, \"What does being in love mean?\"
      • \n
    \n
    3. \n
  3. Have you ever known a couple who believed they were in love and got \n married, only to fail? If so, what do you think accounts for the failure? \n
      \n
    • Answers to this question could reveal more about how people interpret \n eros mythology. Try to summarize the principle their answers \n imply.
      • \n
    \n
    4. \n
  4. McCallum and DeLashmutt think Christian love has to do with serving \n others more than it does with feelings. What do you think of the passages \n they cite on p. (The good Samaritan, 1 John 3:17) to back this up? \n
      \n
    • Observe that the Samaritan both felt compassion and did \n something about it.
      • \n
    • For those who think love is mainly a feeling, ask how this can \n be reconciled with the biblical material. Isn't our modern definition \n of love more focused on feeling and sentiment than the biblical \n view which focuses more on serving and giving?
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n
  5. The authors mention married couples whose \"love seems to desert \n them\" after marriage. Are you aware of any cases where this occurred? \n If so, what does it mean? \n
      \n
    • Under the Eros myth, it might suggest they married by mistake \n and that their love was not \"the real thing.\"
      • \n
    • To biblical Christians, it should suggest the inadequacy of Erosfeelings \n for discovering eternal love.
      • \n
    \n
    6. \n
  6. Are you aware of any cases where a married person came to believe \n he or she was in love with someone other than their spouse? If so, what \n lesson(s) can be drawn from such episodes? \n
      \n
    • Again, Eros feelings for another woman or man are impossible to \n distinguish from Eros feelings we originally felt for our spouse. \n
      • \n
    \n
    7. \n
  7. Your friend says, \"I wouldn't buy a car without driving it around \n the block, and I wouldn't marry someone without a trial marriage.\" \n How would you assess such a statement? \n
      \n
    • That which is reasonable when buying a machine is hardly appropriate \n for a personal being.
      • \n
    • Note the \"self-first\" ethos in this thought: \"I \n have to check to make sure I'm getting all I can get.\" This \n direction is seeking to take, not to give.
      • \n
    \n
    8. \n

Chapter 4
\n Learning Christian Love

\n
    \n
  1. Do you agree with the authors that people need the experience of building \n one or more successful intimate relationships (nonromantic) before they \n can feel confident they will succeed in marriage? Why or why not? \n
      \n
    • For those who answer no, why do they think they will be able to \n do something in marriage that they have not been able to do before \n marriage? (See #3 below.)
      • \n
    • For those who feel intimate relationships with dating partners \n is adequate, ask whether they believe the feelings that now buttress \n that relationship will continue to do so within marriage? Do they \n feel they are different than other married couples?
      • \n
    \n
    2. \n
  2. Can you suggest an alternative capability that would point toward \n likely marriage success?
    3. \n
  3. Respond to the authors' question: \"If we are not successfully \n loving people at the intimate level before marriage, what makes us think \n we will be able to do so after marriage?\"
    4. \n
  4. The authors also say: \"Likewise, if we are married, but find \n no examples of intimate relationship outside our marriage, perhaps the \n problem is not with our spouse after all!\" What are they suggesting? \n Do you agree? \n
      \n
    • Married people having relational problems tend to blame their \n spouses. But if none of their other relationships are successful \n at an intimate level, perhaps they themselves are unable to relate \n at an intimate level.
      • \n
    • Help your married members turn their eyes away from blaming their \n spouses and onto themselves.
      • \n
    \n
    5. \n
  5. Do your own relational inventory. What areas do you see as areas of \n strength? What areas need improvement?

    • Why should marriage be the only major decision in life for which \n we ask no advice? Probably, those who feel no one can advise them \n on this question are betraying a belief in the Eros myth—\"Others \n cannot help because they can't possibly tell how deep our love feelings \n are.\"

  3. Why do the authors think parents and older Christians are a source \n of counsel but not a \"chain of authority?\" What is the difference? \n Do you agree? \n

    • Be aware of the desire of some to offload responsibility for tough, \n dangerous and scary decisions. Stress the union of responsibility \n and decision-making authority: Who will live with the outcome of \n the decision?

      • \n

  5. Is your own involvement in body life superficial? Do you merely go \n to meetings? Or are you vitally involved in relationships and ministry? \n What are some steps you could take to upgrade your level of involvement? \n
      \n
    • Begin with theological and moral convictions. Find like-minded \n people, or convince others. Begin devoting time and energy to one \n another in meetings and other venues to develop body life.

    Chapter 10
    \n Overcoming Obstacles from the Past: Relationships

    \n
      \n
    1. DeLashmutt and McCallum say romantic love is blind. What do they mean \n by this? Drawing from your own experience in this area, do you agree \n with this assertion? \n

      • Eros love feelings are so strong that they tend to create \n an idealized picture of the loved one, filtering out character flaws \n and other personal problems. Couples who are \"in love\" \n also tend to put their best foot forward and relate to each other \n in very positive settings.
        • \n
      • Perhaps those in the group have experienced sudden and distressing \n changes in their feelings about their romantic partners. Ask them \n to share this along with how they responded to it and what they \n learned from this experience.
        \n
        \n

    2. How can you gain a more realistic assessment of the other person before \n marriage? \n
        \n
      • Delay serious decisions (engagement, marriage) until the Eros \n feelings die down enough to get a more objective picture. Sometimes \n you can do this by taking the dating relationship slowly. If the \n relationship has become too \"hot and heavy,\" it may be \n wise to take a break from the relationship in order to get some \n objectivity.
        • \n
      • Focus on what others say about the person. Ask friends and mature \n people who know you both for some honest feedback.
        • \n
      • Probe more deeply when you see something problematic instead of \n ignoring it.
        \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      3. \n
    3. Take an honest inventory of the sexual damage you have incurred. What \n kinds of sexual sin have you been involved in? To what extent and over \n how long a period of time? What negative effects can you identify? How \n much healing has occurred in this area? What negative effects should \n you look out for in marriage? \n
        \n
      • The key here is to be honest about your problems in this area, \n while also affirming the power of God to change you.
        • \n
      • Close friends are a big help in considering this issue. They will \n often have insight on these questions, and they can also encourage \n you.
        \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      4. \n
    4. When would be the best time to frankly discuss each other's past sexual \n involvement? \n
        \n
      • Shortly before engagement seems best. We should resist lengthy \n and detailed sharing in this area until we know (based on things \n discussed in this book) we are both ready for marriage. This kind \n of intimate confession tends to deepen couples' commitment to one \n another, whereas the decision to become committed should be made \n on other bases.
        • \n
      • In the meantime, we should share this area with our close Christian \n friends.
        \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      5. \n
    5. Can you clearly articulate how you contributed to the failure of your \n marriage or serious romantic relationships? How have you made progress \n in these areas? \n
        \n
      • Inability to answer this question may indicate that we need to \n do some praying and careful thinking about this area.
        \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      6. \n
    6. For engaged and married couples, discuss your relationships with both \n sets of parents. Do you foresee or are you experiencing problems with \n them? If so, have you discussed them? Devise a plan for how you can \n address these problems and still initiate genuine love toward them. \n
        \n
      • Check these areas:
        • \n
      • unresolved resentments that should be forgiven
        • \n
      • lack of loving investment on their part or yours
        • \n
      • failure to stand against parental manipulation
        • \n
      • failure to discipline in love
        • \n
      \n
      7. \n
    \n

    Chapter 11
    \n Overcoming Obstacles From the Past: Addictions, Materialism, and Psychological \n Damage

    \n
      \n
    1. For those who have a history of alcohol or drug abuse, consider the \n following questions:
      \n How did this sin area affect your relational life?
      \n
      \n How long have you been free from actual abuse?
      \n
      \n How have you replaced the stimulation you got from drug or alcohol abuse?
      \n
      \n How are your relationships now different from when you abused?
      \n
      \n
      2. \n
    2. Take an honest inventory of the questions posed concerning materialism:
      \n
      \n Do you experience conflict between materialistic and spiritual desires?
      \n
      \n What makes up your private fantasies of the \"ideal life?\"
      \n
      \n How do you habitually respond when spiritual and secular responsibilities \n and opportunities conflict?
      \n
      \n Are you able to be content with what you have materially? Do you consciously \n distinguish between \"wants\" and \"needs?\" What is \n your history of consumer credit debt?
      \n
      \n How regularly and generously do you give your money to God's service?
      \n
      \n What are your short-term and long-term spiritual goals?
      \n
      \n How do you view retirement?
      \n
      \n
      3. \n
    3. For seriously dating or engaged couples, discuss all history of serious \n psychological problems. What help have you received for these problems? \n To what extent are they under control? \n
        \n
      • Problems are \"under control\" when they do not substantially \n interfere with our ability to sustain close love relationships. \n
        • \n
      \n
      4. \n
    \n

    Chapter 12
    \n Personal Maturity

    \n
      \n
    1. If you are single, have you ever spent a significant time as a Christian \n without romantic involvement? If so, what has God taught you about trusting \n him and about your own character during this time? \n
        \n
      • Those who have responded to the trial of loneliness properly will \n be thankful for that trial because they have learned such valuable \n lessons. The lessons may include: increased confidence that God \n will truly meet their needs, a lifestyle built around love-giving \n rather than love-taking, sexual self-control, and transformation \n of character issues that affect relational ability (forgiveness, \n loving confrontation, emotional sensitivity and active listening). \n
        \n
        \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      2. \n
    2. If you are serious dating or engaged, explain why you are confident \n that your partner is committed to spiritual maturity. \n
        \n
      • Watch to see if the answers are only subjective (I can just feel \n his love for God) or if they include the objective. We should be \n able to point to concrete things like successful close love relationships \n with other people, deepening involvement in the Word, fellowship, \n prayer, and Christian ministry.
        \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      3. \n
    3. Take inventory of your maturity in functional responsibility: fiscal \n management and performance at school and work. If you have problems \n here, what practical steps can you take to improve?
