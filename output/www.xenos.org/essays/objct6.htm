26 Geoffrey Parrinder, Worship in the World's Religions, p. 27 We would have to\nside with Parrinder here against Frazer. The argument referred to runs thus, \"But if\nin the most backward state of human society now known to us we find magic thus\nconspicuously present and religion conspicuously absent, may we not reasonably conjecture\nthat the civilized races of the world have also at some period of their history passed\nthrough a similar intellectual phase, that they attempted to force the great powers of\nnature to do their pleasure before they thought of courting their favor by offerings and\nprayer--in short that, just as on the material side of human culture there has everywhere\nbeen an Age of Stone, so on the intellectual side there has everywhere been an Age of\nMagic?\" Sir James George Frazer, The Golden Bough, (New York, NY.: Macmillan\nCompany, 1940) p.55

\n\n

This argument, cited here in full because it is typical of the attitude of many\nscholars, is based on conjecture and misinformation. There is never an absence of religion\nin \"primitive\" societies, nor is there an absence of magic in the so-called\n\"civilized races of the world\", as this paper hopefully shows.
