145Ensign is the author who argues this case most strongly. Yet he is not entirely\nconvincing. The elements he mentions as belonging to Boehmism include;
\n\"The Christian is to have no sect. . .\" (19) This could also have come from an\nidealistic reading of many passages in the Bible (Eph. 4:3ff). There are certainly many\nmodern theologians who have this view without reference to Boehme.
\n\"Predestination to damnation was a blasphemous idea,\" (20) but Spener and many\nothers (such as the Quakers) thought the same thing.
\n\"One must `put on Christ' and experience the `process' of the incarnation within\none's self,\" (20) but it is sometimes a fine line between this language and Pauline\nnotions of identification with Christ (II Cor. 4:7-18). The process idea is also typical\nof other German mystics. Lordship theology was typical of all pietists.
\n\"Sophia mysticism,\" Ensign admits is \"a variation on the typical medieval\nlove-phantasy theme, based on the Song of Solomon.\" (20,21) Indeed this theme was\nvery common in the medieval period, as well as after the reformation (see \"Other\nArndtians\" below). Besides, Ensign admits, \"some [radical pietists] abstained\nfrom either of these sentimental cults,\" (21).
\n\"Celibacy as a spiritually more desirable state than marriage,\" (21) but this\nwas hardly new with Boehme. One need never have heard of Boehme to have this view, which\nhad dominated the middle ages, and is clearly evident in the earliest monastic writers.
\n\"Religion should not be controlled in any way by the state,\" (21) but this was\nthe automatic cry of all those who were being persecuted by the state for Religion.
\nFinally, Ensign not only admits, \"Other origins may be found for many, if not most,\nof these ideas. . .Boehme himself was a part of the mystical-spiritualist tradition.\"\np. 21, but also \"They [the radical pietists] did not consider themselves Boehmists as\nsuch in most instances,\" and \"Writers presenting Boehme's ideas rarely\nacknowledged their dependence on him.\" p. 21.
\nOne must ask at this point whether sufficient value is left of the theory when it is so\nqualified that virtually nothing remains? Unless there is other material that was not\nproduced, this author remains unconvinced that Boehme is the main source of the radical's\ntheology.
