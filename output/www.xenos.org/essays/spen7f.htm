145Ensign is the author who argues this case most strongly. Yet \n he is not entirely convincing. The elements he mentions as belonging \n to Boehmism include;
\n \"The Christian is to have no sect. . .\" (19) This could \n also have come from an idealistic reading of many passages in the \n Bible (Eph. 4:3ff). There are certainly many modern theologians \n who have this view without reference to Boehme.
\n \"Predestination to damnation was a blasphemous idea,\" \n (20) but Spener and many others (such as the Quakers) thought the \n same thing.
\n \"One must `put on Christ' and experience the `process' of the \n incarnation within one's self,\" (20) but it is sometimes a \n fine line between this language and Pauline notions of identification \n with Christ (II Cor. 4:7-18). The process idea is also typical of \n other German mystics. Lordship theology was typical of all pietists.
\n \"Sophia mysticism,\" Ensign admits is \"a variation \n on the typical medieval love-phantasy theme, based on the Song of \n Solomon.\" (20,21) Indeed this theme was very common in the \n medieval period, as well as after the reformation (see \"Other \n Arndtians\" below). Besides, Ensign admits, \"some [radical \n pietists] abstained from either of these sentimental cults,\" \n (21).
\n \"Celibacy as a spiritually more desirable state than marriage,\" \n (21) but this was hardly new with Boehme. One need never have heard \n of Boehme to have this view, which had dominated the middle ages, \n and is clearly evident in the earliest monastic writers.
\n \"Religion should not be controlled in any way by the state,\" \n (21) but this was the automatic cry of all those who were being \n persecuted by the state for Religion.
\n Finally, Ensign not only admits, \"Other origins may be found \n for many, if not most, of these ideas. . .Boehme himself was a part \n of the mystical-spiritualist tradition.\" p. 21, but also \"They \n [the radical pietists] did not consider themselves Boehmists as \n such in most instances,\" and \"Writers presenting Boehme's \n ideas rarely acknowledged their dependence on him.\" p. 21. \n
\n One must ask at this point whether sufficient value is left of the \n theory when it is so qualified that virtually nothing remains? Unless \n there is other material that was not produced, this author remains \n unconvinced that Boehme is the main source of the radical's theology. \n
