This paper surveys the origins and development of one of the most interesting\nunderground movements of the pre-reformation period. They were not evangelical in\ndoctrine, particularly in their soteriology, but they had important connections to later\nreform movements. Specifically, they earned the ire of the Roman church because they\ntranslated the Bible into the vernacular of their day, and allowed lay people to read it\nand preach it. They formed house churches all over Europe in spite of bitter persecution,\nincluding genocidal military campaigns. Their history contains important lessons for those\nwho would champion lay ministry today.
