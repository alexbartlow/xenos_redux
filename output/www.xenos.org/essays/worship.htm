By Lee Campbell, PhD.

\n

INTRODUCTION
\n If it were not for the traditional \n use of the word 'worship' amongst Christians, substantial arguments could \n be raised against its continued use. For one thing, the denotative meaning \n of 'worship' is different from the related Greek and Hebrew terms. For \n another, the connotative meaning of 'worship' in the Christian community \n is substantially different from the biblical teaching on the topic. Since \n the evangelical Christian community is committed to the use of the term \n worship, it certainly bears careful examination so that we mean what God \n intends when we use it.

\n

'WORSHIP' IS AN INADEQUATE \n TRANSLATION OF THE GREEK AND HEBREW TERMS
\n Our word worship derives from the Old English \n weordhscipe meaning worthiness or meritoriousness and thus giving \n God the recognition He deserves. There are some problems with this English \n translation, however, because the Greek & Hebrew terms do not mean precisely \n the same thing.

\n \n

'aboda (Hebrew; also - abad \n or asab) and latreia (Greek; also - latreuo) are \n frequently translated as worship. Although, these are not the only words \n translated worship and even these words are not always translated, 'worship'2. \n When translated as worship in the OT these words typically mean service \n associated with the work done in the temple. In the NT the related Greek \n term latreia either refers back to the OT temple cultus3, \n to the false belief that killing disciples would be regarded as service \n to God4 \n or as an OT allusion that Christians should offer their own bodies (i.e. \n meaning 'life') to God as a sacrifice.

\n \n
\n
\n

Therefore I urge you, brethren, \n by the mercies of God, to present your bodies a living and holy sacrifice, \n acceptable to God, which is your spiritual service of worship.5 \n (Rom 12:1)

\n
\n
\n \n

Paul uses the related \n Greek word leitourgia (i.e. translated service) to refer to a monetary \n gift collected for the Jerusalem Christians6 \n and for the assistance he received from others7 \n and the term leitourgos (i.e. translated serves or servant) is \n used of Christ8, \n angels9, \n rulers10, \n Epaphroditus's delivery of the Philippian gift11, \n and of ministry to the Gentiles12. \n Thus, the term latreia and its cognates are directly associated \n with both service & sacrifice when directed toward God. It might have \n been better if the translators had chosen the words 'serve', 'service' \n and 'minister' instead of worship.

\n

Other terms are \n translated as worship including the Greek word proskyneo13 \n and its Hebrew equivalent shachac.14 \n Both of these terms refer to a posture of submission and thus an acknowledgment \n of God's sovereignty. Obeisance requires an attitude of reverential fear. \n This is evident in the behaviors of bending the knee (i.e. gonu \n or gonupeteo) and bowing down (histahawa or shachac \n {Heb.} or proskyneo {Gk. to kiss forward}) which are associated \n with worship. It should be noted, however, that these postures are associated \n with other things too (i.e. one can have this attitude in petitions to \n God, gods or man).

\n

The terms that communicate \n attitudes of service, submission & reverence (i.e. which are translated \n 'worship') do not indicate how such attitudes ought to be shown. We must \n rely on context to specify ways that God expects us to worship Him. When \n we examine the behavior of worshippers what do we see? Worship may be \n personal or corporate. It took place at and apart from the temple, however, \n God doesn't intend that worship be connected with place but with the heart \n attitude (Jn.4:20ff spirit & truth). It requires unity between believers \n (Mt.5:23f; Luke 10:25ff). An important term associated with worship is \n homologia.15 \n Worshippers frequently spend some or all of their time professing faith, \n praising God, thanking God and proclaiming truths about God.

\n

Another term often \n associated with worship is thusia meaning sacrifice. The reason \n this term should be connected to worship is that such references are allusions \n to the thank- offerings in the OT which were an important daily ritual \n of temple worship.16 \n Interestingly, in the cases where this term is used in the epistles it \n is usually in reference to self-sacrifice in the service of others.17 \n

But even if I am being \n poured out as a drink offering upon the sacrifice and service18 \n of your faith, I rejoice and share my joy with you all. Phil 2:17

'WORSHIP' IS ASSUMED TO \n INVOLVE SINGING AND PRAISING GOD BY MOST BELIEVERS
\n In Putting an \n End to Worship Wars, Elmer Towns asked the questions: What do you \n do in worship? How do you worship? What motivates you to worship? and \n What are the results of worship? From the responses he identified six \n worship styles in America: Evangelistic - winning the lost; Expositional \n - teaching the word; Renewal - excitement, revival, 'touching God'; Body-life \n - fellowship, relationships and small groups; Liturgical - serving & glorifying \n God through liturgy and Congregational - worship expressed by the laity.

\n

Of these, the renewal, liturgical, \n congregational approaches are arguably what most Christians conceive of \n as worship - times of personal and corporate singing and praise. As we \n have seen, however, the biblical principles and commands related to worship \n are much broader and more integrated into the Christian life. Worship \n is the response of grateful and humble people to the living God where \n submission, sacrificial service, praise, profession, testimony and gratitude \n are freely expressed in innumerable ways. This is a much richer concept \n than mere corporate singing and praise once each week for 20 minutes - \n an event that could occur without any actual worship going on at all. \n

\n \n

CONCLUSION
\n As long as we are \n culturally bound to the unfortunate English word, 'worship,' we need to \n keep our understanding biblical rather than anchored in church traditions. \n A worshipper must be a humble person who is willing to:

\n
    \n
  • Subordinate \n their goals to God's goals by prioritizing service toward the Kingdom \n of God;
    • \n
  • Express \n gratitude and praise toward God and
    • \n
  • Tell others \n about God, his truths and the love they have enjoyed as His child. \n
