Introduction

\n

The defense offered by Steven in Acts 7 has confused interpreters as much as any\n passage in the book of Acts. In his excellent commentary on Acts, Howard Marshal says,

\n

. . . the purpose of this speech is still much disputed. In form it is a lengthy\n recital of Old Testament history, discussing in detail what appear to be insignificant\n points and culminating in a bitter attack on the speaker's hearers. What is the speaker\n trying to do?\" 1

\n

later he complains,

\n

. . .it is not clear what the theological point of the details is.\" 2

\n

These are striking statements, coming, as they do, from one who demonstrates keen\n insight throughout the text of Acts. Yet, they are hardly unusual. Dibelius is much\n stronger,The irrelevance of this speech has for long been the real problem of exegesis. It\n is, indeed, impossible to find a connection between the account of the history of Israel\n to the time of Moses (7:2-19) and the accusations against Stephen. . .The major part of\n the speech shows no purpose whatever. . .

\n

and finally,

\n

The most striking feature of this speech is the irrelevance of its main section.3

\n

Gealy asks, \". . . How are we to explain the fact that Steven does not really\n speak to the charges brought against him?\"4 In fact, Gealy thinks that the whole text\n is so unrelated to the flow of the Steven narrative that 7:1-53 must be one of three\n conflated traditions, namely \"an anti-Jewish polemic which Luke fashioned into a\n speech for his purposes.\"5 He thinks that the only point made in the speech is that\n the history of Israel is \"a history of continual disobedience to God\".6

\n

Unfortunately, Gealy has missed the point of this brilliant speech. The speech is\n certainly not anti-semitic,7 and the fact that Israel was disobedient to the law, though\n mentioned, plays a subordinate role in the thought development. It is only certain kinds\n of disobedience that Stephen focuses on. In fact, this speech is the lynch-pin in the\n transition between Christianity as a localized ethnic religion, and a universal body.8\n Stephen the Hellenist was able to see the implications of the Gospel to an extraordinary\n degree. 9

\n

While there are confusing sections in the speech, there are also sections that are well\n understood by modern interpreters. Commonly accepted motifs are: \n

    \n
  1. The history of Israel demonstrates ineptitude (on the part of the leadership especially)\n in seeing the hand of God and in recognizing His agents.
    2. \n
  2. The Temple cultus has been misinterpreted all along, and should now be set aside.\n 10These motifs are clearly argued in this speech. This author has no objection to either\n application. On the other hand, there must be more involved in the argument, because much\n of the detailed material in the speech does not address these two points. There is still a\n substantial body of detailed Old Testament material not included in the argument for these\n two obvious points. It is this extra material (some of it strange), that is the focus of\n this study.
    3. \n
\n\n

The Missing Message

\n

The key to understanding the inclusion of seemingly irrelevant detail in Stephen's\n speech is to realize that they deal with geography. The speech is overly concerned with\n geography, and with the people's and God's relationship to it.

\n

It is the argument of this paper that a mistake is being made by interpreters when the\n second purpose of Stephen's speech is limited to answering charges regarding the temple.\n The nucleus of the thinking here is not only a response to the temple, but to the broader\n and more foundational concept of sacred space or sacred land.11 Stephen will argue that\n the notion of sacred land, used by God in the past for communication purposes, was not,\n and should not be viewed as a limitation upon Him. \"God can work just fine without\n the use of any sacred space,\" is Stephen's argument.

\n

Only this broader understanding will account for all of the material in the speech, as\n well as explaining the strategy Stephen follows in refuting the charges made against him.\n One of the charges brought against Stephen was that he speaks against, \"this\n place,\" (6:13).12 Marshal thinks that the accusation that Stephen blasphemed\n \"this holy place\" (tou hagiou topou toutou vs.14) is a reference to the temple.\n This may be correct, even though the examples he cites do not use the word topos, but\n naos.13

\n

Whether the phrase refers exclusively to the temple, or to the sacred area around it\n (Jerusalem, Mt. Zion or even Israel) it seems clear that the greater question of sacred\n space in the sense of sacred land had come up in the debate earlier.14It would be hard to\n exaggerate the centrality of the sacred land of Israel in the understanding of the\n covenantal blessings at the heart of Judaism at this time.15 At the heart of\n ethnic-religious world views are the twin values of \"Race and Space,\" or to put\n it differently, \"Blood and Soil.\"

\n

These values are hardly unique to first century Judaism. The same terms have been used\n to understand thought systems such as Naziism. Unfortunately, they would adequately\n summarize the ethos of much of modern western Christianity. These values can be dangerous\n substitutes for real spirituality, and may stand as barriers to a truly universal, loving,\n outreaching, and caring form of Christianity. Most of the book of Acts is concerned with\n God's efforts to overcome the inertia that emanates from the unbalanced application of\n these two values in the name of religion.

\n

By speaking to the issue of sacred space, including the sacred city and sacred land of\n Israel, Stephen's speech lays a firm ideological foundation for the subsequent movement of\n the focus of God's work away from both in the following chapters. Immediately, we find\n Philip putting into action the lateral movement that Stephen's analysis of Scripture so\n beautifully justified.

\n

Having asserted this theme, we can now briefly note how it is developed in the text of\n Stephen's speech.

\n

Abraham (vs. 2-8)

\n

That God related personally to Abraham was admitted by all. The important point to\n Steven is that He did so, \". . .while he was still in Mesopotamia, before he lived in\n Haran.\" (Acts 7:2). Indeed, He directed him to \"Leave your country and your\n people. . .\" (7:3), just as He would now direct the righteous of Israel to leave\n their country and their people for the sake of world-wide witness. Stephen admits that\n Abraham was directed to \"this land\" (tain gain tautain), but reminds them that\n he never owned a foot of ground in it (vs.5).16 Thus, Abraham was a foreigner in the\n promised land, just as many of the Hellenists and some Gentiles were.

\n

One might be tempted at this point to argue that this state was hardly God's way, since\n the promise was still unfulfilled. But Stephen reminds them that even after the birth of\n Isaac, \"Your descendants will be strangers in a country not their own. . .\" for\n 400 years (vs. 6). Only then would they come out of that country to serve God in\n \"this place\" (en toi topoi toutoi).17

\n

The significance of the mention of circumcision in this context is that the covenant\n was being practiced while no land was owned by Abraham, thus showing that the enjoyment of\n this covenant was not dependent on being a native of the Holy Land.18

\n\n

The Patriarchs (vs. 8-19)

\n

The career of the patriarchs continues to demonstrate the key motifs that interest\n Stephen. First, they did not recognize that God was with Joseph, so they sold him.19 In\n the same way, the present august assembly (though certainly no greater in stature than the\n patriarchs themselves) may have failed to recognize God's agent again in Christ.

\n

This part of the argument is clear. However, why include the career of Joseph during\n the famine? To Stephen, the important thing is not that Joseph was sold into slavery, but\n that he was sold into slavery \"in Egypt,\" (vs. 9). Did this mean that because\n Joseph was out of the land, he was out of the center of action spiritually? Not at all. In\n fact, the center of action moved with Joseph. \"God was with him,\" affirms\n Stephen (vs. 9).

\n

The subsequent events surrounding the famine demonstrated that God was working through\n Joseph the outcast one, an Egyptian slave living outside of Israel. Indeed, God was\n working so effectively through Joseph that the entire family eventually moved to Egypt,\n and the focus of salvation history during this period shifted completely out of the\n promised land.

\n

It is true that though Joseph and Jacob died in Egypt, their bodies were brought back\n to Palestine. Yet, according to Stephen, it was to the city of Shechem that they were\n taken, in the hated territory of the Samaritans.20

\n\n

Moses (vs. 19-43)

\n

Blasphemy of Moses was part of the charge brought against Stephen, so his analysis of\n this period in salvation history is important. The elements of the story included by\n Stephen focus on: \n

    \n
  1. Moses' relation to other races (vs. 21-23)
    2. \n
  2. The failure on the part of the people (including the leadership) to recognize him, or to\n obey his teaching (vs. 24-28,35,37-39) see esp. vs. 25, he \"thought that his own\n people would realize that God was using him to rescue them, but they did not. and 37\n \"But our fathers refused to obey him. Instead, they rejected him. . .\"21
    3. \n
  3. Moses' and the people's relation to sacred space The last point, regarding sacred space,\n accounts for the seemingly strange and unnecessary material in this section. First we find\n that Moses \"fled to Midian, where he settled as a foreigner,\" (vs. 29). It was\n in Midian, not in Palestine, that he saw the burning bush. It was on an unknown patch of\n ground somewhere near Sinai that God said, \"Take off your sandals; the place where\n you are standing is holy ground,\" (vs. 33). Here was authentic sacred ground if ever\n there was such a thing!
    4. \n
\n

Yet, interestingly, no one in Stephen's day knew where this ground was. Would it be a\n good idea to look for the spot in the hope that it was still sacred? Not at all. The thing\n that made it sacred was the presence of God working there. Now that He had moved on to\n other work, the location of the spot was unimportant.

\n

It would have been a serious mistake to stand around at this site when God had already\n moved on! In the same way, according to Stephen, God was moving in new directions as he\n spoke, but He was being inhibited by institutionalized foot-dragging on the part of his\n audience.

\n

Stephen not only points out that Moses did wonders, but he reminds his audience of the\n venue for those works-- \"in Egypt, at the Red Sea and for forty years in the\n desert,\" (vs. 36).

\n

The passage in 40-43 reporting on the practice of idol worship in the wilderness has\n confused many. Why should Stephen pick out this area of sin to report on? Surely there\n were ample examples of unfaithfulness to the law in Deuteronomy, without having to go to\n the book of Psalms? The answer, I suggest is that this passage is also dealing with the\n issue of sacred space.22

\n

Stephen shows that the inclination of the people has always been to worship and love\n \"the work of their own hands,\" (vs. 41). It was the fact that the animistic gods\n were tangible, visible and man-made that appealed to the ancient Hebrews, and the same\n features, he suggests, commended the temple and the sacred ground idea to his audience.

\n\n\n

The Tabernacle and the Temple (Vs. 44-50)

\n

Stephen illustrates the basic difference between the outlook of man and that of God on\n this point, by comparing the tabernacle and the temple (vs.44-50). The tabernacle hardly\n fits the usual pattern for sacred space. Shrines and temples are usually built around the\n site of a theophany.23 A sacred grove, spring, mountain, or stone are the proper loci for\n religious shrines. But this shrine was mobile. The sacredness of the shrine had to do with\n the \"pattern he [Moses] had seen,\" (vs.44) and the teaching value that resulted,\n not with its location.

\n

Not only this, but the tabernacle sat in the barnyard of a farmer for a period of time\n that must have been about 400 years according to the chronology of I Kings.24 During this\n whole time--almost equal in length to the whole post-exilic history of Jerusalem--the\n location of the temple was irrelevant to God's working. Certainly the space Stephen\n devoted to the history of the tabernacle must relate to the over-all theme of the speech.\n The best guess as to the significant aspect of the tabernacle is the absence of any tie to\n a geographical location.

\n

It is possible that God's refusal to let David build the temple, in spite of the fact\n that he was even more favored by God than Solomon, is used to show that the temple was not\n God's ideal. Certainly, in the original speech, Stephen cannot have failed to teach on the\n great text in II Sam. 7 where God declares that He has, \"been moving around in a\n tent, even in a tabernacle.\" God goes on to ask, \"Did I [ask] why have you not\n built Me a house of cedar?\"25

\n

This need not be seen as an argument that the construction of the temple was a mistake.\n It was not the construction of the temple that was an error, but the false interpretation\n of the temple that had become a deadly error.26 No doubt, it was this danger that caused\n God to reflect some negativity regarding the whole project. Yet, there is no actual\n statement to the effect that God had not ordained the temple. Rather, there is the\n unwavering declaration that \"the Most High does not dwell in houses made by human\n hands.\" (vs. 48).

\n

This was the critical misunderstanding that was inhibiting God's purposes in Stephen's\n day, as it still does today.

\n

Stephen seems to have realized that he had already signed his death warrant by directly\n opposing the theology of the Counsel. He certainly evidences no effort at restraint in the\n denunciation that follows.27 Stephen's Death and Legacy

\n

I think entirely too much importance is attached to Stephen's reference to Christ as\n the \"Son of Man\". The fact that this is the only usage of the term outside of\n the gospels is accounted for by the unique nature of the confrontation, including the fact\n that the audience is made up of Jewish Old Testament scholars. The allusion to Dan. 7:14\n seems natural and realistic in this setting. It is certainly not what makes the speech\n important.28

\n

The fact that the counsel stoned Stephen without Roman approval is an evident breech of\n Roman law, even if the crimes of blasphemy were capitol offenses. Bruce is probably\n correct when he observes that,

\n
\n

. . .during the closing part of Pilate's administration, especially when he was\n resident in Caesarea, the Jewish rulers knew that they could take certain discreet\n liberties.29

\n
\n

It remains possible in this author's mind that the counsel simply functioned as an\n illegal lynch mob.30 The main point is that the church lost a great light the day that\n Stephen died. Stephen was ahead of the development of many of the other leaders in the\n Jerusalem church in his understanding of the relationship of the gospel to the issues of\n race, sacred space, and the radical change in God's program with respect to the Old\n Testament.31 Indeed, it was Philip the evangelist, another apparent Hellenist, who in Acts\n 8, opens the door to Samaritans, and to the Ethiopian Eunich.

\n

It must have seemed like an unmitigated tragedy to have lost a visionary who understood\n better than most of the Apostles at this time some of the most basic aspects of Christian\n spirituality. Yet, the narrative indicates that God was at work, even though the believers\n there would not have known it at the time. The cloaks were laid at the feet of one who\n would later demonstrate every bit as much radicalism as Stephen--Saul of Tarsus.32
