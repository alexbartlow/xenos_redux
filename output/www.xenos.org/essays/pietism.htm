by Gary DeLashmutt

\n\n\n

Introduction

\n\n\n

The purpose of this research project is to examine the early German Lutheran Pietists'\nunderstanding of justification. Specifically, the teaching of Johann Arndt, Philip Jacob\nSpener and August Hermann Francke will be studied. Three specific areas of their view of\njustification will be examined: the basis of justification, the relationship between\njustification and sanctification, and the role of baptism in justification.

\n\n

An effort has been made to identify and study the primary sources of each of the above\nPietists in these doctrinal areas. Direct quotation of their works accompany and\nsubstantiate any assertion concerning their teaching. Secondary sources have also been\nevaluated for additional interaction in the interpretation of the primary sources.

\n\n

Consideration has also been given to the theological and political climate of\nProtestantism during the seventeenth century. This has been done in order to assure\nfairness in evaluating the doctrinal emphases of the Pietists.

\n\n\n

The Background of German Pietism

\n\n\n

Like all religious movements, German Pietism was, at least in part, a reaction to the\nspiritual and political climate of its day. Any attempt to understand the theological\nemphases of Arndt, Spener and Francke must therefore be preceded by an overview of the\nreligious and political climate in which they lived.

\n\n\n

The Religious Situation

\n\n\n

The most important factor in German Lutheranism just prior to the emergence of Pietism\nwas its rigid confessionalism, or Verkonfessionalisierung.

\n\n

After Luther died in 1546, Lutheran scholars tried to precisely formulate the doctrines\nof the Christian faith as they understood them. This was felt to be necessary since the\nLutherans were experiencing acute doctrinal pressure from both aggressive Calvinists and\nJesuit Catholics.Footnote1 Two camps\ndeveloped within Lutheranism: the \"Philippists\", who, following the example of\nLuther's mentor Philip Melanchthon, emphasized the common doctrinal ground that they\nshared with the Calvinists, and the \"Gnesio-Lutherans\", who held rigidly to\nLuther's main tenets and regarded all other groups with suspicion. It was the\nGnesio-Lutherans who won out in this struggle for ascendancy.Footnote2 The Formula of Concord of 1580 formally resolved the\ndoctrinal disputes within Lutheranism.Footnote3

\n\n

With its center at the University of WittenbergFootnote4, the same school from which Luther launched the\nReformation, Lutheranism lapsed into a rigid orthodoxy and sterile scholasticism.\nIronically, though Luther had unseated Thomist scholasticism from its throne of power over\ntheology, the Lutheran scholars of this period consciously reintroduced it in order to\nformulate their doctrinal statements.Footnote5\nThe University of Wittenberg declared that the resulting creedal formulations of\nLutheranism \"possessed the force of divinely revealed and binding truth, not only in\nmatters of doctrine, but in all affairs.\"Footnote6

\n\n

This defensive, heavily dogmatic emphasis produced Lutheran Confessionalism--a dead\northodoxy that Luther would hardly have desired. Stoeffler describes this confessionalism\nand its effects on how people came to view Christianity.

\n\n
\n

It (Verkonfessionalisierung) refers to the rigid confessionalizing of\n Lutheranism which was undertaken by its seventeenth century theologians. The process was\n aided by the reappearance of Aristotelianism in German universities and gymnasia early\n during that century. The result was an unprecedented hardening of Lutheran doctrine. Not\n only did the guardians of orthodoxy endeavor to keep pure the teachings of the communion\n but the truth had to be stated in accepted phrases. Any deviation in phraseology was\n immediately viewed with great suspicion. After John Gerhard the various minutiae of the\n seventeenth century systems of Lutheran theology had to be treated in proper order and\n sequence so as not to raise apprehensions of heresy. In this heavily dogmatic atmosphere\n the essence of Christianity came to be regarded as consisting in a series of rationally\n ordered propositions. Faith had been largely re-defined so as to consist in personal\n assent to those propositions. Confessional theology and Christianity were regarded as\n almost synonymous.Footnote7

\n
\n\n

Brown is similarly critical in his evaluation of Lutheran Confessionalism, viewing it\nas a virtual reversal of the Reformation:

\n\n
\n

Negatively, some have felt that in Protestant Scholasticism we have an\n \"intellectual Pelegianism\" in which the good works of the medieval church were\n exchanged for the works of understanding. Aristotle, who had been thrown out the front\n door, quickly came in the back. Justification by faith became one of the dogmas instead of\n the source of dogma. Luther's God, who was a Thou, became an It. The testimony of the Holy\n Spirit became a mere intellectual process of increasing acquaintance with the truth.\n Though there was an apotheosis of the Bible, the Scriptures were used primarily as proof\n texts to verify the creedal dogmas.Footnote8

\n
\n\n

Spener, writing in Pia Desideria, similarly complained about the scholasticism\nof Lutheran Confessionalism:

\n\n
\n

Although by God's grace we still have pure doctrine derived from the Word of God, we\n cannot deny that much that is alien, useless, and reminiscent of the world's wisdom has\n here and there been gradually introduced into theology.Footnote9

\n
\n\n

Since the pastors were being educated in this spiritual climate, it is not surprising\nthat their preaching consisted mainly of dogmatic expositions of Lutheran doctrine and\npolemical denunciations of Catholicism and Calvinism, rather than applied scripture which\nwould build up the spiritual life of their congregations.Footnote10

\n\n\n

The Political Situation

\n\n\n

The spiritual unhealthy of late sixteenth century Germany was further aggravated by the\npolitical situation. Two factors in the political situation helped stir the spiritual\nhunger of the people who responded to the Pietists' message.

\n\n\n

The Thirty Years War

\n\n\n

The Thirty Years War lasted from 1609 until 1637, although the Peace of Westphalia\nwhich formally ended the war was not signed until 1648.Footnote11 This war, fought largely because of the religious\nintolerance between Catholic and Protestant rulers, devastated Europe worse than any other\nwar before the twentieth century.

\n\n

The war ended without any genuine resolution. The rulers simply wearied of the\nbloodshed and economic loss caused by it. The Peace of Westphalia granted to both rulers\nand subjects \"freedom of religion\"--as long as they were Catholic, Lutheran or\nReformed. Although this increased religious tolerance was an improvement, it was not\npromulgated by the political rulers \"out of a deeper understanding of Christian love,\nbut rather out of a growing indifference to religious matters.\"Footnote12

\n\n

This same indifference spread to the common people, whose lives were devastated by the\nwar. The war had the effect of weakening the people's moral fiber. Cruelty and drunkenness\nbecame commonplace among the peasants. Their spiritual malaise was further aggravated by\nthe ruthless and uninhibited behavior of both rulers and soldiers.Footnote13 \"The seemingly interminable war raised religious\ndoubts among the laity and generated indifference to moral and spiritual matters.\"Footnote14

\n\n\n

The German State-Church Relationship

\n\n\n

For a variety of reasons which lie outside the scope of this project, Luther did not\nsever the state's control over the church at the time of the Reformation. This wedding of\nchurch and state had a damaging effect on the spiritual health of German Lutherans for two\nreasons.

\n\n

First, with the church and state under common jurisdiction, the church's power to\nexercise discipline with its members was limited by the agreement of the state rulers. As\nStoeffler says,

\n\n
\n

Under the circumstances no presbyterial element had an opportunity to develop within\n Lutheranism. In most instances the churches and clergy were at the mercy of territorial\n rulers, the latter being often dissolute and seldom devout. Even if Lutheranism had\n developed a concept of church discipline, which it did not, effective discipline would\n have been difficult under these conditions.Footnote15

\n
\n\n

Since church discipline is one of the means Christ provided to maintain the church's\nspiritual vitalityFootnote16, its\nabsence from German Lutheranism left the church much more vulnerable to spiritual decay.

\n\n

A state-run church tends to lower the collective spirituality of the church in another\nway. Since everyone is supposedly a Christian in a state which is \"Christian,\"\nthe society does not tend to rise to the ethical or spiritual quality of the church.\nRather, the church tends to conform to the society. This was certainly the case in\nseventeenth century Germany.Footnote17

\n\n\n

The Resultant Nominalism

\n\n\n

The factors described above combined in German Lutheranism in the seventeenth century\nto produce a form of Christianity which was nominal both in practice and in doctrine.

\n\n

Confessional Lutheranism was doctrinally nominal because it defined being a Christian\nalmost exclusively in terms of mental affirmation of Lutheran dogma.Footnote18 Nicolaus Amsdorf, a theologian of this period, even went\nso far as to teach that good works were hurtful to the Christian life since they could\nfoster a doctrine of justification by works!Footnote19 Stoeffler describes this doctrinal nominalism:

\n\n
\n

When their rigidly objective interpretation of justification was joined to an equally\n objective interpretation of baptismal regeneration the centrality of the saving\n relationship was rather effectively eliminated from seventeenth century Lutheran\n orthodoxy. The Christian was now thought to be a person who interprets the Bible in terms\n of the Lutheran symbols as the truth of these symbols is expressed in an orthodox system\n of theology. Fiducia had become assensus, the liberty of the\n Christian man had given way to the tyranny of scholastic theology, and the Bible had once\n again become an arsenal of proof texts.Footnote20

\n
\n\n

This gross distortion of the theology of Paul and (to a lesser extent) Luther had the\npredictable effect of producing a behavioral nominalism that was worse than that of other\npost-Reformation Protestant churches. The laity had little appreciation of Christianity as\na way of life as well as a series of doctrinal beliefs.Footnote21 Though the churches were well attended, there was little\nevidence of ethical transformation which was encouraged and manifested in the New\nTestament church. Personal spiritual devotion was replaced by superficial and mechanical\nreligious observanceFootnote22 which\nGod, speaking through the Old Testament prophets, says He hates.Footnote23 In Arndt, Spener and Francke, a similar prophetic voice\nwas sounded.

\n\n\n

Early Lutheran Pietists

\n\n\n

Johann Arndt (1555-1621) has been widely acknowledged as the theological father of\nGerman Lutheran Pietism.Footnote24 As\na Lutheran pastor and a scholar of Luther's writings, his True Christianity (1605)\nwas the first German Pietist work to challenge the spiritual maladies of Lutheran\northodoxy.

\n\n

Philip Jacob Spener (1635-1705) is credited with forging the ideas of Arndt into a\nreform movement within Lutheranism.Footnote25\nLike Arndt, he was a Lutheran pastor and scholar. He was deeply influenced by True\nChristianity, and in 1675, he wrote a foreword to a new edition of True\nChristianity. This foreword, reissued separately a year later, was entitled Pia\nDesideria, or Heartfelt Desire for a God-pleasing Reform of the True Evangelical Church,\nTogether with Several Simple Christian Proposals Looking Toward this End. As the title\nsuggests, this work not only critiqued the spiritual state of German Lutheranism; it also\noutlined a plan of reform. It was greeted with an enthusiastic response by many German\npeople, but with sharp criticism by much of the the clergy. In his subsequent works,\nSpener continued to outline the needed reform of the Lutheran church.

\n\n

August Hermann Francke (1663-1727) was Spener's disciple and friend. As a Lutheran\npastor, he found himself in conflict with other Lutheran clergymen after undergoing a\nprofound conversion experience. As a result of Spener's influence, Francke was invited to\nbecome professor of Greek and Oriental languages at the newly formed University of Halle.\nUnder his leadership, Halle became a key means of Pietist reform as it trained men for the\nLutheran pastorate who were taught the theology and practice of Arndt, Spener and Francke.Footnote26

\n\n\n

The Basis of Justification

\n\n\n

The critical rediscovery of Luther and the Reformers was the doctrine of justification\nby grace through faith alone. God legally cancels the debt of our sins and imputes\nChrist's righteousness to us when we place our faith in Christ and His work on the cross\nto make us acceptable to God. Arndt, Spener and Francke completely agreed with this\ndoctrine. Their impact was the result not of a different view of justification, but of a\ndifferent definition of the faith that effects justification.

\n\n\n

Justification by Faith Alone

\n\n\n

Many Lutheran Confessionalists accused Arndt, Spener and Francke of teaching\njustification by works, or of confusing justification with sanctification.Footnote27 However, an examination of\ntheir writings proves that all three men believed that justification is by grace through\nfaith alone, and that justification must precede sanctification.

\n\n

Because Arndt was aware of the accusations leveled at him, he went out of his way to\nstate his Lutheran orthodoxy in this area of theology. The following quotes clearly reveal\nArndt's view of justification.

\n\n
\n

You must take care that you do not connect your works and the virtues that you have\n begun, or the gifts of the new life, with your justification before God, for none of man's\n works, merit, gifts, or virtue, however lovely these may be, count for anything. Our\n justification depends on the exalted, perfect merit of Jesus Christ, received by faith, as\n it is sufficiently discussed in chapters 5, 19, 34, and 41 of this book, and in the first\n three chapters of Book II. Take great care, therefore, not to confound the righteousness\n of faith with the righteousness of the Christian life, but make a clear distinction\n between them . . . Footnote28

\n
\n\n
\n

Through this faith (in Christ) we receive forgiveness of sins, in no other way than\n through pure grace without any of our own merits (Eph. 2:8) but only by the merits of\n Christ . . . Even if it (our faith) is weak and we are still hemmed\n around with many sins, these are covered over out of grace for Christ's\n sake . . . From this you can see that works cannot make you righteous.\n First, you must be established in Christ through faith and be righteous in him before you\n can do any good work. See to it indeed that your righteousness is the grace and gift of\n God that comes before all your merit.Footnote29

\n
\n\n

In like manner, Spener clarified to his accusers the orthodoxy of his view of\njustification. Three passages will suffice to demonstrate this.

\n\n
\n

We gladly acknowledge that we must be saved only and alone through faith and that our\n works or godly life contribute neither much nor little to our salvation, for as a fruit of\n our faith our works are connected with the gratitude which we owe to God, who has already\n given us who believe the gift of righteousness and salvation. Far be it from us to depart\n even a finger's breadth from this teaching, for we would rather give up our life and the\n whole world than yield the smallest part of it.Footnote30

\n
\n\n
\n

I know, believe and teach that as far as our justification is concerned, it takes place\n out of pure grace for the sake of Christ and is not in the least reflected directly or\n indirectly by our holiness or righteousness. If anyone should endeavor to mix anything of\n human worthiness, I would oppose that person from the bottom of my\n soul . . . none of our works or merits avail anything but only the\n merits of Christ, not what He has done in us but for us by Him. When we say that faith\n saves, I understand that such saving is not a work of faith as if it were a virtue that we\n possessed and therefore belong among works but that one receives justification only by\n God's grace and Christ's merit alone.Footnote31

\n
\n\n\n
\n

\"Ought we not also bring such sacrifices (ourselves and our good works) to God the\n Lord in order to make atonement for our sins?

\" No, for Christ alone has made\n satisfaction for us by his sacrifice, and he who wishes to add to it his own sacrifice for\n atonement blasphemes the sacrifice of Christ (Heb. 10:14).Footnote32\n\n\n

Francke also clearly stated his theology of justification in terms designed to disprove\nall charges of heresy.

\n\n
\n

1. We are justified only by faith in the Lord Jesus without merit or the addition of\n work in that the Heavenly Father because of the perfect satisfaction and the precious\n merit of his Son judges us free and liberated from all our sins.

\n
\n\n
\n

2. Through this justification, which occurs through faith, the justified person becomes\n completely and totally perfect; indeed, it is seen as the justification of God himself, as\n St. Paul writes: God made him who knew no sin to be sin for us that we might become the\n justification of God (2 Cor. 5:21). Just as God looks on the Lord Christ as sin (because\n our sins were reckoned to him), so he sees the sinner as just and completely perfect\n because he gives to the sinner as the sinner's own the innocence and righteousness of\n Christ.

\n
\n\n
\n

3. He who does not have this perfection cannot become holy. Perfection is nothing other\n than faith in the Lord Jesus and is not in us or ours but in Christ or of Christ for whose\n sake we are considered perfect before God and thus his perfection is ours by\n ascription . . . 

\n
\n\n
\n

10. From this it follows that both the following statements are true in a certain\n sense: We are perfect, and we are not perfect. Namely, we are perfect through Christ and\n in Christ through our justification and according to the righteousness of Christ ascribed\n to us. However, we are not and will not be completely perfect in the sense that we will\n nevermore be able to grow, to set aside evil and to take on good toward sanctification.

\n
\n\n
\n

11. The one who does not wish to err in this matter must distinguish well the article\n concerning justification and that concerning renovation or sanctification. Otherwise he\n will increasingly become entangled in controversy. . . . Footnote33 . . . God\n having thus received me into his grace by faith in his son Jesus, I am not at one time\n justified and at another time not: but I am always and constantly in God's\n favor . . . Footnote34

\n
\n\n\n

Faith as Fiducia

\n\n\n

One of the the key contributions of the Pietists to Protestantism's view of\njustification was its insistence that justifying faith is fiducia\nrather than assensus.

\n\n

The Pietists insisted that biblical faith is more than merely mentally assenting to\ndoctrinal truths (assensus); it is a personal faith (fiducia) which lays\nhold of the life of Christ and therefore results in regeneration and sanctification. To\nprove that this definition of faith was no doctrinal innovation, Spener was fond of\nquoting Luther's \"Preface to Romans, \" where he says,

\n\n
\n

But it (unbiblical faith) is a human imagination an idea that never reaches the depths\n of the heart, and so nothing comes of it and no betterment follows it. Faith, however, is\n a divine work in us. It changes us and makes us to be born anew of God (John 1:13). It\n kills the old Adam and makes altogether different men of us in heart and spirit and mind\n and powers, and it brings with it the Holy Spirit. O, it is a living, busy, active, mighty\n thing, this faith, and it is impossible for it not to do good works incessantly. It does\n not ask whether there are good works to do, but before the question rises it has already\n done them and is always at the doing of them . . . \"Footnote35

\n
\n\n

Luther did not develop or emphasize this dimension of faith because his primary battle\nwas against the works soteriology of Roman Catholicism. But the Pietists did\ndevelop and emphasize it because their battle was against the dead orthodoxy of\nConfessional Lutheranism.Footnote36\nThis passage from Luther thus became a key part of their defense that they were not\noverturning the Reformation, but merely continuing it.Footnote37

\n\n

Arndt develops this view of faith as one of his main themes in True Christianity.Footnote38

\n\n
\n

True Christianity consists, not in words or in external show, but in living faith, from\n which arise righteous fruits, and all manner of Christian virtues, as from Christ himself.Footnote39

\n
\n\n
\n

Note that faith consists in living, consoling trust and not in empty sounds and\n words . . . This is true knowledge of God, which arises out of\n experience and consists in living faith. Therefore, the Epistle to the Hebrews calls faith\n a substance, a being, an undeniable witness (Heb. 11:1). This is a piece of the inner,\n spiritual worship, the knowledge of God, which consists in living faith, and faith is a\n spiritual, living, heavenly gift, light and power of God.Footnote40

\n
\n\n
\n

In the living and working faith and in the following of the holy life of Christ, the\n true living knowledge of Christ consists.Footnote41

\n
\n\n

Spener stresses the personal and experiential dimension of biblical faith in\n\"Christian Joy.\"

\n\n
\n

The basis of joy (is) the assurance of divine grace. Joy is not without peace; it\n follows upon justification that is upon forgiveness of sins (Rom. 5:1; 1 John 3:21). True\n faith takes on such assurance; insofar as we are joyous insofar do we\n believe . . . Ps. 73:18. This faith brings it about that we know the\n proper good because we have and have grasped it as a present reality. . .Footnote42

\n
\n\n

In Pia Desideria, Spener stresses the spiritual dimension of biblical faith and\nthe moral change it effects.

\n\n
\n

Accordingly they (i.e., nominal Lutherans) have such a fleshly illusion of faith (for\n godly faith does not exist without the Holy Spirit, nor can such faith continue when\n deliberate sins prevail) in the place of faith that saves . . . So it\n is that all those who live under the rule of sin, with the consequence that they have no\n capacity for the Holy Spirit and hence for true faith, can have no other kind of faith\n than such human delusion.\"Footnote43

\n
\n\n

That Francke agreed with Arndt's and Spener's view of true faith is evident from his\ncomments in \"A Letter to a Friend Concerning the Most Useful Way of Preaching.\"

\n\n
\n

 . . . let a minister carefully and clearly\n distinguish . . . between mere morality and true religion, between the\n moral honest man and the believer who, from a deep conviction of the depravity of his\n nature and the errors of his life, has learned to hate sin from his heart and lives by the\n faith of the Son of God . . . to enquire\n if . . . they can find in themselves the genuine marks of a true\n conversion to God, and a living faith in Christ . . . Footnote44

\n
\n\n\n

The Relationship Between Justification and Sanctification

\n\n\n

It is in their understanding of the relationship between justification and\nsanctification that Arndt, Spener and Francke made their greatest impact on Protestantism.\nThough historians do not deny the fact of their impact in this area, they hotly\ndebate the value of it. Some regard German Pietism as a terrible aberration of\nReformation soteriology.Footnote45\nOthers regard it as the recovery and development of the true soteriological spirit of the\nReformation.Footnote46 It falls\noutside the scope of this project to make a detailed study of Luther's understanding of\nthe relationship between justification and sanctification. Rather, the views of these\nthree German Pietists will be surveyed and evaluated.

\n\n\n

Justification is Evidenced by Sanctification

\n\n\n

It was the emphasis on fiducia that developed German Pietism's view of\nsanctification. The kind of faith which justifies is also the kind of faith that\nregenerates and sanctifies.Footnote47\nIf there is no evidence of sanctification, there is no evidence for regeneration and\ntherefore no evidence for justification. Sanctification thus becomes the proof that one\nhas been justified.

\n\n

Passages from True Christianity and The Garden of Paradise suggest that\nalthough Arndt believed in justification by faith, he saw sanctification as a non-optional\ncomponent of justification to such an extent that sanctification is a practical evidence\nof justification.Footnote48

\n\n
\n

Since Christ now lives and dwells in you through faith, his indwelling is not a dead\n work but a living work . . . it renews you in Christ so that you grow,\n blossom, and live in him. What is the use of a graft in a stem if it does grow and bring\n forth fruit?Footnote49

\n
\n\n
\n

How can a person who believes on Christ at the same time have a desire to sin, not wish\n to leave sin, for which Christ had to pay with his blood and death, and with his\n life? . . . Therefore, all those who call themselves Christians\n crucify Christ again and hold him up to contempt . . . They cannot\n partake of the sufferings of Christ for they have tread on the blood of Christ with their\n feet . . . they must experience the righteous judgment of\n God . . . Footnote50

\n
\n\n
\n

Christ understood the whole newborn man, the tree with its fruits, renewed through\n faith, in which Christ lives and dwells through faith. Such faith he will find in few.\n Where one does not follow Christ in his life through faith, there is neither faith nor\n Christ, but that one is cast out and denied.Footnote51

\n
\n\n
\n

Where Christ is, a holy life exists in man . . . This relationship\n and unity of Christian faith and life is described in 2 Peter\n 1:5ff. . . . Peter says expressly: The person who does not have such\n unity and Christian faith does not know Christ; he has lost faith and walks in darkness.\n That is true faith which renews and makes the whole man alive in Christ so that Christ\n lives and remains in him and he in Christ.Footnote52

\n
\n\n
\n

It is a most false and absurd thing for any one to profess himself a Christian, and yet\n to lead at the same time an unchristian life; or to pretend to faith and the true church,\n and yet never produce any of its true and genuine fruits . . . Footnote53

\n
\n\n

The passages above show that Arndt wrote in such a way that he left himself vulnerable\nto the false charge of teaching justification by works.Footnote54 Even though this charge is refuted by Arndt's\naffirmation of justification by faith, he is in this writer's opinion excessively rigid\nbecause he does not describe sanctification as a gradual process punctuated by moral\nlapses.

\n\n

Spener was also accused of teaching justification by works by many orthodox Lutheran\ntheologians of his day.Footnote55 The\nfollowing statements explain Spener's vulnerability to this charge.

\n\n
\n

 . . . this sin (drunkenness), too, must once and for all be\n renounced if they are to be children of God . . . unless such sins are\n earnestly and resolutely rooted out these vicious and unrepentant persons will lose their\n salvation . . . If some advance the argument that drunkenness cannot\n be so grave a sin because, if it were, there would be very few true Christians among us, I\n shall accept the conclusion . . . \"Footnote56

\n
\n\n
\n

For Godly faith cannot exist without the Holy Spirit, and the Holy Spirit cannot exist\n along side of premeditated and prevailing sins.Footnote57

\n
\n\n
\n

As the faith, which alone justifies us and makes us holy, is inseparable from good\n works, so no one will be justified other than those who are intent upon\n sanctification.\"Footnote58

\n
\n\n
\n

 . . . pure doctrine and holy living must necessarily be united in\n the case of those who want to be saved . . . Footnote59

\n
\n\n

On the basis of passages like these, Brown is correct when he says that Spener saw good\nworks as \"a conditio sin quo non, necessary attributes which\ninhere in salvation.\"Footnote60\nHe also seems to share Arndt's excessively rigid view of sanctification which does not\nallow for serious moral lapses.

\n\n

Francke focused on propagating Spener's understanding of Christianity, so it is not\nsurprising that we find in his works the same emphasis sanctification as the evidence of\njustification.Footnote61 In Francke's\nwritings, though, there is a much greater emphasis on the love of God which motivates\nsanctification and the power of the Holy Spirit which empowers sanctification. Perhaps\nthis emphasis was Francke's corrective to the harsh language used by Arndt and Spener\nconcerning the necessity of sanctification for those who wish to be justified.

\n\n
\n

6. If the newborn Christian acknowledges such sins of the flesh, he strives with all\n earnestness against the evil which arises in his flesh. And he does so not through his own\n power and strength, . . . but through the spirit (sic) and he depends\n on the power of Jesus Christ which is made sanctification for him and conquers the evil in\n him.

\n
\n\n
\n

7. In such sinful habits and crimes the justified man remains, however, never standing\n in one position, but through the grace of God he sets aside ever more and more the evil,\n and day to day grows in faith and in love . . . Footnote62

\n
\n\n
\n

Insomuch as I cease from all my workings and am convinced that of myself I can do\n nothing but sin . . . but depend purely on God's\n grace . . . my heart begets new strength . . . and\n the new man, as a good tree, breaks out in blossoms which send forth a sweet odor and\n bears fruits that are acceptable to God and man. It is not another way by which I have\n been justified and again another by which I seek to be sanctified, but it is one in the\n same way, to wit, Christ. . .As I depend on nothing but Christ whenever I pray for\n forgiveness of sins, so I depend on him alone and turn to his grace and power only\n whenever I desire to be strengthened in faith, love, and hope. Yes, all that I have to do\n is to abide by in the grace which I have received . . . Then the work\n of God will be accomplished in me. For God wants none of my help for the accomplishment of\n his work. If I do but suffer myself to be prepared by him like a child in the mother's\n womb, not resisting the operations of his spirit, then he himself will work in me whatever\n is pleasing to him. Nevertheless it is by no means his will that I should be inattentive;\n and, instead of true resignation, fall into negligence or seek rest and peace in nature.\n For thereby I should even imperceptibly lose his workings.Footnote63

\n
\n\n
\n

 . . . it is a very great moment that a minister not only instruct\n his hearers what they must do and how they ought to act, but that he also labor fully to\n apprise and to convince them by the evidence of Scripture of their own native weakness and\n impotency for all that is spiritually good. And that he further show them, by the same\n word of truth from which they must look for, and from whom they may hope to receive, all\n grace and strength, not only to renew their souls in their first conversion but also\n afterwards to enable them to perform every duty as well of inward religion. They must be\n told that they can do nothing without Christ, according as he has assured us, Jn. 15:5,\n \"Without me ye can do nothing\" . . . Thus the holy apostles\n preached. These were the topics which they insisted much upon, and if their example is not\n followed in this matter, it will be no wonder if our modern preaching comes vastly short\n of the success of theirs. And by this means some of our hearers will be in danger of\n sinking into a mere legal frame and spirit of bondage while they are pressed to duty and\n working, but not encouraged by the grace of Christ nor directed where to look for strength\n to perform it.Footnote64

\n
\n\n
\n

The love of Christ ought to be much more insisted on by preachers than what is commonly\n done, because when we apply ourselves in right manner to his passion, death, and\n atonement, his merit and that purchase of salvation which he hath made for us, the\n knowledge of his love to us, and of our pardon and justification through faith in his\n blood, it is the truest spring and most powerful attraction of our love to him. Now the\n more we love Christ and that for this very reason, `because he first loved us,' the better\n will every branch of our religion flourish, every other grace and every duty will flow\n from its proper fountain. Therefore, the more a minister endeavors to instill this\n principle of sacred love into the hearts of his hearers, the more comfortable success will\n he probably see of his labors in their spiritual improvement and growing obedience to the\n gospel.Footnote65

\n
\n\n

Evaluation

\n\n

Arndt, Spener and Francke reestablished the biblical relationship between justification\nand sanctification which had been practically severed by Confessionalist Lutheranism. By\ninsisting that those who are justified will evidence it by sanctification, they implicitly\ncalled into question the doctrinal validity of Confessionalism, which did not stress any\npractical connection between justification and sanctification. This is probably the reason\nfor the Confessionalist's severe attack on the Pietists.

\n\n

While this emphasis is the proper corrective to dead orthodoxyFootnote66, it can easily lead in practice to a legalistic emphasis\non good works which forgets that sanctification is a gradual process punctuated by moral\nlapses.Footnote67 In this writer's\nopinion, Arndt and Spener were vulnerable to this interpretation by those who read them.\nFrancke appears to have best understood this danger and supplied the corrective to this\ntendency in his emphasis on God's role in sanctification.

\n\n\n

The Relationship Between Justification and Water Baptism

\n\n\n

It is fair to say that in their doctrinal understanding of the ritual of water baptism,\nArndt, Spener and Francke agreed with each other and with the doctrine of classic German\nLutheranism. But they were reacting against what they saw as a mechanical view of baptism\npromoted by their Confessionalist contemporaries.

\n\n\n

Their Doctrinal Understanding

\n\n\n

Arndt, Spener and Francke agreed with Luther that infant water baptism was a means of\n\"prevenient grace\" whereby the infant was placed in objective state of grace by\nGod.Footnote68 In keeping with\nLutheran teaching, they also insisted that baptismal grace must be later confirmed by\nfaith in Christ to actually effect justification.Footnote69 Because they were accused by the Confessionalists of\nPelagianism, all three men sought to refute this charge by affirming the Lutheran view of\nbaptism.

\n\n

Arndt expressed his belief that one's objective standing with God was changed through\ninfant baptism in all of his important written works. Consider the following passages.

\n\n
\n

Dear Lord Jesus Christ, you who have. . .instituted the holy sacrament of baptism. . .I\n thank you from the heart that You have through this sacrament led me into the holy\n Christian Church and thereby made me a partner of all your heavenly and eternal benefits.\n . .through baptism you have clothed me with your holy obedience, merit, righteousness,\n holiness, and innocence. Through the waters of baptism the Holy Spirit has created new\n life and changed a sinner into one who has been justified. . .You have received me because\n of your eternal grace and promise and bound me unto Yourself through this means of grace.\n . .Footnote70

\n
\n\n
\n

Through the Holy Spirit's power and activity, man is newborn. The new birth occurs\n first through the Holy Spirit (Jn. 3:4). This is what the Lord calls \"to be born of\n the Spirit.\" Secondly, it occurs through faith (I Jn. 5:1). In the third place it\n occurs through holy baptism (Jn. 3:5).Footnote71

\n
\n\n
\n

As a means to it (our salvation), holy baptism is ordered by which we are baptized in\n the death of Christ so that we might die with Christ to our sins by the power of his death\n and once again arise from our sins through the power of his resurrection.Footnote72

\n
\n\n
\n

Where the Spirit of God is, there also is the Spirit's power, salvation, regeneration,\n renewal; all this is the work of the Holy Spirit. Therefore we know that we are born again\n through baptism. . .and cleansed from our sins.Footnote73

\n
\n\n

Spener was in complete agreement with Arndt in his view of baptism, as is made clear in\nthe following citations.

\n\n
\n

Nor do I know how to praise Baptism and its power highly enough. I believe that it is\n the real \"washing of regeneration and renewal in the Holy Spirit\" (Titus 3:5),\n or as Luther says in the Catechism, \"it effects forgiveness of sins, delivers from\n death and the devil, and grants (not merely promises) eternal salvation\".Footnote74

\n
\n\n\n
\n

\"How does Baptism point to it (our obligation to build up other Christians)?\"

\n Since by it we are united to Christ, and so all become members of one spiritual body, this\n communion also imposes the obligation that one member shall, according to his ability,\n further the best welfare of the other (I Cor. 12:18; Eph. 4:15f.).Footnote75\n\n\n
\n

In baptism the old man dies so that thereby we testify that we no longer live but what\n the Lord lives and that the new man is hereafter to live and work in us. In the Holy\n Communion we eat the body and blood of the Lord so that he is ever closer and increasingly\n united with us, and that he lives in us much more so than we live in ourselves (Gal.\n 2:20).Footnote76

\n
\n\n\n
\n

\"How do Christians become priests?\"

 As in the Old Testament, priests were\n not elected but born thereto, so also the new birth in baptism is that which gives us the\n divine adoption as sons and the spiritual priesthood connected to it.Footnote77\n\n\n

According to Stoeffler, Francke agreed with the position of Arndt and Spener on\nbaptism.

\n\n
\n

In faithfulness to his Lutheran heritage Francke also spoke at times of baptism as a\n means toward entering the new life . . . It appears to have been his\n belief that the baptized infant finds himself in a relation of special divine solicitude.\n . .\"Footnote78

\n
\n\n\n

The Necessity of Fiducia

\n\n\n

The real difference between Arndt, Spener and Francke and the Lutheran Confessionalists\nwas on their insistence that baptismal grace must be confirmed by personal faith (fiducia)\nin Christ rather than mere mental assent (assensus) to doctrine if it is to have\nany lasting value.

\n\n

The Confessionalists' view of Christianity did not emphasize the necessity of making a\npersonal commitment to trust Christ for one's justification. As has already been shown,\nthey held that mental assent to the creedal statements was sufficient. With such a\nperspective typically comes a corresponding belief in ritual as efficacious in itself.Footnote79 Thus, it was commonly assumed\nthat unless someone expressly denied Christ, they had \"faith\" in Christ and thus\nremained in the state of baptismal grace conferred upon them as an infant. For all intents\nand purposes then, many Germans believed that they permanently entered a state of grace at\ninfant baptism.

\n\n

While the German Pietists did not reject the Lutheran doctrine of baptism, they\nreaffirmed that it could not keep a person in a state of grace unless\nthat individual came to personal faith in Christ. By emphasizing the personal\nnature of saving faith and the moral change by which it was evidenced, they rejected the\nvalidity of mental assent faith. This in turn led them to warn against depending upon the\nabiding efficacy of infant baptism. They saw this negative emphasis as necessary in order\nto call people to genuine faith; one's false security in baptism must often first be\ndestroyed before one will come to true saving faith.

\n\n

In True Christianity, Arndt does not expressly state that infant baptism must be\ncoupled with personal faith in order to stay in a state of grace. Rather, one reaches this\nconclusion as a logical inference by his emphasis on the nature of saving faith and its\nnecessity. As Stoeffler says,

\n\n
\n

\" . . . Arndt held the historic Lutheran view of baptism. In\n point of fact, however, he said little about it and put the emphasis on conversion,\n oneness with Christ, and a holy life.\" Footnote80

\n
\n\n

What was a logical inference from Arndt became an open declaration with Spener. Two\nstatements from Pia Desideria make this clear.

\n\n
\n

Just as the above illusion of faith as the only means of salvation from our side does\n great harm, so from the side of the divine means of Word and sacraments the shameful\n illusion of an opus operatum is added. This is not less harmful to the church,\n leads many people to damnation, and strengthens the aforementioned false notion of what\n true faith is. We cannot deny--on the contrary, daily experience convinces us--that there\n are not a few who think that all that Christianity requires of them (and that having done\n this, they have done quite enough in their service of God) is that they be baptized, hear\n the preaching of God's Word, confess and receive absolution, and go to the Lord's Supper,\n no matter how their hearts are disposed at the time, whether or not there are fruits which\n follow, provided they at least live in such a way that the civil authorities do not find\n them liable to punishment . . . It (baptism) will be in vain that you\n comfort yourself in your Baptism and in its promise of grace and salvation if for your\n part you do not also remain in the covenant of faith and a good conscience or, having\n departed therefrom, return to it with sincere repentance. Accordingly if your Baptism is\n to benefit you, it must remain in constant use throughout your life.Footnote81

\n
\n\n
\n

Nor is it enough to be baptized, but the inner man, where we have put on Christ in\n Baptism, must also keep Christ on and bear witness to him in our outward life.Footnote82

\n
\n\n

Francke continued Spener's emphasis on the uselessness of baptism unless it was\naccompanied by personal faith.

\n\n
\n

Thus you ought not to say, \"I am baptized, I go to church, I am a Christian.\"\n The hypocrites do the same. There is many a person baptized who yet went back on his oath\n and was faithless and fell out of his baptismal covenant . . . You\n must make no decisions (about whether you are justified) because you follow externals\n (i.e. baptism, church attendance, communion). The Scripture is directed to your heart and\n indicates how you must find proper certainty and be sealed by the Spirit of God. You must\n receive the Spirit of God from God and from it you must know how richly you are graced by\n God. See, there you are commanded, there you might know what happens in the heart of God\n for your sake, whether God loves you, whether you are his child.Footnote83

\n
\n\n

Evaluation

\n\n

Arndt, Spener and Francke held an orthodox Lutheran view of baptism. They saw baptism\nas a means of prevenient grace which must be confirmed in adult life by personal faith in\nChrist in order to have permanent validity. They disagreed, however, with both the\nConfessionalists' and the common people's view of baptism who essentially viewed it as a\npermanent entrance into grace. They struggled to maintain the Lutheran view of baptism\nwhile stressing the supreme importance of personal faith in Christ as the sole assurance\nof salvation.

\n\n

It is the opinion of this writer that Arndt, Spener and Francke were struggling with a\nLutheran doctrine which was logically contradictory and biblically unnecessary.

\n\n

There is an inherent logical tension in the Lutheran doctrine on baptism which Arndt,\nSpener and Francke inherited. On the one hand, Lutheran doctrine affirms that baptism\neffects an objective change in the infant's standing with God, placing the infant in the\nsphere of God's grace. On the other hand, it affirms the necessity of exercising faith in\nChrist in order to be justified. Many have noted the difficulty of understanding why faith\nis necessary if baptism places one under grace, or why baptism is effective if faith is\nwhat justifies.Footnote84

\n\n

It is evident from the writings of both Luther and the Lutheran Pietists that the\nsource of this \"tension\" is their interpretation of several New Testament\npassages which teach that \"baptism\" objectively changes our relationship with\nGod.Footnote85 It appears that\nneither Luther nor the Lutheran Pietists ever seriously questioned the Roman Catholic\ninterpretation of these passages as referring to Christian water baptism. If it is granted\nthat these passages refer instead to the baptism by the Holy Spirit into Christ (I Cor.\n12:13), an invisible event which occurs at the moment of faith (Eph. 1:13,14), the\napparent biblical and logical \"contradiction\" disappears. Water baptism is then\nunderstood as an outward affirmation of what has already taken place at the moment of\npersonal faith in Christ (see Acts 10:44-47).

\n\n

Rather than challenge the Lutheran doctrine of baptism, the Lutheran Pietists simply\nemphasized the necessity of coupling baptism with personal faith. But the tension inherent\nin Lutheran baptismal doctrine was such that many later Pietists wrestled with their views\nof baptism.Footnote86 The adoption of\nbelievers-only baptism became one of the distinctives of the Radical Pietists.

\n\n\n

Conclusion

\n\n\n

Arndt, Spener and Francke were in agreement with Luther and Lutheran orthodoxy that\njustification is a gift of legal righteousness received by faith alone.

\n\n

They were insistent that the faith that justifies is a living, personal faith (fiducia)\nrather than a barren mental assent to orthodox creeds (assensus). In this area,\nthey were more in keeping with the teaching of Luther than their Confessionalist\ncontemporaries, though admittedly Luther did not develop this theme to a great degree.

\n\n

Arndt, Spener and Francke were also in agreement with Luther and Lutheran orthodoxy\nabout the meaning of baptism as a means of prevenient grace which must be later confirmed\nby personal faith. But their emphasis on fiducia as necessary for justification led\nthem to decry the false confidence that many Germans had placed in their baptism as an ex\noper operato rite.

\n\n

From their insistence on living faith, they concluded logically, biblically and\nexperientially that sanctification will follow and give evidence of justification. It was\nthis position that called forth from their Confessionalist contemporaries the charge that\nthey were overturning the Reformation by reintroducing works as a condition for\njustification.

\n\n

Throughout their lives, Arndt, Spener and Francke consistently denied that they were\noverturning the doctrine of the Reformation. They did not even claim to be\n\"completing\" the Reformation that Luther began, though some have charged that\nthey did. Rather, they saw themselves as a reform movement within Lutheranism, calling for\na return to the spiritual dynamic between justification and sanctification that they read\nin scripture and in Luther, and that they had experienced in their own lives.Footnote87
