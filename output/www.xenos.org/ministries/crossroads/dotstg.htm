The Author
\n
    \n
  • Why do postmodernists argue that the author doesn't stand over the \n text as an authority?
    • \n
\n
\n

- For the discussion leader: Because authors are merely an expression \n of the cultural context that shapes their thought, values and beliefs. \n So there is nothing original being stated in the text. The text is merely \n an expression of the author's social reality. And that reality may or \n may not be relevant to the reader.

The Text
\n
\n

One of the big problems many postmodernists have with texts is that \n they \"privilege\" certain values and ideas over others. Since \n values and truth claims are social constructs, texts that are \"privileged\" \n (accepted into the social and literary canon) perpetuate views that \n keep power for the dominant culture while marginalizing minority cultures. \n

\n
\n
    \n
  • Is this viewpoint familiar to you? Where have you come into contact \n with it? Do you think this outlook perpetuates cynicism? How so?
    • \n
  • How might the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution be \n viewed in terms of privileging certain values that perpetuate power \n at the expense of minority cultures?
    • \n
\n
\n

- Property rights included ownership of human slaves. \"One man, \n one vote,\" meant \"One man, but not one woman.\"

\n
\n
\n

- Rights under the constitution only applied to citizens, not to Native \n Americans.

\n
\n
\n

- But at the extreme, see revisionist judges who feel we have to read \n the constitution as it would be understood today, not then. Under this \n premise they invent new meanings never considered by the framers.

\n
\n
The Reader
\n
\n

One of the tools postmodernists use to wrestle authority out of the \n text and into the reader is \"deconstruction.\" Deconstruction \n purges texts of socially constructed hierarchies, contradictions and \n identifies things excluded from the text.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Why is being \"logocentric\" such a concern for deconstruction? \n What does this mean?
    • \n
\n
\n

- People are logocentric when they believe there are controlling ideas \n that are objectively true. People who favor one point of view as the \n \"right\" one are said to be logocentric by postmodernists. \n

\n
\n
    \n
  • How does using deconstruction allow the reader to be an authority \n over the text?
    • \n
\n
\n

- The reader discerns what antitheses were at work in the original \n writing. The reader determines what the author's real motives were, \n and why he believed as he did.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Postmodernists say that in the final analysis, no one has final authority \n over the text. Why is this? What is the consequence of this view?
    • \n
\n
\n

- They claim that the individual only has authority over what the text \n means to him or her, not to what it might mean to others.

\n
\n
\n

- Texts' meanings can change depending on the reader.
