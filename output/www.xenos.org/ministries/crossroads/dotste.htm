This chapter makes the distinction between two types of postmodernism: \n skeptical and affirmative. Both types of postmodernism reject the possibility \n of rational objectivity. In the place of reason, they say we are left \n with nothing but power.

\n
\n
Skeptical Postmodernists
\n
\n

Skeptical postmodernists claim that when people make claims to ultimate \n truth, usually religious or philosophical, the one thing we can count \n on is that it will be used to justify self interest and power. Among \n the examples Leffel cites are: so-called \"Manifest Destiny\" \n and the alleged inferiority of African Americans. These are example \n of what postmodernists call \"epistemological tyranny.\"

\n
\n
    \n
  • What does \"epistemological tyranny\" mean? What other examples \n of epistemological tyranny can you think of?
    • \n
  • Leffel cites contemporary music and cinema as examples of skeptical \n postmodernism. Discuss song lyrics and movies you've see that carry \n the cynical view that skeptical postmodernists hold.
    • \n
\n
Affirmative Postmodernism
\n
    \n
  • Affirmatives are sometimes referred to as \"constructivists.\" \n What does this term mean? Can you think of any good examples of constructivism? \n
    • \n
\n
\n

- Talk about examples like the gay rights movement and how they manipulate \n public opinion by creating words like \"homophobia.\" By inventing \n this kind of language, they seek to create a new paradigm for social \n morality, without seriously engaging the deeper moral issues. We just \n label people \"homophobes\" if they have problems with the ethics \n of homosexuality.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Where and to what extent do you see constructivism in the university \n today? Do we see constructivism in other areas of culture too?
    • \n
\n
\n

- Get the group to talk about what they find in literature classes, \n political science and other related fields. There will be many illustrations \n of constructivism from the university environment and the curriculum. \n

\n
\n
\n

- You could also discuss the recent motion pictures, The Scarlet \n Letter and Nixon as affirmative, or ideological reworkings \n of history and literature.

\n
\n
    \n
  • In this chapter, Leffel makes a distinction between postmodern ideology \n and postmodern culture. We can pick up postmodern ideology though comments \n and sentiments people express. Postmodern-influenced people will often \n say,
    \n \"Intuition and feelings might tell us more about reality than does \n reason\"
    \n \"People do what they do because their culture made them what they \n are (we are cultural constructs)
    \n \"Government is nothing but a bunch of self-serving politicians. \n They'll never really serve the people\"
    \n \"The legal system is set up to cater to the rich and powerful. \n You can't get justice if you're a minority or poor.\"
    \n \"No one knows what really happened in history, because people have \n burned the accounts they didn't want to pass along\"
    \n \"You don't look to religion for objective answers. It's just a \n matter of personal belief and what works best for you.\"
    • \n
  • Discuss these views in the group. To what extent does the group identify \n with them? Why or why not? Is it possible some statements might have \n both a bad and a good component? What would be a biblical view of these \n thoughts?
    • \n
