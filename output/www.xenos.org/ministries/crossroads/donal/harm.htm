Donal P. O'Mathuna

\n

\n

Let's assume someone can practice Therapeutic Touch (TT) and avoid occult \n involvement. Let's ignore the controversy over TT's effectiveness. What \n could be the harm in letting people try something which others claim is \n helpful? If practitioners are only waving their hands over a person's \n body, it's hard to envision any harm occurring. However, the more prominent \n proponents of TT claim it can cause physical harm--many presentations \n on TT fail to mention this. If students and patients are to make informed \n decisions about TT, the potential risks must be presented.

\n

Dolores Krieger warns that patients can experience \"restlessness, \n irritability, increasing anxiety and perhaps hostility or pain\" since \n they \"can overdose on human energies.\"1 \n Another book warns that too much TT on someone with a cold or flu can \n \"intensify the symptoms and cause more suffering,\" or can \"further \n inflame the condition\" if the patient has a fever or swelling. More \n seriously, the author states, \"When treating people with cancer, \n do not send energy to the area of the tumor. This will only serve to strengthen \n the disease and make it more virulent.\"2 \n While studies are not cited to support these claims, they reveal serious \n concerns about TT causing harm.

\n

Some patients are said to be more sensitive to TT than others. One author \n claims, \"Children's systems are very sensitive, and, as with medication, \n Therapeutic Touch must be given in smaller, more gentle doses.\"3 \n Brenda Parkes found that TT increased anxiety in elderly patients and \n cautioned against its use with them.4 Another \n article recommends that the \"process be brief for infants, very debilitated \n patients and the elderly. Additionally, it is recommended that directing \n energy to the head be done for only a short time. Otherwise, there are \n no risks to the patient.\"5

\n

Possible harm to TT practitioners is usually described as the healer \n absorbing some of the \"negative energy\" from the patient, which \n can manifest itself as headaches. Practitioners may experience some of \n the side effects found with meditation. Among transcendental meditation \n practitioners, 48 percent reported adverse effects, including anxiety, \n depression, confusion, frustration, mental and physical tension, and inexplicable \n outbursts of antisocial behavior.6

\n

Practitioners from cultures steeped in Eastern meditative practices frequently \n warn of their dangers, especially for Westerners dabbling in unfamiliar \n practices: \"At the initial state of meditation, a cultivator often \n experiences emotional instability, physical abnormalities and hallucinations.\"7 \n \"Visualization is never innocuous. Even minds that are far less disciplined \n than those of advanced yogis or adepts can generate actual changes in \n the energy field. . . . What is important to realize in this connection \n is that ignorance does not always protect us from harm. . . . By naively \n adopting certain visualization practices, we may well endanger our mental \n and physical health, not only in this lifetime, but in future embodiments \n as well.\"8

\n

The Spiritual Emergence Network is an international New Age organization \n set up to counsel people through a \"spiritual emergency.\" These \n crises can be precipitated by events like the death of a loved one or \n a divorce, but also when \"people are brought into more direct and \n conscious relationship to their own life force, or prana.\"9 \n This organization reports that people can become confused and isolated, \n leading to severe depression, anxiety and a lack of interest in everyday \n life, leading some to psychiatric hospitalization or suicide attempts.

\n

In the promotion of holistic approaches to health and healing, the potential \n for holistic harm is rarely addressed. Physical harm may occur, but the \n potential for spiritual, emotional and psychological harm is high. Christians \n especially must be concerned about the harm of leading people into false \n belief systems. Beliefs have consequences (Rom 1:25-27). God forbids occult \n involvement because he loves us and seeks to protect us from harm.
