Introduction

\n
\n

Western people in the 1990's are experiencing the most sweeping shift \n in perspective since the enlightenment. The Death of Truth details \n the dramatic and earth-shaking conversion of western society from the \n modernist world view to the postmodern world view. No book can take \n you more quickly and painlessly into the heart of the new postmodern \n outlook than The Death of Truth. You will feel the lights going \n on time after time as you read this easy-to-understand volume

\n
\n
\n

This Study Guide is designed to help groups discuss the book section \n by section as members read through it on their own. Each week, appoint \n one discussion facilitator. The facilitator needs to carefully read \n the section in The Death of Truth for that week, and look over \n the discussion questions and suggested answers in the facilitator's \n guide ahead of time in order to offer leading thoughts. For each week, \n we have provided more material than you will likely be able to cover. \n Therefore, the facilitator should select the questions for study each \n weak. Avoid lecture, except for short 2 to 3 minute explanations or \n transitions. If people discuss and struggle with these concepts they \n will feel more satisfaction and depth in their learning.

\n
\n
\n

Encourage every participant to buy his or her own copy of The Death \n of Truth, (couples can share) and to bring them to your meetings. \n The Death of Truth is published by Bethany House Publishers, \n and is available through any bookstore, especially Christian bookstores. \n Discounted copies are also available from our Worldwide Web Site at \n http://www.crossrds.org. When the guide says to read a section from \n the book, ask one of the members to read aloud, while the others follow \n along. Reading is an easy way to help start people speaking and participating. \n

\n
\n
\n

As written, this study could take 15 to 20 weeks to complete. If you \n want to shorten the number of weeks, we recommend skipping some of the \n \"Postmodern Impact\" chapters. We do not recommend skipping \n the first three or the last two chapters.

\n
\n
\n

We have arranged each week's study so you can copy the \"Discussion \n Guide\" for your group members while giving only the facilitator \n the \"Facilitator's guide\" which includes suggested answers. \n Questions asking only for personal opinion have been left without suggested \n answers.

Chapter 3
\n Our New Challenge:
\n Postmodernism

\n

Discussion Guide

\n
\n

Be sure group members have read the chapter.

\n
\n
\n

In this chapter we consider how postmodernism offers a response to \n modernist assumptions. Review the chart on page ___ focusing on the \n middle column, labeled \"postmodernism.\"

\n
\n
\n

Perhaps its best to think of postmodernism as the death of truth. \n Consider the statements below, taken at random from a series of recent \n interviews at a major university. The interviewer asked, \"Is there \n such a thing as absolute truth?\" Students answered:

\n
\n
\n

\"Truth is whatever you believe.\"

\n
\n
\n

\"No, there is no absolute truth, and if there was, how would we \n know what it is?\"

\n
\n
\n

\"People who believe in absolute truth are dangerous.\"

\n
\n
    \n
  • Have you encountered comments like this from your non-Christian friends? \n
    • \n
  • What barriers might such views create to evangelism?
    • \n
\n
\n

To understand why people think this way, we need to understand \n the postmodern shift in thought. A key underlying assumption of postmodernism \n is how they conceive human nature. While modernists viewed people \n as autonomous (and capable of independent rational thought), postmodernists \n see human identity and thinking as the product of culture. Leffel states, \n \"postmodernists deny we have a \"self\" that exists independent \n of our social reality. Culture and society create individuals \n as well as all their thoughts and attitudes.\" The following quote \n is by Peter Berger, whose analysis is often cited by postmodernists: \n
\n

\n
\n
\n

\"A thought of any kind is grounded in society . . . The individual, \n then, derives his world view socially in very much the same way that \n he derives his roles and his identity. In other words, his emotions \n and his self-interpretation like his actions are predefined for him \n by society, and so is his cognitive approach to the universe that surrounds \n him.\"

\n
\n
    \n
  • If \"a thought of any kind is grounded in society,\" certain \n important implications follow. These implications are widely accepted \n in today's postmodern influenced social sciences and in popular cultural. \n What are these implications?
    • \n
  • Leffel says, \"one of the main ways society shapes individuals \n is through language.\" This is probably the most abstract and difficult \n parts of the postmodern argument. Postmodernists point out that individuals \n always interact with reality through the medium of language. All mental \n activities, they say, are based in language. We think in words. We communicate \n with words. People are connected to reality through the labels they \n assign to their perceptions and ideas. These labels, or words, are arbitrary, \n and evolve in society. The more abstract (and often more important) \n our ideas are, the more dependent we are on words alone to provide meaning. \n But if language is the way people relate to reality, then we must understand \n the nature of language.
    • \n
\n
\n

Let's think about the components of language that postmodernists \n say makes objectivity impossible:

\n
\n
    \n
  • Semantics--the meaning of words and phrases. Postmodernists \n say that, since societies define words, and our thinking is rooted in \n language, we can't go beyond a culturally relative way of knowing. Knowledge \n claims themselves are just a matter of cultural conditioning. Can you \n think of any evidence that knowledge is not necessarily limited to a \n given culture?
    • \n
  • Syntax--the structure or logic of language. Postmodernists \n say that every language has a structure, or logic. Words are related \n to each other by connective words such as \"if/ then,\" \"and,\" \n \"either/or\" and so on. Postmodernists say that the linguistic \n constructions for the West promote a hierarchical, \"either/or\" \n way of thinking. The language of science, logic, and progress shape \n the way we think. That's why we don't think holistically and \n inclusively. Have you heard this kind of idea before? Where? \n
    • \n
  • Do you think there is a link between the postmodern view of language \n and the demands of \"political correctness\"?
    • \n
  • Do you think there are broader implications to the postmodern view \n that language shapes thought?
    • \n
  • Do you think language controls what you think?
    • \n
\n
Problems with postmodern analysis:
\n
\n

Postmodernists rightly critique the modernists' overconfidence in autonomous \n human reason. However, Leffel argues that they've thrown out the baby \n with the bath water.

\n
\n
    \n
  • They critique the correspondence view of truth. Postmodernists hold \n that all knowledge claims are arbitrary, and that none are ultimately \n more objective than another because we lack certainty. On a practical \n level, what's wrong with this critique?
    • \n
  • Imagine this discussion:
    \n \"There is no such thing as truth\"
    \n \"Really, is that true?\"
    \n \"Yes it is\"
    \n \"Well if that's true than there's at least one true thing--your \n statement! So that means it's not true that there's no such thing as \n truth.\"
    \n \"But my statement is that there is no such thing as truth.\" \n
    \n \"Okay, but then your statement isn't true, is it?\"
    \n
    \n This conversation sounds absurd, and it is. Is the dialog between Christians \n and postmodernists the same or different than this discussion
    • \n
  • Postmodernists say all thought and reason is shaped by language. But \n as Leffel points out, this is inconsistent with some things we know \n about psychological development and the development of language. Leffel \n cites authorities who show that the mind is active and makes clear rational \n distinctions prior to the acquisition of language. How do these \n findings impact that postmodern view of linguistic cultural determinism? \n
    • \n

Chapter 4
\n Postmodernism and \"The Myth of Progress\": Two Visions

\n

Discussion Guide for Two Visions

\n
\n

This chapter makes the distinction between two types of postmodernism: \n skeptical and affirmative. Both types of postmodernism reject the possibility \n of rational objectivity. In the place of reason, they say we are left \n with nothing but power.

\n
\n
Skeptical Postmodernists
\n
\n

Skeptical postmodernists claim that when people make claims to ultimate \n truth, usually religious or philosophical, the one thing we can count \n on is that it will be used to justify self interest and power. Among \n the examples Leffel cites are: so-called \"Manifest Destiny\" \n and the alleged inferiority of African Americans. These are example \n of what postmodernists call \"epistemological tyranny.\"

\n
\n
    \n
  • What does \"epistemological tyranny\" mean? What other examples \n of epistemological tyranny can you think of?
    • \n
  • Leffel cites contemporary music and cinema as examples of skeptical \n postmodernism. Discuss song lyrics and movies you've see that carry \n the cynical view that skeptical postmodernists hold.
    • \n
\n
Affirmative Postmodernism
\n
    \n
  • Affirmatives are sometimes referred to as \"constructivists.\" \n What does this term mean? Can you think of any good examples of constructivism? \n
    • \n
\n
\n

- Talk about examples like the gay rights movement and how they manipulate \n public opinion by creating words like \"homophobia.\" By inventing \n this kind of language, they seek to create a new paradigm for social \n morality, without seriously engaging the deeper moral issues. We just \n label people \"homophobes\" if they have problems with the ethics \n of homosexuality.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Where and to what extent do you see constructivism in the university \n today? Do we see constructivism in other areas of culture too?
    • \n
\n
\n

- Get the group to talk about what they find in literature classes, \n political science and other related fields. There will be many illustrations \n of constructivism from the university environment and the curriculum. \n

\n
\n
\n

- You could also discuss the recent motion pictures, The Scarlet \n Letter and Nixon as affirmative, or ideological reworkings \n of history and literature.

\n
\n
    \n
  • In this chapter, Leffel makes a distinction between postmodern ideology \n and postmodern culture. We can pick up postmodern ideology though comments \n and sentiments people express. Postmodern-influenced people will often \n say,
    \n \"Intuition and feelings might tell us more about reality than does \n reason\"
    \n \"People do what they do because their culture made them what they \n are (we are cultural constructs)
    \n \"Government is nothing but a bunch of self-serving politicians. \n They'll never really serve the people\"
    \n \"The legal system is set up to cater to the rich and powerful. \n You can't get justice if you're a minority or poor.\"
    \n \"No one knows what really happened in history, because people have \n burned the accounts they didn't want to pass along\"
    \n \"You don't look to religion for objective answers. It's just a \n matter of personal belief and what works best for you.\"
    • \n
  • Discuss these views in the group. To what extent does the group identify \n with them? Why or why not? Is it possible some statements might have \n both a bad and a good component? What would be a biblical view of these \n thoughts?
    • \n

\n Health Care

\n

Discussion Guide for Health Care

\n
    \n
  • There are many different types of alternative medicines. Which ones \n have you experienced, or are familiar with? What the underlying assumptions \n and beliefs of these practices?

There is as yet no cure for \n this painful disease which to date has led to 36 deaths.   • Dr. O'Mathúna argues against using anecdotal evidence and personal \n experience as a way to validate medical treatments. What is the difference \n between what he is critiquing and simply getting advice from others \n based on their experiences (which he would recommend doing, especially \n in medical matters)?

  • Dr. O'Mathúna pointed out that practitioners of alternative medicine \n often encourage people to just try their methods and see if they help. \n \"What harm could there be in that?\" they rhetorically ask! \n Well, what harm could there be in just checking out some of these \n practices? Are there any practices you think Christians should be especially \n careful to avoid? Why?

    • \n

Many others place great emphasis on intuition. For \n example, Engebretson and Wardell state, \"The patient should be \n encouraged to trust her or his own intuition and judgment\" about \n alternative healing methods and practitioners (Nurse Practitioner \n 18 (1993): 51-5). Use, for example, Jeremiah 23:26-17, 25-32 and Ezekiel \n 13:2-3 to develop a biblical response to this notion.

    \n
    • \n
  • In her book on New Age experiences (Testing the Spirits, InterVarsity \n Press, 1995), Elizabeth L. Hillstrom notes that the early stages of \n Eastern-style meditation often includes a variety of physiological experiences. \n \"They may include rapturous feelings, electrifying thrills and \n chills that move through the body, sensations of tingling, prickling, \n intense heat or cold, or of bugs crawling on the skin\" (p. 120). \n Do you see any connection between these and the experiences reported \n by practitioners of therapeutic touch?
    \n
    \n
    • \n

People experience these \n crises after starting meditation or any practice which brings them \"into \n more direct and conscious relationship to their own life force, or prana \n in Sanskrit\" (Emma Bragdon, The Call of Spiritual Emergency, \n Harper & Row, 1990, p. 5). The Text
\n
\n

One of the big problems many postmodernists have with texts is that \n they \"privilege\" certain values and ideas over others. Since \n values and truth claims are social constructs, texts that are \"privileged\" \n (accepted into the social and literary canon) perpetuate views that \n keep power for the dominant culture while marginalizing minority cultures. \n

The Reader
\n
\n

One of the tools postmodernists use to wrestle authority out of the \n text and into the reader is \"deconstruction.\" Deconstruction \n purges texts of socially constructed hierarchies, contradictions and \n identifies things excluded from the text.

\n
\n

Chapter 7
\n The Postmodern Method:
\n Education

\n

Discussion Guide

\n
    \n
  • Many Christians argue for \"value-free\" education in the \n public schools. They say teachers should simply teach students facts \n and leave the ethics and belief systems to parents in the home. Other \n Christians hold that \"value-free\" education is impossible. \n For example, forbidding cheating, requiring academic performance, etc. \n are necessary for the educational process, but they are values/ethics. \n What place do you think values, religious/philosophical beliefs, and \n ethics have in public education?
    • \n
  • What are the similarities and differences between the postmodernists' \n espousal of Afrocentric schools and Christians' espousal of Christian \n schools?
    • \n
\n \n
    \n
  • Consider the following passage by a science educator and then give \n your opinion on the questions that follow:
    • \n
\n
\n

\"The study of science and related technology often requires students \n to adapt to a white male culture, to an Eurocentric/androcentric world \n view. The basic assumptions of science, as it is taught to American \n children in textbooks, focus on male as opposed to female and on European \n as opposed to Eastern or African or South American ways of viewing the \n world. The axiological and epistemological beliefs of textbook science \n are tied to a European or white male way of viewing the world. This \n culture values competitiveness and individual achievement. Most modern \n science instruction is based on principles of realism. This value system \n holds that there is an ultimate truth and that humans discover this \n truth in the natural world. The Eurocentric foundation of science focuses \n on European (and on those of European descent) values, attitudes and \n ways of knowing.\"

\n
\n
    \n
  • What postmodern ideas are evident in this passage?
    • \n
  • What are these female and non-European views of truth that the author \n claims to be different than \"realism\"? Do women, men, Europeans, \n Africans, etc., as groups, view truth differently?
    • \n
  • Is it true - wholly or partially - that science is merely a white, \n European, male, worldview and therefore scientific knowledge is infected \n with this bias?
    • \n
  • Are competitiveness and individualism in education a result of white \n European male influence? What does the Bible say about competitiveness \n and individualism that is relevant to schooling?
    • \n
  • How would education be different, if at all, if women and non-Europeans \n were in control of it? Who should control the education system?
    • \n
  • To what extent is it true, if at all, that students \"must assimilate\" \n a certain instructional model in order to be successful at school? How \n might postmodern education change this?
    • \n
\n \n
    \n
  • Consider this passage, also from Barba:
    • \n
\n
\n

Sometimes teachers believe that culturally diverse children do not \n excel because they come from families and communities that do not properly \n prepare students for learning. Teachers occasionally believe that some \n students do not care if they do well in school, and that these students \n are not properly prepared to succeed in an academic environment and \n are not motivated to learn. The cultural deficit model as described \n by Sleeter and Grant (1990) assumes that schools should change learners, \n should help them assimilate to mainstream culture, including the \"culture \n of science.\" From this viewpoint, children need remedial work to \n compensate for their lack of knowledge, skills, and attitudes in science \n and mathematics. Those who advocate the use of this world view regard \n students as being \"at risk\" when they do not share American \n mainstream language and culture. The National Science Foundation pipeline \n approach typifies this world view. If we just call the plumber, if we \n just plug the leaks, then we can \"fix\" the students.

\n
\n
\n

In contrast to the deficit model, those who adhere to a multicultural \n model see the cultural and linguistic backgrounds of diverse students \n as being valuable educational resources. A 'multicultural approach to \n education promotes cultural pluralism and social equality by reforming \n the school program for all students to make it reflect diversity.' If \n one adopts this world view, children who speak a primary language other \n than English are considered an asset in the classroom because they have \n constructed a knowledge of science in a different sociocultural context \n than others in the class and thus bring added resources to the classroom. \n Those who advocate the use of a multicultural approach to education \n see diversity as a strength in the classroom, as a vehicle for increasing \n the learning of all students.

\n
\n
    \n
  • What postmodern theories and attitudes are evident in this passage? \n
    • \n
