  • When discussing Kuhn's and Feyerabend's work, Dr. Campbell refers \n to \"paradigms.\" Have you heard people using this word more \n lately? When postmodernists use the term, a paradigm is a model within \n which one set of truths hold. Other paradigms have their own sets of \n truths. In other words, a paradigm is similar to the idea of a cultural \n \"reality\" or, to use terminology from literary theory, a paradigm \n is similar to a social \"text\" or \"story.\" However, \n postmodernists aren't the only ones who use the term paradigm. How might \n people use the term paradigm without loading it with postmodern ideology? \n
    • \n
\n

- Before postmodernism, a paradigm was a model, or a way of approaching \n an area of thought. For instance, people who believed the world was \n flat, had a collection of reasons for that belief, and found it difficult \n to accept evidence that the world was round. After all, the horizon \n on the ocean was flat, water didn't run off the surface of the world, \n etc. After the world was shown to be round, these people experienced \n a paradigm shift--they had to change not only the way they viewed the \n world from flat to round, but also all the associated factors they earlier \n thought demonstrated the flatness of the world. Note that this does \n not suggest that the world really was flat for them--in fact the real \n world was always round. But to postmodernists, paradigms are reality. \n We cannot speak of a \"real\" world out there.

  • Dr. Campbell names several features used in scientific research intended \n to reduce bias and enhance objectivity. He included replication, blind \n testing, peer review and falsifiability. Do you think these aspects \n of research promise that scientific research will be relatively objective? \n How might each fail to do what it was intended to do?
    • \n
\n

- Replication could fail because the other research team produce the \n same results, but accept the interpretation on those results suggested \n by the first researcher. Later, it may be proven that while the results \n were valid the interpretation was wrong.

\n
\n
\n

- Blind testing could demonstrate a true correlation between one thing \n and another, but still fail to reveal the true cause and effect relationship \n between them. Just because we show that two things are statistically \n correlated, does not mean we have demonstrated what is the cause and \n what is the effect. Blind testing eliminates researcher bias, but cannot \n fix an improperly structured experiment.

\n
\n
\n

- Peer review can fail because other scientists may be locked into \n a paradigm that causes them to exclude contradictory information. For \n instance, stomach ulcers have only recently been shown to be the result \n of bacterial infection. For decades scientists have missed this fact \n in unison.

\n
\n
\n

- Some correct findings cannot be falsified, and some false conclusions \n cannot be demonstrated as false either. Particularly in more theoretical \n fields of study, conclusions are arrived at inductively, and are only \n accepted as the most probable conclusion.
