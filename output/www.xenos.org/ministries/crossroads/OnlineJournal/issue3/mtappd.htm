There is no evidence \n in these historical references that the psalmist viewed the \n narratives they referred to as anything other than actual history. \n The events cited correspond directly to the descriptions of \n those same events in the earlier parts of the Torah.[3] \n In some cases the psalmists recount and apply the historical \n information. In other cases they derive deeper meaning from \n the text, although the deeper meaning is completely compatible \n with it. In a few cases the psalmists reveal something new about \n the historical event that was not contained in the original \n text.

sacrificial system \n

    \n
  • \n

    It is fairly \n obvious that the wording of Psalm 51, \"you do not delight \n in sacrifice…you do not take pleasure in burnt offerings\" \n is remarkably similar to Samuel’s question, \"does \n the Lord delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices…\" \n Yet 1 Samuel 15:22 emphasizes that obedience is better than \n sacrifice whereas Psalm 51 emphasizes that contrition & \n repentance are better than sacrifice.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Again, the language \n of 1 Samuel 15:22 is much like that of Psalm 40:6-8. In this \n case, both passages emphasize that obedience is better than \n sacrifice.[4] \n

[2]Most \n of these references are to Psalms connected to particular events \n in the life of David. I will assume, in most cases, that the superscript \n or postscript information associated with a Psalm is legitimate. \n Some historical superscripts seem unsubstantiated by the content \n of the psalm that follows (e.g. 34). It's not clear whether this \n is because titles were speculative additions to the psalm, whether \n the psalms are generalized principles relevant to the historical \n antecedent or whether, as some argue, the superscripts are really \n subscripts for the previous psalm. In most cases, however, these \n sidebar comments are very ancient. According to Anderson these \n are not only found, \"… in MT, Qumran, LXX, and Targums, \n but it ought also to be noted that even by the time of the LXX \n translation (2nd - 3rd century BC) the technical terms contained \n there were so antiquated and obscure that the translators had \n a fair degree of trouble interpreting them. This is true also \n for the Targums. Furthermore, we find similar super/postscripts \n in other parts of Scripture (cf. Hab 3:1, 19b; Isa 38:9). There \n thus seems to be no reason not to take the super/postscripts seriously.\" \n This type of material differs from other references to the Pentateuch \n & Former Prophets because ostensibly the author is reflecting \n on the meaning of the events in his own personal history.
