This genealogy \r\n of Christ tracing his lineage back to Abraham establishes \r\n his racial, tribal and Davidic identity through adoption \r\n by Joseph (v.20 Joseph son of David). Official genealogies \r\n always moved from the oldest to the most recent.

\r\n
  • \r\n

    The connection \r\n to Abraham is important to begin to argue that he was \r\n the seed of Abraham, through whom all nations would \r\n be blessed (Gen.18:18; 22:15-18).

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    The connection \r\n to Judah could indicate fulfillment of the prophecy \r\n concerning Judah (Gen.49:10), that the ruling authority \r\n would not pass from Judah until the advent of he to \r\n whom it belongs.

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    The successive \r\n mention of two gentile mothers, who were clearly separated \r\n by several generations unmentioned, is meaningful in \r\n its irony that the archetypal king of Israel had gentile \r\n maternal roots. Not by power, might or genetic purity \r\n but by the Spirit of God would his promises be kept. \r\n

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    The association \r\n with the lineage of David is the beginning of an argument \r\n that Jesus could legally be the king from the line of \r\n David, through adoption, who would have an eternal kingdom \r\n (2Sam.7:2; Isa.11:1,2-6; 9:6).

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    The mention \r\n of Jehoiachin (a.k.a. Jeconiah) raises the tacit problem \r\n that a genetic descendent of Joseph would not be able \r\n to sit on the throne of David (Jer.22:30). A problem \r\n resolved by Christ's adoption into Joseph's line rather \r\n than descent from Joseph's line.

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    The mention \r\n of Zerubbabel is also interesting because of Hag.2:23 \r\n where God says that the grandson of Jehoiachin would \r\n have ruling authority (i.e. you will be my signet ring \r\n is the indicator of authority that God promised he would \r\n deny Jehoiachin, Jer.22:24).
