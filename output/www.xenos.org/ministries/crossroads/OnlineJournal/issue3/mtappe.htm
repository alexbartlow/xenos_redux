\n APPENDIX \n E - METHODS OF PREDICTIVE PROPHECY INTERPRETATION\n

Relevant \n Interpretative Schools[1]

\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
\n

Hermeneutical \n Approach\n

\n

Definition\n

\n

Grammatical \n Historical\n

\n

The grammatical \n historical technique assumes that words and sentences have \n fairly stable meanings during historical periods. As much \n as possible, passages are taken to have a straightforward \n meaning that can be determined through an understanding \n of vocabulary and syntax. This method allows for the use \n of literary devises like simile and metaphor. Usually, however, \n identification and interpretation of such devises is authorized \n by the text itself. The historic context of various texts \n is thought to be critically important for the interpretation \n process. This method is concerned with interpretation in \n context. It recognizes that biblical revelation is progressive \n and so interpretation of earlier material cannot be derived \n from our understanding of God’s activities obtained \n from later material. This method assumes that scripture \n is not contradictory and so opposing alternative interpretations \n are rejected. This method interprets uncertain material \n in light of incontrovertible texts.\n

\n

Midrash\n

\n

The term midras, \n \"to seek, examine, investigate\"; this refers to \n a Hebrew method of citing, interpreting and then amplifying \n an OT passage. This approach is defined in various ways \n by modern thinkers. Part of this is due to the many approaches \n found in rabbinical materials. For instance, Hillel's rules \n of interpretation are remarkably like the grammatical historical \n method but the additional rules of later rabbis are much \n more eisegetical. As a result, midrash, as practiced, was \n a mixed bag of exegetical but largely eisegetical methods \n including:

\n
    \n
  • \n

    Pesher \n - Contemporization of OT scripture (i.e. see below).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Apocalyptic \n - contemporization of portions of OT scripture.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Peshat \n - This method assumes that all text has literal meaning, \n even metaphorical language. From the 300’s AD the \n Hebrew term peshat[2] \n is used for Jewish literalistic interpretation. Typically, \n historical background is regarded as unnecessary to \n the interpretive process.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Allegorical \n - the belief that OT texts have real and ideal meanings \n and that the chief goal of interpretation is finding \n the higher meaning (e.g. Philo of Alexandria often used \n it)

    \n
    • \n
\n

Midrash also \n refers to the oral and then, later, the written collections \n of midrash expositions and applications.[3] \n Haggadah midrash refers to the ethical and expository \n interpretation of non-legal materials from the Hebrew Bible. \n Halakah midrash applied the general principles of \n OT laws to specific situations. This was an application \n of the Torah in a kind of 'case law' format. \n

\n

Application \n - This refers to the use of principles derived from antecedent \n texts in novel situations. As defined here, this approach to scripture \n does not confuse application with interpretation. Rather, application \n arises from interpretation. Obviously then, applications are going \n to differ depending on the hermeneutical approach used by the \n interpreter. Applications were clearly derived from earlier revelation \n by OT authors. For example, the psalmists use of the Pentateuch \n is typically application oriented but the applications are context \n sensitive and thus objective in their use of antecedent texts. \n

Methods of Predictive \n Prophecy Interpretation Applied to Matthew 2:15

\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
\n

Method\n

\n

Definition\n

\n

Perspective \n on prophecy[4]\n

\n

Pesher\n

\n

The term pesher \n means, \"to explain.\" In fact, however, pesher \n is an application of OT scripture with little to no concern \n for the context of the passage applied. Pesher may \n refer either to commentaries on the OT found amongst the \n Dead Sea scrolls or to the interpretive technique typical \n of these commentaries. Pesher interpreters assume \n that OT authors were speaking to the contemporary audience. \n This form of interpretation is tied to a word, text or OT \n allusion, which is then related to a present person, place \n or thing. The interpretations are generally aloof from the \n source context and appear to lack any coherent methodology. \n

\n

Pesher \n is part of what could be called midrash but is being distinguished \n here because it could be considered an independent tool \n of interpretation. On one hand, pesher may be regarded \n as a school of interpretation because it is used \n for many different kinds of scriptures. On the other hand, \n pesher may be held to be a tool of interpretation \n used by interpreters from various hermeneutical schools \n for a limited subset of scriptures

\n

Out of \n Egypt I have called my son, was about Christ's flight \n to and return from Egypt. There is no attempt to justify \n or correlate this use of Hosea with the context of the rest \n of Hosea's prophecy.\n

Retrospective; \n subjective
\n

Single Intent\n

\n

Concerning \n predictive prophecy, some proponents argue that God & \n the prophet's meaning are one and the same, thus the later \n meaning proposed by a Bible author is the same as the original \n meaning. Proponents are rightly concerned with the pitfalls \n of eisegetical approaches to scripture that arise when it \n is granted that a text may have more than one meaning.

\n

When Matthew \n suggests that, Out of Egypt I have called my son, \n was fulfilled by Christ's flight to and return from Egypt \n he gives us God's meaning. Hosea meant for this passage \n to be predictive of the messiah.\n

Projective \n & retrospective; objective, but not if pressed too far.
\n

Sensus Plenior\n

\n

This approach \n proposes that successive interpreters, perhaps with divine \n authorization, derive novel meaning from the original author’s \n text. That is, God had a double meaning in mind but only \n revealed the other sense to a later author. Thus, God guided \n Isaiah to say certain things with meaning in his own time \n but that God also intended Christ to fulfill.[5] \n This approach was developed in response to NT uses of the \n OT that seem to treat apparently non-predictive or 'single \n meaning' OT material as predictive prophecy.

\n

When Matthew \n suggests that, Out of Egypt I have called my son, \n was fulfilled by Christ's flight to and return from Egypt \n he means something quite different than Hosea meant. God \n had Hosea chose this words because Christ was going to fulfill \n them one day. No one could have anticipated this without \n Matthew's prophetic insight. \n

Retrospective \n but only through revelation; subjective
\n

Typological\n

\n

This perspective \n is defined in various ways. Generally speaking, typological \n interpretation involves identification of people, places, \n events or objects with significance that exceeds their immediate \n meaning. Once identified a type can be used to expose deeper \n meanings from typological reiterations or recurrences.

\n

This method \n may be difficult to distinguish from sensus plenior or straightforward \n application in some cases. For example, was the brass serpent \n a type of Christ or was it simply an illustration application \n of the principle of substitution? Did God reveal to Moses \n that this event foreshadowed a more complete propitiation \n or did Christ expose a second meaning unknown to Moses?

\n

When Matthew \n suggests that, Out of Egypt I have called my son, \n was fulfilled by Christ's flight to and return from Egypt \n he means Jesus was a type of Israel - ideal Israel. \n

Retrospective; \n potentially subjective.
\n

Analogical correspondence\n

\n

This approach \n suggests that the NT authors are simply noting material \n from the OT that is analogous to their own situation. Proponents \n advocate that the term 'fulfillment' is probably used by \n NT authors in its broader sense of \"completed\" \n or \"filled to overflowing,\" rather than \"prediction/outcome\". \n Proponents also hold that a citation may really serve as \n a pointer to an entire genre of OT scripture, thereby amplifying \n the analogical correspondence.

\n

When Matthew \n suggests that, Out of Egypt I have called my son, \n was fulfilled by Christ's flight to and return from Egypt \n he means that Jesus recapitulated the history of Israel. \n Although, unlike Israel, Christ returned as an obedient \n servant.\n

Retrospective; \n objective
\n

Motif fulfillment\n

\n

This approach \n is consonant with analogical correspondence at many points. \n For instance, proponents would agree the term 'fulfillment' \n is used more broadly in many cases and that a simple citation \n may, in fact, refer to an entire block of relevant material. \n This position would argue, however, that when an author's \n material touches on well developed motifs he is cognizant \n of a deeper meaning carried in his words beyond the immediate \n context. This deeper meaning explains earlier expressions \n of the motif and also anticipates subsequent reiterations \n of the same motif.

\n

When Hosea \n records, Out of Egypt I have called my son, he is \n tapping into an exodus motif that was expressed in the original \n event; reiterated and extended to \"the king\" of \n Israel by Balaam (Nu.24:8); reiterated when Joshua entered \n Palestine; reiterated when the principle of redemption was \n applied repeatedly in OT didactic material; that would be \n reiterated later when Israel was restored after her impending \n discipline (Hos 6:1-3; 8:1-10:5) and again when God would \n permanently redeem his people. Matthew was simply noting \n something implicit in Hosea, namely, Christ was the ultimate \n fulfillment of God's promised redemption of Israel (Hos.11:1-14:5). \n Hosea certainly understood that his recollection of the \n Exodus was anchored in God's past redemptive history as \n well as his future promise of final redemption. And, this \n is exactly what Matthew did by simply pointing out its manifestation \n in Christ. Christ returned to Israel from Egypt, as an obedient \n son and also as God coming again to dwell in the tents of \n Shem. The resonance with the exodus motif is so remarkable \n that Matthew could say Christ 'filled up to overflowing' \n the entire theme. If we were contemporaries of Matthew we \n too could have anticipated a final redemption of Israel \n and rejoiced when we saw its penultimate fulfillment in \n the first advent of Christ and hoped in its ultimate fulfillment \n in his second advent.\n

Projective \n & retrospective; objective.
