Facilitator's Guide, Religion Week 2
\n The Cardinal Sin of Objectivity

\n
    \n
  • The authors claim, \"Both evangelicals and modernists have historically \n believed in the use of reason, beginning with the law of non-contradiction: \n A is not non-A.\" Do you agree with this claim? Can you think of \n any exceptions?
    • \n
  • For those who believe there are exceptions to the law of non-contradiction, \n if rationality fails in one area, how do we know it is valid in any \n area?
    • \n
\n
\n

- Anyone who claims reason doesn't apply to some area of inconsistency \n in his own argument but does apply everywhere else is guilty of the \n logical fallacy called \"special pleading.\" When we special \n plead, we demand special rules for our own argument but we are not willing \n to let the opposition have the same or similar special rules in their \n argument. In other words, once we say there are exceptions to laws like \n non-contradiction in one case, we must allow that other exceptions may \n exist in other areas. Reason suddenly has no authority in defining reality. \n

\n
\n
\n

- Remember, saying something is beyond our comprehension is different \n than saying it is beyond the realm of the rational. See the example \n of a Xerox copier--we may not understand how it works (it is beyond \n our comprehension) but that doesn't mean it is irrational. That which \n is beyond my comprehension is not necessarily beyond someone else's \n comprehension. God, after all, comprehends everything

\n
\n
\n

- Realize that some theological formulations have historically been \n phrased as contradictory by theologians who placed no confidence in \n reason. Before accepting such formulations, make certain they are biblical \n and not merely traditional.

\n
\n
    \n
  • The authors say, \"Religion based only on personal experience \n and 'what's true for me' is perfectly compatible with the postmodern \n world view.\" Isn't experience important in Christianity? How would \n you distinguish between Christianity and other experience-based religions? \n
    • \n
\n
\n

- Experience is important in Christianity. Christianity is a personal \n relationship with God through Jesus Christ, and a relationship, to be \n personal, must be an experience. However, experience isn't what makes \n Christianity true. God's truth is true regardless of what I experience. \n Experience might help to verify the truth to me as one line of \n evidence. It does not make something true for me.

\n
\n
\n

- Experience plus rational evidence is completely different \n than experience instead of rational evidence. Experience also \n must be judged by truth, not vice versa. This means experience must \n ultimately be judged by reason when biblical revelation bears on my \n experience.

\n
\n
\n

- For instance, I may feel that a Bible teacher blessed me so much \n during a teaching, that he must be of God. Later, I discover that the \n text he relied on actually means something else in context. At that \n point, I should judge my experience as mistaken, no matter how pleasurable. \n

\n
\n
    \n

    • \n
\n
\n
