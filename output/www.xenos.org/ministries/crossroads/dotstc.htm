Facilitator's Guide, Chapter 2:
\n Our Old Challenge: Modernism

\n
\n

The chart on page 21 provides an outline of the issues discussed in \n chapters 2-4. Briefly review the chart, focusing on the column under \n modernism.

\n
\n
\n

The easiest way to understand postmodernism is to see that it is a \n rejection or revolt against the assumptions of modernism. This chapter \n is arranged around the four key areas of modernist assumptions:

\n
\n
    \n
  1. Human nature
    2. \n
  2. Human autonomy
    3. \n
  3. Human knowledge
    4. \n
  4. Human progress.
    5. \n
\n
    \n
  • As you review the chart's left column, how do you see these assumptions \n working out in our culture today? To help answer this question, think \n about each of the following four quotes:
    • \n
\n
\n

\"Each human being is a superbly constructed, astonishingly compact, \n self-ambulatory computer.\" Carl Sagan

\n
\n
\n

- Modernists view people as machines. No wonder some are prepared to \n practice abortion and euthanasia. Compare Sagan's statement to the next \n quote.
\n

\n
\n
\n

\"Man must realize that a fundamental law of necessity reigns throughout \n the whole realm of Nature and that his existence is subject to the law \n of eternal struggle and strife. He will then feel that there cannot \n be a separate law for mankind in a world in which planets and suns follow \n their orbits, where moons and planets trace their destined paths, where \n the strong are always the masters of the weak and where those subject \n to such laws must obey them or be destroyed. Man must also submit to \n the eternal principles of this supreme wisdom. He may try to understand \n them but he can never free himself from their sway.\"

\n
\n
\n

- Don't tell you people until after they discuss the quote for awhile, \n but this quote is from Adolf Hitler, Mein Kampf, p. 140! Where \n did this mechanistic thinking about humans lead in his case?
\n

\n
\n
\n

\"Nothing is in the mind that isn't first in the senses.\" \n Classical statement of empiricism

\n
\n
\n

- Isn't it true that we can receive knowledge from sources other than \n the five senses? Can't God speak to us through spiritual conviction?
\n

\n
\n
\n

- If modernistic thinkers only know what comes to their senses, as \n they say, how did they find out that no one else can know anything by \n any other means? If you or I had a direct experience with God, a modernist \n observer would have no way to determine whether it was authentic using \n only his senses, right?
\n

\n
\n
\n

- Therefore, empiricism leads to an arrogance in the form of claiming \n to know the exact limits of human learning and knowledge, even though \n no empirical means exists for acquiring such knowledge. This is a blind \n faith claim, not an empirical finding.
\n

\n
\n
\n

\"Such is the aim of the work that I have undertaken . . . to show \n by appeal to reason and fact that nature has set no term to the perfection \n of human faculties; that the perfectibility of man is truly infinite; \n and that the progress of this perfectibility from now onward is independent \n of any powers that might wish to halt it, has no other limit than the \n duration of the globe upon which nature has cast us.\" The Marquis \n de Condorcet

\n
\n
\n

- How has this optimism worked out? He thinks man has no limits. Have \n we become more aware of our limits recently?

\n
\n
\n

- See the arrogance in this statement. Such arrogance has offended \n not only postmodernists, but also theists.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Modernism has produced unexpected negative outcomes in society. See \n how many your group can name.
    • \n
\n
\n

Possible answers:

\n
\n
\n

- Reducing all knowledge claims to scientific verification eliminates \n the possibility of knowing spiritual or moral truths.

\n
\n
\n

- Human dignity lacks any meaningful basis, because as biological machines, \n there really isn't any difference between people and animals.

\n
\n
\n

- No wonder modernistic, materialistic theories like Marxism have resulted \n in unbelievable immoral atrocities.

\n
\n
\n

- Progress, as it is conceived by modernists, has had a disastrous \n effect on the environment. It has also been at the expense of native \n populations and given rise to colonial exploitation.

\n
\n
\n

- Religion, and specifically Christianity, has been effectively pushed \n out of its position of respect in society. Millions have been convinced \n that Christianity is unscientific.

\n
\n
\n

- We do not accept, however, that war and violence are the result of \n modernism. (See Chapter 14) Also, realize that religious worldviews \n have produced atrocities as well. Why is this?
