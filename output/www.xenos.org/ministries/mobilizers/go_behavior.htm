Page \n 18 — \n Behavior

\n

Pain, Pleasure and Deprivation

\n

Pain is not something to be shunned at all cost, nor is \n pleasure something to be sought at any cost. Both are simply part of life, \n not its main focus (John 10:10; 16:33; 2 Timothy 3:12; Philippians 1:29). \n The hardy person does not insist on having all his needs met. He is able \n to forego gratification. He knows that Philippians 4:11-12 precede Philippians \n 4:19, and he remembers that Jesus had unmet needs too. Willing to be content \n in all circumstances, this person is non-demanding, not necessarily choosing \n to do without, but able to accept what is available.

\n

Do I grumble or complain constantly? Frequently? How \n easily do I feel sorry for myself? What do I do when I feel like that? \n Am I “going without” anything today? How do I feel about that? \n What do I have to have to be happy?

\n

Balance

\n

This individual shows balance in his emotional, spiritual, \n physical, relational and professional life. He does not habitually overindulge \n in one area to the detriment of another, not needing “highs” \n or shots of adrenaline in order to survive and enjoy life. He is not easily \n addicted to anything. (This does not refer to substance abuse or other \n compulsive destructive behaviors, but is about the excessive “need” \n for certain good things in order to be happy—exercise, companionship, \n approval, etc.) the hardy person knows both how to do without for the \n short haul and how to be creative in meeting his legitimate needs over \n the long haul. (I Timothy 4:11-13).

\n

If my life could be represented by a page, how full would \n it be with the routine things expected of me, along with my own commitments? \n How big are the margins? How do I use my free time? What is missing in \n my life? What is out of balance? Who is being hurt by the way I'm living \n my life right now? What am I willing to change?

\n

Leadership

\n

The hardy person does not need to be in charge nor does \n hi abdicate responsibility when others are in control. He is a team player. \n Leadership is neither something to be sought nor something to be feared. \n He is willing to lead or follow (to submit to those whom God has placed \n in authority), believing that “God delights to work through the leaders \n he places over us” (Romans 13:1; Hebrews 13:17). No matter who is \n in charge, he maintains his commitment to the task. This person is not \n passive, controlling, or helpless. Barnabas illustrates this attitude \n beautifully (Acts 9:26-28; 11:22-26; 12:25; 13:1-15). The sermon Paul \n preached in Acts 13:16-41 seems to have established Paul as the leader \n from that time onward, but before that, Barnabas was in charge.

\n

How do I respond when I am told to do something I do \n not agree with? When do I find it most difficult to submit to authority? \n Why is this? How do I respond when I am asked to accept leadership?

\n

Perspective

\n

The hardy person is not narcissistic, knowing “the \n plot doesn't revolve around me; the story doesn't begin or end with me—it's \n His story. I am part of something bigger than I, and even if I don't see \n the logic or the resolution of this situation, I will keep on trusting \n God, who is sovereign in all situations.” (Daniel 3:16-18). This \n person can handle defeat because he is assured of ultimate victory, realizing \n his approval comes from God, not from people (2 Tim. 2:15).

\n

How much appreciation and/or acknowledgment do I need \n from others? When has someone else gotten credit for something I have \n done? How did I feel? What did I do? How would I react if that happened \n now?

\n

Self-Knowledge

\n

Non-defensive, the hardy personality displays an extraordinarily \n high level of self-awareness. He knows his strengths and weaknesses and \n is comfortable with these. This frees him from self-absorption and allows \n him to practice the agape love of 1 Corinthians 13:4-7. He is not blind \n or in denial, not is he compelled to compare himself with others (2 Corinthians \n 10:12). He is aware of his own level of need for rest and relaxation and \n addresses these and other needs as they arise.

\n

Who am I? What qualities in myself do I thank God for? \n Where do I need to grow? What dissatisfies me about myself? By what standard \n do I measure myself?

\n

Accountability to God

\n

A hardy person does not tolerate known sin in himself. Known \n thoroughly by God (Psalms 139:1-18), he invites God's searchlight (vs. \n 23-24) and lives in the presence of the Holy Spirit (Ephesians 4:30), \n dealing courageously with his own sin (1 John 1:8-9) and carefully and \n compassionately with the sin of other people (John 8:7, 2 Corinthians \n 6:14; Ephesians 5:11; 1 Thessalonians 3:13). He “keeps short accounts,” \n forgiving offenses as they occur, asking for forgiveness when appropriate. \n

\n

What things are sin to me? Is anything I used to consider \n wrong becoming acceptable to me? How is my hunger for righteousness manifested \n in my life? What could I do that would increase my spiritual health? Do \n I feel bitter toward anyone? What will I do to take care of that? Have \n I neglected to ask forgiveness for any known offense? When I think of \n forgiving others, what comes to mind? How do I feel? Where am I not at \n peace in my relationships?

\n

Responsibility
\n

\n

Able to accept responsibility, the hardy person sees himself \n as having an impact on life, an effect on those around him. He is comfortable \n taking credit for the good he does, and willing to take the blame for \n the bad that he does. He is not a blamer of others. He understands that \n wherever he is, he is making something better or worse. While he does \n not deny or rationalize his mistakes, or avoid responsibility, neither \n does he assume total responsibility. (Responsibility is not a biblical \n word, but we believe it is a biblical concept).

\n

Have I ever been unjustly blamed? What was my response? \n How could I have responded in a more healthy way? When I make a mistake, \n how do I feel? What do I do? How do I receive credit? When credit for \n something I've done is taken by someone else, what do I do or think?

\n

Generosity
\n

\n

A hardy person lives “life richly—giving love, \n energy, resources, time.” He is generous, open, trusting, working, \n giving. He does not hoard. He sees himself as one living in community, \n and shares in order that his brother should not be in need (Ruth 2:14-18; \n Luke 6:38; 2 Corinthians 8). He also realizes that receiving from others, \n allowing them to be part of God's provision, can be an act of generosity \n and is necessary for his own well-being.

\n

How do I invest myself in my world of family, friends, \n coworkers and others? What am I willing to let people do for me?

\n

Gratitude
\n

\n

The hardy person walks in gratitude. He believes that “we \n are…mutually dependent on one another” (Romans 12:5). He does not \n consider himself 'entitled' to what he is given but, realizing that everything \n he receives has cost someone else, lets people know their gifts of time \n and substance are valued. He accepts his own weaknesses, helplessness \n and need of “one another” care without shame, graciously allowing \n others to minister to him. He values the synergy of reciprocal ministry. \n “Cultivate thankfulness” (Colossians 3:15b, The Message).

\n

Who have I thanked this week? Who do I need to thank? \n How do I react when people thank me?

\n

Hope/Joy
\n

\n

This person has a wonderful sense of hope which anchors \n him (Hebrews 6:10), giving him stability in time of storm (Hebrews 10:23). \n This confidence in God makes a daily, positive difference in his life \n (1 John 3:3). The hardy person is not habitually pessimistic, critical, \n or easily discouraged when situations look bleak. His hope frees him to \n experience joy in his daily life (Romans 12:12).

\n

On a scale of zero to 100, with zero being a complete \n pessimist, 100 being a complete optimist, where do I fall most of the \n time? Do I inspire optimism or negativity in others?

\n

Resourcefulness
\n

\n

The hardy person is innovative and creative. This refers \n to the way he conceptualizes and utilizes options and resources. “I \n have only two pieces of wood and a string. Let's see what I can do to \n fix that pump.” He is not easily defeated, waiting for someone else \n to do something, not does he demand the ideal. This person is inventive, \n looking actively for options, not helplessly waiting to be bailed out. \n His approach to problem solving is “Let's see what I can do.” \n Paul's escape in a basket (2 Corinthians 11:33) wasn't very dignified \n but it certainly shows initiative on someone's part.

\n

How easy is it for me to give up when faced with an obstacle? \n How hard is it for me to find another way to deal with a situation when \n things go wrong?

\n

Flexibility
\n

\n

A hardy person is able to enjoy second choice. “I really \n wanted to do that, but it's raining and I can't. I'll do this instead.” \n This person can enjoy what is, rather than pout about what he cannot have. \n Disappointment does not send this person into a tailspin (Philippians \n 4:12-13).

\n

How, specifically, have I been disappointed recently? \n How did I feel about that? What did I do? Say? How long did it take me \n to recover? Find a second choice?

\n

Sense of Humor
\n

\n

Hardiness is reflected in the individual's use of humor, \n i.e. he can laugh at himself, does not take himself too seriously, is \n not easily offended if someone laughs at him. He teases others gently. \n He manifests spontaneity and flexibility, enjoys the richness and variety \n of God's creation.

\n

What makes me laugh? What have I laughed at this week? \n When have I laughed at myself?

\n

Rejection
\n

\n

The hardy person is not so fragile that failure defines \n or defeats him. He learns from his mistakes. He has the ability to pick \n up the pieces and restart after a failure, a rebuff, or a rejection. This \n makes him easy to supervise (e.g. a consultant overseeing a language project). \n “Having fallen down and hurt myself won't keep me from trying again.” \n

\n

How much of my energy is spent remembering hurts? Who \n are the people I do not enjoy? Who do I think does not like me? Why not? \n Where is there anger in my life? How does it show itself? Has my anger \n turned to bitterness in any way? What can I do about this? Am I willing \n to be evaluated? By whom?

\n

Courtesy
\n

\n

A hardy person knows that the lubricant of life is good \n manners, a respectful awareness of others and an easing of their way by \n attention to cultural niceties. “I am not an island. I cannot do \n as I please or act out negative feelings. I am aware of my impact on others.” \n “…Steadily pouring yourselves out for each other in acts of love, \n alert at noticing differences and quick at mending fences” (Ephesians \n 4:2-3, The Message). John 19:26-27 records Jesus' entrusting His mother's \n care to John. This meant Mary would not have to beg or worry. Anticipatory \n effort to make another's life smoother is the essence of good manners. \n

\n

How do others experience me in my daily interactions \n with them? Do I treat people as I would like to be treated? Do I respect \n others' needs and wishes? Do I recognize them?

\n

Use of Time
\n

\n

A hardy person is a self-motivated starter when necessary. \n He can reframe boredom into some semblance of productivity, making an \n unexpected 'loss' of time into something useful. He is able to benefit \n from reflective and/or interactive times and balance personal and social \n time for his own well being. (Colossians 3:23 The Message; Ephesians 5:16; \n Colossians 4:5)

\n

How was I thwarted, delayed or sidetracked this week? \n How did I react? How satisfied am I by the way I used time this week? \n Where would I make changes?

\n

Support
\n

\n

The hardy person identifies and maintains a personal support \n system. He can develop healthy relationships in a group (family, church, \n mission, neighborhood, etc.), setting appropriate boundaries, realizing \n the mutual impact people have on each other. (1 Corinthians 12:7-26; Ephesians \n 4:15-16)

\n

How much time did I spend this week communicating with \n supporters, fellowshipping with friends, reaching out? Where do I need \n to make changes? How can I do this?

\n

Learning
\n

\n

A hardy person is open to new ideas and ways of doing things. \n He is a life-long learner, willing to give up the comfort of the “way \n we've always done it” for the challenge and risk of implementing \n creative new ways and ideas. He does not hesitate to ask for input from \n others. (Proverbs 1:2-5; The Message; Proverbs 9:9 NKJV; 16:23 NKJV)

\n

How do I respond to correction? How open an I to changing \n the way I do things? How open am I to new information, methods, etc? In \n what area would I like to develop? What can I do to accomplish that?

\n

Conflict Resolution/Confrontation
\n

\n

Hardiness can withstand attack and face confrontation without \n a loss of inner balance. Such a person works to listen non-defensively, \n to hear and assess input without owning what is said, and to respond honestly \n to the issues raised. He is willing to change where he needs to but does \n not give in to manipulation. He is neither controlled nor devastated by \n others' opinions.

\n

Can I disagree with someone on an issue without taking \n it personally? Without attributing bad motives to him? How do I feel when \n confronted? How do I act? What would help me deal more healthily with \n criticism? On what issues, what conditions am I willing to confront someone \n or take a stand? \n

\n

These behavioral, observable demonstrations of hardiness \n are skills and attitudes anchored in God's Word, achieved with God's help, \n and practiced to God's glory. Growth is made possible by accurate self-knowledge \n nourished by Scripture, empowered by the Spirit of God, in company with \n like-minded followers of Jesus. We will become strong enough. “We \n pray that you'll have the strength to stick it out over the long haul—not \n the grim strength of gritting your teeth but the glory-strength God gives. \n It is strength that endures the unendurable and spills over into joy, \n thanking the Father who makes us strong enough to take part in \n everything bright and beautiful that He has for us” (Colossians 1:11-12 \n The Message).

\n

Note: The masculine pronoun is used for purposes \n of simplicity. The committee is fully aware that women are equally included \n in all of the above.
