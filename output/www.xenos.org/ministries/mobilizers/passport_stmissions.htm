Short-term \n Missions—Do They Work?
\n by Gwen Lee
\n

\n

Today, \n there is a phenomenal growth of short-term mission teams. For about the past \n 15 years the Church has employed the strategy of intensive two-week, two-month \n and two-year overseas missions teams to spread the gospel of Jesus Christ \n to more than 1.3 billion unevangelized persons in the world.

\n

Sounds \n exciting, doesn’t it? Still, an important question arises: Are short-term \n trips effective? Does the time and money funneled into these teams contribute \n to the Biblical mandate of evangelizing the world?

\n

Unfortunately, \n limited research is availabe to help find the answer. However, according to \n an interesting study done in 1991 by STEM (Short-Term Evangelical Missions), \n substantial changes took place in the perceptions and behavior of their short-term \n participants as a result of the participant's experience. These changes include:

\n
    \n
  • higher \n commitment level to world missions
    • \n
  • longer \n and more focused prayer for missions
    • \n
  • increased \n monthly financial giving to missions
    • \n
  • increased \n involvement in mission-related activities and education
    • \n
  • increased \n desire to return to the mission field.
    • \n
\n

All \n of these changes are likely to contribute to world evangelization. As STEM \n concludes, “A well-planned short-term mission experience is an effective \n strategy available to the Church for developing and unleashing potential human \n and material resources to complete the Great Commission.”
