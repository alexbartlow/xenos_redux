Paganism in the Old Testament

\n\n

It is perhaps under appreciated that portions of the OT are\nintended to be polemics against pagan mythology. Allusions to Caananite creation myths are\nincorporated into many passages arguing for God's supremacy. Rahab, The Proud One,\nLeviathan, The Twisting One, Yam and The Sea are all titles of someone or something\nwith which Yaweh does battle.[1]  \n5 of these 12 or so passages connect this battle with creation itself.

\n\n

He quieted the sea (Yam) with his power and by his\nunderstanding he shattered Rahab. By his breath the heavens are cleared; his hand pierced\nthe fleeing serpent. - Job 26:12-13

\n\n

You divided the sea (Yam) by your strength; you broke the\nheads of the sea-monsters in the waters. You crushed the heads of Leviathan; You gave him\nas food for the creatures of the wilderness. You broke open springs and torrents; you\ndried up ever-flowing streams. Yours is the day, yours is the night; you have prepared the\nlight and the sun. You have established all the boundaries of the earth; you have made\nsummer and winter. - Ps.74:13ff

\n\n

Comparisons between battle myths from the ancient Near East[2]  and biblical narratives suggest to\nsome scholars[3]  that Hebrew\naccounts of Yaweh-monster battles are anti-pagan polemics rather than evidence that Hebrew\ntheology is borrowed from surrounding nations.

\n\n

Three features are typical of Near Eastern battle myths\naccording to Wakeman: 1. A repressive monster[4]\n  prevents creation; 2. The monster is defeated by a god[5]  who thereby looses certain life forces[6]  and 3. The victorious god masters the\nlife forces to create[7]  and is\nelevated to king of the gods status.

Sumerian - the feats and\n exploits of Ninurta Asag (lit. to restrain) restrains\n Kur (waters) Ninurta (son of Enlil the\n separator of heaven & earth) destroys Asag causing a great flood. Ninurta restrains the flood to\n form the Tigris river. Ninurat appoints his mother Ki as a sort of mother earth. Ninurat\n is elevated to the high god position.
Indian - in the Vedic texts\n Vritra (lit. covering) a serpent\n that binds and restrains the cosmic waters pregnant with the sun. Vritra is one of a group\n of beings called the Danava. Indra the separator of heaven\n & earth is one of a group of beings called Adityas. Indra breaks Vritra's back, belly\n or mountains thus releasing the pregnant waters. Indra separates Sat (extant) from\n Asat (non-existent). Sat is ordered by Indra.
Akkadian - the Enuma Elish\n is an account of the Chaldean cosmogony. The primordial father Apsu (lakes,\n marshes & groundwater) and the primordial mother Tiamat (salt marsh,\n unspeakably hideous, gigantic and powerful) and Mummu (a sort of grand vizier). Apsu &\n Tiamat gave rise to the other gods but found them irritating. Ea (son of Anu, son of Anshar\n & Kishar) puts Apsu to sleep and then kills him; he trapped Mummu. Later Ea & wife\n conceive Marduk. Tiamat attempts to exact revenge but is defeated by Marduk who kills her.\n Marduk cuts her in half forming\n the sky and earth with the respective body parts.
Semitic - various Torah\n passages. Rahab & Leviathan are the same\n critter (dragon, proud, twisting) associated with the sea.[8]  Yaweh cut Rahab into pieces,\n pierced the dragon (Isa.51:9 & Job.26:13); Yaweh crushed Rahab (Ps.89:10) Yaweh rules & quells the\n turbulent sea that's associated with the dragon (Ps.89:10; Job 26:12)
\n
\n\n

Waltke argues that the Torah use of Canaanite mythology did\nnot affirm that the Semites believed such a creature existed. He says they used the myths\nto argue the superiority of Yaweh: * \n\n

    \n
  • These were strict monotheists (Job 31:26-28; Isa. 51:9;\n 44:6; 45:6-7) and as such would not have affirmed the existence of other gods. *
    • \n
  • Pagan references do appear in the Torah but in poetic\n passages where imagery is important for vivid illustration; it's not pagan theology that's\n borrowed, it's pagan imagery. *
    • \n
  • Strict monotheists would not have attempted to support their\n monotheistic view from pagan mythology, rather they used the mythological imagery to argue\n that Yahweh is the creator; that Yahweh defeated Pharaoh (Rahab was a nickname for Pharaoh\n at the time of the exodus and remember, God dried up the restraining sea Isa.51:9f;\n Ps.87:4) and the prophets used the imagery to convey Yahweh's contemporary and future\n defeat of his enemies culminating with the defeat of Satan Isa.27:1; Rev. 12:7-9
    • \n
\n\n

Thus, in every case where these references are used it is\nto affirm the triumph of Yahweh over his foes, the establishment of his rule on earth and\nhis superiority over the pagan gods.
