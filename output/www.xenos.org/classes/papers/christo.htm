By Dennis McCallum and Gary DeLashmutt

\n\n\n
\n\n\n

Since the focus of Christianity is on Christ, the believer must have a\nclear understanding of his person and work. Christology is the study of his person;\nsoteriology is the study of his work. The two are obviously related: who Christ is will\ndetermine what he is able to do. Virtually every heresy denies the deity of Christ and\ntherefore also denies to some extent his work on the cross for us. As a rule, liberal (and\nsome Neo-Orthodox) theologians deny Christ's deity, while some fundamentalists tend to\nunderemphasize his humanity.

\n\n\n\n

I. INCARNATION - HYPOSTATIC UNION

\n\n
A. Definition: Two natures (full deity and full humanity)\ninseparably united in the one person of Christ.
\n\n
B. Support:
\n\n
    \n
  1. Full Humanity: Jn. 8:40; I Tim. 2:5; Heb. 2:l4,l7
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Importance: Because man brought in sin and death\n (Rom. 5:l2-l9); in order to die; in order to be a true mediator-priest\n (Heb. 2:l7,l8).
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. Sinless Humanity: II Cor. 5:2l; Heb. 4:l5;\n I Jn. 3:5.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Importance: To be able to die for others (Heb. 7:26-28).
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. Full Deity: Jn. l:l-3; l0; 30; Heb. l:l0-l2; Rom. 9:5;\n Col. 2:9.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Importance: To reveal God to us (Jn. l:l8; Heb. l:l-3);\n to pay for an infinite amount of sin and absorb an infinite amount of wrath in a finite\n amount of time (Heb. l0:l0,l2).
    • \n
\n\n\n\n

II. KENOSIS

\n\n
A. Definition: At the incarnation, Christ gave up not only the\nenvironment of the Godhead and his position as Ruler, but also the use of his divine\nattributes.
\n\n
B. Support:
\n\n
    \n
  1. Jesus demonstrated non-use of omniscience (Mt. 24:36), omnipotence\n (Mk. 6:5), omnipresence (Jn. 4:4), righteousness (Heb. 4:l5;9:l4), justice\n (Jn. 5:30), and infinitude (I Cor. l5:3).
    2. \n
  2. Phil. 2:6,7. Jesus did not \"hold onto\" his equality with\n God, but rather \"emptied\" (Grk.: kenoo) himself of his divine privileges.\n
    3. \n
  3. Jn. l4:l0. Jesus \"can do nothing of Himself\"\n (Jn. 5:l9), but \"the Father abiding in Him does His works\"\n (Jn. l4:28). See also Acts 2:22; 10;38.
    4. \n
  4. Heb. 2:l7 - Jesus had to be made like us in all things (see also\n I Cor. l5:2l).
    5. \n
\n\n
C. Importance: Jesus lived just like we do--by dependence on God and\nempowered by the Holy Spirit (Jn. 14:10-17).
\n\n\n\n

III. DESCENT

\n\n
A. Definition: Sometime between his death and resurrection, Jesus\nvisited the waiting place of the dead.
\n\n
B. Support:
\n\n
    \n
  1. I Pet. 4:6. Christ announced his victory and their salvation to\n Old Testament believers.
    2. \n
  2. I Pet. 3:l9. He proclaimed defeat and judgement to either\n Noahic demons or the unbelievers of Noah's day.
    3. \n
  3. Eph. 4:9. \"Descent\" refers either to the incarnation,\n Christ's burial, or the underworld.
    4. \n
\n\n
C. Note: The meaning of the above passages used to support this\ndoctrine are not very clear, and no other essential doctrine is based on this tenet.\nTherefore, avoid dogmatism on this issue.
\n\n\n\n

IV. RESURRECTION

\n\n
A. Definition. Christ rose bodily from the dead, resulting in an empty\ngrave. Theories that the resurrection was spiritual or in the minds of the apostles are\nexcluded.
\n\n
B. Purpose
\n\n
    \n
  1. It validates the authority and truthfulness of Jesus and his message\n (Mt. l2:39,40; Jn. 2:l9; see also I Cor. l5:l4).
    2. \n
  2. It objectively demonstrates our forgiveness: that Christ's death was\n effective for forgiving sins (Rom. 4:25; I Cor. l5:l7;\n I Pet. l:3). The image of the Old Testament priest re-emerging from the Holy of\n Holies is alluded to in the book of Hebrews and elsewhere. The fact that the priest\n survived was evidence that the sacrifice had been accepted by God.
    3. \n
  3. It affirms the fact of the believer's future resurrection and eternal\n life (I Cor. l5:l8-23).
    4. \n
  4. It assures the believer's position in Christ (Rom. 6:4,5; Eph. 2:6)\n and the power to live the Christian life (Eph. l:l9,20).
    5. \n
\n\n
C. False explanations and their refutations
\n\n
    \n
  1. Stolen Body Theory. Someone stole the body, which explains the\n disciples' belief in the resurrection as well as the empty tomb.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Refutation: Who would steal it? The Romans and the Jews would want Christ\n to be provably dead. A practical jokester wouldn't risk death. The disciples wouldn't be\n martyred for what they knew to be a lie.
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. Swoon Theory. Jesus didn't really die. He was given a drug while\n on the cross which simulated death, and later recovered, revealed himself to his disciples\n as resurrected, and then lived out the rest of his life in hiding.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Refutation: After hanging on a cross for several hours, being pierced in\n his side and wrapped mummy-style, how did he break the burial linen, roll the stone away,\n overpower the guards, and after crawling to the upper room (on his elbows and knees),\n succeed in convincing his disciples that he was resurrected?
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. Hallucination Theory. The disciples didn't really see Jesus raised\n from the dead. They hallucinated seeing him, because they wanted so badly for him to be\n alive.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Refutation: This still doesn't deal with the empty tomb. Evidence for the\n existence of mass hallucinations under any circumstances is lacking. In addition, his\n disciples sometimes didn't even recognize him at first (Lk. 24:l6,3l). Jesus went out\n of his way to make sure they knew he wasn't a spirit or hallucination (Jn. 20:25,27;\n Lk. 24:36-43). Neither were they necessarily inclined to look for a resurrection\n (Lk. 24:11).
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. Legend Theory. The story of the resurrection is not factual, but\n reflects a legend which the early church came to believe.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Refutation: The gospels and epistles have been accurately dated to 50-95\n A.D. There was not enough time for such a false legend to arise, since eye-witnesses could\n easily discredit it. Also, this does not explain why the disciples were willing to die for\n their message when they knew full well that it was a lie.
    • \n
\n\n\n\n

V. ASCENSION

\n\n
A. Definition. Christ was taken up into the sky bodily, and seated at\nthe right hand of God in heaven.
\n\n
B. Purpose:
\n\n
    \n
  1. He may have presented his sacrifice to the Father\n (Heb. 9:l4--although this may have occurred while he was dead).
    2. \n
  2. He went to prepare a place for believers (Jn. l4:2,3).
    3. \n
  3. He went to be \"glorified\" which allowed the Holy Spirit to be\n sent to the church (Jn. 7:38,39). The exact meaning of this term is unclear.
    4. \n
\n\n\n\n

VI. SESSION

\n\n
A. Definition. Christ sits at the right hand of God, interceding for\nbelievers and guiding the church as its head during the present age.
\n\n
B. Purpose:
\n\n
    \n
  1. To mediate between us and the Father (I Jn. 2:l,2;\n Heb. 7:25).
    2. \n
  2. To rule and guide the church (Eph. l:20,23).
    3. \n
\n\n\n\n

VII. SECOND ADVENT

\n\n
A. Definition. Christ will physically return to earth to rule,\ndescending bodily from the sky (Acts 1:9-11).
\n\n
B. Purpose:
\n\n
    \n
  1. To take over the world and rule it as king for a thousand years\n (Rev. 19:11-16).
    2. \n
  2. Reward the members of the church for their deeds done in the body\n (II Cor. 5:10; I Cor. 3:10-15).
    3. \n
\n\n

ASSIGNMENT:

\n\n
1. Know the refutations of the false resurrection theories.
\n\n
2. Know the four doctrinal implications of the resurrection and the\ndesignated memory verses for each.
