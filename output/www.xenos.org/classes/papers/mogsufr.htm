I. How to Teach It

\n
    \n
  1. The \"inner and outer man\" motif. Paul sometimes uses language referring\n to the \"flesh\" or sin nature as the outer man (II Cor. 4:7-18; Rom. 7). The main\n idea here is that suffering serves to \"break down\" the outer man so that the\n life of Christ can be \"manifested\" or revealed through the believer. This\n process is seen as important for victory over the power of sin (Rom. 7:24), and for\n effectiveness in ministry (II Cor. 4:12). This approach is taught effectively by Watchman\n Nee, in The Release of the Spirit.
    2. \n
  2. The \"life out of death\" motif. The pattern of death, resurrection and\n victory is evident in numerous passages. Both justification (Rom. 4:25) and our position\n in Christ are gained in this way (Rom. 6:1-11). In addition, the Bible teaches that there\n ought to be a process of death-resurrection involved in spiritual growth (Lk. 9:22-24; Jn.\n 12:24 Rom. 8:13; II Cor. l:4-9; Phil. 3:10-11; I Pet. 4:1). In these passages,\n \"death\" refers to the breakdown of the old pattern of self-sufficiency through a\n gradual process of suffering. See a representative example of this approach in Miles\n Stanford, Principles of Spiritual Growth.
    3. \n
  3. Strengthening faith, or deepening our understanding. Through suffering, we should\n gain an understanding of how to apply God's word to real situations and problems. This\n kind of wisdom cannot be gained without suffering. Likewise, only the one who has trusted\n God in the midst of suffering has experienced faith at the deepest level (Phil. 4:l0-l2;\n Job; Rom. 5:3-5; Jas. 1:2-5; II Cor. l:4; I Pet. 1:6,7).
    4. \n
  4. The \"parent-child discipline\" metaphor. Heb. 12:5-13 teaches that God\n disciplines us as a loving father in order to bring us to maturity. This training process\n includes more than just correcting us when we sin. It also includes working through\n seemingly unrelated suffering to expose and correct character flaws. If we cooperate with\n God in this painful process, we can look forward to eventual healing in these areas.
    5. \n
\n\n\n\n

Other Principles

\n\n
    \n
  1. We could suffer unnecessarily as a direct result of sinful choices on our part (I Cor.\n 11:30-32; Col. 3:25; I Pet. 4:15). In these cases, it is also possible that the suffering\n will cause growth, although even more growth would result if we avoided such suffering.
    2. \n
  2. Suffering is necessary for all believers. The statements in Phil. 4:11,12, for instance,\n could not be made unless suffering has occurred. Likewise, Heb. 12:8 states that all\n of God's children have become partakers of his discipline.
    3. \n

The Believer's Response to Suffering:

\n\n
    \n
  1. Suffering should neither be sought nor avoided. The issue of whether or not a given\n course will cause suffering is irrelevant. Only the will of God should determine our\n decisions.
    2. \n
  2. Note the tendency of the immature to sometimes equate problems resulting directly from\n their own sin with \"bearing the cross\" (I Pet. 4:l5). This includes sins of\n omission. By spiritualizing failure, they tend to perpetuate it. Is it God's will to bear\n a given type of suffering, or to take steps to correct it? The scriptures above apply\n primarily to suffering encountered because you are doing God's will, not because you are\n violating it.
    3. \n
  3. Wrong responses:
