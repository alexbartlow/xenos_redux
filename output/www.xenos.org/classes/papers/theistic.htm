\n\n\n\n

There are three major approaches to theistic apologetics in common use\ntoday. They are the Classical approach, the Evidentialist approach, and the\nPresuppositional approach.

\n\n\n\n

Section 1
\nClassical Apologetics

\n\n

Modern Classical Apologists include Charles Hodge, B.B. Warfield,\nCharles Hartshorne, F. R. Tennant, Norman Geisler, R.C. Sproul, and John Gerstner.

\n\n

The importance of studying classical apologetics stems from the fact\nthat most secular philosophy courses only dialogue with the classical arguments. In\naddition, Classical Apologetics are an item of dogma for the Roman Catholic Church. As\nChristiani puts it,\n

    \n
  • \"Before starting to examine modern forms of apologetics,... Are such\n forms permitted; are they lawful?...Yes, but with a few conditions: first, they do not\n contradict classical apologetics and, secondly, that they are in some way connected with\n it. No one may reject classical apologetics, for, as we have seen, it has been sanctioned\n by the Vatican Council, and the principles then laid down have been repeated by St Pius X\n in his Encyclical Pascendi. These two documents, which we must not reject, indicate the\n proper end for Catholic apologetics, and the essential means for gaining this end.\"[footnote 1]
    • \n
\n\n

As a result of these two facts, most students today are led to believe\nthat these arguments are the main line of Christian defense, even though many of them are\nnot particularly effective. Classical apologetics should be understood then, not only for\nuse in evangelism, where appropriate, but also in order to be prepared to critique some of\nthe weaker arguments.

\n\n

There are three classical arguments for the existence of God.

\n\n

I. The Ontological Argument

\n\n

A. Explained

\n\n

This argument attempts to prove that the existence of God necessarily\nfollows from the idea of God.

\n\n

Proslogion 2 form (positive form):[footnote\n2]

\n\n
    \n
  1. Everyone can conceive of God (the infinite, eternal necessary Being of\n Scripture).
    2. \n
  2. God is that being, greater than which cannot be conceived.
    3. \n
  3. A non-existence God is not that greater than which cannot be conceived,\n because a greater God can be conceived-- one who does exist.
    4. \n
  4. Therefore God exists.
    5. \n
\n\n

Descartes' Meditation #3 (another positive form)

\n\n
    \n
  1. Everyone can conceive of God.
    2. \n
  2. The idea of God is that of an infinite perfect unlimited Being.
    3. \n
  3. For everything there must be a cause as great or greater than the effect.
    4. \n
  4. Therefore, for the idea of God which we have, there must be an infinite,\n perfect, unlimited cause.
    5. \n
  5. But we are not infinite, perfect or unlimited--and thus could not create\n such an idea ourselves.
    6. \n
  6. Therefore there must be a cause outside of ourselves, which is infinite,\n perfect, and unlimited, which caused the idea of God which we have in our minds.
    7. \n
  7. This Cause is the God of the Bible
    8. \n
\n\n

Proslogion 3 form (a negative form)

\n\n
    \n
  1. No contradictory thing can be conceived of.
    2. \n
  2. No inconceivable thing can exist (where inconceivable because\n contradictory).
    3. \n
  3. God is by definition the greatest thing which can exist.
    4. \n
  4. God either exists just as an idea, or really exists.
    5. \n
  5. To really exist is greater than to be just an idea.
    6. \n
  6. Therefore a God who is just an idea is a contradiction in terms.
    7. \n
  7. Therefore a God who is just an idea is inconceivable.
    8. \n
  8. Therefore a God who is just an idea cannot exist.
    9. \n
  9. Therefore God must really exist.
    10. \n
\n\n

B. Critiqued

\n\n

The Ontological argument is not effective and should not be used.\n

    \n
  1. We cannot deduce the existence of a being from the idea of that being.\n The attempts to do so usually involve untrue premises or conclusions that do not follow\n from the premises.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • -- In Proslogion 2 form, #4 does not necessarily follow from #1-3.
    • \n
  • -- In Descartes' Meditation #3, premise #3 is unproven. Further, it is\n the God we think of that is perfect, not our thought of Him. This undermines #4.\n Conclusion #6 is false. We are not claiming to understand infinity, only to be aware of\n it.
    • \n
  • - In Proslogion 3 form, step #5 is neither a logical conclusion nor an\n axiom, but an unproven value judgment.
    • \n
\n\n

II. Teleological or Design Argument

\n\n

A. Explained

\n\n

Argues that the presence of order in the universe requires the existence\nof an orderer.

\n\n

A typical formulation of the argument.

\n\n
    \n
  1. The universe manifests evidence of design.
    2. \n
  2. All design demands a designer (which entails x,y,and z characteristics).
    3. \n
  3. Therefore, the universe must have a designer.
    4. \n
  4. This designer is the God of the Bible.
    5. \n
\n\n

Paley's Watch argument.[footnote 3]

\n\n
    \n
  • -- While walking through a field, one finds a watch on the ground. He\n naturally and rightly concludes that it had a watchmaker. Likewise, if one studies the\n more complex design in the natural world, he cannot but conclude that there is a world\n Designer behind it.
    • \n
\n\n

B. Critiqued

\n\n

There are limitations to the teleological argument that we should be\naware of. However, if used properly, this argument can be helpful.[footnote 4]\n

    \n
  1. David Hume produced effective criticisms of the teleological argument in\n his essay, \"Dialogues Regarding Natural Religion.\"[footnote 5] The main salient points were that
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • The term \"design\" needs to be defined. He shows that there is\n unavoidable subjectivity involved in the concept of design. Two observers can look at the\n same thing without agreeing that it shows design. Any universe must have\n \"design\" in order to exist. Can we describe what a universe without\n design would be?
    • \n
  • It is also claimed that the theist selects some features in the universe\n to demonstrate that God must have made them, but attributes other features of the world\n (such as evil and suffering) to man. This is seen as special pleading. Special\n pleading means that one side of an argument is claiming rules for itself which it will not\n extend to the other side. In other words, it is improper to point to the nature of the\n cosmos to prove the existence of God, but to plead that some of the features of that\n cosmos must be excepted.
    • \n
  • It is argued that the presence of whatever design there is, might be an\n isolated exception. Perhaps we are an island of design in a vast ocean of chaos. This\n argument is pointing to the difficulty inherent in the area of probability. No matter how\n improbable something is, it could still happen. If a million-sided dice is rolled, then\n any number that comes up is one chance in a million. Yet, it did come up on the first\n roll! A person could also win the state lottery even though it is unlikely. In the same\n way, our own world may have occurred by chance, no matter how unlikely.
    • \n
  • Paley's watch argument is attacked on the basis of special pleading as\n well. What makes the watch stand out in the field where it is found? If the answer is\n \"the presence of design\", then it is evident that the design of the watch set it\n apart from the rest of the field-- (that is, the watch shows design, while the field does\n not). How then can the watch be used to demonstrate that the field has design, when it is\n the difference between the watch and the field that drew our attention to it in the first\n place?
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. The main problem with the teleological argument is the effort usually\n made to frame it as an inescapable deductive proof of theism. It would be far better to\n frame this argument as an inductive argument which points to the presence of a designer as\n one of the more likely explanations for apparent design. This has to do with the\n \"burden of proof\" issue: namely, that the Christian need show no more proof for\n his position than anyone else.
    2. \n
  2. Although the teleological argument makes the existence of a designer\n likely, the argument by itself does not lead necessarily to belief in biblical monotheism.\n It could, for instance, also support polytheism.
    3. \n
  3. Critics feel the Darwinian theory of natural selection has destroyed the\n teleological argument, by showing that changes come from purely natural causes rather than\n by special design. This is not true. While natural selection may account for which forms\n \"survive\", it cannot account for which forms \"arrive\". Stated\n differently, a distinction must be made between change within given levels of\n complexity, versus the emergence of new levels of complexity. For example, a screw cutting\n machine may accidentally produce a backward threaded screw. However, it will not produce a\n T.V.!
    4. \n
\n\n

III. The Cosmological Argument

\n\n

A. Defined

\n\n

The Cosmological argument differs from the Teleological argument in that\nit considers why anything exists whereas the Teleological considers the nature\nof what does exist.

\n\n

A summary of Geisler's Cosmological Argument
\n(a deductive argument with an
\na posteriori first premise)

\n\n
    \n
  1. Some contingent [dependent] beings exist
    2. \n
  2. All dependent beings must have a cause or explanation for their dependent\n existence (cf. Principle of Sufficient Reason)
    3. \n
  3. An infinite regress of existentially dependent causes is impossible
    4. \n
  4. Therefore there must be a first uncaused Cause of the dependent beings.
    5. \n
  5. This Uncaused Cause is the God of the Bible.
    6. \n
\n\n

Sproul's cosmological dilemma

\n\n

Either the Universe is:

\n\n
    \n
  1. Illusion-- (but I think, therefore I am- Who is thinking of\n non-existence?)
    2. \n
  2. Spontaneously Generated-- (but this is an unthinkable thought, because it\n is an effect with no cause)
    3. \n
  3. Eternal-- (but it would still need a cause)
    4. \n
  4. Created by something eternal.
    5. \n
\n\n

A simpler statement

\n\n
    \n
  1. It is assumed in science and reasoning that any effect or event must have\n a cause. Since the universe is an effect, or result, then the universe as a whole has a\n cause.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Illustration-- A 10' wooden ball would seem to require a maker, or\n a cause for its existence. It should be clear that one several times as large also\n requires a cause. If a 100 foot ball would require a cause, how much more a ball 25000\n miles in diameter, not to mention, one the size of the universe?
    • \n
\n\n

Scientific Arguments

\n\n
    \n
  1. The scientific laws governing energy transfer (thermodynamics) suggest\n that the amount of energy in the universe is constant, though changing in form.
    \n
    \n Furthermore, in all closed systems we observe a leveling process. Physicists point out\n that there is a universal tendency toward greater entropy. This means, among other things,\n that the amount of energy available for use is always decreasing. In a sense, the Universe\n is running down, which means that the universe should eventually reach a state of complete\n entropy.
    \n
    \n This implies that the Universe is not eternal, because if it was eternal, it would have\n reached a position of complete entropy an eternity ago. This suggests that the Universe\n had a cause which is beyond itself.[footnote 6]
    2. \n
  2. The \"red shift\" Doppler effect shows astronomers that the\n universe is expanding. Such movement could not have been in progress forever. This is the\n origin of the so-called \"big bang\" theory, which is more or less axiomatic\n today.
    \n
    \n There is no known reason why a big bang would have occurred, or why the present inertia of\n the stars would ever reverse direction, as in the theory of the oscillating universe. This\n is to say that the theories that might account for such oscillation are unproven, and\n involve phenomena which have never been observed.
    3. \n
\n\n

B. Critiqued

\n\n

The main critique of the cosmological argument is that it involves\nspecial pleading. \n

    \n
  1. If everything requires a cause, then God requires a cause. If God\n requires no cause, then not all things require a cause.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • However, if it can be shown that the eternal existence of God is\n different than the eternal existence of the Universe, this argument might hold up.
    \n
    \n John Hick has argued that since God is personal, He is not subject to natural law, (e.g.\n thermodynamic laws) which apply to the physical realm. Therefore, there are fewer problems\n with His eternity than there would be with the eternity of the physical universe.[footnote 7]
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. The position taken in the cosmological argument regarding an infinite\n regress of cause and effect (namely that an infinite regress is not possible because the\n whole string requires a cause) fails to come to grips with the word \"infinite\".\n If a cause and effect chain is infinite, then the reason for it is always the\n previous cause. To appeal to any outside cause is to beg the question entirely.
    2. \n
  2. The appeals to scientific laws and to the big bang are primarily useful\n for showing that there are problems with the naturalistic view. They do not prove the\n existence of God. However, by comparing the theistic model with the naturalistic model in\n the light of these laws of nature, it is possible to show that theism would remove some\n problems that naturalism cannot. the result is greater probability for theism.
    3. \n
\n\n

Summary:

\n\n

The classical arguments cannot prove the existence of God. If they\ncould, the explanation would be pages long, and incomprehensible to the average man.\nFinally, if classical apologetics could prove the existence of God, it would not\nnecessarily be the God of the Bible.

Section 2
\nEvidentialist Apologetics

\n\n

Evidential Apologetics are the product of the rise of modern science and\nmodern historiography. During the period of the enlightenment, Christians were eager to\nshow that a scientific approach to Christianity was possible.

\n\n

The type of scientific method used was a forensic, or inductive\napproach, which is similar to courtroom evidence. Forensic science collects evidence after\nthe event (e.g. an autopsy) and seeks to find the most plausible explanation for the\nevent. Such an argument does not claim to \"prove\" Christianity, but only to\nrender it probable.

\n\n

Today, Evidentialism is the main approach in use in American\nfundamentalist and evangelical circles. Spokesmen for the approach include Josh McDowell,\nC. S. Lewis, John W. Montgomery, Clark Pinnock, Hal Lindsey, and many others. Many other\nauthor's combine evidential arguments with other approaches.

\n\n

An Evidentialist Argument

\n\n

A. The Historical Argument (an inductive argument with a posteriori\npremises).

\n\n
    \n
  1. Historiography, textual criticism, archaeology, etc. demonstrate that the\n Bible is a reliable history book.
    2. \n
  2. The Bible teaches that Jesus lived, and claimed to be God.
    3. \n
  3. Jesus could only be (l) Lord, (2) a liar, (3) a lunatic
    4. \n
  4. Various parts of the description of Jesus' conduct and the response to\n his character rule out his being a lunatic.
    5. \n
  5. Other parts of his conduct and a lack of clear motive rule out his being\n a liar.
    6. \n
  6. Therefore Jesus was whom he said he was: Lord, and God.
    7. \n
\n\n

Additional Steps, tending to add verification to steps 4,5,6

\n\n
    \n
  • Jesus fulfilled prophecy in a way that a liar or lunatic could not
    • \n
  • Jesus performed miracles in a way that a liar or lunatic could not
    • \n
  • The historical fact of the empty tomb and the resurrection account cannot\n be explained if Jesus was a liar or lunatic
    • \n
  • The phenomenon of the faith, integrity, and sacrifices of the early\n Church cannot be explained if Jesus was a liar or lunatic
    • \n
\n\n

Critique of the Evidentialist Approach

\n\n

The evidentialist approach has strengths in certain contexts. It is used\nin Scripture, by Christ, the Old Testament, and by the apostles.[footnote 8] However, it is essential that the Christian\napologist realize some limitations of this method.\n

    \n
  1. Modern evidentialist apologists have made exaggerated claims regarding\n our ability to verify the historicity of the Gospels. Although we can demonstrate\n relatively good historical value in the gospels, it would be a mistake to think that\n secular scholars will be impressed to the extent that they would accept supernatural\n aspects of the narrative.
    2. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Evidentialists have failed to point out that the Gospels lack one feature\n that is considered important for credibility by modern historians-- namely correlation.\n That is, even though the gospels can be correlated with other sources regarding geography\n and non-Christian historical facts, they can not be compared with outside sources\n regarding the words, acts, and character of Jesus. This does not mean that the gospels are\n not historically sound. It only means that they are not as iron-clad as some authors have\n claimed.
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  1. The empty tomb argument is based in part on an argument from silence.\n This fact is mitigated somewhat by the fact that it is a conspicuous silence (because the\n body could have been exhumed) but it remains a sore point which has not been admitted or\n dealt with by evidentialists.
    2. \n
  2. The Lord Liar Lunatic dilemma is not a very strong argument from the\n standpoint of cogency. On the other hand, it is effective in confronting the hearer with\n his choices, and eliminating the likelihood of the \"good man\" theory. It does\n not usually deal with the legend theory sufficiently.
    3. \n
  3. Some Evidentialists have a reputation for credulity and deliberate\n distortion, especially when citing authorities who supposedly agree with the position\n taken, when they actually do not. There are even cases of gross misinterpretation and\n quotations taken out of context apparently on purpose. The work of many evidentialists has\n the appearance of \"working from the bottom line up\" (looking for anything that\n will prove the point) rather than careful scholarship.[footnote\n 9]
    4. \n
\n\n

Strengths of the Evidentialist Approach

\n\n

There are appropriate uses of the evidentialist approach. The main thing\nis to avoid attempting to make the evidences do more than they can legitimately do. This\nhas to do with the burden of proof question.[footnote\n10]\n

    \n
  1. The evidential arguments have strengthened the faith of many Christians.
    2. \n
  2. When addressing non-Christian's, the argument from fulfilled prophecy is\n the best part of the evidentialist argument to use. This argument has definite value in\n terms of establishing the likelihood of biblical revelation and the uniqueness of Christ.
    3. \n
  3. Evidentialist arguments can and should be used to create curiosity and\n willingness to hear a more complete presentation of biblical teaching. They should also be\n used to correct the misconception that Christianity is without evidence. They are, in\n other words often pre-evangelistic in scope.
    4. \n

Section 3
\nPresuppositional Apologetics

\n\n

Presuppositional apologetics have become prominent in recent years due\nto the work of several reformed scholars including Cornelius Van Til, Gordon Clark, E. J.\nCarnell, and Francis Schaeffer.

\n\n

At a time when classical apologetics are in general discredit among\nscholars in the secular world, presuppositionalism has enjoyed increasing respect in\nscholarly circles. Modern Christian communicators should read a good selection of the\nwritings of these authors, and become able to employ this approach when appropriate.

\n\n

I. Presuppositional Theory

\n\n

The central idea behind presuppositional apologetics is the fact that\neveryone does have certain presuppositions, no matter what their world view. This fact is\nobvious, although the exact presuppositions held in a given case may not be obvious. It is\neven possible that the presuppositions are not clear to the one that holds them.\nTherefore, there often needs to be a step of discovery of the other's presuppositions\nbefore applying the method.

\n\n

In some models (Van Til's for instance) there is the assumption that the\npresuppositions of all non-theists are the same. In others, this question is left open at\nfirst.

\n\n

II. Presuppositional Models

\n\n

Van Til's Presuppositional Method[footnote\n11]

\n\n
    \n
  1. Gain awareness of each other's presuppositions (epistemological\n self-consciousness) and of their absolute difference.
    2. \n
  2. Place oneself upon the presuppositions of the unbeliever for arguments\n sake--show that they do not provide an adequate reference point for predication (i.e., a\n sufficient base for reasoning at all).
    3. \n
  3. Show that on Christianity's presupposition (the ontological Trinity as\n ultimate Creator and Interpreter), rationality is upheld. Only Christianity does this\n (facts and laws analysis).
    4. \n
  4. Press the claims of the Gospel.
    5. \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Point out that since rational conversation has gone on, the unbeliever\n must abandon his presuppositions and adopt Christian ones.
    • \n
  • Point out why unbelieving presuppositions were chosen, and call for\n repentance and faith.
    • \n
\n\n

Schaeffer's One on One Approach[footnote\n12]

\n\n
    \n
  1. Establish communication and common meanings.
    2. \n
  2. Dialogue to discover the other's presuppositions.
    3. \n
  3. Carefully push the non-Christian's presuppositions to their logical\n conclusion with an attitude of compassion.
    4. \n
  4. Find the point of tension, pressing the unbeliever to compare the logical\n conclusion of his beliefs to the real world, himself and his actual conduct.
    5. \n
  5. Show that the Christian world-view better allows him to live in the real\n world, thus suggesting that this world view is true.
    6. \n
\n\n

Communication Guidelines:

\n\n

It is the view of this author that presuppositional apologetics are a\nbetter argument than most other approaches in the area of cogency (i.e. being convincing).\nHowever, most evangelists find that there are problems using this approach in witnessing.

\n\n

One problem that is frequently encountered is that the hearer becomes\nargumentative and resistant if he feels that he is being \"bested\" in a debate.\nIn other words, the presuppositional approach is confrontative, and therefore runs the\nrisk of further agitating the ego of the hearer. He begins to feel that he must not only\nsubmit his ego to God, but also to the apologist.

\n\n

It is therefore recommended that the presuppositional approach be used\nespecially in cases where resistance is being encountered, or where an argumentative\nattitude is already evident. Another situation that is good for a presuppositional\nargument is public speaking. In this case, since the opponent is imaginary, the crowd is\nnot provoked to ego-involved behavior.

\n\n

Another problem has to do with the difficulty of establishing step #3 in\nVan Til's method or #4 in Schaeffer's method. These are sophisticated arguments that can\nbe hard to simplify unless they are understood well. Additional reading is imperative if\nthis ability is to be acquired. An example of how this is done can be seen in the\nfollowing example.

\n\n

Taylor's Explanation of the Effects of Theistic Presuppositions on\nEpistemology

\n\n

Suppose that you are riding in a railway coach and glancing from the\nwindow at one of the stops, you see numerous white stones scattered about on a small\nhillside near the train in a pattern resembling these letters: THE BRITISH RAILWAYS\nWELCOMES YOU TO WALES. Now you could scarcely doubt that these stones do not just\naccidentally happen to exhibit that pattern. You would, in fact, feel quite certain that\nthey were purposefully arranged that way to convey an intelligible message.

\n\n

At the same time, however, you could not prove, just from a\nconsideration of their arrangement alone, that they were arranged by a purposeful being.\nIt is possible -- at least logically so -- that there was no guiding hand at all in back\nof this pattern...It is possible that the stones, one by one, rolled down the hill and,\nover the course of centuries, finally ended up in that interesting arrangement... For\nsurely the mere fact that something has an interesting or striking shape or pattern, and\nthus seems purposefully arranged, is no proof that it is . . . .

\n\n

(So far, this is a standard teleological argument. What follows is the\nconnection between the teleological and the presuppositional arguments.)

\n\n

Here, however, is the important point which it is easy to overlook;\nnamely, that if, upon seeing from the train window a group of stones arranged as\ndescribed, you were to conclude that you were entering Wales, and if your sole reason for\nthinking this, whether it was in fact good evidence or not, was that the stones were so\narranged, then you could not, consistently with that, suppose that the arrangement of the\nstones was accidental. You would, in fact, be presupposing that they were arranged that\nway by an intelligent and purposeful being or beings, for the purpose of conveying a\ncertain message having nothing to do with the stones themselves. Another way of expressing\nthe same point is, that it would be irrational for you to regard the arrangement of the\nstones as evidence that you were entering Wales, and at the same time to suppose that they\nmight have come to that arrangement accidentally, that is, as the result of the\nordinary interactions of natural or physical forces...it would be irrational for one to\nsay both that his sensory and cognitive faculties had a natural, non-purposeful origin and\nalso that they reveal some truth with respect to something other than themselves,\nsomething that is not merely inferred from them.[footnote\n13] (emphasis mine)

\n\n

A sound presuppositional apologetic belongs in the arsenal of the modern\nChristian communicator, because it is frequently very effective in public speaking, and in\ngaining respect in one on one conversations, especially with well educated and intelligent\nhearers.

The passages are interspersed with the four anonymous servant songs\nwhich, according to the New Testament, refer to Christ.
\n
\nFor usage of this method by Christ, see Lk.24:44 where Jesus points to the Old Testament,\nas well as to his own words as providing verification through prediction.
\n
\nFor the apostles, Acts 17:2,3 shows that Paul customarily reasoned from the scriptures,\nciting predictive prophecy of Christ in order to persuade his audience. I Cor. 15:1-8 is\nan evidentialist argument based on eyewitness accounts, and predictive prophecy. I Jn.\n1:1-3 and II Pet. 1:16-21 are also evidentialist arguments.
