Abrahamic Covenant (Genesis 15:1—21)

\n\n\n

This promise is the guiding theme of the Bible from Genesis through Revelation. It\nclarifies God's intent for man and the primary agency through whom his plan is to be\neffected. All of what we discuss in this survey of the Old Testament will relate directly\nto this.

\n\n

From a literary perspective, the promise also guides the interpretation of the rest of\nthe book of Genesis through Joshua. It is like a thesis statement for these narratives. \n\n

    \n
  • Provisions of the covenant:
    • \n
\n\n
\n

vs. 1-5. An heir. This is implicit in the promise to make him a great nation, but it is\n emphasized in light of the fact that his wife was old and baron.

\n

vs. 17-21. Land. this is the land of Canaan, or Palestine. God would give this land to\n Abraham's descendents some 400 years later, after his has finished his work with the\n indigenous population (v. 16--more on this next week).

\n
\n\n\n\n
\n

Gen. 15:6. This is of vital importance, because it shows the nature of how a person\n relates to God. This is always how a person is made right before God. The people of God\n from all ages approach God in the same way (see Galatians 3:6-9). We must be clear that in\n the Old Testament, people approached God the same way we do: by faith.

\n

Gen. 15:7-13. God \"cuts\" his covenant with Abraham.

    \n\n\nThe Law\n
    \n\n\n \n \n \n \n
    Israel is a unique people on the earth (Ex. 19:3—6).\n They are uniquely \"God's possession.\" They are the subjects of his liberating\n hand (v. 4). Further, they were a people with a purpose. They are to be \"a kingdom of\n priests and a holy nation\" (v.6). The role of the priest is to intercede on behalf of\n others. Israel was to be obedient to the Law, and in so doing, would exist as a witness to\n God to her neighbors. This is how, in part, God would bless the nations through Israel\n (Gen. 12:3).

    Notice that there is a condition to the Law (v. 5). This does not make\n conditional the unconditional promise God made to Abraham (Gen. 12:1-3). Rather, it makes\n obedience to the Law a condition of the ongoing experience of God's blessing on Israel as\n a \"special treasure\" and \"a nation of priests.\" We will see in the\n history of Israel that God remains faithful to his covenant to Abraham, but when Israel is\n disobedient to the Law, it loses God's protective hand and its witness of God to the\n world. Israel will experience conquest and exile because of her violation of the Law, but\n God will preserve this unique covenant people, and he will accomplish his plan to bless\n the world through Israel.

    \n
    \n\n\n\n\n

    Three divisions of the Law

    \n\n\n

    Decalog or Ten Commandments (Ex. 20:3—17)

    \n\n\n

    The Decalogue is a statement of the moral character of God. As the possession of a holy\nGod, Israel is to be a holy nation. \n\n

      \n
    • No other gods. This law demands that Israel worship YHWH only. While the Old Testament\n is clear that there is only one God, Israel was prone to the polytheism of her neighboring\n cultures (see Deuteronomy 32:21). This is expanded in the \"Shema\" of Deut.\n 6:4-9.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • No Idolatry. Idol worship was the center of animistic worship in the ANE. It was based\n in the concept of \"sympathetic magic.\" Fertility cults express this well. See\n also the exposure of idolatry in Isaiah 44:14-17.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • No vain oaths. This is a prohibition against using the name of God for a vain or empty\n oath. Swearing by God's name deceitfully is what is in view. It could also include a\n prohibition of invoking the name of God for it's magical powers, as was common in the ANE.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Sabbath Day. The seventh day is to be a day of rest. It commemorates God's creation and\n his abundant provision for his people. We will also see that the Sabbath law is expanded\n in the civil law to include Sabbath years.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Honor parents. To honor is to prize. The main concern is to provide for them in their\n old age and to respect them as authorities. This is the first of three laws that deal with\n the family. The family was, and is, the foundation of society.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Do no kill. To kill means literally \"murder.\" It is not teaching pacifism,\n because we note that the law prescribes the death penalty and terms of war. The sanctity\n of life is the foundation of the command.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • No adultery. Taking another man's wife is prohibited. Sexual morality is rooted in God's\n design for people (see Gen. 2: 23,24).
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Do not steal. This is a statement of property rights.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • False witness. People have a right to their reputations, and the protection of the\n truth.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Coveting prohibited. This law addresses the heart. Biblical morality deals not simply\n with actions, but with the condition of the heart (see James 1:14,15; 4:1,2).
      • \n
    \n\n\n

    Civil Law (Ex. 21-23; Deut. 19-23) \n\n

      \n \n
    • Civil law is \"case law,\" putting into practice the moral commands of the\n decalogue. It is not exhaustive, but addresses common situations. It provides guidance for\n the judges.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Concern for \"due process.\" E.g.: Ex. 22:2,3; Levitical cities of refuge.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Concern to prevent clan violence. The is the reason for the \"eye for eye\"\n expression (Ex. 21:23-25; Deut. 19:21). The legal principle is \"lex talionis.\"\n The punishment should fit, not exceed the crime.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Concern for social justice. Sabbath year legislation (Ex. 21:2-6; Deut. 15: 1-18). The\n concern is for preventing economic class distinctions from generation to generation.\n Violation of this law plays a significant role later in Israel's history.
