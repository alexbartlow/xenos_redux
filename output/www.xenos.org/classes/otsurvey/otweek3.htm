Israel's foreign policy (Deut. 20:10-18) \n\n

\n
  • \"Far off nations.\" Proclaim terms of peace. If they wish to enter into a pact,\n then leave them alone.
    • \n\n\n
      \n
    • \"Cities in Canaan.\" These cities are subject to the \"ban.\" Ban is\n complete annihilation. The entire Canaanite population is to be utterly destroyed.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Why the ban? The ban raises significant questions in the mind of most readers. Could the\n God of grace and mercy really demand such extraordinary measures? It conjures up images of\n Cambodia under the Khamir Rouge, and solidifies the impression that the God of the Old\n Testament is a wrathful God, in sharp contrast to the God of the New Testament. Let's take\n a closer look at the legislation and the Canaanite culture.
      • \n
    \n\n
    \n
      \n
    • Canaanite culture, like all of the cultures of the ANE, centered around its religion. By\n all standards of comparison, the Canaanite religion was enormously dehumanizing.
      • \n

    \n The Canaanites worshipped Molech (sitting on\n left).

      \n
    • El was the primary god of the Canaanite pantheon. He is a creator god. Creation occurs\n by sexual procreation with female deities. Thus, worship of El takes on sexual form.\n Priestesses were essentially sex slaves of the cult.
      • \n
    \n
    \n\n
    \n
    \n
      \n
    • Ba'al or Hadad. Son of El. He was the head of the Canaanite pantheon. He was younger and\n more virile than El. He is the god of rain, and therefore, like El, tied to fertility.
      • \n
    • Annat, wife of Astarti. Very similar to Kali of India. She was the goddess of sex, war\n and fertility. She is both sensuous and dangerous. She is a sacred prostitute for the\n gods, terms, \"holy one.\" Shown riding naked on a lion with a flower in one hand\n and a serpent in the other. She delights in blood. A very violent and fickle goddess.\n Human heads form a necklace in depictions of her. There was both the practice of ritual\n prostitution and human sacrifice to Annat and El.

    • Historicity of the conquest. According to biblical chronology, the conquest began in\n about 1400 BC. According to archaeological excavation of Tell el-Sultan (Jericho), level\n 4, Egyptian pottery from the 15th century BC has been found. Further, of the 150,000\n pieces of pottery recovered from this level, none dates to later than 1400 BC. There is no\n evidence of Mycenaean pottery at this level either. This is significant, because other\n finds have shown that after 1400 BC, there are extensive caches of Mycenaean pottery in\n the area.

      \n
    • Amarna Tablets. Series of correspondences between petty kings of Canaan seeking military\n assistance from Egypt. The names of the kings match the narrative of kings and cities in\n Joshua. We know the tablets to have been written between 1400-1370 BC. The tablets speak\n of an invading people, called the \"habiru.\" Many scholars link this term to the\n Israelites (Egypt's Seti I and Ramses II identify the Habiru with the Hebrews in their\n later conquest of Bethshan c. 1310 BC).

    \n\n\nThe Judges (Judges)\n\n\nIsrael: 1377-1055 BC\n\n\n

    \"Judges\" is the history of this era. The thesis for this 300 year era of\nIsrael's history is summarized in Judges 2:11-22. The main theme can be viewed as a cycle\ndescribed in this passage:

    \n\n

    \n\n

      \n
    • v.11-13. Israel serves the Baals. This is referred to as \"playing the harlot.\"\n The \"people of God's own possession\" rejected their relationship to God, and\n turned to serve Canaanite gods.
      • \n
    • v.14,15. God withdraws his protection from Israel in response to their apostasy. He\n gives them into the hands of \"plunderers who plunder them.\" The land is\n occupied; the people are scattered and often slaughtered.
      • \n
    • v.16-18. God raised up judges to rule over Israel. During the rule of the judge, the\n enemy would be repelled and the people restored to their land.
      • \n
    • v 19. After the death of the judge, the people would return to their apostate ways.
      • \n
    \n\n\n

    Who were these judges? Judges were God's primary instrument to rule the people\nduring this era who were no longer obedient to the Law. \n\n

      \n
    • Judges brought about conformity to the Law and destroyed alters of worship to Canaanite\n gods.
      • \n
    • Judges were political leaders. They led primarily as a military leader.
      • \n
    • Judges were called by God. They were not appointed by the people. The people of Israel\n rewarded them and their families with large shares of land and often cash gifts. Most\n importantly, they were given the Spirit of God to enable them to be God's instrument\n (3:10). It was God who raised up the judges, because the nation was a\n \"theocracy.\" There was no central government. Unlike a king, the judge led\n solely by charisma. They had no legal power. They could not enforce taxes or raise an army\n by draft. They had no political power to act autonomously. They had only moral and\n spiritual leadership. The Spirit would come upon them and they would be able to accomplish\n God's will.
      • \n
    \n\n\n

    Postscript: The end of the era of the judges. \n\n

      \n
    • Concluding comment from Judges. Things are not well for Israel. There is substantial\n tribal suspicion that has weakened Israel spiritually and militarily. The nation was\n verging on moral anarchy. Note the refrain at the end of Judges, \"In those days there\n was no king in Israel; everyone did what was right in his own eyes\" (Jud. 21:25).
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Footnote on Ruth. The story is an analogy of God as go’el. The narrative takes\n place during the judges era. Ruth establishes David's genealogy. In this way, Ruth\n introduces us to David (Ruth 4:18-22). It shows God's faithfulness to a woman who was not\n a Hebrew, but who migrated to Israel to become an example compassion and love. It stands\n as a bridge between the turbulent time of the judges and the Golden Age of Israel under\n the united monarchy.

    What went wrong? \n\n

      \n
    • Immediate cause. The Philistine invasion. They emerged in 1190 from the northern\n coastline to invade Israel. We know much of them from Egyptian records. The \"Harris\n Papyrus\" found in Egypt, shows Phililstine battles against Ramses III. These\n invasions brought to an end Egypt's hegemony of the region. The Philistines were among the\n first to use iron (1 Sam. 13). They were enormously powerful. Israel was no match for\n their military hardware or skill.
      • \n
    \n\n
      \n
    • Spiritual cause. Psalm 78:54-64. For the past 300 years, Israel had indulged in\n spiritual harlotry. Because of her unfaithfulness, God withdrew his protection from her.\n As a result, the military powers in the region had their way with Israel. The scripture\n makes it clear that the central problem was a spiritual one, not military.

The northern tribes select Jeroboam,\n son of Nebat as their king. The northern tribes carry the national name Israel, while the\n southern kingdom uses the tribal name Judah.

      \n
    • The nation is divided in three ways:
      • \n
    \n\n
    \n
      \n \n
    • Religious division.
      • With the temple in Judah, the Levites flee to the southern\n kingdom. This leaves the north without the temple and priests. Thus, Jeroboam sets up his\n own high places for worship. This was the primary sin of the northern kingdom. It was a\n pagan fertility cult that retained some of the forms and language of the historic Hebrew\n faith. In the narrative of Kings, this cult is referred to as \"the sin of\n Jeroboam.\" The deterioration of the northern kingdom from a religious perspective\n took three turns. (i) The sin of Jeroboam, which is worshiping pagan gods in the mountains\n (1 Kings 12:26-33). This continued until king Ahab married an non-Hebrew Jezebel. She\n introduced the worship of Ba'al as the official religion of the northern kingdom. The\n religion that Joshua was to destroy now becomes the official religion of God's people (see\n 1 Kings 16:30-33). (iii) King Jehu reformed Israel by ridding the nation of Ba'al worship,\n but it was an incomplete reform. He merely returned the nation to the worship of the cult\n introduced by Jeroboam (see 2 Kings 10:29-31).\n
    \n
      \n \n
    • Political division.
      •  There emerged two separate nations, with very little cooperation\n between them. This is of crucial importance, because of the third national division....\n
    \n
      \n \n
    • Military division.
      •  There was no cooperation in battle between the two kingdoms.\n Indeed, on more than one occasion, Israel and Judah formed military alliances against each\n other. The division of the nation and the unfaithfulness to God brought about the\n destruction of the northern kingdom in 722 BC under the power of the Assyrians (2 Kings\n 17:1-18). Central to Assyria's method of conquest was to force colonization and\n intermarriage of the vanquished peoples. This leads to the complete destruction not only\n of a national entity, but a cultural identity. That is why we speak today of the \"10\n lost tribes of Israel.\" They literally vanish from significant history at this point.\n \n

    • While the southern kingdom, Judah, was more faithful to God, she too fell to pagan\n fertility worship. But God dealt differently with Judah than with Israel. Remember that\n God had promised to David that his seed would rule over Israel forever. Because God is\n faithful to his promise, he preserved Judah even in its destruction. There were\n significant reform movements in Judah that delayed the ultimate conquest of the kingdom\n (Josiah's reform 2 Kings 22:1-13). Yet, like Israel, the unfaithfulness of Judah led to\n the conquest of the kingdom by Babylon in 586 BC. Nebuchadnezzar destroyed Jerusalem,\n looted and raised the Temple and led the royal family and others into exile in Babylon.

    A Remnant Preserved \n\n

      \n \n
    • The prophet/historians who wrote Kings and Chronicles were careful to document that the\n Davidic line remained intact even in captivity. The hope of the Davidic kingdom never died\n out. Judah was appointed to a 70 year captivity (Jer. 25:11,12; 2 Chron. 36:20,21). After\n this period, Artaxerxes, the Persian king allows Nehemiah to lead a resettlement of the\n Jerusalem. The Old Testament comes to a close ~400BC with a remnant of Judah in the land,\n and much anticipation of the revealing of the Davidic king.
