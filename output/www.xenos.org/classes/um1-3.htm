Paul emphasizes spiritual maturity and character over gifting. \n It is possible to be very gifted and knowledgeable, yet immature or carnal. \n Immature people often get into leadership, where they do the church much harm \n (see Diotrophes 3 Jn. 9). There is nothing wrong with the desire to be a Christian \n leader (1 Tim. 3:1), but it must be for the right reason.

\n

Since these qualities describe spiritual maturity, they are \n helpful in that they describe the character that the Holy Spirit is seeking \n to produce in all of our lives. Not surprisingly, most of these qualities are \n prescribed elsewhere in the New Testament for all Christians. If we allow the \n Holy Spirit to transform us into a man or woman of God, we can be sure that \n God will put us into the roles of leadership that he has prepared for us.

\n

Optional Discussion:
\n The Holy Spirit uses passages like this to turn up \"blind spots\" \n in areas so that we will allow him to change us over a period of time. As he \n convicts you, how should you respond?

\n

Answers include:
\n Acknowledge to him your lack, along with how you see this lack concretely manifesting \n itself in your life currently.

\n

Agree that you want him to change you in this area. \n

\n

Agree that you cannot change yourself, but that \n you believe that he can change you in this area, no matter how deeply entrenched \n it is.

\n

Ask him to give you practical steps of faith to \n take.

\n

Look for those who are strong in this area, observe \n how they exhibit this quality, and talk with them about how they developed it \n (Phil 4:9; 1 Cor. 11:1; Heb 13:7).
\n

Regular Discussion:
\n As general roadmaps to what spiritual maturity looks like, these lists of \n character requirements are directly applicable to our own lives.

\n
    \n
  • \n

    Why are each of the following character traits \n important in Christian living and ministry?

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    How can we cooperate with God to supply us \n with each requirement?

    \n
    • \n
\n

Above reproach
\n (1 Tim. 3:2; Titus 1:6,7)--anepilempton: unaccusable; anegkleton: \n unreprovable

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This is the summation of all other characteristics. \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Not only the absence of disqualifying factors \n is in view, but positive things are evident

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    A good reputation spiritually (Acts 6:3; 16:2). \n

    \n
    • \n
\n

Husband of one wife
\n (1 Tim. 3:2)--mais gunaikos andros: \"one-woman man\"

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This probably does not refer to polygamy (which \n was not common in the Roman empire), but rather that sexual morality is \n an established life-style.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This qualification does not exclude divorcees; \n present life-style only is in view (as with all of the qualifications). \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This includes flirting, porno habits, inappropriate \n \"counseling\" of the opposite sex, etc.

    \n
    • \n
\n

Temperate
\n (1 Tim. 3:2)--nephalion: sober

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This is the opposite of being mentally and \n spiritually dense. It is linked with alertness in 1 Thess. 5:6 and 1 Pet. \n 5:8.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The person has a clear perspective on life, \n and a correct spiritual orientation.

    \n
    • \n
\n

Prudent
\n (1 Tim. 3:2; Titus 1:8)--sophrona: thoughtful, self-controlled, sane \n

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person is mentally healthy (Mk. 5:15; 2 \n Cor 5:13).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    He has an honest evaluation of himself which \n involves neither arrogance nor self-hate (Rom. 12:3).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The person evidences the ability to be reasonable, \n sensible, able to keep one's head (Titus 2:6; 1 Pet. 4:7).

    \n
    • \n
\n

Respectable
\n (1 Tim. 3:2)--kosmion: well-ordered

\n
    \n
  • \n

    A habit of orderliness and stability has been \n established (see 1 Tim. 2:9; 1 Pet. 3:4).

    \n
    • \n
\n

Hospitable
\n (1 Tim. 3:2; Titus 1:8)--philoxenon: \"lover of strangers\" \n

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person takes a genuine interest in new \n people. This would include both an outreach orientation and the willingness \n to open his home to others (Heb. 13:2).

    \n
    • \n
\n

Able to Teach
\n (1 Tim. 3:2)--didaktikon: skilled at teaching

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The elder must understand Scripture well enough \n to be able to effectively exhort in sound doctrine and refute those who \n contradict (Titus 1:9).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This does not necessitate being a gifted large-group \n teacher.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    \"Grounded in the Word\" means that \n the elder can explain and apply biblical concepts in your his words, and \n discern error when he hears it.

    \n
    • \n
\n

Not Addicted to Wine
\n (1 Tim. 3:3; Titus 1:7)--me paroinon: \"not lingering over wine\" \n \n

    \n
  • \n

    The person has a demonstrated freedom from \n drunkenness, or substance abuse. There is no dependence on alcohol or other \n drugs.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Able to give up freedom to avoid stumbling \n a weaker brother (1 Cor. 8)

    \n
    • \n
\n

Not self-willed
\n (Titus 1:7)--me authade: not a usurper

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This is linked with rebelliousness in 2 Pet. \n 2:10, and with usurpation of rightful authority in 1 Tim. 2:12.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    There should be a proven ability to defer to \n others.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Avoids a \"my way or the highway;\" \n attitude.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    To \"defer\" means that you actively \n get behind the others' way and help it to succeed.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Implies he is able to apologize

    \n
    • \n
\n

Not quick-tempered
\n (Titus 1:7)--me orgilon: not inclined to anger

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person is not vengeful or violent, brooding \n or bitter (see Eph. 4:29,31), especially when he doesn't get his own way. \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    When Moses struck the rock (Num. 20) he was \n refused entry into Canaan. When leaders misrepresent God by making him seem \n more angry than he really is, it's a serious thing (Jas. 1:19,20)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Leaders may get angry, but they should be slow \n to anger rather than having a short fuse.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The leader must be under control, avoiding \n violent outbursts

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Elders must be able to drop offenses, not hold \n onto them

    \n
    • \n
\n

Not pugnacious
\n (Titus 1:7; 1 Tim. 3:3)--me plekten: not a striker

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person is not prone to physical or verbal \n abuse (i.e. slander, put-downs, etc.)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Not a fighter

    \n
    • \n
\n

Gentle
\n (1 Tim. 3:3)--epieike: gracious, forbearing

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person is not unduly rigorous or legalistic \n in his treatment of people.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    He is kind, empathetic and patient with all \n people.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The opposite of quick-tempered, or pugnacious. \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    People are fragile. We need to consider how \n our words and actions will affect them. See 2 Tim. 2:24,25; 1 Thess. 2:7; \n Gal. 6:1; Eph. 4:3; Col. 3:12,13; 1 Tim. 6:11; Gal. 5:22,23; Jas. 3:17. \n

    \n
    • \n
\n

Uncontentious
\n (1 Tim. 3:3)--amachon: peaceable

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This means not looking for ways to disagree \n or oppose; not loving to fight or quarrel.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The person possesses a positive and constructive \n point of view.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This is the opposite of being self-willed. \n

    \n
    • \n
\n

Free From the Love of Money
\n (1 Tim. 3:3)--aphilagruron: not greedy

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This means the ability to be content with what \n one has materially (1 Tim. 6:8).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The person is not motivated by financial considerations \n in ministry goals (see Acts 20:33)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    True love for Christ and his work will become \n eclipsed by greed (see Mat. 6:24). Our day is replete with newsy examples \n of the error of money-love in the church.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    See 1 Tim. 6:6-11,17-19. Mature elders should \n give away much to others, and should live a simple life-style in order to \n curb temptation.

    \n
    • \n
\n

Manages own household well
\n (1 Tim. 3:4,5; Titus 1:6)--prohistemenon: to stand before; manage; to \n lead, used of an army commander standing before his men

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This is a demonstrated ability to lead spiritually \n and effectively in marriage and/or a rooming situation

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The elder's family should respect him and voluntarily \n follow his leadership

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Examining one's family life tends to ensure \n that the person is spiritually authentic and not two-faced.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Christian leader's first responsibility is \n to their own family. Prioritizing and practicing biblical principles with \n family and home is crucial in cooperating with God

    \n
    • \n
\n

Not a new convert
\n (1 Tim. 3:6)--me neophuton: \"newly planted\"

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person has been a walking Christian long \n enough to be tested by God (see 1 Tim. 3:10)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The person should have experienced success \n without becoming conceited

    \n
    • \n
\n

Having a good reputation with those outside
\n (1 Tim. 3:7)--exothen: used by Paul for non-Christians (Col. 4:5) \n

\n
    \n
  • \n

    Non-Christians are unable to discredit the \n person.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    They speak well of him generally, and accusations \n are easily exposed as false (1 Pet. 3:16).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The person is spiritually authentic and not \n two-faced. This is has important, obvious implications for evangelism.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The elder resists a Christian ghetto mentality, \n and fosters a constant awareness of the watching world

    \n
    • \n
\n

Loving what is good
\n (Titus 1:8)--philagathon:= loving good

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person's lifestyle demonstrates that God's \n way is enjoyed (see Rom. 12:2)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    There is no questionable dichotomy between \n the person's recreational life and ministry

    \n
    • \n
\n

Just
\n (Titus 1:8)--dikiaon:

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The person is fair and impartial in his dealings \n with people (1 Tim. 5:21).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    When favoritism and particular biases are adopted, \n the biblical concepts of righteousness and goodness fade, and with that, \n God's agenda and priorities.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    To gain victory in this area, one must be well \n aware of what his own bias tendencies are, and must resist those in favor \n of biblical truth

    \n
    • \n
\n

Devout
\n (Titus 1:8)--hosion: Practical seriousness and zeal for God's will

\n
    \n
  • \n

    A single-mindedness for God and His work.

\n Now Flip the coin; what if the elder had the moral, but not the functional requirements? \n Of the functional requirements and the moral requirements, do you think one \n is more important than the other? Why?
