Understanding culture

\n\n\n

What is culture?\n\n

    \n \n
  • Core beliefs, social values and sense of history experienced\n by a people who are distinguished from others based on these things.
\n
    \n\n
      \n \n \n
    • Culture based on ethnicity is increasingly irrelevant
\n
    \n\n\n \n
  • The meaning of culture is reflected in art, literature, law,\n religion. . . .
\n

Why culture matters\n\n

    \n \n
  • While we are individuals, we are also social beings and can\n never separate ourselves from those influences that shape our expectations, world view,\n identity, and values.
\n\n
    \n
  • Culture expresses both the dignity of man in the image of a\n creative, intelligent God and human fallenness. Art, literature, law, religion. . . We\n should be able to appreciate and find common ground, as the mark of God’s image\n cannot be erased (contextualization).
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  • The less we think about culture, the more we are influenced\n by it. To lack understanding of culture is to be conformed to it. See Romans 12:1,2; Eph.\n 2:1-3.
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  • Authentic witness. Christians need to have the ability to explain\n what people recognize on the level of experience. That’s part of what it means\n to be lights in the world.
    • \n
\n\n
    \n
  • A study of culture shows over time what ideas are true and\n livable, and which ones aren’t. In this sense, history is the proving grounds for\n truth claims.
    • \n
\n\n\n\n
\n
\n
\n

\"There is a flow to history and culture.\n This is rooted and has its wellspring in the thoughts of people. People\n are unique in the inner life of the mind--what they are in their thought\n world determines how they act. This is true of their value systems\n and it is true of their creativity. It is true of their corporate actions,\n such as political decisions, and it is true of their personal lives.\n The results of their thought world flow through their fingers or from their\n tongues into the external world. This is true of Michelangelo’s\n chisel, and it is true of a dictator’s sword.\" How Should\n We Then Live, 19. 

\n
\n
\n
\n\n
    \n
      \n
    • History reveals the adequacy of a culture’s foundations\n or presuppositions.
      • \n
    \n
\n\n
\n
\n
\n

\"It is important to realize what a\n difference a people’s world view makes in their strength as they\n are exposed to the pressure of life. . . . [Christians strength in\n Roman culture] rested on God’s being an\n infinite-personal God and his speaking in the Old Testament, in the life\n and teaching of Jesus Christ, and in the gradually growing New Testament.\n He had spoken in ways people could understand. Thus the Christians\n not only had knowledge about the universe and mankind that people cannot\n find out by themselves, but they had absolute, universal values by\n which to live and by which to judge the society and the political state\n in which they lived. And they had grounds for the basic dignity and\n value of the individual as unique in being made in the image of God.\" How\n Should We Then Live, 22. 

\n
\n
\n
\n\n
    \n
      \n
    • Understanding the historical progression of ideas is key to\n Schaeffer’s apologetics. This point is central to Schaeffer’s apologetics.\n That’s why his key works, The God Who Is There, Escape From Reason and How\n Should We Then Live are substantially an examination of the history of Western\n thought.
