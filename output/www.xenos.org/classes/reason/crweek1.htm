1. Understanding the role of the mind in spiritual growth\n (Romans 12:1,2)

The mind is at the center of spiritual warfare

Active mind is required to avoid deception

\n

\"We assume beliefs are under conscious control at all times.\n But beliefs can be created merely by passively accepting information without attempting to\n analyze it… In other words, when distractions derailed their train of thought,\n volunteers [in psychological experiments] who had been given reason to doubt false\n information nevertheless tended to accept that information as true.\" Bruce\n Bower, Science News

\n

Entertainment focus of mass media subverts critical thinking.

\n
\n

E.g.: news media

\"Used in one way, the press, the radio and the cinema are\n indispensable to the survival of democracy. Used in another way, they are among the most\n powerful weapons in the dictator's armory...In regard to propaganda the early advocates of\n universal literacy and a free press envisaged only two possibilities: the propaganda might\n be true, or it might be false. They did not foresee what in fact has happened, above all\n in our Western capitalist democracies--the development of a vast mass communications\n industry, concerned in the main neither with the true nor the false, but with the unreal,\n the more or less totally irrelevant. In a word, they failed to take into account man's\n almost infinite appetite for distractions...Only the vigilant can maintain their\n liberties, and only those who are constantly and intelligently on the spot can hope to\n govern themselves effectively by democratic procedures.\" Aldous Huxley, Brave New\n World Revisited

1. Intellectual life in the early church

\n
\n

Arguing from scripture and general revelation (Rom. 1:18ff; Acts\n 17:16ff)

\n
\n \n
\n

Doctrinal test for prophets and teachers (1 Corinthians 14:29)

\n
\n \n
\n

Worship that emphasized content (1 Corinthians 14:19)

Ongoing vitality of the early church depended on Christians who\n were:

\n

Confident enough in their understanding to withstand slander and\n intense persecution (see Tacitus, Annals XV,44).

Provide a reasoned critique of gentile paganism and Judaism.

Two deadly developments in the post-apostolic era:

\n \n

Orthodoxy was maintained by power, not persuasion

World views and why they matter

\n\n
\n

1. Everyone has \"basic beliefs\" whether they are\n consciously aware of it or not.

\n \n

2. Our basic beliefs are developed within our culture often without\n much conscious reflection

\n
\n

Taken together, a set of basic beliefs is a world view:\n Reality, humanity, values, truth

\n

Most people’s basic beliefs are incoherent—they contradict\n one another

\n

Intellectual maturity involves conscious reflection on these beliefs\n and the willingness to work toward coherence

\n

Spiritual authenticity demands that we examine what we believe

\n \n
\n

3. The adequacy of our basic beliefs (that they correspond to\n reality) will determine the quality of our lives

\n
\n\n
\n
\n

\"Therefore everyone who hears these words of Mine, and acts\n upon them, may be compared to a wise man, who built his house upon the rock. And the rains\n descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew, and burst against that house; and yet\n it did not fall, for it had been founded upon the rock. And everyone who hears these words\n of Mine, and does not act upon them, will be like a foolish man, who built his house upon\n the sand. And the rain descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew, and burst\n against that house; and it fell, and great was its fall.\" Matthew 7:24-27

\n
\n
\n

Unbiblical basic beliefs don’t correspond to reality as God has\n made it and cannot be consistently lived out

\n
\n

We have a basis to critically interact with secular thinking

\n
\n \n
\n

We have an obligation as ambassadors of Christ to interact with\n secular thinking for the sake of the lost

Four basic world views

\n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
 RealityHumanityTruth Values
Theism:

Christianity

\n

Judaism

\n

Islam

Infinite personal God exists. He created a\n finite material universe. Reality is both material and spiritual.Humans are the unique creation of God\n \"in his image.\" Humans are personal, eternal, spiritual and physical beings.Truth about God is disclosed by\n revelation. Truth about the material world is known through reason and sense experience.\n We can know only partially, but truly.Morality is objective—the expression\n of the nature of an eternal personal Being.
Naturalism:

Atheism

\n

Agnosticism

\n

Marxism

Physical universe is all that exists. A\n \"closed system of cause and effect.\" There is no spiritual dimension.Humans are entirely material beings, the\n product of an ultimately purposeless evolutionary process.The nature and limitations of human\n knowledge are determined by sense experience and scientific reasoning.No objective values exist. Morals are\n individual preferences or socially accepted norms.
Pantheism:

Hinduism

\n

Buddhism

\n

Taoism

Only the spiritual dimension exists.\n Awareness of a physical order is illusion (maya). Ultimate reality, Brahman, is impersonal\n and unknowable.Humans are one with reality. \"Atman\n (human essence) is Brahman.\" Individual identity and personhood are illusion.Truth is experience of unity with the\n universe. Truth is beyond all description—rational thought is of no use to\n understanding ultimate reality.Because reality is impersonal, there are\n no objective values. No real distinction between good and evil.
Postmodern-ismReality is a socially constructed\n \"paradigm.\" There is no objective, universally shared realityHuman identity is a social construct,\n socially determined. There is no individual identity.Truth is a social construct—beliefs\n that are meaningful to those within the cultural paradigm. Truth is relative to the\n paradigm.Moral values are socially constructed.\n Tolerance, inclusion, openness are the primary postmodern virtues

Three basic principles for reading (or listening) for world view\ncontent

\n\n\n
\n

1. What assertion is being made about reality, humanity,\n truth or values?

\n
\n

Identify phrases and terms

\n

What world view is being expressed?

\n
\n

2. What must be assumed for key statements to make sense?

\n
\n

What world view is assumed?

\n
\n \n
\n
\n

3. Identify any reasons or evidence given in support\n of assertions

\n
\n

Are these reasons or evidence adequate? (next week)
