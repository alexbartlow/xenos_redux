\n

Many philosophers say… you can’t describe a genetic predisposition and\n suppose that because it is part of human nature, it is somehow transformed into an ethical\n precept. We must put moral reasoning in a special category, and use transcendental\n guidelines as required.

\n

No, we do not have to put moral reasoning in a special category and use transcendental\n premises, because the naturalistic fallacy is itself a fallacy. For if ought is not is,\n what is? To translate is into ought makes sense if we attend to the objective meaning of\n ethical precepts. They are very unlikely to be ethereal messages awaiting revelation, or\n independent truths vibrating in a nonmaterial dimension of the mind. They are more likely\n to be products of the brain and culture.

\n

The empiricist view, searching for an origin of ethical reasoning… The individual\n is seen as predisposed biologically to make certain choices. Through cultural evolution\n some of the choices are hardened into precepts, then into law, and, if the predisposition\n or coersion is strong enough, into a belief in the command of God… The general\n empiricist principle takes this form: Strong innate feeling and historical experience\n cause certain actions to be preferred; we have experienced them, and have weighed their\n consequences, and agree to conform with codes that express them. Let is take an oath upon\n the codes, invest our personal honor in them, and suffer punishment for their violations.

\n

The empiricist view concedes that moral codes are devised to conform to some drives of\n human nature and to suppress others. Ought is the translation not of human nature but of\n the public will, which can be made increasingly wise and stable through an understanding\n of the needs and pitfalls of human nature.

\n

… People resist biological explanations of their higher cortical functions [so]\n very little progress has been made in the biological exploration of the moral\n sentiments… Paleolithic egalitarian and tribalistic instincts are still firmly\n installed. As part of the genetic foundation of human nature, they cannot be replaced. In\n some cases, such as quick hostility to strangers and competing in groups, they have become\n generally ill adapted and persistently dangerous. Above the fundamental instincts rise\n superstructures of arguments and rules that accommodate the novel institutions created by\n cultural evolution. These accommodations, which reflect the attempt to maintain order and\n further tribal interests, have been too volatile to track by genetic evolution; they are\n not yet in the genes.

\n

There is a hereditary selective advantage to membership in a powerful group united by\n devout belief and purpose. Even when individuals subordinate themselves and risk death in\n a common cause, their genes are more likely to be transmitted to the next generation than\n are those of competing groups who lack comparable resolve.

\n

Mathematical models of population genetics suggest the following rule in the\n evolutionary origin of altruism: the individual pays, his genes and tribe gain, altruism\n spreads.

\n
\n

A set of ideas that explain data

\n

Five tests of world views

\n

Adequacy

\n
\n

Explains all the relevant data—or more completely than rival hypotheses

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

External consistency

\n
\n

Conforms to other well-established theories and bodies of knowledge

\n

\"Burden of proof\" rests with the theory that contradicts what is well\n established

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Internal coherence

\n
\n\n
\n
\n

All aspects of the hypothesis are self-consistent

\n
\n
\n

An hypothesis is incoherent if:

\n
\n

One or more ideas conflict with each other

\n

It can not meet its own criterion for truth

\n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Fruitfulness

\n
\n

Hypothesis can be successfully applied to life and new situations

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Biblical

\n
\n

The extent to which the hypothesis is consistent with biblical Christianity

\n

Commonly encountered errors in reasoning

\n\n
\n

Argument from authority. When the soundness of an argument is based on the\n authority of a person speaking outside of their area of expertise.

\n
\n

Need to distinguish between facts and interpretations of facts by experts

\n
\n \n
\n

Darwin knew that accepting his theory required believing in philosophical\n materialism, the conviction that matter is the stuff of all existence and that all mental\n and spiritual phenomenon are its by-products....Yet, as pointed out by contemporary\n evolutionary scholar Douglas Futuyma, seldom do the detractors of the Darwinian world view\n take note of its positive implications. In Darwin’s world we are not helpless\n prisoners of a static world order but, rather, masters of our own fate....And from a\n strictly scientific point of view rejecting biological evolution is no different from\n rejecting other natural phenomenon such as electricity and gravity. (Levine, Joseph S.\n and Miller, Kenneth R., Biology Discovering Life, 1994

\n
\n

Equivocation. An argument in which a word is used in\n two different ways without clarifying the key differences in meaning.

\n
\n

How does the author use key terms? Are they importing a new meaning without\n justification or explanation? (e.g.: Willimon’s essay on \"truth\")

\n
\n

Against the person. Rejecting the person rather than assessing the merits of\n their argument

\n
\n

\"You too\" fallacy

\n
\n

Genetic fallacy. Rejecting the content of a person’s belief or argument\n based on the psychological origin of the belief

\n
\n

We hold many views based on teaching from our youth, but that’s not why we\n currently hold these views—relating to morality, God and related concepts.

\n

A two-edged sword

\n
\n

Naturalistic fallacy. Deriving \"oughts\" from facts about what\n \"is\"

\n
\n

Moral prescriptions can not be found in descriptive statements

\n

Naturalists may be very moral people, but naturalism provides no basis for\n objective morality
