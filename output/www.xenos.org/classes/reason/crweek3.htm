Understanding skepticism

\n
\n

Strong skepticism: \"There are no certainties. It’s arrogant to say that\n you know anything absolutely.\"

\n
\n

Is this an assertion of truth? (incoherent)

\n

Is this an assertion of opinion? While everyone is entitled to their opinion, what are\n the merits of this one?

\n
\n

We seem to know some things truly, though not completely (inadequate)

\n

We assume a substantial level of knowledge in practical life (unfruitful)

\n
\n
\n

Weak skepticism: \"The burden of proof is always on the believer to\n establish their case\". Rational people will always withhold belief.

\n
\n

To meet all knowledge claims with unbelief presumes at least one important thing as\n true: \"Rational people are not justified in believing unless the possibility of error\n is removed\"

\n

But how do we know this claim is true? Shouldn’t we also be skeptical about our\n skepticism? Has the skeptic not made a naïve faith commitment without any shred of proof?

The various impressions and speculations which\n influence credulous people here and there are of little importance, nor does it matter to\n what extent cheap slogans have spread an atmosphere inimical to science. \"A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence. .\n . A miracle is a violation of the laws of nature; and as a firm and inalterable experience\n has established these laws, the proof against a miracle, from the very nature of the fact,\n is as entire as any argument from experience can possibly be imagined. . . Nothing is\n esteemed a miracle, if it ever happened in the common course of nature. It is no miracle\n that a man, seemingly in good health, should die on a sudden: Because such a kind of\n death, though more unusual than any other, has yet been frequently observed to happen. But\n it is a miracle, that a dead man should come to life; because that has never been observed\n in any age or country. There must, therefore, be a uniform experience against every\n miraculous event, otherwise the event would not merit that appellation. And as a uniform\n experience amounts to a proof, there is here a direct and full proof, from the nature of\n the fact, against the existence of any miracle. . .\" (David Hume, An Inquiry\n Concerning Human Understanding, X, I: 110, 114, 115).

\n \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n
\n

\"According to Hume, probability rests on what may be\n called the majority vote of our past experiences. The more often a thing has been known to\n happen, the more probable it is that it should happen again; and the less often the less\n probable. Now the regularity of Nature’s course, says Hume, is supported by something\n better than the majority vote of past experiences; it is supported by their unanimous\n vote, or, as Hume says, by ‘firm and unalterable experience.’ There is, in fact,\n ‘uniform experience’ against miracle; otherwise, says Hume, it would not be a\n miracle. A miracle is therefore the most improbable of all events. It is always more\n probable that the witnesses were lying or mistaken than that a miracle occurred. Now of\n course we must agree with Hume that if there is absolutely ‘uniform experience’\n against miracles, if in other words they have never happened, why then they never have. Unfortunately\n we know the experience against them to be uniform only if we know that all the reports of\n them are false. And we can call the reports to be false only if we know already that\n miracles have never occurred. In fact, we are arguing in a circle.\" C.S. Lewis, Miracles,\n 102.

Premise One: Universal moral judgments are made

\n
\n

Universal fact: All humans in all cultures and throughout time make moral\n judgments. For these judgments to have any real meaning, it presumes a standard--something\n to measure judgments by.

\n

For standards to have meaning, they must be objective.

\n
\n

\"What was the sense in saying the enemy were in the wrong unless Right is\n a real thing which the Nazis at bottom knew as well as we did and ought to have practiced?\n If they had no notion of what we mean by right, then, though we might still have had to\n fight them, we could no more have blamed them for that than for the color of their\n hair.\" (Mere Christianity, 19)

\n
\n

Objection One: Anthropological Argument

\n
\n

There is no \"law of human nature.\" Morality is a social convention, differing\n widely from culture to culture.

\n
\n

Enormous similarity between moral codes

\n \n

Objective standards v. culturally conditioned rules

\n \n

In practice, we presume the objectivity of our judgments

\n

Everyone recognizes their own hypocrisy:

\n
\n

\". . .we fail to practice ourselves the kind of behavior we expect from other\n people\" (Mere Christianity, 20).

\n
\n
\n
\n

Objection Two: Morality is \"herd instinct\"

\n
\n

Animals are genetically programmed to act for the good of the herd.

\n
\n

But this does not capture to whole experience of moral awareness:

\n
\n

\"Feeling a desire to help is quite different from feeling that you ought to help\n whether you want to or not. . .Supposing you hear a cry for help from a man in danger. You\n will probably feel two desires, one a desire to give help (due to your herd instinct), the\n other a desire to keep out of danger (due to the instinct for self preservation). But you\n will find inside you, in addition to these two impulses, a third thing which tells you\n that you ought to follow the impulse to help, and suppress the impulse to run away. Now\n this thing that judges between two instincts, that decides which should be encouraged,\n cannot itself be either of them\" (Mere Christianity, 22).

\n
\n
\n
\n

Objection Three: Morality is learned behavior

\n
\n

Socialization, education, conditioning explain moral behavior

\n
\n

But learning moral values from family or school does not mean that the idea is merely\n social convention any more than the fact that we learn math at school makes it a matter of\n social convention.

\n

Social conventions differ from culture to culture (what side of the road to drive on,\n etc.), but the uniformity of ethical ideas suggests that there is something transcultural\n in moral truth.

\n

The idea of \"moral progress\" would be meaningless without an actually\n existing, objective standard of ethical evaluation.

\n
\n
\n

Understanding the moral law: prescription and description

\n
\n

Laws of nature are descriptive. They describe how, in fact, things in nature\n operate.

\n

Moral laws are prescriptive. They are only in part concerned with how, in fact, people\n behave (descriptive), and focus on how people ought to behave.

\n

Naturalistic fallacy: Is (description) does not imply ought (prescription). That is,\n ought cannot be derived from a factual account of nature or human conduct. Moral ought\n must be imposed on it from \"outside.\"

\n \n
\n
\n \n
\n\n
\n

Premise two: Universal moral judgments are only possible if\n theism is assumed

\n \n
\n\n\n
\n
\n
The moral law says something about the nature of reality. What hypothesis best\n explains the existence of the moral law?\n
\n

Naturalistic (materialism) hypothesis: The universe is a closed system\n of cause and effect.

\n

Religious hypothesis: An infinite mind exists. There are both material and immaterial\n dimensions to reality. The universe is an open system of cause and effect.

\n \n \n
\n

Naturalism is insufficient hypothesis

\n \n
\n
\n
\n

Science can not determine which hypothesis is right. Science tries to answer how\n the universe operates as it does, the metaphysical hypotheses try to explain why.\n Science may eliminate certain options, but can’t finally establish either position.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

\"Why does the universe exist, or behave the way it\n does? The position of the question, then, is like this. We want to know whether the\n universe simply happens to be what it is for no reason or whether there is a power behind\n it that makes it what it is. Since that power, if it exists, would not be one of the\n observed facts but a reality which makes them, no mere observation of the facts can find\n it\" (Mere Christianity, 33).

\n \n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

The moral law requires the religious hypothesis.

\n
\n
\n

Given that human conscience is aware of a real moral law, naturalism can not provide a\n basis for it.

\n

Nature is value-neutral. It just is.

\n

Morality is about what ought or ought not to be: good/evil; right/wrong;\n should/shouldn’t. . .

\n
\n

Rival conceptions of God

\n \n
\n
\n
\n

Pantheism can’t work.

\n
\n

If there is an objective moral order, pantheism is false: A dilemma

\n

1. God is both good and evil (Vishnu and Shiva). But if both are equally absolute,\n equally ultimate, there is no way to distinguish what is good and what is evil. How can we\n say the cancer is bad and love is good if they are both equally manifestations of god?

\n

2. On the other hand, if reality is ultimately neither good nor evil, then there is no\n moral law.

\n
\n

Dualism. We often hear that you can’t have good without evil. How would you know\n if something is good unless you also knew about evil? So you can’t have one without\n the other.

\n
\n

What if there are two eternal powers: Good and evil? Neither is more absolute than the\n other.

\n
\n

But if both powers were equal and independent of each others, then how would be\n distinguish which is good and which is evil? We would need to appeal to some standard\n above and independent of both.

\n

The idea of an absolute evil is impossible. Both because it is unintelligible (above)\n and because it does not properly understand what evil is. Evil is a parasite on goodness\n (\"privation of the good\"). For example, sexual immorality is a distortion of\n sexuality, which God created as a good.
