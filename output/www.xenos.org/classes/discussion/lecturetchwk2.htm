Planning a discussion

\n \n

Planning a discussion begins in the same way as planning a \n regular teaching, or lecture.

\n

If you are teaching a passage expositorily (either didactic \n or narrative) you need to

\n
\n
\n

1. Do your inductive study taking special care with the \n \"strategic\" question. The strategic question points to the author's \n purpose for saying what he says. That usually means your purpose as an expository \n teacher will be similar, or at least related. To make your points seem like \n they rise up out of the text, you need to be in line with the author's purpose, \n although the differences between his audience and yours will dictate some \n modifications.

\n

2. Then create appropriate antitheses for each strategic \n point. The antitheses may be:
\n - What will happen if we fail to do, know, or believe what he says
\n - What we typically see in real life instead of the thesis
\n - The opposite of the thesis (good with abstract ideas or moral qualities)
\n (See sample below) Antheses are used in teaching to make the presentation \n interesting. The violence of collision between your theses and antitheses \n makes the teaching seem dynamic and makes your points seem important. This \n collision, or debate, brings in the emotional component in preaching or \n in discussion.

3. Select the points you want to focus on—usually 2 \n or 3 points unless your are going to compile a list together

\n
\n
\n

List-compiling is a form of brain-storming where the \n group assembles a list of causes, possibilities, results, reasons, examples, \n etc. on a given topic. Brain-storming is highly engaging and encourages \n creative thought, but can be weak in instruction value

\n
\n
\n

4. Determine which points could reasonably be discovered \n by the group during discussion, and which will need to be supplied by you, \n the leader. You may call on them to:

\n
\n
\n

come up with everything from the theological points \n on

\n

or you may supply the strategic thesis, and ask them \n for antitheses

\n

or only ask them for application

\n

or, in certain passages, even the background may be elicited \n in discussion (i.e. teaching them how to \"use their eyes\" in \n interpretation)

\n
\n
\n

5. Prepare an introduction including the information needed \n to get going and something emotionally stirring. Could be a paradox, a passionate \n appeal, a challenge etc.

\n

6. Prepare questions for each of the sections you plan to \n discuss. When preparing questions:

\n
\n
\n

Consider Lowman’s comment: To promote discussion, \n questions must give students permission to be wrong: cf. \"How does \n the text define entropy?\" or \"what is the definition of existentialism?\" \n vs. \"What is your first association to the word entropy?\" or \n \"What does existentialism mean to you?\" Pay attention to your \n wording.

\n

After composing a question, imagine the group giving \n answers. Will they be able to answer? Would the answers be interesting? \n Remember, you know what answers you're after. Will they?

\n

Is there any controversy or need for judgment in your \n question? Or are the answers too obvious? Avoid overly-simple questions.

\n

Your questions may run several deep on each point. Usually, \n only the first is planned, along with your goal. Then keep probing with \n spontaneous questions until your goal is reached.\"

\n
\n
\n \n

7. Prepare transitions between sections, and contingencies \n in case discussion lasts too long. Decide what you can jettison.

\n

8. Prepare a conclusion

\n
\n

If you are using a tape or realmedia presentation in preparation.

\n
\n

1. Make sure you understand the passage. The speaker hopefully \n has done your inductive study for you, but you should check his/her work. \n

\n

2. Identify the main burdens, or themes the speaker is focusing \n on. Which points do you agree with, and feel are needed in your group?

\n

3. If the tape is from an hour long teaching, but you plan \n to teach home church, decide which points you are going to edit out.

\n

3. Determine which points would lend themselves to discussion, \n and compose questions accordingly. Prepare to deliver the other points yourself.

\n
\n

If you are planning to cover a narrative spread over numerous \n passages (i.e. not expositorial coverage of a specific narrative in one passage, \n like the life of David)

\n
\n

1. Prepare your introduction

\n

2. Prepare a list of passages to read, and plan on assigning \n these to readers in the group wherever appropriate. Will you analyze the story \n as you read it (section by section) or go through the whole story before analyzing \n what it means? (Passages too long to read or lacking immediate interest should \n be summarized by you)

\n

3. Identify the themes you want to focus on and antitheses \n for each—will you call on them to discover the themes? Or will you supply \n the themes and call on them for antitheses and application?

\n

4. Prepare contingencies for unexpected duration of discussion \n in various parts. What parts will you dispense with if the group wants to \n focus on fewer themes?

\n

5. Prepare your conclusion

\n
\n

If you are teaching a topic

\n
\n

1. Study your topic and identify your main points, Scriptural \n backing for each, other evidence, antitheses for each, and illustrations. \n

\n

2. Prepare an introduction

\n

3. Decide what you are going to supply, and what you are \n going to call for, via discussion. Gauge the knowledge level of the group, \n including new people. Questions that seem too hard might intimidate new people. \n Questions that require specific answers may intimidate. But over-simplified \n questions seem like recitation. You may:

\n
\n
\n

- Give a general introduction and then try to lead them \n in discovery of your main points

\n

- Give an introduction and present a contradiction: Either \n two passages that seem to contradict, or two contradictory positions that \n you argue persuasively. Call on the group to resolve the conflict, either \n in favor of one or the other point, or with a new position.

\n

- Give a general introduction and argue your main points. \n Then call on them for antitheses for each main point, considering what \n the implications for each are

\n

- Introduce and argue your main points. Then call for \n comparisons with similar or opposing views or with related issues

\n

- Introduce and argue your main points. Then call on \n the group for illustrations, especially in their own experience

\n
\n
\n
\n

Finishing Touches

\n
\n

1. Before God, what part of the plan really \n fires you with passion? Have you worked with this enough to develop a burden \n for your points? How are you going to let that passion fly?

\n

2. Check your questions with someone who \n understands discussion. Have you accidentally included questions that are \n boring, hard to understand, or are recitation?

\n

3. Are you familiar enough with your passage \n and subject to be flexible and relatively note-free? You can't engage people \n in discussion if your are looking at your notes all the time.

\n

4. Have you prayed by yourself and with your \n group members that the Holy Spirit will energize the church for sharing?

\n

5. Consider an audience plant: If you know \n someone in your group who has experience in the area you are discussing, why \n not call that person and say, \"We're going to be discussing this area, \n and I wonder if you'd be willing to share about that time you...\"

\n

6. Consider audience preparation: Most home \n churches have an email group set up. Why not send out a message to the people \n before your meeting: \"We're going to be studying such and so, and these \n are some questions to think about for discussion...\"
