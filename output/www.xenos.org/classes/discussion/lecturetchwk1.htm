Discussion as Instruction: Theory

\n

Each type of instruction has different strengths and weaknesses. \n For example:

\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
Modeling is good for \n conveying subjective qualities that are hard to describe in words. But modeling \n left by itself is easy to misinterpret. The learner may conclude that someone \n they model is doing things for reasons other than the real ones. Modeling \n also requires very small numbers of learners for maximum effectiveness. \n The subjective elements the model seeks to convey (like her urgency for \n the things of God) are only apparent to those who are personally close enough \n to appreciate what words and actions mean in the context of the model’s \n life. This makes modeling extremely time-intensive.
Lecture is good for \n conveying large quantities of information to large numbers of people, but \n lacks any way to know whether that information is being perceived or is \n in useable form. Learners may be cataloguing information that they will \n never be able to apply to life situations. Or, for all we know, they may \n be learning nothing at all. Yet, when well done, lecture is one of the most \n persuasive forms of communication. A good lecture can arrest the attention \n of even very large groups of people. On the other hand, if one lectures \n in a very small group, it can seem pretentious and unnatural.
Written outlines or essays \n can convey large quantities of information to either large or small groups, \n but are easily misinterpreted unless very carefully written. Such writing \n is time consuming, but so are all modalities of teaching if done well. In \n addition, written material has one great weakness: it must be read to be \n effective. On the other hand, if learners don’t understand, they can \n re-read the piece as often as they like.
\n

These examples illustrate why good teachers rely on a number \n of instruction modalities rather than just one approach.

\n

We will be discussing one of the most powerful channels of \n instruction—guided group discussion. Like other methods, this channel has \n strengths and weaknesses. Compile a list of weaknesses and strengths before \n going on.

\n\n

Group Discussion Strengths and Weaknesses

\n \n\n

We will be referring often in this class to Mastering the \n Techniques of Teaching (Second Edition), Joseph Lowman (San Francisco, Josey-Bass \n Inc., Publishers, 1995) Lowman is a recognized authority on college-age instruction. \n All page numbers at ends of citations are from this excellent book.

\n\n

Definition

\n \n

For our purposes, discussion will refer to leader-guided or \n moderated group discussion. This will usually follow a pattern where the leader \n teaches partly by lecture, or at least offers an introduction and conclusion. \n Guided discussion may involve some peer-to-peer interaction, but most of the \n interaction will be from learners to the leader or the group in general. The \n leader offers a short summary and comment to most, or all, sharing.

\n

Technique

\n

\n

Introduction:

\n

Some discussions seem to drag in fits and awkward silences, \n creating tension in the group that further inhibits discussion. Other discussions \n seem to thrive in eager sharing, with people actually competing to get their \n point in. Why is this?

\n

The most likely reason for such difference is the introduction. \n In our introduction, we touch on the skills of public speaking, or homiletics. \n The leader must stimulate and excite the group about the subject under consideration. \n During this first part ot the meeting, the burden is completely on the leader \n to:

\n
\n
\n

Raise the group's consciousness of the issues: Defining \n what the issues are, and why they are urgent

\n

Emotionally engage the group with the subject and with \n the leader

\n

Achieve arousal: People sit up, furrow their brows, smile, \n laugh, and in other ways show that they have been impacted emotionally, \n whether excited, disturbed, insulted (be careful with this one), or inspired.

\n
\n
\n

When people come into a meeting, they are not ready to discuss \n anything. Most people come to meetings with cold hearts and empty minds. They \n do not have any thoughts to share, or any desire to speak. Leaders who try to \n start out with a discussion question are mistaken.

\n

Achieving such arousal is not necessarily a long project. It \n could be accomplished in a couple of minutes, although you could spend up to \n 15 or even 20 minutes, if the you feel the need to lay a more complete groundwork. \n These judgments are based on the subject matter and the audience. Longer introductions \n have the potential to lay out more elaborate content, but may exhaust the audience \n attention-span.

\n

To become adept at this part, consider taking a class \n in homiletics.

\n

Probes

\n

The instructor will normally introduce \"probes\" or \n questions intended to prod members toward a particular line of thought. However, \n discussion is not recitation. Recitation is when the instructor gives students \n an opportunity to clarify content or the instructor asks questions requiring \n specific knowledge of study content, frequently from assigned readings (like \n the teacher in \"The Paper Chase\"). We are not suggesting such recitation \n is wrong or harmful, only that such is not what we are studying in this class.

\n

What then, are these questions, or probes, that elicit discussion? \n Several patterns are successful. Here is a partial list:

\n
\n
\n

Set up an apparent contradiction in your introduction, \n and ask the group how it might be resolved

\n

Ask them how a particular truth might apply either to life \n in general, or to specific situations you imagine

\n

Give them a statement from a third party (either imaginary \n or an authority) and ask them to react to it

\n

Ask how someone from x, y, or z perspective would answer \n a particular question

\n

Ask what whether what you just distilled from a text or \n narrative is different or the same as something else with which they are \n already familiar (e.g. Is this teaching about letting each person have their \n own conviction from Rom. 14 different in any way from relativism?)

\n

Devil’s advocacy: challenge a position they all seem \n to accept axiomatically with some problems

\n

Discovery: What do you think is this passage really saying? \n

\n

Personal experience: Who wants to share an experience \n where this truth has made a difference?

\n

Comparing and contrasting: Lowman says, \"Asking students \n to compare and contrast concepts, theories, and individuals orally in class \n helps to clarify the relationships within a content area. 172

\n
\n
\n

As learners answer each question, the leader responds with \n a short summary statement and a further probe, until moving on to another subject.

\n

Objectives

\n

After learning the particulars in a field of knowledge (such \n as vocabulary and grammar rules in a language), the next stage in learning is \n to progressively differentiate and relate the new particulars to existing particulars. \n Discussion is particularly suitable for these later stages in learning. [see \n exercise on Ausubel’s stages of learning]

\n

Consider Lowman’s 5 types of thinking best developed in \n discussion: Personal identification: What would Paul have felt during his imprisonment \n in Rome?

\n
\n
\n

Objective or critical thinking: What are \n some problems with the notion of having no consciousness of sin in Heb. \n 10? Or, How would a Calvinist answer this?

\n

Diagnostic thinking: Ask students to draw \n conclusions from a data set

\n

Independent thinking: Why did they reach \n the conclusion they did?

\n

As-if thinking: Challenge them to predict \n a future outcome based on data

\n

Problem solving: Asking them to propose solutions \n to the problems under study

\n

Increasing awareness of value controversies: \n \"These both sound good, let’s hear more evidence for each

\n
\n
\n

Finally, consider these qualifications on leaders’ questions \n by Lowman:

\n
\n

Avoid questions that can be answered by short factual statements \n or yes or no responses. Keep queries short and simple. If students must work \n to decipher your questions, they are less likely to respond to it. Discussion \n questions should be easily understandable by students, put forth decisively, \n and followed by silence. [Lowman 180]

\n

The underlying assumption is that as people struggle with \n concepts and their own thoughts and feelings, they will become very receptive \n to new information and ideas that help them resolve the questions before them. \n Research shows that when learners struggle for answers in a area they retain \n those answers longer and more accurately. Further, when learners discuss related \n issues under skillful guidance, they link the ideas in an associational network. \n Such networks are remembered far longer than isolated concepts. 162

\n

More importantly, such discussion has the potential to develop \n actual thinking skills that will never result from lecture. While an audience \n might admire and enjoy a lecturer’s thinking, we have no reason to believe \n they would be able to imitate such thinking on their own in situations not \n mentioned in a lecture. As Lowman observes, \"asking learners to apply \n in your group what they have learned [through discussion] requires them to \n demonstrate understanding, not merely memorization.\" 162 \n This is what we want from our people—not just the ability to do what \n a tape-recorder can do (spit back knowledge) but to creatively handle that \n knowledge in ways that are useful in ministry and Christian living.

\n
\n

Seen this way, we realize discussion is an essential type of \n instruction where learners get to try their own wings like baby birds. I believe \n a guided discussion format is the best approach for many Xenos cell groups, \n small groups, and home churches. Authorities agree that even in large groups \n of over 100 a skillful lecturer can incorporate periods of interaction with \n good effect.

\n

However, guidance is essential. Many discussions lead nowhere, \n and any conclusions reached are not memorable. As Lowman says, \"Discussion \n should be for an intended purpose, not simply to hear students’ voices.\" \n The notion that any discussion or interaction will naturally lead to advancement \n in learners’ lives is naïve.
