The underlying assumption is that as people struggle with concepts \n and their own thoughts and feelings, they will become very receptive to new \n information and ideas that help them resolve the questions before them. Research \n shows that when learners struggle for answers in a area they retain those answers \n longer and more accurately. Further, when learners discuss related issues under \n skillful guidance, they link the ideas in an associational network. Such networks \n are remembered far longer than isolated concepts. 162

\n

More importantly, such discussion has the potential to develop \n actual thinking skills that will never result from lecture. While an audience \n might admire and enjoy a lecturer’s thinking, we have no reason to believe \n they would be able to imitate such thinking on their own in situations not mentioned \n in a lecture. As Lowman observes, \"asking learners to apply in your group \n what they have learned [through discussion] requires them to demonstrate understanding, \n not merely memorization.\" 162 This is what we want from our people—not \n just the ability to do what a tape-recorder can do (spit back knowledge) but \n to creatively handle that knowledge in ways that are useful in ministry and \n Christian living.

\n

Seen this way, we realize discussion is an essential type of \n instruction where learners get to try their own wings like baby birds. I believe \n a guided discussion format is the best approach for many Xenos cell groups, \n small groups, and home churches to use most of the time. Authorities agree that \n even in large groups of over 100 a skillful lecturer can incorporate periods \n of interaction with good effect.

When the discussion leader learns to do all these things by \n habit and instinct, leading discussion becomes an enjoyable and relaxing way \n to teach. She is able to draw on the gifts and knowledge of fellow Christians \n in the group for help rather than feeling responsible for a solo performance.

\n

While not as glorifying to the leader as a blockbuster sermon, \n a rich discussion will leave members feeling like they contributed, learned, \n and heard God speak to their hearts. It also helps develop social ties and understanding \n between members. Viewed this way, we see that leading discussion is one of the \n most self-effacing modes of teaching.

\n

Many teachers feel uneasy with guided discussion, and prefer \n lecture. This could reflect a fear of losing control. The spontaneity and unpredictability \n of discussion strikes fear into the heart of those who lack confidence in their \n own knowledge of Scripture, in God’s leadership of the group, and in the \n capabilities of fellow leaders. Or, to put it differently, heavy controllers \n make poor discussion leaders.

It is possible that leading discussion might involve more learned \n skills than does effective preaching. Good preaching often seems possible for \n some, but not for others. Leading discussion also draws on the abilities of \n the group, which in our context, include mature Christians who are ready to \n help.

\n

Potential Weaknesses

\n

Teachers who understand, embrace, and practice discussion leading \n will gain an important tool in their arsenal. Groups led by good discussion \n leaders tend to be engaged, interested, and enthusiastic. Discussion should \n not be the only mode of instruction, but it will enhance other modes by supplying \n what they cannot.
