\"Salvation\" means \n rescue or deliverance. The gospel is that through Christ, God freely \n delivers us from sin.

\n

Read Phil. 1:6*. For I am confident \n of this very thing, that He who began a good work in you will perfect it until \n the day of Christ Jesus.

\n

This passage implies that salvation \n is given to us in three distinct stages: \"he who began a good work . \n . . will continue to perfect it . . . until the day of Christ Jesus.\" \n The three biblical terms that are most often used to describe these three \n stages are justification, sanctification, and glorification. \n Consider the following distinctions between these stages:

\n
\n
\n

Note: Each word has \n a semantic range (e.g. \"love\" in the English language means many \n different things depending on who we are saying it to). Therefore, we cannot \n assume that every time we see the word \"sanctification\" used in \n the Bible, that it always refers to spiritual growth. The author of Hebrews \n uses this term, sanctification, to refer to what we here call \"justification\" \n (see Heb. 10:10,14; 12:14). Paul uses the noun form (\"saints\") to \n refer to all people who have been justified—not to an elite class of \n super-mature Christians (see 1 Cor. 1:2). How would we determine the exact \n meaning of the word? Immediate context and author's normal usage of the word. \n

\n

See Romans 5:1-5 (and Rom. 8:1-23): \n Identify the three phases of salvation in these passages.

Developing spiritual maturity \n means that we will be growing in intimacy in our relationship with Christ \n (John 17:3; Phil 3:8,10; 1 John 2:13,14). This relationship develops in several \n ways that we will discuss. As teachers, we are committed to helping you understand \n the process of growth (Eph 4:11-15). What would it look like in your life \n if you were neglecting this area? For more on cultivating your intimacy with \n God, check out For the Love of God by D.A. Carson, Bruce Wilkinson's \n Secrets of the Vine and the Sacred Romance Drawing Closer to the \n Heart of God by Brent Curtis and John Eldredge.

\n

Spiritual maturity involves \n \"growing up in all aspects into him.\" What are the different \n aspects of spiritual maturity? This is the first order of business—to \n get a clear biblical picture of the Goal. If you don't have \n this, it's going to be pretty difficult to know how you're progressing, and \n pretty easy to get off track.

\n
\n

(Eph 4:14,15) \n As a result, we are no longer to be children, tossed here and there by waves, \n and carried about by every wind of doctrine, by the trickery of men, by \n craftiness in deceitful scheming; (15) but speaking the truth in love, we \n are to grow up in all aspects into Him, who is the head, even Christ . . . 

\n
\n

In Jesus' teaching on spiritual \n growth in John 15, he speaks of three different aspects in the context \n of an intimate personal relationship. We will use this passage as our base \n text. John elaborates on each of these aspects in 1 John. Footnote1A \n John was concerned to explain what Christian spirituality is in contrast to \n the false spirituality espoused by the Gnostics. As he does this, it becomes \n clear that he stresses three aspects of spirituality. They are woven throughout \n the epistle. These areas are not airtight categories. They are all interrelated \n and are pursued in the context of a personal relationship with God...

1) \n The Love of Christ

\n
\n
Matt. 22:37-40; Jn. 13:34,35; \n Rom. 8:4; 13:8-10; 1 Cor. 13:1-3; 1 Tim. 1:5; 1 Peter 1:22; 4:8; 1 Jn. 4:20, \n 21 \n

(John 15:12) \"This is \n My commandment, that you love one another, just as I have loved you.\"

\n

In our culture, spirituality is \n for self: self development, self progress, self healing. (See overhead \n promoting Deepak Chopra & Wayne Dyer's audio tape series.)

\n

Biblical love is different. It \n is directed outside ourselves toward other people (1 Cor. 13:1-3; \n 1 John 4:20,21). Biblical love is a commitment to give of one's self \n in every area for the good of the other (Phil. 2:3,4). It is the central \n ethic in the Bible (Matt 22;37-40; 1 Tim 1:5; 1 Peter 4:8). We can think \n of practicing biblical love along two axes, How we love & Who we love: \n

\n

How we love:

\n
    \n
  • sacrificial rather \n than conditional
    • \n
  • disciplining rather \n than mushy sentimentalism or compromise of Biblical principles just \n to \"keep the peace\".
    • \n
  • forgiving rather than \n resenting or reserving the right to retaliate.
    • \n
  • emotional rather than \n defining love as only functional things we do for the other.
    • \n
\n
\n

The father's actions in the parable \n of the Lost Son illustrate these aspects of love (Luke 15).

\n
\n

Whom we love:

\n
    \n
  • Existing relationships (1 Thes \n 3:12)
    • \n
\n
\n

Depth- increasing the intimacy \n of existing relationships whether within the church or outside the church.  \n

\n
\n
    \n
  • Developing friendships with \n new people (1 Thes 3:12; Matt. 5:46; Luke 10:25-37; Matt. 28:19)
    • \n
\n
\n

Breadth- extending ourselves \n beyond the scope of our existing relationships. This includes evangelism, \n follow-up, hospitality, serving the poor, missions, etc. This is very \n different than how we used to pick our friends, based on natural affinity.

\n
\n

What happens to those who \n neglect this area?

\n
    \n
  • \"Dead Sea\" Christians: \n all inflow through Bible study, etc.—but no outflow in active love. \n They become stagnant spiritually.
    • \n
\n
\n

Any \n spirituality that does not evidence itself in biblical love toward other \n people is deficient or false (see 1 Cor. 13:1-3). A self-centered spirituality, \n no matter how knowledgeable, moral or experiential, is a contradiction \n in terms!  Christians who know lots of Bible, never cuss, etc.—but \n have little use for anyone else.

\n
\n
    \n
  • You will be seduced by \n the world system. The pre-career aged people move into hedonism and \n after that into materialism.
    • \n
\n
\n

This is the \"Y in the \n road\" for most Christians in our affluent culture.   Is your \n Christianity only for you or is it primarily for others?  Will \n you pursue God's purposes in the world while living out your career, \n etc., or will your career, pursuit of money, and hobbies exclude your \n \"usefulness to the Master for every good work\" (2 Tim. 2:20,21).

\n
\n

How does God help us to develop \n in this area?

\n
    \n
  • He provides us with models of \n the loving lifestyle in the Body of Christ and opportunities to serve \n and reach out with others.
    • \n
\n
\n

This is the reason for our home \n group membership requirement for taking this class.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Blesses us deeply when we do \n choose to love others biblically (John 13:17; Acts 20:35).  This \n was Jesus' repeated teaching and modeling to the disciples.
    • \n
  • He gradually shows us our unique \n role/gifts as we begin to serve others—which is very motivating \n because we see the true purpose for our lives.
    • \n
  • He brings people into our lives \n that expose and help develop our weak areas.
    • \n
\n
\n

The Holy Spirit prompts us to \n love all people in all ways as Christ has loved us. We naturally want \n to love only certain people, and in certain ways.  If you are growing \n spiritually, you will be seeing him revealing how he loves you, uncovering \n the weaknesses in your love life, and providing concrete situations \n for you to develop in these areas.

\n

Some personalities/temperaments \n tend to just \"rub us the wrong way\" or we just don't feel \n like there is a natural affinity for them.  What are we going to \n do?  Ask God, \"What do you want me to learn about how to love \n this person?\" OR try to figure out \"How can I get away from \n this person?\" (Examples: outreach for introverts; close friendships \n for extroverts; confrontation for a fearful person; compassion for aggressive \n people; emotional and personal intimacy for impersonal functioners).

\n
\n

What happens to those who focus \n only on this area?

\n
    \n
  • We provide only shallow, superficial \n love. Unless our acts of love are being directed by biblical discernment \n we can't really help others grow spiritually (truth sets us free - Jn. \n 8:31,32).
    • \n
\n
\n

Illustration: Love is like a \n flowing river. The water signifies the love, but it must be contained \n by the banks of truth. Otherwise it is diffused and shallow. When the \n river is contained and directed, it embodies power.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Those who ignore their own moral \n transformation by their preoccupation with serving others wind up disqualifying \n themselves.
    • \n

2) The Mind of Christ
\n See these verses: Jn. 17:17; \n Rom. 12:2; Eph. 4:17:23; Col. 1:9, 10

\n

(John 15: 7) \"If you abide in Me, and My words \n abide in you, ask whatever you wish, and it shall be done for you.

\n

A growing understanding of God's \n perspective of the world, man, how to relate to him, etc. as revealed in his \n word and to be able to find the major doctrines in the Bible.

\n

(Isaiah 55:8,9) \"For \n My thoughts are not your thoughts, Neither are your ways My ways,\" declares \n the LORD. 9 \"For as the heavens are higher than the earth, So are My \n ways higher than your ways, And My thoughts than your thoughts.

\n

This is the growing understanding of \n the biblical worldview, God's perspective on reality as taught in scripture—especially \n the doctrine of grace. There can be no true spirituality which is not rooted \n in and constantly fed by objective, biblical truth. To the extent that your \n view of spirituality denigrates this, it is deficient.

\n

Be careful to avoid the FALSE dichotomy \n between \"head knowledge\" (intellectual understanding) and \"heart \n knowledge\" (experiential apprehension). God does want us to experience \n his truth on an experiential level, but this must be preceded by intellectual \n understanding. Therefore it's never heart knowledge instead of head knowledge, \n but experience flowing from head knowledge.

\n

What happens to those who neglect \n this area?

\n
    \n
  • We will be conformed to the \n world.   (Rom. 12:2 is an either/or proposition) We will be influenced \n by our culture which promotes spiritual hostility to propositional assertions \n about God and the biblical world view in particular. There is little or \n nothing in the world system around us that encourages people to maintain \n a biblical world view.
    • \n
\n
\n

E.G. There are no billboards reminding \n us that all we are and own belongs to God and therefore we should give generously \n to his work.

\n

E.G. Secular radio does not encourage \n us to \"consider others more important than ourselves.\"(Phil.2:3,4) \n The constant influence of our culture will direct us away from God's perspective \n without deep and growing knowledge of the major doctrines of the Bible and \n their application.

\n
\n
    \n
  • We will be a prime candidate \n for spiritual deception by errant teaching. Many of Paul's letters \n (e.g., Colossians) are written to counter deception—what does Paul \n emphasize? Doctrine!
    • \n
  • We will adapt the tendency to \n feel like we \"have it down\" and therefore don't need this \n kind of constant exposure.
    • \n
\n
\n

(13) For everyone who partakes \n only of milk is not accustomed to the word of righteousness, for he is a \n babe. (14) But solid food is for the mature, who because of practice have \n their senses trained to discern good and evil.

BILLY GRAHAM \n & OSWALD SANDERS: \"If I had it to do over again, I have would spent \n more time studying the Bible as a younger Christian.\"

How does God help us to develop \n in this area?

\n
    \n
  • The Holy Spirit helps us to develop \n in this area by illuminating our minds to understand the meaning, significance \n and personal application of biblical truth (2 Tim. 2:7 - EXAMPLES; Eph. \n 3:14-21).
    • \n
  • God will show us our need for growth \n in this area by sending us people we don't know how to help because we don't \n know the Word.

  • He also works in our lives to give \n us opportunities to apply the truth we're learning. Sometimes we will learn \n it in from study or a teaching, and then get a real-life opportunity to \n put it into practice (STUDENTS IN THIS CLASS), and sometimes vice-versa.
    • \n
  • He provides us with teachers to \n help us make sense of the Bible and learn how to study it for ourselves.  \n Are we taking advantage of these opportunities?
    • \n

  • They're not interested in serving \n others or sharing their faith.

    • \n

3) The Character of Christ

\n
\n

See: Rom. 13:8-14; Eph. 4:17-6:9; \n Col. 3:5-4:1; 1 Tim 4:12; 2 Tim. 2:19-22; James 1:26,27

\n

(John 15:10) \"If\n you keep My commandments, you will abide in My love; just as I have kept\n My Father's commandments, and abide in His love.

\n

This is the deep-seated change \n in our character, behavior, values and attitudes \n that God works to gradually effect. Because God is a moral Being, \n true spirituality has moral content. Any view of spirituality that does \n not emphasize this is deficient at best, and heretical at worst.

\n

Contemporary spirituality does not\n evaluate behavior on the basis of an objective moral standard. Instead,\n introducing a moral component to spirituality is seen as Victorian and\n repressive.

\n

God wants to eliminate and replace. \n Negatively, he exposes sinful areas he wants to eliminate. Positively, he \n wants to cultivate character qualities like self-discipline, courage, patience, \n kindness, etc. (Gal. 5; 1 Cor. 13).

\n

He also wants to both mow the \n field and get at the roots!

\n
\n

Not only the obvious and most destructive \n areas God goes after from the beginning (SUBSTANCE ABUSE, PHYSICAL VIOLENCE, \n THEFT, SEXUAL IMMORALITY)...

\n

...but also deeper attitudinal \n issues (BITTERNESS, NEGATIVITY, PRIDE, COVETING, MATERIALISM, LOVE DEMANDS \n ON OTHERS, SELF-PITY, etc.). His ability to prioritize certain moral changes \n is evidence of God's personal knowledge of each of us and his love for \n us.

\n
\n

What happens to those who neglect \n this area?

\n
    \n
  • If we ignore God's moral correction, \n we will harden our hearts against his voice (Heb. 3:7ff; Psalm 32:8-10; \n 1 Tim. 4:2; Eph. 4:17-19).
    • \n
  • You won't be able to build deep \n relationships (marriage, discipleship) unless you allow God to sanctify \n you in-depth in this area. I.E. You will be hard to live with because \n of your sin issues!
    • \n
\n
\n

You will also tend to be intolerant \n of others' sins because you are unwilling to come to grips with your own \n imperfections.

\n

A conspiracy of silence may develop \n in a relationship or a group. When we are in sin and unrepentant, we are \n unwilling to admonish others who are in similar circumstances.

\n
\n
    \n
  • No matter how gifted we are, we \n will be disqualified from significant ministry. E.G. \"Since I'm involved \n in ministry, it doesn't really matter whether I allow God to sanctify \n my anger problem with my spouse.\"  E.G. \"I'm really working \n hard at my ministry so I don't need to apply myself in my secular job.\" \n
    • \n

How does God help us to develop \n in this area?

\n
    \n
  • God personally prioritizes what \n he wants to work on at this time, and leads us accordingly. This frees \n us from the overwhelming chore of trying to change ourselves on our own \n timetable.  (Ps. 139:23,24)
    • \n
  • God motivates us to turn away \n from destructive sinful attitudes by providing positive experiences and \n convinces us that his ways are better than our old ways. We also see that \n our sinful ways are destructive to ourselves and others.
    • \n
  • God provides positive and negative \n models of our needed character change.
    • \n
  • God brings people and situations \n into our lives that expose the areas he wants to work on (SPOUSES; ROOMMATES; \n BOSSES; etc.; Rom. 5:3-5; Jm. 1:2-4).
    • \n
\n
\n

He also supplies us with models \n who give us hope that we can be changed, and who supply us with practical \n steps to progress.

\n
\n

What happens to those who focus \n only on this area?

\n
    \n
  • You will become self-centered, \n judgmental, and a superficial moralist — I.E. a Pharisee (Mt. 23:23-27).  \n A commitment to love (above) is what exposes need for moral transformation.  \n Therefore, ironically, although claiming to focus on moral transformation, \n unless you are ALSO investing in biblical love relationships you won't \n experience in-depth moral transformation.
    • \n
  • You will also tend to set up a \n system of rules that aren't moral (biblical) and treat them as important \n as biblical imperatives
    \n (Mark 7:6-8,13).
    • \n
  • This can lead to despair.  \n If we are honest about our moral shortcomings when focusing on them, AND \n don't have a clear understanding of the grace of Christ and how it applies \n to that sin, we will despair.
    • \n

The \n references to these three emphases from the other epistles are too numerous \n to mention.
