There are many fundamental \n contradictions as we review this chart. For example, God can't \n be personal and impersonal at the same time. Salvation can't be conscience \n existence and personal annihilation at the same time. The way of salvation \n can't be a free gift and a wage earned at the same time. The differences \n are very clear and all religions cannot be held as true.

\n

Don't just contrast Christianity \n to the other religions. Evaluate the claims of Hinduism from an \n Islamic perspective, or the claims of Buddhism from a Jewish perspective.

\n \n
\n

ANDERSON (Christian): \"The \n fact is that generalizations about religion are almost always misleading. \n Nothing could be further from the truth than the dictum . . . ‘Religion \n has not many voices, but only one.’ . . . Even \n the most cursory examination of the theology of these different religions \n reveals far more contradiction than consensus.\" (Sir Norman Anderson, \n Christianity and World Religions: The Challenge of Pluralism [Downers \n Grove: InterVarsity Press, 1984, p. 15).

\n

Zaehner (Hindu): \"To \n maintain that all religions are paths leading to the same goal, as is \n so frequently done today, is to maintain something that is not true . . . (T)he \n basic principles of East and West . . . simply are \n not starting from the same premises. The only common ground is that the \n function of religion is to provide release; there is no agreement at all \n as to what (we) must be released from. The great religions are talking \n at cross purposes.\" (Colin G. Chapman, The Case For Christianity \n [Grand Rapids: Eerdmans Publishing, 1981], p. 143).

\n

Clendenin (Christian): \"Contrary \n to the idea that all the religions teach the same thing, by virtue of \n their historical particularity and specificity the many religions offer \n us radically divergent pictures of God, the world, life, death, the afterlife, \n and humanity . . . Historically and empirically it \n is obvious that a common essence is precisely what religions do not have; \n they aim at different goals, teach contradictory doctrines, and prescribe \n radically different experiences. Religion as a common genus simply does \n not exist.\" (Daniel B. Clendenin, Many Gods, Many Lords [Grand \n Rapids: Baker Books, 1995], pp. 64, 108).

\n \n

Netland (Christian): \"It \n is difficult indeed to escape the conclusion that some of the central \n affirmations of Christianity, Hinduism, Buddhism, Islam, and Shinto are \n opposed; as long as the meanings of the doctrines within the respective \n religious communities are preserved, they cannot be jointly accepted without \n absurdity.\" (Harold Netland, Dissonant Voices: Religious Pluralism \n and the Question of Truth [Grand Rapids: Eerdmans, 1991], pp. 110, \n 111).

\n

Panikkar (Pluralist): \"(Pluralists \n must abandon their quest for a common essence because) the incommensurability \n of ultimate systems is unbridgeable . . . (and any) \n alleged common denominator is a sheer reductionist abstraction.\" \n (Raimundo Panikkar, \"The Jordan, the Tigris, and the Gangis,\" \n In Hick and Kitter, eds.,. The Myth of Christian Uniqueness \n [Maryknoll, NY: Orbis Books, 1988], p. 110).
