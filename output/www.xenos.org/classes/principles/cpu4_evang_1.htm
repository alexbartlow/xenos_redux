Evangelism and missions is central to the purpose \n of the church

(REMIND THAT GREAT COMMISSION IS BOTH OUTREACH AND DISCIPLESHIP; outreach \n is getting more people into his kingdom-discipleship is more kingdom into \n his people

\n

Matt. 28:18-20 And Jesus came up and spoke to them, saying, \"All \n authority has been given to Me in heaven and on earth. (19) \"Go therefore \n and make disciples of all the nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father \n and the Son and the Holy Spirit, (20) teaching them to observe all that I \n commanded you; and lo, I am with you always, even to the end of the age.\" \n

\n

\n

The purpose of the church is not to perpetuate itself. The church is commissioned \n as God's instrument to reach lost people. It is lost people and not found \n people, that are the central focus of God's concern (Mark 2:17; Luke 15). \n Of course, God has great concern for all Christians, but his concern extends \n to people all across the world who live without him. When a church adopts \n a mission that excludes evangelism as its central purpose, the clock is ticking \n toward the breakdown and destruction of that church.
\n

\n

Other things that commonly usurp an outreach focus: good fellowship \n (sweet, but what happens to our fellowship if don't have outreach? , deep \n knowledge, political agenda, family protection, corporate worship, etc. THINGS \n THAT ARE NOT WRONG, BUT ARE INADEQUATE AS A FOCUS
\n Therefore, the church must not make these its primary focus (THE GOOD IS OFTEN \n THE ENEMY OF THE BEST)
\n

\n

What happens if the church loses an evangelism focus? (NOT ALL THESE \n HAPPEN ALL THE TIME, BUT MANY WILL)

\n
\n

1. Vitality and excitement wane because new converts are the lifeblood \n of the church. They \"validate\" the gospel and increase our own \n confidence in it and God's power.
\n

\n

2. No new people means no home group plants which means no new leaders \n and workers needed which means less motivation for learning and sanctification \n among members. (FOR PEOPLE TO GROW THEY NEED MINISTRY RESPONSIBILITY)
\n

\n

3. We get more self-focused because there are no new converts to \n care for.
\n

\n

4. Relational conflict increases because instead of banding together \n to help witness and care for the new converts, we start looking at how others \n aren't meeting our needs (Movie: Remember the Titans -- black and \n white students in a Virginia high school forced to integrate overcome racial \n differences in their drive to win a state championship).
\n

\n

5. The church gets involved in \"brass-polishing\" or \"gingerbread\"-doing \n less important things that further detract from our real mission.
\n

\n

6. An \"ivory tower\" Bible-club mentality develops and \n people debate nonessential issues and can't relate to non-Christians.
\n

\n

7. A \"ghetto\" mentality develops that justifies lack of \n contact with non-Christians-which will lead to increased vulnerability to \n tribalism, materialism, and hidden sin. (IN HISTORY MOVEMENTS TEND TO GO \n INWARD-THERE IS A SPECIAL DANGER IN ADULT HOME GROUPS)
\n

\n

8. The church becomes calloused to the plight of the lost (apathy). \n Not just that they are going to hell in the next life, but that they \n are miserable in this life. (MT 9 SHEEP W/O A SHEPHERD)
\n

\n
\n

Eph. 2: 12 remember that you were at that time separate from Christ, \n excluded from the commonwealth of Israel, and strangers to the covenants \n of promise, having no hope and without God in the world.

Harvest vs. Search Theology: 2 different views \n of the our responsibility in evangelism

\n

One says, \"All that it important is swinging the ax\" The others \n says \"yes, but is the woodpile getting bigger?\" And if not, \n we ought to look at how we are swinging, how sharp the blade is, what kind \n of wood are we swinging at etc.
\n

\n

Christians often disagree on whether or not one should account for results \n of the church's evangelistic efforts. Some consider this to be vital to a \n faithful evangelistic witness and would consider their church as failing if \n there were prolonged periods of time without evangelistic fruit. Others consider \n a focus on counting attendance, conversions, church growth, etc. to be ungodly. \n We introduce these terms to initiate you into this debate.
\n

\n

Search Theology

\n
\n

Definition: Searches for people through the faithful proclamation \n of the Gospel, leaving results between God and the hearer. OK, BUT DOES \n THAT GO FAR ENOUGH?
\n

\n

Examples: Go door to door or hand out tracks (OR ANY OTHER METHOD)-if \n it doesn't yield, you don't question it because results are up to God.
\n

\n

Search theology is flawed because:

\n
\n
    \n
  • It does not go far enough. It neglects analysis of results.
    • \n
  • The Great Commission provides a command which includes results.(MAKE \n DISCIPLES; ALL ETHNOI; THINGS WE CAN TRACK)
    • \n
  • The example in Acts: Luke includes numbers in his report of the early \n church. (2:41; 6:7, 9:31; 12:24; 16:5; 19:10,20)
    \n
    • \n
  • It lacks a dynamic approach (rather than wooden or mechanistic) to evangelism \n and therefore its adherents do not persuade (Acts 17:2-4; 18:4; 2 Cor. 5:11, \n 20), persist (Acts 17:17), or adjust (1 Cor. 9:19-23-CULTURAL FACTORS). \n
    \n
    \n

    E.G. READING THE SITUATION AND ADJUSTING YOUR APPROACH
    \n

    \n

    NOTE THE DIFFERENCE B/W PETER'S APPROACH IN ACTS 2 AND PAUL'S IN ACTS \n 17

    \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Search theology has been used to justify failure in evangelism. THERE \n IS SUCH A THING AS A PROPER DISSATISFACTION

    \n

Harvest theology (AGAIN, THE WOOD PILE)
\n

\n

Definition: Finding and winning receptive people (harvesting) through \n faithful proclamation of an understandable Gospel, keeping an eye on results \n to determine effectiveness and future methods.

\n

Examples: LEARNING ABOUT POSTMODERN THINKING AND ADDRESSING IT CONSCIOUSLY \n IN TEACHINGS RATHER THAN JUST TALKING TO THE MODERNIST. USING A METHOD THAT \n WORKED WELL WITH STUDENTS AND KEEPING IT WHEN THEY GET MARRIED AND HAVE \n KIDS.

\n

Harvest theology is Biblical because:

\n
    \n
  • It does reflect on results.
    • \n
  • It is dynamic.
    \n
    • \n
  • Focuses on the responsive field (like Xenos with students-NOT SIMPLY \n BECAUSE THEY ARE OUR KIDS)
    \n
    • \n

Key implication of Harvest: Contextualization

\n


\n Key ideological shifts in today's culture
\n

\n \n
\n

How is it that vital evangelistic movements die? Sometimes it's because they \n departed from the gospel, but more often it's because they lose touch with \n the culture.
\n

\n

We must not only know what the good news is, but must also know our culture's \n current ideological objections to the gospel and how to respond. We need to \n discern the \"spirit of the age,\" recognizing that it changes in \n important ways. For example, not too many years ago, skeptics rejected Christianity \n because it was thought to conflict with science. Today, just as many people \n reject Christianity, not because it has been carefully examined and found \n inconsistent with science, but merely because it claims to be universally \n true! This shows a significant shift in how our culture thinks.
\n

\n

To get things rolling, the class could note other reasons that people reject \n Christianity.
\n

\n

We would never think of sending missionaries abroad without knowledge of \n the language and culture of the target field. Today we must do the same thing \n at home. We are cross-cultural missionaries right here!
\n

\n

We must try to understand the key ideological shifts of our culture. How \n has thinking changed in the western world in the past generation?

2. What the critics thought

\n
    \n
  • Critically minded skeptics appealed to reason and evidence \n (especially science)
    • \n
  • Christians would defend their faith (apologetics) \n using classical arguments (design of the universe, design of man, and \n the study of 'how we know what we know') or evidence (historical evidence \n for the resurrection, for the reliability of the Bible, etc.). The rational \n critic would often find these types of arguments persuasive.
    • \n
\n 		\n

2. What the critics think

\n
    \n
  • Critically minded skeptics find the claims and content \n of Christianity dogmatic and offensive. Christianity can't possibly \n be universally true because it excludes other religious voices and traditions. \n \"Hmmm, that's great for you, but you can't impose your truth on \n me.\" Truth and reason bounce off many people today.
    • \n
  • Christians still need to make use of classical arguments \n and evidences, but also need to be able to defend the notion of absolute \n truth and critique relativism. Therefore it will take more patience \n and \"pre-evangelism\" is often needed.

    For Class teachers: some additional info about truth:

    \n
      \n
    • It is objective, not merely subjective (ROM 3:4). \n We have access to revealed truth, but we have not mastered it or \n been mastered by it completely. Our aim is to be biblical people. \n (1 Thess 2:15) We are not always objective, but our aim is to be \n objective, fair, and just. Our response to the truth should be personal \n and thus has subjective elements. The truth exists outside of us \n and is not effected by our force of will or our feelings.

We do not have universal knowledge, nor do we know \n how to relate truth to every situation, but our aim is more knowledge \n and discernment for its application.

      • \n
    • It is antithetical, not synthetic (Matt. 12:30; \n Ex. 20:1-6). We acknowledge supra-rational aspects of God, but we \n do not invoke \"mystery\" in order to sanction contradiction, \n intellectual laziness, or relativism.
      \n
      • \n
    • Truth is a bestowal of God's grace, not my propaganda. \n Truth is given for our good, not to control and exploit us (ROM \n 1:16).

Two definitions of \"tolerance\"
\n

\n
\n

1. BIBLICAL: Personal respect and love for individuals despite deep \n disagreement with their beliefs. The former (and biblical) definition \n of \"tolerance\" made a distinction between people and their religious \n beliefs. It meant that people should have the legal freedom to practice \n the religion of their choice, and that you should personally respect and \n love them, even if you conclude that their beliefs are false. We should \n expressly affirm this kind of tolerance and deplore this kind of intolerance. \n
\n

\n

2. CONTEMPORARY: Includes the old view, but adds that we must affirm \n the validity of all beliefs
\n Today's \"tolerance\" has removed the distinction between persons \n and their beliefs. It means that you must never call others' beliefs false \n or untrue, or you are an arrogant, intolerant bigot. But this definition \n confuses tolerance with truthfulness.
