Recall last week

\n \n
 
\n

Seduction - using \n distraction to entice us from Christ.

\n
\n

Purpose: to steal our \n affections from Christ through distraction/idolatry

\n
\n
\n

Avenues: The kosmos—the \n whole system of values that is Satan's influence for evil. This is a \n huge theme in the New Testament! Consider the following representative \n verses:

\n
\n
\n
\n

Satan is the creator \n and ruler of the kosmos.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(1 John 5:19)  \n We know that we are of God, and the whole world lies in \n the power of the evil one.

\n

(2 Cor. 4:4) \n  . . . in whose case the god of this world has \n blinded the minds of the unbelieving, that they might not see the \n light of the gospel of the glory of Christ, who is the image of \n God.

\n

(Eph. 2:2) \n  . . . in which you formerly walked according \n to the course of this world, according to the prince of the power \n of the air, of the spirit that is now working in the sons of disobedience. \n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

Therefore, the goal of \n the kosmos is to directly oppose God's goal for our lives.

\n
\n
\n
\n

(1 Cor. 2:12)  \n Now we have received, not the spirit of the world, but the Spirit \n who is from God, that we might know the things freely given to us \n by God . . . 

\n
\n
\n

There are two totally \n different ways of viewing things, and the \"spirit which is from \n the world\" is not morally neutral (see \n 1 Cor. 3:19; Isa. 55:8,9; Jn. 10:10).

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(Titus 2:11)  \n For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation to all men, \n instructing us to deny ungodliness and worldly desires and to live \n sensibly, righteously and godly in the present age . . .  \n

\n
\n

God's way for a \n fulfilled and effective life is diametrically opposed to worldly \n desires. \n

\n
\n

(2 Pet. 1:4) \n For by these He has granted to us His precious and magnificent promises, \n in order that by them you might become partakers of the divine nature, \n having escaped the corruption that is in the world by lust.
\n (Same as Titus 2:11,12) \n

\n
\n
\n

(2 Pet. 2:20) For \n if after they have escaped the defilements of the world by the knowledge \n of the Lord and Savior Jesus Christ, they are again entangled in \n them and are overcome, the last state has become worse for them \n than the first.

\n
\n
\n
\n

The world \"defiles,\" \n \"entangles,\" and can \"overcome\" people—including \n Christians. How sad it is to see people who have been \n rescued from its bondage to allow themselves to be sucked back \n into it! (See Isaiah 57:10; Hebrews 3:13)

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(James 4:4) \n You adulteresses, do you not know that friendship with the world \n is hostility toward God? Therefore whoever wishes to be a friend \n of the world makes himself an enemy of God.

\n
\n

Loving the world is incompatible \n with loving God.

\n
\n
\n
\n

Key Components \n of the Kosmos (from 1 John 2:15,16):

\n
\n

(1 John 2:15,16*) \n Do not love the world, nor the things in the world. If anyone loves \n the world, the love of the Father is not in him. (16) For all that \n is in the world, the lust of the flesh and the lust of the eyes and \n the boastful pride of life, is not from the Father, but is from the \n world.

\n

The kosmos takes \n areas that are potentially good, but twists them into idols that distract \n us from coming to Christ and/or neutralize us as \n Christians. These are the \"things of the world\" \n mentioned in vs 15.

\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • \"Lust of flesh\" \n (Hedonism):
    • \n
\n
\n
\n

There is nothing wrong \n with physical pleasure in itself. God gave us bodies with the \n capacity for enjoying pleasure.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n

    The \"lust of the flesh\" \n is the inordinate desire for physical pleasure. It includes not \n only obvious things like DRUG & ALCOHOL abuse, SEXUAL perversion, \n etc.—but also COMFORT (indolence as higher priorities \n than serving Christ), GLUTTONY; CIGARETTES.

    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • \"Lust of eye\" \n (Materialism):
    • \n
\n
\n
\n

\"Lust of the \n eye\" refers to inordinately desiring things that our eyes \n see (see Aachan in Josh. 7:21). There is nothing \n wrong with owning and even enjoying material objects. But this \n easily becomes a form of idolatry so that we derive our sense \n of meaning, significance, security, etc. from owning and enjoying \n them (more in 2 WEEKS).

\n

Materialism \n includes not just people like BILL GATES. It also is a \n crucial part of the American dream (\"PERSONAL PEACE \n & AFFLUENCE\").

\n

We do not need \n to have a lot of possessions to be materialistic, materialism \n is a mindset. We find ourselves consumed with thoughts \n of wanting more rather than considering how thankful we are for \n what we have and how we can use our resources to serve others. \n

\n

Remember that materialism \n is competitive for many. It's not important to have a nice car, \n but to have a \"nicer\" one than the next guy. For others, \n it is the pursuit of comfortable living (the absence of material \n suffering) that drives it.

\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • \"Boastful pride \n of life\" (Egotism)
    • \n
\n
\n
\n

It is an inordinate \n desire for the praises of men. There is nothing wrong \n with having a healthy sense of self—knowing that you are \n a significant person who can accomplish valuable goals, impact \n others, etc. But Satan twists this into something very different \n (Lamech in Gen. 4:19-24).

\n

As Christians we \n are significant because we are forgiven, adopted and gifted by \n God. Instead of living based on these realities, we try \n to attain significance based on other humans' approval.

\n

Not just HITLER/ROCK STAR—also \n justification of selfishness vs. sacrificial, serving lifestyle \n (CORPORATE LADDER-CLIMBING; SPORT CELEBRITIES; LUST FOR PEOPLES' \n \"RESPECT\"; STAY-AT-HOME MOMS WHO FEEL INSIGNIFICANT). \n

\n

Not only that we seek positive \n attention, but we fear the censure of others when we make decisions \n to serve God. EG the stay-at-home parent or the person who declines \n career advancement to serve his/her family or church.

\n

When we are into this lifestyle, \n we are enslaved to the opinions of others. Do you want the final \n verdict on your life to be, \"People really admired me?\"

\n
\n

Note: The \"law \n of diminishing returns\" operates in all three of these areas! \n

\n
\n

The more we pursue and \n wrap ourselves up into these areas, the less true fulfillment \n we experience and then we try even harder in these areas. We need \n a clean break from the beliefs (lies) and practices that say these \n areas will fulfill us.

\n
\n
\n

The kosmos is \n effective because of its extreme subtlety (i.e. — it \n is easy to be deceived. i.e. — you could be hip deep as we speak \n and not be aware).

\n
\n

(Eugene Peterson) \n \"The world is an atmosphere, a mood. It is nearly as hard for \n a sinner to recognize the world's temptations as it is for a fish \n to discover the impurities in the water. There is a sense, a feeling \n that things aren't right . . . but just what \n it is eludes analysis.\" (Eugene Peterson, quoted in Stephen \n Eyre, Defeating the Dragons of this World (Downers Grove: \n InterVarsity Press, 1987), p. 19)

\n

Satan with the world-system \n is like a fisherman with a sparkling lure. The lure is the imitation \n of the real food, and is designed to hide the barbed hook. We follow \n it along, enticed by our senses, convinced it will satisfy. We find \n the hook lodged in our throats, and ourselves being reeled in before \n we are aware of what has happened. And like the fish, once caught, \n we have to be willing to endure some pain as we fight to free ourselves. \n

\n

Temptation

\n
\n

Definition: existential \n enticement and suggestion to sin

\n

Avenues: Satan tempts \n us through both thoughts (Acts 5:3) and circumstances (1 Cor. 10:13; \n Lk. 4:1-13).

\n
\n

All temptation involves \n a rejection of God's truth. Through temptation, Satan tells \n us (like he told Eve in Gen. 3) that God's way is unfulfilling, \n unreliable, etc.—and that by choosing to disobey God's moral \n will we are only doing what we need to do to achieve fulfillment, \n happiness, security, etc.

\n
\n

Purposes: Since Satan \n knows that sin won't cause you to lose your salvation, what is his purpose \n in tempting you?

\n
    \n
  • \n

    He hopes to enslave \n you (or keep you enslaved) to sin, which will keep you \n from being an effective worker for God (Rom. 6:16; Jn. 8:34). Especially \n if you are responding to God's conviction to get free from a certain \n sin-habit, expect the devil to heap on the temptation.

    \n
    • \n
  • He hopes to destroy \n your witness to the world
    • \n
\n
\n

Titus 2:7,8 In everything \n set them an example by doing what is good. In your teaching show integrity, \n seriousness and soundness of speech that cannot be condemned, so that \n those who oppose you may be ashamed because they have nothing bad \n to say about us.

\n
\n
    \n
    \n

    If Satan can tempt you to \n fall in a way that non-Christians see, your credibility and witness \n can be damaged. (EG Lazy worker, gossip, outbursts of anger at work, \n self-righteousness, selfishness, etc.). Of \n course, we cannot be perfect and therefore must be prepared to admit \n failures and apologize when necessary to maintain integrity.

    \n
    \n
  • \n

    He hopes to infect \n the Body of Christ. If you submit to defeat, this attitude \n may begin to spread to others (1 Cor 5:6).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    He hopes to set you \n up for accusation (see below).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    His ultimate goal is to \n hurt God. (If an \n evil man wished to hurt an enemy he may choose to seduce his child \n and steal his affections.) Eph. 4:30; Hos. 2:16.

    \n
    • \n
\n

Countermeasures:

\n
\n

Question: \n \"How do you know when Satan is tempting you or it is just your \n flesh?\" We can't always tell the difference—both are probably \n always involved. It is more important to respond victoriously than \n it is to discern the primary source.

1.  Practice prevention \n by staying actively involved in growth and ministry!

\n
\n

David was especially vulnerable \n to temptation because he wasn't in the battle where he belonged. \n \"Idle time is the devil's playground.\"

But \n David stayed at Jerusalem. (2) Now when evening came David \n arose from his bed and walked around on the roof of the king's house, \n and from the roof he saw a woman bathing; and the woman was very \n beautiful in appearance. Accusation \n

\n
\n

Definition: personal denunciation \n based on real or alleged sins in order to alienate from God and demoralize. \n

\n
\n

Example: You \n have succumbed to a temptation. You've gotten angry, again. Now you \n are confronted with the accusation that you are completely ill-suited \n for ministry, and that there is little hope for significant change. \n You find yourself defeated and depressed. Satan is the source of this \n accusation.

\n
\n

(Rev. 12:10)  \n And I heard a loud voice in heaven, saying, \"Now the salvation, \n and the power, and the kingdom of our God and the authority of His \n Christ have come, for the accuser of our brethren has been \n thrown down, who accuses them before our God day and night. \n

\n
\n
\n

Avenues: both directly \n and through other people

\n
\n

What is the difference \n between accusation and conviction?

\n

Note: Both \n work through the conscience, by registering a painful or discomforting \n verdict on your actions. Accusation will commonly follow on the heels \n of conviction. You must accept conviction while rejecting accusation. \n

\n
    \n
  • Conviction draws \n us near to God while accusation alienates us from God. \n
    • \n
  • Conviction focuses \n on our actions while accusation uses our actions as an \n inappropriate basis for undermining our relationship with God. (EG \n we feel unworthy, inadequate, judged, rejected, etc. )
    • \n
  • Conviction will \n normally be clear and specific, while accusation tends \n to be vague and generalized.
    • \n
  • Conviction is constructive \n and forward-focused while accusation is destructive and \n backward focused. God wants us to learn from our failures, and as \n soon as we have learned he wants us to move forward. Satan wants \n to keep hammering us about past actions even if we have repented. \n
    • \n
\n
\n \n

Levels:

\n \n
    \n
  • Satan will attack \n new Christians' assurance of salvation (\"And \n you call yourself a Christian?\").
    • \n
  • Older Christians will \n be accused about their identity, ability to change &/or minister. \n (\"You'll never change. You'll always be ____________. \" \n \"You are completely ineffective in evangelism—why even try?\" \n \"After being in the flesh all week, what makes you think you \n can teach with spiritual impact?\")
    • \n
\n

Countermeasures:

\n
\n

1) If untrue: \n

    \n
  • Affirm what God \n has promised you, what he has done through you.
    • \n
  • Get objective input \n and affirmation from others.
    • \n
\n

2) If true (And it \n often is):

\n
    \n
  • Don't defend yourself \n as righteous. RATIONALIZING, MINIMIZING, and BLAME-SHIFTING \n are all ways of doing this. The answer to this kind of accusation \n is God's grace, not your righteousness!
    • \n
  • Confession: Agree \n with God about your sin, and take full responsibility for what you \n have done (Ps. 51 & its relationship to Ps. 32).
    • \n
\n
\n
\n

You should also agree with \n God about your forgiveness.

\n

Note: \n Sometimes you will not get relief until you confess to others. \n

\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • Action: \n Choose to draw near to God (Heb. 10:22), and choose to step \n out to serve.
    • \n
\n
\n
\n

(Heb. 9:14)  \n how much more will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal \n Spirit offered Himself without blemish to God, cleanse your conscience \n from dead works to serve the living God?

\n
\n
\n

Note: If you can't \n tell whether it is true or not, get objectivity from a mature Christian \n who knows you well.
