Introduction: Review Keys To Growth

\n\n
\n

Remember: The goal \n of spiritual growth is not only formation of Christ-like character and thinking, \n but ultimately influencing others for Christ (GREAT COMMISSION). Suffering \n prepares us for bearing fruit.

\n

One key way by which we grow is to \n build up the inner man (we wiill explain in context of Walking by the Spirit \n in Week #8). As we take the initiative to do this, \n we are able to affect others spiritually.

\n

But there is still the problem of the \n \"outer man\" (2 Cor. 4:7-16)--the flesh, or sin-nature. Building \n up the \"inner man\" doesn't really affect it. For this problem, God \n has another outward process by which our outer man is broken down so that \n the life of Christ can be released.

\n

This is the primary role of suffering \n in the Christian life (2 Cor 1:8,9), and we will not get beyond a primitive \n level of spiritual maturity unless we learn this truth and respond properly. \n Christians who don't understand and respond to suffering in a Biblical fashion \n often \"bail out!\"

Three Causes of Suffering

\n\n
\n

People often say that suffering is \n a big problem for Christianity--either God is powerful enough to stop suffering \n but isn't loving, or he wants to stop suffering but lacks the power to do \n so. Actually, the Bible presents us with a comprehensive (although not exhaustive) \n explanation.

\n

We can distinguish between three kinds \n of suffering. God sovereignly works through all of these sufferings \n to accomplish his purpose in our lives. We may often experience \n all three kinds of suffering involved in a single situation, as we will see. \n

\n
\n \n

1. Fallen World

\n \n
\n
\n

Christians (including committed Christian \n workers) are not exempt from the sufferings that come from living \n in a fallen world

\n

Examples: Sickness \n &/or crippling accidents; financial stress from mechanical failures, \n job loss, etc.; death of loved ones; children rebelling; impact of others' \n wrong choices (Divorce; crime), Genetic problems whether physical or mental.

\n
\n

The \"Health & Wealth\" \n teaching that those who have enough faith will not experience these sufferings \n is wrong!

\n
\n

If you are a growing \n Christian, will you experience more or less of this kind of suffering?

\n

When these things happen, many Christians \n ask \"Why is God doing this to me?\" or \"Why is God allowing \n this to happen to me?\"

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

To the first question, the answer \n is usually \"God did not do this to you.\" These sufferings are \n simply part of living in a fallen world (Unit 1, and Unit 4; Francis Schaeffer's \n video in study center helps address this -- the interview at the end of \n the \"How Then Shall We Live?\" video series, or see Schaeffer's \n Letters -- as he explains his view of God while he is dying of cancer). \n We should especially reject the idea that God is paying us back for sins \n we have committed, since the Christian is forever exempt from God's retributive \n wrath.

But also, we will not perfectly comply with these two conditions \n on this side of heaven).

2. Wrong Choices

\n\n\n \n
\n
\n

Natural Consequences: \n God often simply lets us \n experience the negative consequences of our wrong choices. This is what \n Solomon refers to as the \"reproofs of life\" (Prov. 6:23). Many \n Christians wrongly believe that being under grace means that God will protect \n us from this, but there is no scriptural promise to this effect--and many \n passages which warn us against such presumption (1 Cor. 6:17,18; Rom. 1:24-27 \n [see also Ps. 81:10-12]

\n
\n

Examples: Poverty from sloth or \n unwise spending leading to heavy debt load; damage in marriage from prior \n sexual promiscuity; more difficulty in career because of laziness in school.

\n
\n

(Rom. \n 1:24-27) Therefore \n God gave them over in the lusts of their hearts to impurity, that their \n bodies might be dishonored among them . . . (26) \n For this reason God gave them over to degrading passions; for their \n women exchanged the natural function for that which is unnatural, (27) \n and in the same way also the men abandoned the natural function of the \n woman and burned in their desire toward one another, men with men committing \n indecent acts and receiving in their own persons the due penalty of \n their error.

3. Following Christ (2 Cor. 4:7-12**)

\n
\n \n
\n

Reflections on suffering and spiritual \n maturity

(J.I. Packer) \"We should not be. . . too \n taken aback when unexpected and upsetting and discouraging things happen \n to us now. What do they mean? (Oswald Sanders) \"How is the process of forming \n Christ within the believing life advanced? Every circumstance of our lives \n has been planned with this end in view. How should we respond to suffering?

\n \n
\n

While we should initiate in \n feeding the inner man (i.e., \"present yourself to God.\" \"walk \n according to the Spirit\"), with the breaking of the outer man we should \n respond properly to God's initiative (contra ASCETICISM).

\n
\n \n

Our response to God's discipline/breaking \n process is crucial. We do not have the ability to escape from suffering, but \n we do have the ability to nullify God's good purpose for it.

\n

Listed below are the proper and improper \n responses to suffering most emphasized by the Bible. Identify your most common \n wrong responses, and give special attention to replacing them with the proper \n response.

\n\n
\n

1. Don't be surprised. \n

\n
\n
\n
\n

(1 Pet. 4:12) Beloved, do not be surprised at the fiery ordeal among \n you, which comes upon you for your testing, as though some strange thing \n were happening to you.

\n
\n
\n

(2 Tim. 2:3,4) Suffer hardship with me, as a good soldier of Christ \n Jesus. 4 No soldier in active service entangles himself in the affairs of \n everyday life, so that he may please the one who enlisted him as a soldier.

\n \n

(1 Pet. 4:1,2) Therefore, since Christ suffered in \n his body, arm yourselves also with the same attitude, because he who has \n suffered in his body is done with sin. 2 As a result, he does not live the \n rest of his earthly life for evil human desires, but rather for the will \n of God.

\n

New Christians should be informed \n of this ASAP. (Acts 14:22 - \" . . . strengthening the souls of the \n disciples, encouraging them to continue in the faith, and saying, `Through \n many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God.') \"Forewarned is \n forearmed!\"

\n

Older Christians also need to be \n reminded when things get harder than they thought

\n
\n

(Heb. 12:5)  \n \" . . . and you have forgotten the exhortation which is addressed \n to you as sons, `MY SON, DO NOT REGARD LIGHTLY THE DISCIPLINE OF THE LORD, \n NOR FAINT WHEN YOU ARE REPROVED BY HIM'\"

\n
\n

Arm yourself to suffer (1 Pet. 4:1,2; \n 2 Tim. 2:3; Phil. 1:29). We must prepare our minds, accepting the fact that \n hardship is certain.

\n
\n
\n
\n

2. Don't compare your \n sufferings to others 

\n
\n
\n
\n

(John 21:18-22) \"Truly, truly, I say to you, when you \n were younger, you used to gird yourself, and walk wherever you wished; but \n when you grow old, you will stretch out your hands, and someone else will \n gird you, and bring you where you do not wish to go.\" (19) Now this \n He said, signifying by what kind of death he would glorify God. And when \n He had spoken this, He said to him, \"Follow Me!\" (20) Peter, turning \n around, saw the disciple whom Jesus loved following them; the one who also \n had leaned back on His breast at the supper, and said, \"Lord, who is \n the one who betrays You?\" (21) Peter therefore seeing him said to Jesus, \n \"Lord, and what about this man?\" (22) Jesus said to him, \"If \n I want him to remain until I come, what is that to you? You follow Me!\"

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

\"Why aren't they suffering as \n much as I am? They must be more spiritual.\"\"I must be more spiritual \n than them since I'm not suffering.\" These meditations are foolish, \n and give birth to self-pity, envy and pride--the very things from which \n God is seeking to deliver us!

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

God has his own plan for each of \n us. Our concern for others should be on how we can encourage them to follow \n Christ. Our main focus should be on following him and learning what he wants \n us to learn.

\n
\n
\n
\n

3. Do focus on God's \n promises.

\n
\n
\n
\n

(2 Cor. 4:17,18) For momentary, light affliction is producing for \n us an eternal weight of glory far beyond all comparison, (18) while we look \n not at the things which are seen, but at the things which are not seen; \n for the things which are seen are temporal, but the things which are not \n seen are eternal.

\n

(Heb. 12:11*) All discipline for the moment seems not to be joyful, \n but sorrowful; yet to those who have been trained by it, afterwards it yields \n the peaceful fruit of righteousness.

\n

(1 Pet. 5:9,10*) But resist him, firm in your faith, knowing \n that the same experiences of suffering are being accomplished by your brethren \n who are in the world. (10) And after you have suffered for a little while, \n the God of all grace, who called you to His eternal glory in Christ, will \n Himself perfect, confirm, strengthen and establish you.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

In This Life: more fruit (Jn. 15:2; \n Phil 1:12,13 - including increased impact on others); the peaceful fruit \n of righteousness (Heb. 12:11*); perseverance, proven character & hope \n (Rom. 5:3,4); strengthen, confirm, perfect & established (1 Pet. 5:10); \n purifying your faith (1 Pet. 1:6,7)

\n

In The Next Life: reward (Rom. 8:18; \n 2 Cor. 4:17); full understanding (1 Cor. 13:12). Like a TAPESTRY, we only \n see the underside of it during this life. It is full of knotted threads \n of different colors, and we see no real beauty or pattern. But in the next \n life, God will show us the top of the TAPESTRY. We will see how it all fits \n together, and how his hand was always working with perfect love and wisdom \n to fashion our lives into something that gave glory to him.

\n
\n
\n
\n

4. Do give thanks while \n you are suffering.

\n
\n

(James 1:2-4*) Consider it all joy, my brethren, when you encounter \n various trials, (3) knowing that the testing of your faith produces endurance. \n (4) And let endurance have its perfect result, that you may be perfect and \n complete, lacking in nothing.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

This is a key way we can express \n our trust that God is sovereignly working through this suffering (Rom. 8:28). \n

\n
\n

Antithesis: complaining; self-pity; \n becoming embittered toward God; etc.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

It is also a privilege to \n suffer for the cause of Christ (Phil. 1:29; Acts 5:41)! There is cause greater \n than our own personal comfort: The cause of Christ.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

Antithesis: We don't see the willingness \n to suffer for anything amongst most western people. American hedonism/narcissism--no \n cause is greater than my own comfort.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

This is a choice (imperative), not \n a feeling! We can and should choose to thank God on the basis of his truth, \n in spite of our contradictory thoughts and feelings. The result of turning \n to God in the midst of suffering is a growing trust and realization of his \n infinite provision and commitment.

\n
\n

5. Do tell God how you feel, \n but determine to follow his will.

\n
\n

(Matt. 26:39-42) Going a little farther, he fell with his face \n to the ground and prayed, \"My Father, if it is possible, may this cup \n be taken from me. Yet not as I will, but as you will.\" (40) Then he \n returned to his disciples and found them sleeping. \"Could you men not \n keep watch with me for one hour?\" he asked Peter. (41) \"Watch \n and pray so that you will not fall into temptation. The spirit is willing, \n but the body is weak.\" (42) He went away a second time and prayed, \n \"My Father, if it is not possible for this cup to be taken away unless \n I drink it, may your will be done.\"

\n \n

Some Christians think it is unspiritual \n to admit to God that they are in pain, or to ask God to deliver them from \n suffering--but Jesus (and many other Old Testament authors) shows us this \n is not the case. To draw near to God in this way is a precious feature of \n our personal relationships with him.

\n

But alongside this freedom, there \n must be the determination to do what God wants us to do rather than to go \n outside God's will to spare ourselves from suffering. Undergirding this \n determination, of course, is the choice to believe that God's will is both \n wise and loving and good.

\n

We need to prize perseverance, endurance \n the way God does (70+ times in the New Testament)--to \"play with pain,\" \n to stay at our posts instead of deserting (2 Tim. 4:10). See also Heb. 12:3-5; \n 1 Cor. 10:13; Rom. 5:4

\n \n
\n
\n

 Conclusion

\n\n \n
\n

(John Newton) \n I asked the Lord that \n I might grow in faith and love and every grace,
\n might more of His salvation know and seek more earnestly His face.

\n

'Twas He who taught me thus to pray, \n and he, I trust, has answered prayer;
\n But it has been in such a way as almost drove me to despair.

\n

I thought that in some favored hour \n at once He'd answer my request
\n and by His love's constraining power, subdue my sins and give me rest.

\n

Instead of that, He made me feel \n the hidden evils of my heart,
\n and bade the angry powers of hell assault my soul in every part.

\n

Nay more, with His own hand he seemed \n intent to aggravate my woe,
\n Crossed all the fair designs I schemed, blasted my gourds, and laid me low. \n

\n

\"Lord, why is this?\" I \n trembling cried. \"Wilt Thou pursue this worm to death?\"
\n \"This is the way,\" the Lord replied, \"I answer prayer for \n grace and faith.

\n

\"These inward trials I employ \n from self and sin to set thee free,
\n And cross thy schemes of earthly joy that thou might'st find thy all in \n Me.\" Footnote 3
