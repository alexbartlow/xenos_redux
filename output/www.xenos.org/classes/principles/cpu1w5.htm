Introduction:

\n
\n

Several questions arise in \n connection with the doctrine of substitutionary atonement: \n

    \n
  • Once I've made a decision for Christ, \n can I lose my salvation?
    • \n
  • Do I receive my salvation via a \n process—or all at once?
    • \n
  • Is salvation earned (or kept) by \n good works or observing rituals?
    • \n
  • Can I forfeit my salvation through \n sin?
    • \n
  • Could someone say they have received \n Christ and not grow spiritually?
    • \n
  • What about people who claim they \n received Christ, but no longer believe in Christ?
    • \n
  • How should I respond to those who \n reject eternal security?

The Biblical Basis of Eternal \n Security

The \n following biblical terms describe in more practical and concrete ways the \n new standing we have received through Christ's substitutionary atonement. \n We also call this our position in Christ.

\n \n

Passages which emphasize \n eternal security:

\n \n
\n

(1 Pet. 1:3-5) \n Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who according to His \n great mercy has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection \n of Jesus Christ from the dead, (4) to obtain an inheritance which is imperishable \n and undefiled and will not fade away, reserved in heaven for you, (5) who \n are protected by the power of God through faith for a salvation ready to be \n revealed in the last time.

\n
\n

This passage stresses that \n our glorification is a done deal (\"imperishable;\" \"undefiled;\" \n \"will not fade away;\" \"reserved in heaven for you.\"). \n In the meantime, Christians are \"protected by the power of God.\" \n

\n
\n

(Rom. 8:1*,31-39) \n There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus . \n . . (31) What then shall we say to these things? If God is for us, who is \n against us? (32) He who did not spare His own Son, but delivered Him up for \n us all, how will He not also with Him freely give us all things? (33) Who \n will bring a charge against God's elect? God is the one who justifies; (34) \n who is the one who condemns? Christ Jesus is He who died, yes, rather who \n was raised, who is at the right hand of God, who also intercedes for us. (35) \n Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall tribulation, or distress, \n or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or peril, or sword? (36) Just as \n it is written, \"FOR THY SAKE WE ARE BEING PUT TO DEATH ALL DAY LONG; \n WE WERE CONSIDERED AS SHEEP TO BE SLAUGHTERED.\" (37) But in all these \n things we overwhelmingly conquer through Him who loved us. (38) For I am convinced \n that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor things present, \n nor things to come, nor powers, (39) nor height, nor depth, nor any other \n created thing, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is \n in Christ Jesus our Lord.

\n
\n

Vs 1 stresses no condemnation from God period. Note KJV and NKJV textual error: \" . . . who walk not after the flesh but after the Spirit.\" Fifty-four scholars of the original languages translated the KJV from 1605-1611. Unfortunately, the earliest Greek manuscripts they had access to were from about 1000 A.D. and all contained the phrase translated, \"who walk not after the flesh, but after the spirit.\" When earlier manuscripts became available, translators came to the conclusion that the phrase had been inserted, and likely from Romans 8:4. Therefore most modern translations omit the phrase.
\n The New King James and the King James 2000 continue to include this phrase even though it lacks manuscript attestation.
\n
\n (Bruce Metzger) \"The \n King James scholars in 1611 had to use printed editions of the Greek NT \n that rested upon the late Greek manuscripts, none of which was older than \n the 10th or 11th century. Translators today use editions of the Greek New \n Testament that rest upon manuscript evidence from the third and fourth centuries.\" \n (Metzger, Bruce, The New Testament: Its Background, Growth, and Content, \n [2nd ed., Nashville, Tn: Abingdon Press, 1983], p. 286)
\n
\n What difference would it make? This makes sanctification a condition for \n justification (Catholic theology). CONTEXT: Why would Paul praise God (7:25) \n as a Christian sinner, if God's acceptance is conditional to walking by \n the Spirit?

\n

Vs 31-34 stresses that God \n will never bring a charge against or condemn believers because Christ justifies \n us and intercedes for us. Explain Christ's intercessory ministry: \n he applies his finished work as often as we sin (Heb. 7:25). Vs 38,39 \n are a comprehensive statement—no one and nothing (inside or outside \n the Christian) can separate us from the love of God.

\n
\n

(Jn. 10:27-30**) \n My sheep hear My voice, and I know them, and they follow Me; (28) and I give \n eternal life to them, and they shall never perish; and no one shall snatch \n them out of My hand. (29) My Father, who has given them to Me, is greater \n than all; and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand. (30) \n I and the Father are one.

\n
\n

(Millard Erickson) \"Verse \n 28 is especially emphatic . . . In the clause `and they shall never perish,' \n John uses the double negative \"ou mh\" with the aorist subjunctive, \n which is a very emphatic way of declaring that something will not happen \n in the future. Jesus is categorically excluding the slightest chance of \n an apostasy by his sheep. A literal translation would be something like \n `They shall not, repeat, shall not, ever perish in the slightest.' . . . \n All in all, this passage is as definite a rejection of the idea that the \n true believer can fall away as could be given.\" (Millard Erickson, \n Christian Theology [Grand Rapids: Baker Book House, 1987], p. 1010) \n

\n
\n

(Eph. 1:13) \n In Him, you also, after listening to the message of truth, the gospel \n of your salvation— having also believed, you were sealed in Him with \n the Holy Spirit of promise, who is given as a pledge of our inheritance... \n

\n

(Eph. 4:30*) And do \n not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, by whom you were sealed for the day of \n redemption.

\n
\n

In both of these passages, Paul speaks \n of the Christian as having been \"sealed.\" When \n the Roman emperor stamped the wax seal of an official document with his \n signet ring, this seal communicated that the power Rome stood behind safe \n passage of the document to its destination. In the same way, God \n has given believers the Holy Spirit as his \"seal\" which guarantees \n our safe passage to heaven (1:14 \" . . . inheritance, with \n a view to the redemption of God's own possession;\" 4:30 \" . . \n . by whom you were sealed for the day of redemption.\") Although we \n can grieve/displease God by our poor behavior, this does not change our \n status with him.

\n

\"Pledge\" is a similar idea. \n It means \"down payment.\" When we pledge that we will pay the full \n purchase of a house, we put down a percentage of the price in cash. God \n has given us his Holy Spirit as his \"down payment\" that he will \n complete our salvation (see also Rom. 8:23 - \"first fruits \n of the Spirit\").

\n

In Eph. 1:13, the Greek emphasizes \n that our sealing in Christ occurs immediately upon believing the gospel \n (\" . . . having believed, you were at that time \n sealed . . . \").

\n
\n \n

(1 Thess 5:23,24) Now may the God of peace Himself sanctify you entirely; \n and may your spirit and soul and body be preserved complete, without blame \n at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. (24) Faithful is He who calls you, \n and He also will bring it to pass.

\n \n

(Phil. 1:6) \n For I am confident of this very thing, that He who began a good work in you \n will perfect it until the day of Christ Jesus.

\n
\n

The work of salvation, which \n God has begun with our justification, he will finish at the day of \n Christ (his return).

\n
\n

Additional scriptural supports \n for eternal security:

\n
\n

(Rom. 8:29) \n For whom He foreknew, He also predestined to become conformed to \n the image of His Son, that He might be the first-born among many brethren \n . . .

\n

(Eph. 1:4,5) . . . just as \n He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world, that we should be \n holy and blameless before Him. (5) In love He predestined us to adoption \n as sons through Jesus Christ to Himself, according to the kind intention \n of His will .

\n

Predestination means \"set \n the destiny of.\" These passages do not teach that God chooses who will \n believe in Christ. But they do strongly affirm that God sets the ultimate \n destiny of the believer. You have free will to choose whether you \n will step back from the rim of the Grand Canyon or jump over it—but \n if you choose to jump, though you can do a lot of things on the way down, \n your destiny has been set—you will splatter all over the canyon floor. \n In the same way, we have free will to choose to receive or reject Christ. \n But once we choose to receive him, you can choose to grow or not grow, but \n God sets our ultimate destiny—we will be completely conformed to Christ \n and be glorified.

\n
\n

(1 Cor. 1:8) \n . . . who shall also confirm you to the end, blameless in the day of our Lord \n Jesus Christ.

\n
\n

With all of the moral failure the \n Corinthian church had (sexual immorality, drunkenness at communion, divisions, \n softness on Jesus' resurrection, etc.), Paul could not have made this affirmation \n unless he believed in eternal security.

\n

(Millard Erickson) \"If \n salvation could be lost, there would have to be some reversal of regeneration. \n But can this be? Can spiritual death actually come to someone in whom the \n Holy Spirit dwells, that is, to someone who has already been given eternal \n life?\" (Millard Erickson, Christian Theology, p. 988)

\n

\n\n

Personal \n importance of eternal security

\n \n
\n
    \n \n
  • We cannot relate to God \n with confidence unless we know he accepts us as we are, with all of our \n sins. (Reflect on Romans 8:1- \"...there is therefore now no \n condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus.\")  Also, Christians \n who don't know their security in Christ remain superficial in their awareness \n of their sinfulness or defensive about it—they can't bear this light \n because their acceptance before God is still practically based on their \n righteousness. (GO BACK TO THE RIGHT-HAND COLUMN OF ATONEMENT WORDS CHART. \n These terms all elaborate on our security in Christ.)
    • \n
\n
\n

(Richard Lovelace) \"'I \n am accepted' - accepted as though my life displayed the spiritual perfection \n of the Messiah himself - ought to be the automatic response of \n our hearts whenever we wake, like the compass needle that always points \n north. This is a response which is always relevant to our current spiritual \n condition. We never make such progress in sanctification that we can depend \n on it for (God's) acceptance. And our continuing record of sin and failure \n never expands beyond the limits of the love of Christ, who has covered our \n debts for all time, past, present, and future . . . Most Protestants have \n therefore concluded that assurance of salvation is necessary for healthy \n spirituality. Christians need to know that they have a secure status as \n adopted sons and daughters of God in order to behave naturally in his presence. \n For us to be phasing in and out of sonship according to our behavior, constantly \n testing our experiences to make sure we are in a 'state of grace,' short-circuits \n the reality of grace.\" (Richard Lovelace, Renewal as a Way of Life, \n pp. 142,143.)

\n
\n
    \n
  • We cannot relate to others \n appropriately unless we prioritize God's acceptance of us. 
    • \n
\n
\n

God's acceptance and forgiveness \n of us is the dynamic that motivates us to accept and forgive others. Unless \n we are secure in Christ, we will tend to take our security from others' \n acceptance of us—which leads to a host of relational dysfunctions. \n

\n
\n
\n

Ministry application \n

\n
\n

Security in Christ affects...

\n

How we communicate the gospel \n to others.

\n
\n

We should be clear that genuine \n willingness to bow to Christ and humbly receive forgiveness is stressed, \n but just as clear that salvation is a free gift, and that you \n may come as you are without any actual ethical change preceding your decision. \n

\n
\n

How we assure new Christians. \n

\n
\n

We should stress what the \n Bible says about their new standing in Christ (position) as foundational \n because this is objective and unchanging. Experiential \n changes (conviction of sin, Bible coming alive, desire to share your faith, \n recognition of kinship with other Christians, prayer becomes personal, etc.) \n are important and should be pointed out by us because new Christians often \n do not see them—but these should be subordinate because they are subjective \n and fluctuating.

\n
\n

How we discern the validity \n of professions of faith.

\n
\n

If there is no subjective evidence \n at all (see above), or if he seems confused about the gospel, we may want \n to consider explaining the gospel to him again.

\n

But if the person is able to articulate \n why they are saved, we should accept this at face value and urge them to \n walk with Christ and grow spiritually. This was Paul's approach with carnal \n Christians like the Corinthians.

\n

If, on the other hand, the person \n once professed faith in Christ but now clearly denies him, we should take \n him at his word and try to lead him to Christ—regardless of your view \n of eternal security.

\n
\n

Note: Paul's opposition \n to the Judaizers is a warning of the tendency to erode our security based \n on the finished work of Christ. His response to the Judaizers is an example \n of how to deal with this tendency. He has no give on this issue, and is willing \n to fight over it (Gal. 1:6-9; 5:14; Phil. 3:1-3). We must be aware of contemporary \n Judaizers and know how to refute them if we want to have healthy Christian \n lives and help others have the same. Besides the obvious works-righteousness \n cults (Jehovah's Witnesses, Mormons, etc.), there are four objectionable views \n we will examine.

\n
\n
\n

Four Biblically Objectionable Views

\n
\n

1: \n Radical Arminianism

\n
Arminianism is named for Jacob \n Arminius (1560-1609), a Dutch theologian who was a strong proponent of man's \n freewill. We'll talk more about Arminius and his views next week. Radical Arminian \n groups (some Nazarenes; Church of Christ; etc.) teach that true Christians can \n lose God's acceptance by falling into sin (drunkenness; sexual immorality; etc.). \n They usually use the following verses to support this belief. \n

Before we plunge into this, \n we should note that there are two different versions of Non-Eternal Security: \n

\n
\n
    \n
  • Rejecting Christ (Heb. \n 6,10; 2 Tim. 2:12) - Acceptance is based solely on the finished work of \n Christ. Just as we choose freely to receive this gift, we may choose freely \n to reject it. This view makes better sense of the passages cited (since \n they emphasize denying Christ rather than committing sins), and does not \n violate salvation by grace through faith alone. Properly understood, it \n doesn't really threaten the Christian's security since they don't intend \n to reject Christ.
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Losing Salvation By Moral \n Lapses or Failure To Mature (Radical Arminianism)- \n \"Backsliding\" here means reverting to sinful habits and thereby \n forfeiting God's acceptance. This is heresy, because if we can lose God's \n acceptance by our sins, then we must be maintaining God's acceptance by \n our good works.
    • \n
\n
\n

\"Wesleyanism . . . finds \n the doctrine of sanctification not only biblical and practical but also \n essential to salvation . . . The responsibility for continued obedience \n is binding on the Christian. The gospel requires much more than the 'acceptance' \n of Christ as a Personal Savior . . . The whole Bible, and particularly \n the New Testament, gives repeated counsel and command to the believer \n to take very positive steps toward moral recovery. These requirements \n are absolute. They are things the believer must do . . . Eternal destiny, \n not merely rewards, is at stake. God's forgiveness does not exhaust the \n obligations of the gospel; it begins the life of obligation.\" (Mildred \n Bangs Wynkoop, Foundations of Wesleyan-Arminian Theology [Kansas \n City: Beacon Hill Press, 1967], pp. 122,124.)

\n
\n
\n

Note: \n We admit that the plain sense of some of these passages seems to indicate \n you can lose your salvation. But we must harmonize the Bible because God is \n its author. It is easier to harmonize these verses with the eternal security \n passages than it is to try to harmonize the eternal security passages with \n these verses.

\n

(Heb. 6:4-6) \n For in the case of those who have once been enlightened and have tasted of \n the heavenly gift and have been made partakers of the Holy Spirit, (5) and \n have tasted the good word of God and the powers of the age to come, (6) and \n then have fallen away, it is impossible to renew them again to repentance, \n since they again crucify to themselves the Son of God, and put Him to open \n shame.

\n
\n

These are not Christians: \n \n

    \n
  • All of the phrases in vs. 4,5 \n stop short of definitely describing a regenerate person. Even \"partakers \n of the Holy Spirit\" can refer to people who \"shared in\" \n (metecho) the Holy Spirit by witnessing his work through real Christians \n when they were in fellowship.
    • \n
  • Note also that these people probably \n have blasphemed the Holy Spirit (vs 6: \"it is impossible for them \n to be renewed to repentance . . . \"). Since they have received the \n full light that God has to give them and have rejected it, there isn't \n any further possibility of salvation for them.
    • \n
  • OR \"since\" in vs 6 may \n also be translated \"while\"—in which case the author is \n simply saying they can't be accepted by God as long as \n they reject Jesus as their Savior.
    • \n
\n

The phrase \"fallen away\" \n does not refer to a sinful lifestyle, but to returning to the temple worship.

\n

That these are Christians \n who have chosen to reject salvation in Christ.

\n
\n \n

(Heb. 10:26-31) For if we go on sinning willfully after receiving \n the knowledge of the truth, there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins, \n (27) but a certain terrifying expectation of judgment, and THE FURY OF A FIRE \n WHICH WILL CONSUME THE ADVERSARIES. (28) Anyone who has set aside the Law \n of Moses dies without mercy on the testimony of two or three witnesses. (29) \n How much severer punishment do you think he will deserve who has trampled \n under foot the Son of God, and has regarded as unclean the blood of the covenant \n by which he was sanctified, and has insulted the Spirit of grace? (30) For \n we know Him who said, \"VENGEANCE IS MINE, I WILL REPAY.\" And again, \n \"THE LORD WILL JUDGE HIS PEOPLE.\" (31) It is a terrifying thing \n to fall into the hands of the living God.

\n \n
\n

The key here is to interpret \"sinning \n willfully\" in light of the following context. Vs 29 defines it as rejecting \n Jesus as the Messiah, denying that his death had any atoning value, and \n insulting the Spirit's conviction about Jesus—not simply any \n sin we might purposefully commit.

\n

Vs 29 says Jesus' death \"sanctified\" \n these people. If the author uses \"sanctified\" (10:14) as a synonym \n for justification, then weren't these people saved? It may mean that his \n death sets all people apart in a more general sense (as in 9:13; 13:12 i.e. \n unlimited atonement).

\n

Some Christians interpret this passage \n as a Christian (26- \"receiving the knowledge of the truth...\"  \n vs 29- \"he was sanctified...\").

\n
\n

(Gal. 5:4) \n You have been severed from Christ, you who are seeking to be justified by \n law; you have fallen from grace.

\n
\n

The \"you\" here seems to \n refer to the Galatian Christians rather than the Judaizers (see 5:7-10). \n He is trying to get them to see that accepting circumcision is accepting \n justification by works, which is antithetical to why they trusted in Christ \n (to be justified by grace through faith). If they begin to operate with \n this mentality, they will pull the plug on God's sanctifying power (vs 2: \n \" . . . Christ will be of no benefit to you . . . \"). \n
\n Gal. 5:1 It was for freedom \n that Christ set us free; therefore keep standing firm and do not be subject \n again to a yoke of slavery. 2 Behold I, Paul, say to you that if you receive \n circumcision, Christ will be of no benefit to you.
\n
\n Paul argues in a similar fashion in Gal. 3:3 and Eph. 4:17-21.

\n
\n

(Col. 1:21-23a) \n And although you were formerly alienated and hostile in mind, engaged in evil \n deeds, (22) yet He has now reconciled you in His fleshly body through death, \n in order to present you before Him holy and blameless and beyond reproach—(23) \n if indeed you continue in the faith firmly established and steadfast, and \n not moved away from the hope of the gospel that you have heard . . .

\n
\n

Paul may be using the terms \"holy \n and blameless and beyond reproach\" to refer to spiritual maturity (see \n Phil. 1:10; 2:15; 1 Tim. 3:10; Titus 2:8 for such usage). If this is the \n case, he is simply saying that God has reconciled (justified) us through \n Christ in order to present us to himself as spiritually mature people, and \n that this will happen as long as we continue in the faith.

\n

Also, remember Paul's reason for \n writing this letter. Gnostic false teachers have apparently eroded the Colossians \n confidence that they are saved, and Paul is seeking to restore that confidence \n (cf. 2:10-15). It is therefore a misuse of this passage to say he is warning \n them that they are not secure in Christ.

\n
\n

2 Tim 2:12 If \n we deny Him, He also will deny us . . .

\n
\n

Note: The issue \n is rejecting Christ, not committing certain sins.

\n

Paul is quoting a \"theological \n confession\" that may be using \"we\" in the widest sense of \n the word—all different kinds of responses to Christ.

\n
\n

\"If we died with him, we shall \n also live with him.\"
\n This could refer to anyone who receives Christ.

\n

\"If we endure, we shall also \n reign with him.\"
\n This probably refers to the walking Christian.

\n

\"If we deny him, he also will \n deny us.\"
\n This could refer to people who had apparently received, but then later \n denied him, or simply to people who never respond to the gospel.

\n

\"If we are faithless, he remains \n faithful . . .\"
\n This probably refers to a common Christian struggle with being consistently \n faithful.

\n
\n
\n

2: Roman Catholic Soteriology \n

\n

According to Catholic theology, \n our salvation is not secure. Why is this?

\n

The Roman Catholic church relies \n on two sources of authority for doctrine: the Bible and church tradition which \n includes papal teaching and edicts of Roman Catholic church councils.

\n
\n

(Millard Erickson) \n \"Instead, it posits two equal authorities, the Bible, and the unwritten \n tradition of the apostles, preserved, interpreted, and made explicit by \n the church.\" (Millard Erickson, Christian Theology , p. 1010) \n

\n

This second source of authority is \n found in papal teaching and the Roman Catholic church councils.  Since \n scripture is viewed through church tradition, the tradition ends up being \n the final authority in matters of doctrine.

\n

\"Sacred tradition and \n sacred Scripture form one sacred deposit of the Word of God committed to \n the Church . . . the task of authentically interpreting the Word of God, \n whether written or handed on, has been entrusted exclusively to the living \n teaching office of the Church, whose authority is exercised in the name \n of Jesus Christ . . . It is clear, therefore,that sacred tradition, sacred \n Scripture and the teaching authority of the Church, are in accord with God's \n most wise design, are so linked and joined together that one cannot stand \n without the others, and that all together and each in its own way under \n the action of the one Holy Spirit contribute effectively to the salvation \n of souls.\" (Second Vatican Council, \"Dogmatic Constitution on \n Divine Revelation,\" no. 10)

\n
    \n
  • Sola Scriptura: Over \n against this, the Reformers affirmed sola scriptura (the Bible \n alone is the authority for doctrine).
    • \n
\n
\n

Catholic canon law teaches \n that saving grace, while purchased only by Christ's work on the cross, is \n conferred in an ongoing way through the sacraments which must be officiated \n by the ordained priesthood.

\n
\n

(CCC) \"The \n Church affirms that for believers, the sacraments of the New Covenant are \n necessary for salvation.\" (Catechism of the Catholic Church \n , p. 292)

\n

(CCC) \"The \n sacraments are efficacious signs of grace, instituted by Christ and entrusted \n to the Church, by which divine grace is dispensed to us.\" (CCC, \n p. 293)

\n

The key sacraments that confer saving \n grace are baptism (through which original sin and \n all personal sins are forgiven - CCC, pp. 324,325), confirmation \n (through which one receives the Holy Spirit - CCC, p. 333), confession \n & penance (through which mortal sins are confessed to \n and absolved by the priest through prescribed prayers, etc. - CCC, \n p. 373), and the Eucharist (which is \"offered \n in reparation for the sins of the living and the dead\" (CCC, \n p. 356).

\n
\n

In Catholicism, therefore, \n one can never be sure of God's full and final acceptance in this life.

\n
\n

(Cardinal O'Connor) \"Church \n teaching is that I don't know, at any given moment, what my eternal future \n will be. I can hope, pray, do my very best—but I still don't know. \n Pope John Paul II doesn't absolutely know that he will go to heaven, nor \n did Mother Theresa of Calcutta . . .\" (Cardinal O'Connor, The New \n York Times, February 1, 1990, p. B4.)

\n
\n
    \n
  • Sola Fide: Over \n against this, the Reformers affirmed sola fide: salvation is a free \n and permanent gift received directly from God by faith alone when an individual \n comes to Christ.
    • \n
\n

The Catholic system is wrong \n in three important areas. \n

    \n
  • First, it holds that \n forgiveness is actually conveyed through the rituals (sacramentalism), \n while the Bible insists that personal faith alone secures saving \n grace. The Old Covenant system never dispensed true forgiveness; \n it was only an incomplete picture of the once-and-for-all forgiveness bought \n by Christ which we can receive once-for-all (Heb. 10:10-14).
    • \n
  • Second, it holds to a \n special caste of priests. In the New Covenant, all true Christians \n are priests in the full sense of the word because we all have direct access \n to God through Christ (Heb. 10:19-22) and because we all can communicate \n God's grace to others through the Word (1 Pet. 2:9).   This doctrine \n is referred to as the \"priesthood of all believers.\" Christ is \n our only mediator (1 Tim. 2:5)
    • \n
  • Third, it reverts to \n the ritualism of the Old Covenant, which Heb. 9 insists has \n been fulfilled and made obsolete by the finished work of Christ. This highly \n ritualistic way of relating to God, which was appropriate in the Old Covenant \n because of its instructional value, has been replaced by the centrality \n of a personal relationship with God through the indwelling Holy Spirit (Gal. \n 4:3-11). Ritual in the New Covenant has a much smaller role because of this \n (from hundreds of prescribed rituals to two; from strictly regulated rituals \n to loosely regulated).
    • \n
\n

Important qualification: We \n are not saying that there are no saved Catholics! There are, in fact, many \n Catholics know the Lord and serve him. We are simply saying that they are \n saved and serving in spite of Catholic theology, not because \n of it.

\n

Practical Advice: \n Don't go out and attack your Catholic family and friends! Many Catholics don't \n even know what their church teaches. Instead, emphasize the biblical teaching \n on salvation as a free gift and your personal testimony. Many get saved from \n this, and then begin to see for themselves that their old church was off in \n its teaching . . .

\n
\n

3: Water Baptism

\n
\n

Many people and denominations teach \n that water baptism is necessary for salvation. Passages such as Acts 2:38, \n Mark 16:16, 1 Peter 3:21, Romans 6:3,4 and Col. 2:12 seem to teach this position. \n However, on closer examination, this teaching is not scriptural for the following \n reasons:

\n
\n
    \n
  • The biblical teaching on salvation \n by grace through faith alone is very clear (John 1:12; Eph 2:8,9; \n Acts 16:30,31; Romans 3:24; Titus 3:5).
    \n
    • \n

    Gal. 2:16 is especially \n significant in light of its historical setting. Paul is refuting the Judaizers' \n insistence that faith in Christ is not sufficient for salvation, but that \n circumcision is also required. These are parallel teachings in that both require \n submission to a biblical ritual for salvation.

    \n
  • Passages used to support this \n teaching can be harmonized to the \"salvation by faith alone\" position \n described above.
    \n
    • \n

    Acts 2:38 gives two \n imperatives (\"repent\" and \"be baptized\"), followed by \n two results (\"forgiveness of sins\" and \"you shall receive the \n gift of the Holy Spirit\"). Note that in Peter's next speech (Acts 3:19), \n he gives one imperative (\"repent and return\") and promises the same \n two results (\"sins wiped away\" and \"times of refreshing from \n God's presence\"). If water baptism is required for forgiveness and the \n Holy Spirit, why did Peter omit this imperative? Therefore, it is best to \n understand the promises of Acts 2:38 as connected to the command to \"repent\" \n alone. Peter also announced that it is God's will for believers to be water \n baptized, but he did not mean that baptism was necessary for salvation.

    \n

    Mark 16:16 is part \n of the \"long ending\" of Mark (vs 9-20) that was not part of the \n original text. For more information on this textual issue, see Mark Lane, \n The Gospel According to Mark (Grand Rapids: Eerdmans Publishing Co., \n 1974), pp. 601-605.

    \n
\n
\n

In 1 Pet. 3:21, \n Peter says, \". . . corresponding to that, baptism now \n saves you.\" The \"that\" refers to how God rescued Noah and his \n family from the flood (vs. 20). \"Corresponding\" is antitupon, \n or \"anti-type.\" Peter thus identifies the ark rescue as a prophetic \n type of our rescue through Christ. Noah and his family were NOT saved by being \n water baptized, but by being put into (baptized into) the ark. In the \n same way, believers in Christ are rescued from God's judgment by being put \n into (baptized into) Christ. Therefore, the \"baptism\" in vs. 21 \n refers to the baptism by the Holy Spirit into Christ (see 1 Cor. 12:13), \n not water baptism. Peter seems intent on making this point by adding \". . . \n not the removal of dirt from the flesh . . .\"

\n

Rom. 6:3,4 and Col. 2:12 \n both refer to Spirit-baptism into Christ rather than to water baptism, since \n Paul says clearly in 1 Cor. 12:13 that Spirit-baptism is the baptism \n that unites us with Christ.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Consider also these additional \n points:
    • \n
\n
\n

In Acts 10:47, \n Cornelius and his household receive the Holy Spirit immediately when they \n believe the gospel. Peter insists that they be allowed to undergo water baptism \n because they have receive the Spirit. Since receiving the Holy Spirit is synonymous \n with salvation and necessary in order to be a Christian (Rom. 8:9), water \n baptism clearly had no bearing on their salvation. If this was the case with \n Cornelius and his household, why would God change something this important?

\n

In 1 Cor. 1:17, \n Paul says, \"Christ did not send me to baptize, but to preach the gospel.\" \n How could Paul say this if water baptism was necessary for salvation? Paul \n believed Christians should be baptized, but he obviously saw baptism as so \n much less important than explaining the gospel that he could pit them against \n each other in this verse. Why? Because the gospel saves when people believe \n it (Rom. 1:16), while water baptism does not save.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Is water baptism required \n or not?
    • \n
\n
\n

Yes and No. Clearly Jesus commanded \n us to be baptized and to baptize (Matt. 28:19). In this sense, baptism \n is required. We should be baptized after receiving Christ because Jesus has \n told us to proclaim our faith in him in this way. But, as we have seen above, \n baptism is not required for salvation. Like many other commands in scripture, \n we should obey them out of gratitude for our salvation, not as requirements \n for our salvation.

\n
\n

4: Lordship Theology

\n
\n

Lordship theologians affirm \n eternal security, but they define saving faith in a way that requires complete \n commitment to Christ and denies the possibility of carnal Christians. \n This has the same practical effect as the above \"losing salvation through \n moral lapses\" view: unless you live a righteous life, you have no basis \n for thinking you are saved.

\n

(John F. MacArthur) \" \n . . . eternal life is indeed a free gift. . .that does not mean there is no \n cost in terms of salvation's impact on the sinner's life. This paradox may \n be difficult but it is nevertheless true: salvation is both free and costly \n . . . It (saving faith) is an exchange of all that we are for all that Christ \n is. And it denotes implicit obedience, full surrender to the lordship of Christ. \n Nothing less can qualify as saving faith . . . This is the kind of totally \n committed response the Lord Jesus called for. A desire to follow him at any \n cost. Absolute surrender. A full exchange of self for the Savior. It is the \n only response that will open the gates of the kingdom.\" (John F. MacArthur, \n The Gospel According To Jesus (Grand Rapids: Zondervan, 1988), pp. 140, \n 141)

\n
\n

This is double-talk. If salvation \n is free, it costs us nothing; if it is costly to us, then it is not free.

\n
\n

(John F. MacArthur) \n \"(Saving faith) retains no privileges. It clings to no cherished sins, \n no treasured possessions, no secret self-indulgences. It is unconditional \n surrender, a willingness to do anything the Lord demands.\" (John F. MacArthur, \n The Gospel According To Jesus (Grand Rapids: Zondervan, 1988), pp. 139, \n 140)

\n
\n

This is absolutely ridiculous. He \n is making the pinnacle of spiritual maturity the minimum requirement for \n salvation. Accordingly, we must attain to a perfect attitude of submission \n before we can be forgiven.

\n

Why do the epistles urge Christians \n to commit themselves more fully to Christ (Rom. 12:1; 1 Pet. 3:15) if saving \n faith is full commitment? Why do the epistles acknowledge that there are \n carnal Christians (1 Cor. 3:3,15), and why do they urge Christians \n not to submit to sin (Rom. 6:12,13)?

\n

To demand from a person a \n \"complete change of heart, attitude, interest, and direction\" \n is to make regenerate behavior from an unregenerate person the requirement \n for regeneration!

\n

(D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones) \n \"It comes to this, that unless my life is a righteous life, I must \n be very careful before I claim that I am covered by the grace of God in \n Jesus Christ . . . Some of the most vital questions \n that I am asked, then, are these. Do you know God? Do you love God?  \n Can you say honestly that the first and most important thing in your life \n is to glorify Him and that you so much want to do this that you do not care \n what it may cost you in any sense? . . . Let every man \n examine himself.\" (D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones, Studies in the Sermon \n on the Mount, Vol. 1 (Grand Rapids: Eerdman's Publishing Co., 1977), \n pp. 208,209.)

\n

Note: We must also \n affirm that saving faith involves sincere humility, bowing to God (Jn. 7:17). \n We agree that mental-assent faith only is not saving faith—there must \n also be personal trust and commitment to Christ. We also affirm that full \n commitment to Christ is our goal in the Christian life—but it is not \n the definition of saving faith!
