Overview

\n\n\n

Introduction

\n
\n

God's will for our lives is fruitfulness \n (John 15:16)—to become ever more effective in impacting others \n with the life, love and truth of Christ (God's will is not just my \n own healing, happiness, etc.). But growing Christians soon discover there \n is a barrier preventing us from fruitful service and sabotaging our efforts.

\n \n
\n

(Watchman Nee)   \n \"Anyone who serves God will discover sooner or later that the great \n hindrance to his work is not others but himself . . . \"Footnote1B

\n \n

(Watchman Nee) \"If \n you have a very clumsy servant and he just sits still and does nothing, \n then his clumsiness does not appear. If he does nothing all day he will \n be of little use to you, it is true, but at least he will do no damage that \n way. But if you say to him: \"Now come along, don't idle away your time: \n get up and do something,\" then immediately the trouble begins. He knocks \n the chair over as he gets up, stumbles over a footstool a few paces further \n on, then smashes some precious dish as soon as he handles it. If you make \n no demands upon him his clumsiness is never noticed, but as soon as you \n ask him to do anything his awkwardness is apparent at once. The demands \n were all right, but the man was all wrong. He was as clumsy a man when he \n was sitting still as when he was working, but it was your demands that made \n manifest the clumsiness which, whether he was active or inactive, all the \n time were in his make-up.

\n
\n
\n
    \n
      \n

      We are all sinners by nature. If \n God asks nothing of us, all seems to go well, but as soon as he demands \n something of us, the occasion is provided for a grand display of our sinfulness. \n The Law makes our weakness manifest. While you let me sit still I appear \n to be all right, but when you ask me to do anything I am sure to spoil it, \n and if you trust me with a second thing I will as surely spoil that also. \n When a holy law is applied to a sinful, man, then it is that his sinfulness \n comes out in full display.

      \n

      God knows who I am: he knows that \n from head to foot I am full of sin; he knows that I am weakness incarnate; \n that I can do nothing. The trouble is that I do not know it [last week's \n topic: our new identity]. I admit that all men \n are sinners, and that therefore I am a sinner; but I imagine that I am not \n such a hopeless sinner as some. God must bring us all to the place where \n we see that we are utterly weak and helpless. While we say so, we do not \n wholly believe it, and God has to do something to convince us of the fact.\"Footnote2B

      \n
    \n
\n
\n
\n

Therefore, if we want to be deeply \n fruitful, we're going to need to develop an appreciation for just how deeply \n sinful we really are. This can be a very sobering week of study.

\n
\n

Tonight's goals:

\n \n
\n

To gain an in-depth biblical understanding \n of fallen human nature.

\n

To become more aware of how your \n own sin-nature operates.

\n
\n\n

The biblical view of sin includes attitudes, motives, and subtle behavioral \n habits as well as isolated acts of disobedience.

\n
\n

See Mark 7:21-23; Gal. 5:19-21; Eph. \n 4:26-5:4; Col. 3:5. Note how Jesus and Paul mix the internal attitudes in \n with the external actions. The emphasis is on the internal change.

\n
\n

(Mark. 7:21,22) For from within, out of the heart \n of men, proceed the evil thoughts, fornications, thefts, murders, adulteries, \n (22) deeds of coveting and wickedness, as well as deceit, sensuality, \n envy, slander, pride and foolishness. 23 \"All these \n evil things proceed from within and defile the man.\"

\n
\n
\n \n
    \n

    Antitheses:

    \n
    \n

    (Richard Lovelace) \"Today's \n non-Christians, encouraged by popular psychology which accentuate positive \n thinking and self-acceptance, think of themselves as fundamentally good \n people trying to do the right thing, but missing the mark occasionally \n . . . Many Christians also are inclined to think that sin is a rare exception \n in their lives. They define it as occasional acts of willful disobedience \n to known laws of God, such as the Ten Commandments. In this definition, \n sin is reduced to conscious rebellious actions—major transgression \n with serious consequences . . . it is obvious that this view screens out \n most of the serious sin in our lives. In concentrating on actions, we \n uncover only the tip of the iceberg. Overlooked are our sinful attitudes, \n the continuing resistance to God's will which give rise to actions. Biblical \n definitions of sin give much more prominence to attitudes or continuous \n patterns of behavior than to isolated acts.\"Footnote3B \n

    \n
    \n
\n \n

Healthy, growing Christians therefore \n experience a strange paradox:

\n
\n
    \n
  • On the one hand, we (and others) \n gradually see a definite improvement in our moral lives in many areas. \n
    • \n
  • But on the other hand, we realize \n how much more deeply sinful we are than we initially realized.
    • \n
\n

As we grow, God keeps peeling off \n layer after layer, revealing new areas and levels of sin. So, although we \n are making progress, we often feel like we're losing ground!

\n \n

(Gal. 5:17*) For the flesh sets its desire against \n the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh; for these are in opposition \n to one another, so that you may not do the things that you please.

\n
\n

As disturbing as this is, it is \n not a sign of spiritual sickness, but of spiritual health. It is normal \n and healthy to experience this internal conflict between God's Spirit \n and your sinful nature (Gal. 5:17*). What does it mean if we can't identify \n any struggle with our sinful nature lately?

C.S. Lewis

\n
\n

(C.S. Lewis)  \"Burrowing \n into his past, he was appalled at what he found…'I have found out \n ludicrous and terrible things about my own character. Sitting by, watching \n the rising thoughts to break their necks as they pop up, one learns to \n know the sort of thoughts that do come. And, will you believe it, one \n out of every three is a thought of self-admiration: when everything else \n fails, having had its neck broken, up comes the thought \"What an \n admirable fellow I am to have broken their necks!\" I catch myself \n posturing before the mirror, so to speak, all day long. I pretend I am \n carefully thinking out what to say to the next pupil (for his good, of \n course) and then suddenly realize I am really thinking how frightfully \n clever I'm going to be and how he will admire me…. when you force \n yourself to stop it, you admire yourself for doing that. It's like fighting \n the hydra…There seems to be no end to it. Depth under depth of self-love \n and self-admiration…Pride…is the mother of all sins, \n and the original sin of Lucifer.\" (Green and Hooper, C.S Lewis: \n A Biography. p. 105.)

\n
\n \n

How does this square with our culture's \n emphasis on loving yourself first and most?

\n
\n

E.G. (\"You can't love others \n unless you learn first how to love yourself.\"), positive self-esteem \n (\"I'm good enough, I'm smart enough—and gosh darn it, I'm worth \n it!\"), no negative \"self-talk\" allowed, victimization (\"I'm \n an angry person because my parents mistreated me\"; \"I'm a negative \n bitter person because my husband is mean to me.\"), and disease-model \n addiction (\"My obesity is not a sin; it is a sickness\"). These \n two views are not complementary—they are antithetical! This view \n is a replacement of God's grace.

\n \n

(Richard  Lovelace)    \n \"Christians whose spiritual lives are grounded and nurtured only \n on self-esteem and positive thinking, without a vision of the depth of \n sin, are going to be lacking in depth, reality and humility . . . If we \n cannot face the bad news about the depth of sin . . . we cannot fully \n grasp the good news of salvation and the transformed life in Christ . \n . . Christians who are sensitive to the depth of their own sin are a lot \n easier to be with, because they are not as inclined to be judgmental.\"Footnote5B \n (Richard F. Lovelace, \"Renewal as a Way of Life\", p.70).

\n \n

Note: Often persons who ascribe \n to the therapeutic view of life will show an inadequate appreciation of \n God's righteousness & grace. While it is true the Bible argues for \n human worth & dignity based on their nature as God's image bearers, \n this is never submitted as a basis for self-esteem in the Bible but as \n a basis for treating others with dignity (Gen.9:6; Jam.3:9).

Qualifications:

\n
\n

1. No matter how sinful you think \n you are…you are worse. 

\n \n

2. No matter how merciful you think \n God is…He is more merciful.

\n \n

If you miss the first point you will \n be superficial, fake, arrogant people. Without increasing awareness of your \n own sinfulness you will be superficial, self-righteous, self-sufficient \n and thus comparatively unfruitful.

\n \n

If you miss the second point and \n only get the first you will be fatalistic people. It is possible to become \n inappropriately sin focused:
\n      1. Forgetting your true identity;
\n      2. Forgetting God's mercy;
\n      3. Having perfectionistic expectations or impatience \n about 'getting over' a sin (Gal.5:16-18) - for your sin or those of others.

\n \n

Someone may seem fatalistic when \n they are actually manipulating others by running themselves down. This is \n often done to get affirmation or as a substitute for actual confession of \n particular sin.

Biblical Terminology & Definition \n of Sin Nature

\n
\n

Terminology: The Bible \n uses many terms for our sin nature.

\n
\n

\"Flesh\"

\n
\n

Gal. 5:17; (NIV translates \"sinful \n nature\")

\n \n

Semantic Range: Each word has a \n range of meaning, the specific meaning of which must be determined by \n its immediate context. Paul uses the same word (sarx) in different \n ways: physical body (Gal. 2:20), human accomplishment (Phil. 3:4)—and \n sinful nature.

\n

Col. 2:11 mentions the \"removal\" \n (apekdusis) of the flesh (NASB). This word can also have the meaning \n \"disarmed\"—and the verbal form of it is translated this \n way Col. 2:15.

\n
\n \n

\"Body of sin\"

\n
\n

(Rom. 6:6)  . . . knowing this, that our old \n self was crucified with Him, that our body of sin might be done away with, \n that we should no longer be slaves to sin . . . 

\n
\n

\"The body of the flesh\" \n - It is somehow connected to our physical bodies.

\n
\n

(Col. 2:11)  . . . and in Him you were also \n circumcised with a circumcision made without hands, in the removal of \n the body of the flesh by the circumcision of Christ . . . 

\n
\n \n

\"The sin which indwells me\" \n (ROM 7:7-25**) - It is deeply ingrained and internal.

\n
\n

(ROM 7:17,20) So now, no longer am I the one doing it, but sin which \n indwells me . . . (20) But if I am doing the very \n thing I do not wish, I am no longer the one doing it, but sin which dwells \n in me.

\n
\n \n

\"Heart\" - It is deeply \n deceptive; only God can truly expose it to us.

\n
\n

(Jer. 17:9) The heart is more deceitful than all else And is desperately \n sick; who can understand it? I, the LORD, search the heart . . . 

\n
\n

\"Outer man\" - (2 Cor. 4:7-18;  \n More on this later)

\n
\n

(2 Cor. 4:7,16) But we have \n this treasure in earthen vessels, that the surpassing greatness of the \n power may be of God and not from ourselves . . . Therefore \n we do not lose heart, but though our outer man is decaying, yet our inner \n man is being renewed day by day.

\n
\n
\n \n

Definition of Sin Nature: \n

\n
\n

The inherited and deeply ingrained \n drive to rule our own lives, rebel against God, protect and exalt ourselves, \n and meet our own needs our own way apart from God.

\n \n

Remember:  The \"old self\" \n (ROM 6:6) is not the same as sin nature (\"the body of sin\"). \n \"Old self\" is our old legal identity in Adam which is \"crucified\" \n (gone) for Christians.

\n \n

Remember: Our sin nature (\"the \n body of sin\"), however, is \"deposed, but not destroyed\" (Review \n ROM 6:6). Though no longer in authority over us, it still exists (until \n glorification) and is active and can enslave us if we submit to it (ROM \n 6:16).

\n
\n

Discovering our Sinful \n Nature

\n
\n

Since by definition the sinful nature \n is deceptive, let's consider two areas where we can (if honest) discover our \n sin nature: Temperaments and Close Relationships.

\n

Area 1: Temperaments \n

\n
\n

Definition:

\n
\n

Temperaments are a collection of \n personality traits within each of us that are exhibited as we respond \n to life's situations and relationships.  These are descriptions of \n traits that tend to cluster in a person.  

\n

People, though uniquely made, exhibit \n patterns of behavior, thought, and attitudes that can be generally (not \n rigidly or stereotypically) grouped together. Hippocrates in the 5th & \n 4th centuries BC identified these four temperaments: Sanguine, Choleric, \n Melancholy, Phlegmatic. It's worth noting that he observed these traits \n and patterns BEFORE attempting to determine how they were determined.  \n General trait theory is a legitimate area of psychological study.   \n Usually people exhibit a mixture of two or more temperaments, one usually \n being dominant.

\n \n
\n

(Gordon Allport) \"The characteristic \n phenomena of an individual's nature, including his susceptibility to \n emotional stimulation, his customary strength and speed of response, \n the quality of his prevailing mood, and all the peculiarities of fluctuation \n and intensity of mood, these being regarded as dependent on constitutional \n make-up, and therefore largely hereditary in origin.\"Footnote11B \n

\n \n

Myers-Briggs (thinking/ feeling, \n judging/ perceiving, intuition/ sensing, extrovert/ introvert) is a \n contemporary example of a personality trait theory which uses different \n terminology.

\n

See also O. Hallesby, \"Temperament \n and the Christian Faith\"

\n
\n \n

Warning: Don't use temperaments \n carelessly:

\n \n
    \n
  • Don't use them to unrighteously \n condemn others. Instead we should use them to help others. Temperaments \n are helpful in identifying a person's strengths to be developed, and \n in identifying and addressing their chronic (besetting) sins.
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Don't use them insensitively \n around others who are unfamiliar with the terms.  Good community \n often does have \"in-house\" terms that are helpful and efficient, \n but must be used sensitively.
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Don't use them with the view \n that some temperaments are superior/inferior to others.
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Don't throw out the use of temperaments \n just because \"it rubs you the wrong way.\"  Instead, work \n through your bias against them and learn how to use this in a godly \n way to help your own growth (so that you can serve others more lovingly), \n as well as others.
    • \n

Traitors \n

\n
\n
    \n
  • Appreciate temperamental diversity. \n In some areas, we are simply not as strong as someone more temperamentally \n suited (e.g., the melancholic will never be as effusive as the sanguine; \n the phlegmatic will never be as visionary as the choleric, etc.). Nevertheless \n we can make substantial progress in areas of temperamental weakness. We \n should not condemn ourselves in the non-strengths, but accept and embrace \n God-given diversity in one another—much like spiritual gifts. Don't \n envy other's temperamental strengths.
    \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Besetting sins often run along temperamental \n lines: specific areas of vulnerability to sin/ deeply ingrained sin habits. \n Since we are totally depraved, our temperaments have been corrupted by the \n fall therefore we find that many of our besetting sins run along temperamental \n lines: e.g., the melancholic's critical spirit; the choleric's aversion \n to apologize; the phlegmatic's laziness; the sanguine's lack of self-discipline. \n
    \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Don't use temperaments to rationalize \n your sin (ROM 6:6 says otherwise). \"That's just the way I am! That's \n just my personality.\" Our tendency is to rationalize these besetting \n sins as amoral, like our temperamental non-strengths (qualities that people \n with other temperaments have that I lack). \n However, when we hold them up to the light (God's love that we are called \n to express), we see plenty of need for God's sanctifying grace. These are \n the areas, as they come under God's discipline and grace, that revolutionize \n our relationships and ministry. \n
    \n

    Put yourself under grace—not \n to excuse but to acknowledge change in these areas will come gradually. \n

    \n
    \n

    A good sign of this is your \n ability to poke fun at yourself and allow others to as well, in these \n temperamental sins.  The power of sin is diffused when you and \n others know what it is, it's out on the table, and God's grace covers.

    \n
    \n

    Put others under grace—forbearance—because \n they also will only change gradually.

    \n
    \n
    • \n
\n

Area 2: Close \n Relationships

\n \n
\n

Marriage, roommates (promote ministry \n houses for singles), people we serve with—relationships bring our sinful \n nature to the surface (remember \"clumsy servant\")!! Deep and consistent \n involvement in the Body of Christ allows us to develop close relationships.  \n If we stay aloof from relationships and hold people at arms length, we will \n never get a good look at how sinful we are, and sadly, never a clear look \n at God's grace, the very thing we claim we want most.

\n \n

Key Principle:

\n
\n

The great temptation, of course, \n is to focus on how others fail to love us properly. God's way, however, \n is to have us look primarily at the \"log in our own eye\" (Matt. \n 7:3-5). If we do this, we will continue to grow regardless of how people \n treat us, and we will eventually have much better relationships!

\n \n
\n

(Matthew 7:3-5) And why do you look at the speck that is in your brother's \n eye, but do not notice the log that is in your own eye? (4) Or how can \n you say to your brother, 'Let me take the speck out of your eye,' and \n behold, the log is in your own eye? (5) You hypocrite, first take the \n log out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to take the speck \n out of your brother's eye.

\n
\n \n

When it comes to relationships, \n instead of trying to \"Find the right person,\" we need to try \n to \"Become the right person.\" Many singles need to think this \n through as all too often they make the mistake of focusing on finding \n the right person instead.

\n \n

Once we adopt this focus, we will \n begin to see certain areas of our sinful nature. Let's consider a few \n common examples of sin issues that come to light especially in close relationships. \n

\n
\n \n

Examples

\n
\n

A. Anger & Bitterness

\n \n
\n

(Eph. 4:26,31) Be angry, and yet do not sin; do not \n let the sun go down on your anger . . .(31) Let all bitterness \n and wrath and anger and clamor and slander be put away from you, along \n with all malice.

\n \n

Righteous vs. unrighteous anger

\n \n
\n
    \n
  • God's purpose has been violated \n vs. your selfish demands/ wanting to \"get your way\"
    • \n
  • Under control vs. \"puking\"
    • \n
  • For their good vs. to pay \n them back
    • \n
\n
\n \n

Our culture teaches it is healthy \n to \"vent\" anger. The Bible teaches that this way of handling \n our anger is foolish and destructive. (FULL BLADDER vs. Prov. 17:14).

\n

Placing unrighteous demands and \n expectations on others is idolatry and should be replaced with turning \n to God to meet our needs.

\n

Unrighteous anger takes on various \n forms: silent treatment, rages, sarcastic cuts, etc.

\n
\n \n

B. Critical Spirit

\n
\n

(Eph. 4:2,3)  . . . with all humility and gentleness, \n with patience, showing forbearance to one another in love, (3) being \n diligent to preserve the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace.

\n \n

Fault-finder; quick to notice \n and point out others mistakes & sins—without redemptive suggestions; \n judges motives. Predominant feedback you give people is when they do \n something wrong versus when the do things right.

\n
\n \n

C. Envy & Jealousy

\n \n
\n

(James 3:14,15) But if you have bitter jealousy and selfish ambition \n in your heart, do not be arrogant and so lie against the truth. (15) \n This wisdom is not that which comes down from above, but is earthly, \n natural, demonic.

\n \n

Constant comparison with others; \n glad when they fail & mad when they succeed

\n
\n \n

D. Inconsiderate & Insensitive \n

\n \n
\n

(Titus 3:2,3)  . . . to malign no one, to be uncontentious, \n gentle, showing every consideration for all men. 3 For we also once \n were foolish ourselves, disobedient, deceived, enslaved to various lusts \n and pleasures, spending our life in malice and envy, hateful, hating \n one another.

\n \n

Doesn't focus on others in conversation \n because of being preoccupied (thinks of things before people); don't \n respect others' property or rights; justify their actions based on pragmatic \n considerations—no matter how it affects people.

\n
\n \n

E. Hyper-sensitive

\n \n
\n

(Prov. 19:11) A man's wisdom gives him patience; it is to his glory \n to overlook an offense.

\n \n

(Prov. 12:16) A fool shows his annoyance at once, but a prudent man \n overlooks an insult.

\n \n

Thin-skinned; overreact to correction, \n kidding, etc.

\n
\n \n

F. Ungodly Controlling

\n \n
\n

(Luke 10:38-42) Now as they were traveling along, he entered a certain \n village; and a woman named Martha welcomed him into her home. (39) And \n she had a sister called Mary, who moreover was listening to the Lord's \n word, seated at his feet. (40) But Martha was distracted with all her \n preparations; and she came up to him, and said, \"Lord, do You not \n care that my sister has left me to do all the serving alone? Then tell \n her to help me.\" (41) But the Lord answered and said to her, \"Martha, \n Martha, you are worried and bothered about so many things; (42) but \n only a few things are necessary, really only one, for Mary has chosen \n the good part, which shall not be taken away from her.\"

\n \n

Treats amoral areas as moral \n (PACKING CAR TRUNK—only one right way; DOING DISHES)

\n
\n \n

G. Autonomy

\n \n
\n

(Prov. 18:1) He who separates himself seeks his own desire, he quarrels \n against all sound wisdom.

\n

(Prov. 12:15) The way of a fool is right in his own eyes, but a wise \n man is he who listens to counsel.

\n \n

Making decisions without asking \n advice or not considering the counsel given, considering how our decisions \n will affect others, doing Christian work without asking for the help \n of others (possibly even seeing it as an invasion of your territory), \n etc.

\n
\n \n

H. Omission

\n \n
\n

(James 4:17) Therefore, to one who knows the right thing to do, and \n does not do it, to him it is sin.

\n \n

We typically see sin as something \n we DO—not something we FAIL to do. Yet failing to serve others in relationships \n in areas of legitimate need is clearly wrong (1 Jn 3:17).

\n \n

A pattern of passivity or laziness \n in a close relationship is always damaging.

\n \n

Common for men—to see functional \n needs of wives, but neglect emotional things. Our culture is weak in \n teaching this—but that's no excuse for our failure.

\n

A hint I am struggling with omission \n is regular comparison with others, particularly committers (\"I \n am glad I don't do what they do.\").

\n

Although we all struggle with \n not fulfilling our full potential, the omitter is more likely to be \n satisfied with where they are rather than coming out of their comfort \n zone.

\n
\n
\n \n

We need to be aware of these, admit \n them to our close relationships (even take some kidding about them), give \n them the \"green light\" to bust us when we're doing them—and then \n respond when they do!!!

\n
\n \n

NOTE: There is actually a third area \n that also provides an excellent (though often very convicting) grid through \n which God reveals aspects of our sin nature that He'd like to sanctify—the \n world system.  The world, or kosmos (the Greek term), has a \n semantic range but in this context the definition most used is the system \n of values and priorities which is controlled by Satan and used to seduce people \n from ever coming to a relationship with Christ or Christians from investing \n heavily into Christian work to be used by God.

\n
\n

The world's values are opposed to \n God's and will often entice our sin nature into pursuing goals which are \n contrary to God's.  We gravitate towards some of these values more \n than others. If we look honestly at the three areas of the world's values \n that John speaks to (materialism, hedonism, egotism—1 Jn. 2:15,16), \n often we will experience God convicting us that we are not committed to \n his values.  For a more thorough treatment of this subject, see our \n notes from unit four: Satanology: Covert Tactics \n and Response.

\n
\n
\n

Cooperating \n With God \n

\n

Key Principle:

\n
\n

If you want to be more effective \n for the Lord in ministry (\"useful to the master\"), you must cooperate \n with him in your sanctification!

\n \n
\n

(2 Tim. 2:19-22**) Nevertheless, the firm foundation of God stands, \n having this seal, \"The Lord knows those who are His,\" and, \"Let \n everyone who names the name of the Lord abstain from wickedness.\" \n (20) Now in a large house there are not only gold and silver vessels, \n but also vessels of wood and of earthenware, and some to honor and some \n to dishonor. (21) Therefore, if a man cleanses himself from these things, \n he will be a vessel for honor, sanctified, useful to the Master, prepared \n for every good work. (22) Now flee from youthful lusts, and pursue righteousness, \n faith, love and peace, with those who call on the Lord from a pure heart.

\n
\n
\n \n
    \n
  1. View this struggle and deepening \n awareness of your sinful nature as normal and a sign of health (Gal. 5:17*). \n MANAGE YOUR EXPECTATIONS!
    2. \n
  2. Identify your \"besetting sins\" \n by praying for insight (Ps. 139:23,24), reviewing temperament weaknesses, \n and asking Christians who know you well (are you ready to listen?). See \n Unit 3 \"Encouragement and Admonition\".
    3. \n
  3. Prayerfully reflect on and thank \n God for the identity truth that applies specifically to your area of sin \n (ROM 6; Unit 2, Week 3 notes). This combats fatalism when you're dealing \n with new revelation of sinful nature. It also helps you deal with the root \n problem of many of your symptoms.
    4. \n
\n
\n
    \n
    \n

    C.S. Lewis writes, \"For (Alexander \n Whyte), one essential symptom of the regenerate life is a permanent, and \n permanently horrified, perception of one's natural and (it seems) unalterable \n corruption. The true Christian's nostril is to be continually attentive \n to the inner cesspool.  Another \n author, quoted in Haller's Rise of Puritanism, says that when he \n looked into his heart, it was \"as if I had in the heat of summer looked \n down into the Filth of a Dungeon, where I discerned Millions of crawling \n living things in the midst of that Sink and liquid Corruption.'  (C. \n S. Lewis)  I won't listen to those who describe that vision as merely \n pathological. I have seen the 'slimy things that crawled with legs' in my \n own dungeon. I thought the glimpse taught me sense. But Whyte seems to think \n it should not be a glimpse but a daily, lifelong scrutiny. Can he be right? \n It sounds so very unlike the New Testament fruits of the spirit—love, \n joy, peace. And very unlike the Pauline programme: 'forgetting those things \n which are behind and reaching forth unto those things that are before.'…I \n know that a spiritual emetic at the right moment may be needed. But not \n a regular diet of emetics! If one survived, one would develop a 'tolerance' \n of them. This poring over the 'sink' might breed its own perverse pride.\" \n (CS Lewis. The Inspirational Writings of CS Lewis. (New York: Inspirational \n Press. 1994) p. 307.)

    \n
    \n
\n
\n
    \n
  1. Develop a strategy for combating \n your besetting sins, and be sure that it includes both \"RESIST\" \n and \"REPLACE\" (but don't forget #3's application of identity truths) \n as well as confession (See \"A Strategy for Combating the Besetting \n Sins of the Phlegmatic Temperament.\").
    2. \n
\n
\n

Memory Verses

\n
\n
\n

\n

2 Tim. 2:19-22** - \n There is a direct proportion between our sanctification and our ability \n to bear fruit for God.
