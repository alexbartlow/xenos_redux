Since the focus of Christianity \n is on Christ, the Christian worker must have a clear understanding of his \n person and work.

\n

Christology is the study \n of his person; soteriology is the study of his work. The two \n are obviously related: who Christ is will determine what he is able to do. \n Virtually every heresy denies the deity of Christ and therefore also denies \n to some extent his work on the cross for us. As a rule, liberal (and some \n neo-orthodox) theologians also deny Christ's deity. Fundamentalists, reacting \n against this heresy, sometimes underemphasize his humanity.

\n

The so-called \"quest for \n the historical Jesus\" was an attempt by liberal theologians to discover \n what Jesus was actually like. Because of their a priori rejection \n of miracles, the expectation of their inquiry was that the real Jesus would \n prove to be different than the Jesus portrayed in scripture:

\n
\n

(Millard Erickson)\" \n . . . the earthly Jesus was depicted as basically a good man, a teacher \n of great spiritual truths, but not the miracle-working, preexistent Second \n Person of the Trinity.\" (Erickson, Millard,Christian Theology [Grand \n Rapids: Baker Book House, 1985], p. 663.)

\n
\n

Attempts to separate the Jesus of history \n from the miracle-working so-called Christ of faith proved futile.

\n
    \n
  • Different scholars arrive at wildly \n different conclusions about what Jesus was like. There aren't objective \n criteria for distinguishing the \"real\" Jesus from the biblical \n accounts of him.
    • \n
  • Once you take away the miraculous, \n the deity claims, the sayings that are attributed to the early church, there's \n hardly anything left. The Jesus Seminar claims that only 20% of the recorded \n sayings of Jesus are authentic, and an even lower percentage of the recorded \n deeds.
    • \n

Incarnation: The \n point in history when the second person of the Trinity entered the human race. \n The word literally means \"enfleshment.\"

\n
\n

Definition:

\n
\n

At the Incarnation two natures \n (full deity and full humanity), were inseparably united in the one person \n of Jesus Christ. Whereas the Trinity involves three Persons who \n share the same nature, the hypostatic union involves one Person who has \n two natures.

\n

Col. 2:9* clearly teaches \n this doctrine. Against Gnostic false teachers who denied Jesus' \n deity and (probably) fleshly body, Paul insists that \"in him dwells \n the fullness of deity in bodily form.\"

\n
\n

(Col. 2:9*) For in Him all the fullness of Deity dwells in bodily \n form . . .

\n
\n

Jn. 1:1,14 also affirm the \n hypostatic union. The Word \"was\" is in the imperfect \n tense which might be translated \"always was\" God (vs 1). Verse \n 1 stresses the eternality, distinct personality and deity of the Word. In \n verse 14 the emphasis is on a point in time event: \"became\" flesh \n (vs 14).

Could Jesus fall to temptation?

\n \n
\n

Reformed syllogism: \n God can't be tempted (Jas 1:13). Jesus is God (Col 2:9). Therefore, Jesus \n could not sin (impeccability).

\n

However, Jesus was also human \n and humans can be tempted. Jesus was tempted (Mt. \n 4) like we are (Heb 2:18; 4:15,16). These biblical accounts \n that he successfully overcame temptation and that we can go to him for help \n in overcoming temptation strongly imply that these temptations were real \n in the sense that Jesus could have fallen to them.

\n

Did Jesus have a sin nature?

\n \n
\n

Scripture does not \n answer this question directly. However, it infers that Jesus did \n not have a sin-nature.

\n
\n

In Rom. 8:3, Paul stops \n short of affirming that Jesus had \"sinful flesh\"—he \n says Jesus \"came in the likeness of sinful flesh.\"

\n

Rom. 5:12-19 provides two \n important reasons why Jesus did not inherit the full effects of Adam.

\n
\n

First: He is \n the \"second Adam\" (vs 14). This would suggest that, like Adam \n before the Fall, Jesus made his choice to obey God without a sin-nature. \n

\n

Second: Vs 16,18 \n also say that those \"in Adam\" are born under condemnation—which \n clearly was not the case with Jesus (Matt. 3:17).

\n
\n
\n

\"Then Jesus didn't agonize \n as much as we do!\" Only those who successfully resist temptation \n feel its full weight. And a lot more was at stake for Jesus (the salvation \n of humanity), and he undoubtedly experienced a greater frequency and degree \n of temptation than we do.

Definition:

\n
\n

At the Incarnation, Jesus \n gave up not only the environment of the Godhead and his position as Ruler, \n but also the use of his divine attributes. (If you fail \n to say \"use,\" you are using heretical language \n and your answer will be counted as wrong!!!)

\n

By taking on human nature, \n he accepted certain limitations upon the functioning of his divine attributes. \n These limitations were not the result of a loss of divine attributes, but \n of the addition of human attributes. (OLYMPIC SPRINTER IN 3-LEGGED RACE; \n CHAMPION BOXER FIGHTING WITH ONE HAND TIED BEHIND HIS BACK; PARENTS BATTING \n \"LEFT-HANDED\" AGAINST KIDS)

Support:

\n
\n

He gave up a perfect environment. \n

\n

(John 1:1,14) In the beginning was the Word, and \n the Word was with God, and the Word was God . . . (14) \n And the Word became flesh . . .

\n

The emphasis here \n is on Jesus exchanging his perfect environment of being with God (the \n Father) in order to enter the environment of fallen and sinful humanity \n to be our Savior.

He gave up his position as \n Ruler.

\n
\n

(Phil. 2:6,7*)  . . . who, \n although He existed in the form of God, did not regard equality with God \n a thing to be grasped, (7) but emptied Himself, taking the form of a bond-servant, \n and being made in the likeness of men.

\n

Phil. 2:6,7* states that \n Jesus did not \"hold on to\" (harpagmos) his equality with \n God. The context emphasizes that he relinquished his position \n as Ruler, and exchanged it for one of a servant.

Both Jesus and the apostles \n provide a positive answer (both specifically and generally)—that \n he performed these works by the power of the Holy Spirit.

Christ rose bodily from the \n dead, resulting in an empty grave. Positive evidence for Christ's \n resurrection (for more information on this topic, see The Case \n For Christ, by Lee Strobel, Zondervan:

\n
    \n
  • The Christian movement began \n in the very city where its founder had been executed and buried only days \n earlier. This movement was based solely on the apostles' claim \n (along with 500 other people—see 1 Cor. 15:6) that they had witnessed \n Jesus alive after his execution. The leaders of the city (both Jewish and \n Roman) were hostile to this movement, and could have easily refuted this \n claim by exhuming the body. The best explanation for this historical fact \n is that the tomb was indeed empty and that the witnesses were telling the \n truth. \"If the birth of Christianity leaves a hole in history the size \n of Jesus' resurrection, with what do you propose to fill it?\"
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • With the possible exception \n of John, the apostles were all executed rather than retract their claims \n that they had witnessed Jesus alive after his execution. It is \n difficult to believe that they would all have done this if they knew were \n lying. Chuck Colson points out that all of those involved in the Watergate \n cover-up spoke up as soon as they were threatened with imprisonment. How \n much more would at least some of the apostles have broken ranks if they \n knew they were lying about Jesus' resurrection?
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • The conversion of Paul is \n difficult to explain apart from his own explanation. He was a leading \n enemy of the Christian movement, completely indisposed to believe that Jesus \n was the Messiah. Yet, in a very short period of time, he converted to belief \n in Jesus and became Christianity's most eloquent and adamant proponent. \n Attempts to explain Paul's conversion apart from Christ's appearance (guilt \n complex; epileptic seizures; etc.) are inadequate.
    • \n
\n

Inadequate explanations:

\n
    \n
  • Stolen Body Theory: \n Someone stole the body, which explains the disciples' belief in the resurrection \n as well as the empty tomb.
    • \n
\n
\n

Refutation: Who \n would steal it? The Romans and the Jews would want Christ to be provably \n dead. A practical jokester wouldn't risk death. The disciples wouldn't be \n martyred for what they knew to be a lie.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Swoon Theory: \n Jesus didn't really die. He was given a drug while on the cross which \n simulated death, and later recovered, revealed himself to his disciples \n as resurrected, and then lived out the rest of his life in hiding.
    • \n
\n
\n

Refutation: After \n hanging on a cross for several hours, being pierced in his side and wrapped \n mummy-style, how did he break the burial linen, roll the stone away, overpower \n the guards, and after crawling to the upper room (on his elbows and knees), \n succeed in convincing his disciples that he was resurrected?

\n
\n
    \n
  • Hallucination Theory: The \n disciples didn't really see Jesus raised from the dead. They hallucinated \n seeing him, because they wanted so badly for him to be alive.
    • \n
\n
\n

Refutation: This \n still doesn't deal with the empty tomb. Evidence for the existence of mass \n hallucinations under any circumstances is lacking. In addition, his disciples \n sometimes didn't even recognize him at first (Lk. 24:l6,3l). Jesus went \n out of his way to make sure they knew he wasn't a spirit or hallucination \n (Jn. 20:25,27; Lk. 24:36-43). Neither were they necessarily inclined to \n look for a resurrection (Lk. 24:11).

\n
\n
    \n
  • Legend Theory: \n The story of the resurrection is not factual, but reflects a legend which \n the early church came to believe.
    • \n
\n
\n

Refutation: The \n gospels and epistles have been accurately dated to 50-95 AD There was not \n enough time for such a false legend to arise, since eyewitnesses could easily \n discredit it. Also, this does not explain why the disciples were willing \n to die for their message when they knew full well that it was a lie.

Essential:

\n
\n

Jesus' humanity, deity and \n resurrection are essential because scripture says they are (see \n 1 Jn. 2:22,23; 4:2,3,14,15; 5:1; 2 Jn. 1:7; 1 Cor. 15:12-34; Rom. 10:9). \n

His sinlessness and return \n are essential because they have direct bearing on salvation. The \n New Testament is clear and dogmatic on these issues for this reason.

Issues like whether Jesus \n went to Hades, whether he had a sin-nature, whether he exercises all his \n divine attributes now, whether he will forever remain incarnate, etc. are \n not emphasized by the New Testament and therefore should be viewed as non-essential. \n
