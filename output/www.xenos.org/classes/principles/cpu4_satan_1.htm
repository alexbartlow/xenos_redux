Introduction

\n
\n

C. S. Lewis spoke of the two most common \n errors people tend to make about Satan: \"There are two equal and opposite \n errors into which our race can fall about the devils. One is to disbelieve \n in their existence. The other is to believe, and to feel an excessive and \n unhealthy interest in them. They themselves are equally pleased by both errors, \n and hail a materialist or a magician with the same delight.\"

\n
\n
\n

Following the naturalism of the nineteenth \n and twentieth centuries, liberal and neo-orthodox Christianity rejected Satan's \n literal existence as primitive superstition. They reinterpreted the biblical \n accounts of demon-possession in accordance with then-current psychological \n theories. While evangelicals never went this far because of their high view \n of scripture, they were sometimes guilty of mere mental assent without maintaining \n an effective theology of spiritual warfare.

\n
\n
\n

Pentecostals and charismatics, on the \n other hand, have always emphasized the importance of spiritual warfare. They, \n however, have often fallen into the opposite error against which Lewis warned. \n Deeds of the flesh become demons to be exorcised. For example, on the topic \n of sanctification, some Christian authors emphasize freedom from demonic influence \n rather than ongoing choices to walk in the Spirit. For some, fear of Satan's \n plots and powers overshadows Christ's victory over him (e.g. satanic ritual \n abuse), and superstitious remedies become more popular than solid biblical \n response (e.g., spiritual mapping; exorcism rituals). Over the last twenty \n years, our culture has also become much more open to occultic spirituality, \n so this error is now at least as prevalent as the first one.

\n
\n
\n

This is the first of three lessons \n on Satanology: the biblical study of Satan, demons and spiritual warfare. \n Tonight we will consider the \"career\" of Satan (origin, fall, and \n destiny) and his overt tactics. The next two weeks will be devoted to his \n covert tactics.

\n

We stand to suffer greatly if we do not \n understand the reality of Satan and his efforts to attack us (I Peter 5:8,9; \n Eph. 6:12).  It is very dangerous to be in the midst of a war and not \n realize it.

Reasons against verses \n 11-19 referring to Satan:

\n
\n

1. The description in verse \n 13 seems to be of a physical being.

\n

2. His destruction (vs 18) also \n sounds physical and permanent, whereas angels do not have earthly bodies \n and Satan is elsewhere described as being perpetually alive in Hell after \n the final judgment (Rev. 20:10).

\n

3. Note the substantial parallelism \n between the first ten verses and the last eight.  In both cases the \n individual is described as the leader of Tyre, wise, wealthy, participating \n in \"trade,\" and destroyed by God.

\n

RESPONSE: Biblical authors often \n refer to spiritual beings using \n physical terminology (e.g.: Daniel 10, Ezekiel 1; commander of the Army \n of the Lord in Joshua 5:13-15), and in fact angels sometimes take on human \n form (Hebrews 13:2). \n As mentioned above, there is precedence in the bible for identifying a human \n leader with a spiritual being.
\n

\n
\n

This passage is consistent with \n other biblical teaching on Satan:

\n
\n

1.  Satan was created \n by God (vs 15). He is a creature who is inferior to God, so \n there is no dualism (Col. 1:16).

\n

2.  He is the highest \n created being (vs 14), superior to other angels (Jude 9) and humans \n in intellect, authority, etc.

\n

3.  He was originally \n perfect (vs 12,15a) as the creature of a perfect God, so God did \n not make him evil.

\n

4.  He became evil by \n exercising his free choice to rebel against God (vs 16) because \n of his pride.

\n
\n

This would explain Paul's warning \n that elder's not be \"conceited and fall into the condemnation incurred \n by the devil\" (1 Tim. 3:6). Elders often receive praise, \n and they must be careful to give that praise on to God rather than keeping \n it for themselves.

Introduction to Spiritual Warfare

\n
\n
\n

(Eph. 6:12) For \n our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against \n the powers, against the world forces of this darkness, against the spiritual \n forces of wickedness in the heavenly places.

The Bible emphasizes three \n key attitudes in the area of spiritual warfare:

\n
\n

1) Alertness:

\n
\n

(1 Pet. 5:8*) Be \n of sober spirit, be on the alert. Your adversary, the devil, prowls about \n like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour.

\n

Be a supernaturalist. Assume Satan \n is at work: anticipate where he is working; pray for discernment; check \n with fellow-workers, etc.

\n
\n

2) Militance:

\n
\n

(Rev 12:11*) And \n they overcame him because of the blood of the Lamb and because of the \n word of their testimony, and they did not love their life even to death. \n

\n

Satan loves to intimidate us so \n that we flee from our spiritual responsibilities.

\n

Satan would agree with Sun Tzu \n who wrote the Art of War: \"Supreme excellence consists in breaking \n the enemy's resistance without fighting.\" (as quoted in Bevin Alexander's, \n \"How Great Generals Win\", p. 31).

\n

That's what he wants to do to us. \n We must determine to endure suffering and keep going no matter what the \n cost is. This is key to spiritual warfare.

\n
\n

3) Confidence:

\n
\n

(1 John 4:4*) \n You are from God, little children, and have overcome them; because greater \n is He who is in you than he who is in the world.

\n

(Eph. 6:10-18**)  \n Finally, be strong in the Lord, and in the strength of His might. (11) \n Put on the full armor of God, that you may be able to stand firm against \n the schemes of the devil. (12) For our struggle is not against flesh and \n blood, but against the rulers, against the powers, against the world forces \n of this darkness, against the spiritual forces of wickedness in the heavenly \n places. (13) Therefore, take up the full armor of God, that you may be \n able to resist in the evil day, and having done everything, to stand firm. \n (14) Stand firm therefore, HAVING GIRDED YOUR LOINS WITH TRUTH, and HAVING \n PUT ON THE BREASTPLATE OF RIGHTEOUSNESS, (15) and having shod YOUR FEET \n WITH THE PREPARATION OF THE GOSPEL OF PEACE; (16) in addition to all, \n taking up the shield of faith with which you will be able to extinguish \n all the flaming missiles of the evil one. (17) And take THE HELMET OF \n SALVATION, and the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God. (18) \n With all prayer and petition pray at all times in the Spirit, and with \n this in view, be on the alert with all perseverance and petition for all \n the saints . . .

Stand on the authority of God's \n Word and Christ's victory over Satan. This prevents us from becoming paranoid \n about Satan

This guards you against \n imbalance as well as heresy. This is why we emphasize personal \n study; expository teaching; inductive study. We also should emphasize \n what the Bible emphasizes.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(Acts 20:20,27). . . how \n I did not shrink from declaring to you anything that was profitable, \n and teaching you publicly and from house to house . . . (27) \n For I did not shrink from declaring to you the whole purpose of God.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

2. Develop the ability to think \n critically from a biblical perspective. \n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

You can be biblically knowledgeable \n and still be deceived if you don't develop this. Especially when interacting \n with \"Christian\" material, clarify the presuppositions and compare \n them to the Bible (\"Christian\" counseling which \n accepts inner child; self-esteem).

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(Heb. 5:14) \n But solid food is for the mature, who because of practice have their \n senses trained to discern good and evil.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

3. Stand firm in the grace of \n Christ.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

As long as we approach God and \n others on that basis, we create a situation that Satan cannot defeat. \n He is always trying to move us onto the ground of works. Grace is the \n message that attracts the non-Christian, and motivates the believer to \n love.

\n

This helps us make decisions based \n on God's word rather than the opinions of others (including Christians \n who may be judging us). We are able to think clearly about finding God's \n will because we're not short-circuited by concern about others' opinions.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(Acts 20:32) \n And now I commend you to God and to the word of His grace, which is \n able to build you up and to give you the inheritance among all those \n who are sanctified.

\n
\n
\n

(2 Tim. 2:1) \n You therefore, my son, be strong in the grace that is in Christ Jesus. \n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

4. Be open to corrective input \n from other Christians. \n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

If even apostles needed correction \n from time to time (Gal. 2; Acts 15), how much more do we! Some are \n more gifted in discernment; others are more mature—so take advantage \n of their contribution. Elders have a special responsibility to discern \n doctrinal error and protect the church through exposing it (Titus 1:9 \n >> \"power religion\" and \"counterfeit spirituality\"). \n But because any group can engage in \"group-think,\" we should \n also look for this from outside our own group (conferences; \n books; bringing other workers in).

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

(Prov. 27:17) \n Iron sharpens iron, so one man sharpens another.

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

Watch for the \"pendulum swing.\" \n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

When Christians realize they have \n been imbalanced in one area, they commonly overreact to the other extreme. \n \"It is always easier to go to the consistent extreme than to remain \n at the point of tension.\" (Spiritual experience; ministry \n vs. sanctification; home involvement vs. church life)! Strengthen \n the complementary truth—don't denigrate the original truth (1 Cor. 12:21-25). \n

\n
\n

Doubt

\n
\n

Definition: \n seeks to erode a Christian's confidence in God

\n
\n
\n

Avenues: \n

\n
\n
\n
\n

External: \n secular \"authorities\" (college professors: \n \"You can't be a biblical Christian and have intellectual credibility\"); \n religious \"authorities\" (clergy; seminary profs: \"You \n can't take the Bible that seriously.\")

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

Internal, existential doubts: \n (\"What if this whole thing is just something we're making up?\"). \n Some are more temperamentally inclined to this. It often accompanies periods \n of trial (ministry failure: early buzz wearing off; dark \n night of the soul; tragedy).

\n
\n
\n \n \n

Note: \n James 1:6 uses this word to mean one who is \"double-minded\" (vs 8) \n which is later defined as a Christian who has compromised his commitment \n to Christ by loving the world (Jas. 4:8). In other words, \"doubter\" \n here means one who is in volitional unbelief.

\n \n\n
\n

Countermeasures: \n It is not a sin to have doubts; it is what you do with doubts when they emerge \n that matters.

\n
\n
\n
\n

1. Share your doubts with God \n and other Christians! \n
\n This diffuses the accusation that no other Christians wrestle with doubt. \n They can also often provide answers to your doubts.

\n
\n
\n \n \n

2. Study the biblical passages \n where God's people express their doubts. \n
\n Job, Lamentations, many psalms, etc. are very helpful. The fact that others \n (including biblical authors!) have had similar doubts is encouraging. The \n fact that they eventually resolved their doubts is also encouraging and \n sometimes instructive.

\n

3. Check your personal walk.
\n Lack of spiritual feeding and ministry will rob us of subjective personal \n assurance and make us more vulnerable to doubt. Willful rejection of God's \n moral discipline may also make us vulnerable to doubt.

\n

4. Expose yourself to answers \n regarding theological/philosophical doubts. \n
\n You're not the first one to ever have this kind of doubt! One of the \n main values of apologetics is to strengthen Christians against doubt. Many \n suffer needlessly from doubt because they are too lazy to do this work. \n
\n We run the risk of suppressing our own doubts with self-soothing behavior.

\n

Good reading: Guinness. Doubt. \n Yancey. Disappointment with God. Schaeffer. True Spirituality.

\n

5. Monitor your thought-life and \n take doubtful thoughts captive to Christ (2 Cor. 10:3-5*). \n
\n Learn to doubt your doubts! Satan will accuse God to us (e.g., that he will \n abandon you), and unless we respond aggressively by rejecting such thoughts \n on the basis of scripture, they will tend to grow into serious doubts.

\n

Consider: \"Doubt \n is to faith what fear is to courage.\"
