Gen. 22:1-14 - What do we learn about \n substitutionary atonement from this passage?

\n \n
\n

Abraham and Isaac had knowledge \n about animal sacrifice, including the acceptable animal (vs 7,8).

\n \n

God orchestrated this event, in \n part, as a prophetic type of the way he would provide a sacrifice for \n our sins.

\n
    \n
  • Vs 14 is a prediction that God \n would one day provide his sacrifice on Mt. Moriah. Mt. Moriah is the \n site of Jerusalem, the Temple (2 Chron. 3:1), and Jesus' death. \n
    • \n
  • God, like Abraham, offered up \n \"his son, his only son, whom he loved\" (Jn. 3:16 probably \n purposefully echoes this wording).
    • \n
  • Jesus, like Isaac, carried the \n instrument of his own death to the site (Jn. 19:17).
    • \n
  • Isaac was old enough to carry \n wood for the sacrifice up the mountain. We can infer from this that \n he was probably in his teen years or older. It's unlikely that Abraham \n in his old age could have forced Isaac down on the altar. Isaac likely \n offered himself voluntarily in faith, just like Jesus did. \n
    • \n
  • Isaac's deliverance from \n death was a prophetic type of Jesus' resurrection (see Heb. 11:19). \n
    • \n
  • Isaac was the progenitor of \n the promised \"seed\" (Gen. 12:3). Through this event, God explains \n further how the \"Seed\" would make God's blessing available \n to all the nations—by dying a substitutionary death. \n

    Note: Although \n Abraham evidently did not yet know this, God later made it very clear \n that he detested child sacrifice as a form of religious worship (Lev. 18:21; \n Jer. 32:35). Furthermore, it is not intrinsically morally objectionable \n for God to require the life of human beings. He does this ultimately \n at judgment, and he required the life of his own Son.

    \n
    • \n
\n

Was Jesus referring to this incident \n in Jn. 8:56,57? \"My day\" may well refer to Jesus' crucifixion \n (\"my hour;\" \"my time\"). Jesus may be saying that Abraham \n saw in this event a picture of Jesus' most important act.

\n
    \n
  • Jesus may have actually been \n \"the angel of the Lord\" who directed him not to kill Isaac \n but offer up the ram instead. Vs 12 indicates that the angel of the \n Lord, while he speaks of God in the third person, is also the One to \n whom the sacrifice is offered. (See also Judges 13:18,22).
    • \n
\n
\n
    \n
  • Summary: The original \n audience could see that God provided a substitute and possibly that this \n place would be the place of future atonement. 
    • \n
\n
\n

In view of subsequent revelation, \n the location (Moriah) and the identity (only Son) of God's atonement, \n and his resurrection are clear.

\n
\n \n

Ex. 12:1-14 \n - The good news is that God is going to judge Egypt so that Pharaoh lets \n God's people go. The bad news is that the Israelites will be judged also—unless \n they observe this ritual. If they do observe it, his judgment will \"pass \n over\" those houses. Note these key elements in the ritual meal:

\n
    \n
  • The sacrifice must be without \n physical defect (vs 5).
    • \n
  • It must be killed (vs 6). \n
    • \n
  • Its blood (proof of its \n death) is what causes God's judgment to \"pass over\" (vs 7,22,23). \n
    • \n
  • Displaying the blood, eating in \n haste (unleavened bread), and being dressed in readiness demonstrated \n their faith that God would deliver them through his appointed means. God's \n provision must be appropriated through faith.
    • \n
  • God commanded that they celebrate \n Passover yearly as a memorial (Ex. 12:14,26,27).
    • \n
  • God commanded that they celebrate \n Passover in the land (vs 25). Deut. 16:5,6; 2 Chron. 6:6). \n This has prophetic significance, as we shall see (Deut. 16:5,6; 2 Chron. \n 6:6 - eventually God specified that Passover be celebrated in Jerusalem).
    • \n
  • Summary: Substitute must \n be without defect and be personally appropriated through faith to be effective; \n location of sacrifice (Jerusalem).
    • \n
\n \n

Lev. 4 \n - The tabernacle was a mobile tent that God ordered the nation of Israel \n to put up every time they pitched camp. It illustrated his desire to dwell \n among his people, the problem that prevented him from fully doing this—and \n the solution he would one day provide for this problem. The problem is human \n sinfulness, illustrated by the physical barriers (veils before the holy \n place and the holy of holies) and the necessity of priests. The solution \n is substitutionary atonement offered through God's chosen mediator-priest. \n Note the common elements:

\n
    \n
  • They must slay an animal of God's \n choosing that is without defect >> blameless substitute (vs \n 3,23,28,32).
    • \n
  • The offerer must lay his hands \n on the animal's head, symbolically identifying the sacrifice with \n his sins (vs 4,15,24,29,33).
    • \n
  • The priest must offer the animal \n for them >> mediator (vs 5,16,25,30,34). Because this was \n only a picture, the (sinful) priests had to offer a sacrifice for their \n own sins before they could act as a mediator for others.
    • \n
  • They had to go through these grisly \n ritual sacrifices virtually every day. This not only kept them focused \n on the problem of sin and God's remedy (substitutionary atonement); it \n also indicated that these sacrifices were ultimately insufficient (Heb. \n 10:1-4), in that the people had no direct access to God, and they had \n no assurance of complete forgiveness.
    • \n
\n
\n

Note: \"Unintentional \n sins\" (vs 2,22,27; Num. 15:27-29) evidently refer to \n sins that do not involve an intentional rejection of God and/or his provision \n for forgiveness.

\n

\"Presumptuous sins\" \n (Num. 15:30,31; Deut. 17:12,13) evidently refer to defiant sins that \n reject God and his means of provision. This is what the author of Hebrews \n is referring to in Heb. 10:26-31.

\n

God forgives sin, but won't forgive \n a sinner who won't repent.  The unrepentant is the presumptuous \n sinner.

\n

Note: Leaders \n were required to offer male goats (Lev. 4:22,23), while common people \n were required to offer female goats (Lev. 4:27,28). Since male goats \n were more valuable because of their greater capacity to produce offspring, \n those in positions of greater responsibility evidently had to make more \n costly sacrifices.

\n
\n \n
    \n
  • Summary: Sin is the main \n issue (sacrifices needed daily - see Heb. 7:27;10:11)! Identification \n of substitute with person's sin; offering through a mediator.
    • \n
\n \n

Lev. 16** - Day of Atonement symbolism \n

\n \n
\n

Vs 1,2: Aaron's two sons were killed \n because they offered \"strange fire\" (Lev. 10:1-3). We may \n come into God's presence only in the way which he prescribes—or \n else his holiness will consume us. (This is an example of \"presumptuous \n sin.\")

\n

The high priest is the only \n one who can enter the Holy of Holies (vs 4). He must dress and bathe in \n a way which symbolizes purity (vs 4). He symbolizes God's chosen mediator. \n The high priest must offer a sacrifice for his own sins (vs 11)—one of \n the inferiorities of this symbolic system (Heb. 7:26-28).

\n

The two goats (vs 5) are \n both sin-offerings representing the nation of Israel. The fact that they \n are chosen by lots for their roles (vs 8) teaches that the sacrifice is \n chosen by God rather than by humans. The fact that they are offered to \n the Lord at the doorway of the tent of meeting may emphasize that God \n is communicating substitutionary atonement to all the people. It may also \n symbolize Christ's public death.

\n

The \"ark of the covenant\" \n means the \"box of the evidence\"—God's legal indictment \n of their sins (MANNA: rejection of God's provision [Ex. 16:32-34]; \n ROD: rejection of God's leadership [Num. 17:10]; TABLETS: rebellion \n and stubbornness [Deut. 31:26,27]). The cherubim are angels (often \n associated with God's righteousness), so their downward look illustrates \n God's focus on their sins.
\n The high priest slays one of the goats and offers its blood on the mercy \n seat (\"mercy\" means \"to cover\" or \"to atone\") \n to atone for the nation's sins that year (vs 16,17). (OVERHEAD ON ELEMENTS \n ON/INSIDE ARK OF THE COVENANT >> Ex. 25:10-22) This symbolizes the \n death of a blameless substitute for the guilt of our sins.
\n

\n

The high priest then lays his hands \n on the other goat and confesses Israel's sins as he does so—symbolizing \n identification. Then he chases the animal off to the wilderness. This \n symbolizes that fact that the guilt of their sins has been sent off because \n of the atoning death of the substitute (vs 20-22). \"Forgiveness\" \n (Greek: aphiemi) means \"a sending off/away\" of our true \n moral guilt.

\n

Again, the fact that this sacrifice \n had to be reenacted year after year indicated the insufficiency of the \n system (Heb. 10:1-4).

\n

Note to teachers: We are assuming \n that when the ark was in the tabernacle, it contained manna, Aaron's rod, \n and the Law (see Ex. 16:33-34; Num. 17:1-11; Ex. 25:16;31:16; Heb. 9:4). \n But when the ark was moved to Solomon's temple, the ark apparently only \n contained the tablets of the law (1 Kings 8:9).

\n
\n \n
    \n
  • Summary: Greater \n development of separation caused by sin and removal of guilt through the \n substitute's death.  In addition, we find that the intercessor must \n be clean and his return from the offering signified God's acceptance of \n the sacrifice.
    • \n

Isa. 52:13-53:12** \n - This passage clearly teaches that God never viewed the animal sacrifices \n as efficacious in themselves. They were always a prophetic picture of God's \n chosen Servant—a blameless Jewish Person whose voluntary death would \n pay for humanity's sins. Isaiah 52:13-53:12 is the culmination of four passages \n that progressively reveal more information about the suffering servant (42:1- \n 9, 49:1-13, 50:4-11). Note the sacrificial system/substitutionary atonement \n language:

\n \n
\n

53:5 \"pierced through \n for our transgressions . . . crushed for our iniquities . . . by his scourging \n we are healed . . . \"

\n

53:6 \" . . . the Lord \n caused the iniquity of us all to fall on him . . . \"

\n

53:8 \" . . . he was \n cut off out of the land of the living for the transgression of my people, \n to whom the stroke was due . . . \"

\n

53:10 \" . . . if he \n would render himself as a guilt offering . . . \"

\n

53:11 \" . . . as he \n will bear their iniquities . . . \"

\n

53:12 \" . . . he himself \n bore the sin of many . . . \"

\n

Note: 52:14,15 clearly \n say that his death will also be the basis of forgiveness for Gentiles \n (\" . . . he will sprinkle many nations . . . \"). 
\n Some commentators translate the Hebrew word \"nazah\", \n here translated \"sprinkle\", as to \"startle\". But this \n form of the word is used only one other place in the OT - Lev 16:14 - \n where the high priest sprinkles blood on mercy seat to atone for sin. \n Moreover, yazah in Isaiah 52:15 is in the \"Hiphil imperfect\" form (future \n causitive) of nazah, which is always translated \"to sprinkle.\" - See Koelher \n and Baumgardner, Hebrew and Aramaic Lexicon of the OT v. 2 (Brill, \n 1995), 683.

\n

Note: What about \n the Servant described in the past tense? This is a stylistic device (\"prophetic \n past tense\"), in which God often describes future events in the past \n tense to emphasize his sovereignty over human history. Furthermore, the \n context indicates that this Servant's death is a future event (52:13-15; \n 53:11 \"will\").

\n \n

Note: What about \n the view that the Servant is the nation of Israel? 53:8 says the Servant \n is killed \"for the transgression of my people.\" He is also righteous, \n unlike Israel. He is described as an individual, not as a nation.

\n

53:10 \"...He will \n see His offspring.   He will prolong His days.\"

\n

Resurrection is predicted.

\n
\n \n
    \n
  • Summary: God will send \n a blameless Jewish Person to atone for humanity's sins, and he will die \n and be resurrected.
    • \n

(John 1:29) \n The next day he saw Jesus coming to him, and said, \"Behold, the Lamb \n of God who takes away the sin of the world!

\n \n
\n

John declares that Jesus is the \n fulfillment of the sacrificial system who \"bears\" the whole \n world's guilt.

\n

(John 1:14) The \n word became flesh and dwelt (tabernacled) among us.
\n Jesus is the true tabernacle \n and his sacrifice is the true sacrifice.

\n

Note: John evidently \n did not understand that there would be two comings of the Messiah, the \n first one of which would end in his death. John expected Jesus to be a \n king, as his question in Matt. 11:3 makes clear. Like the other Old \n Testament prophets, John did not understand how these messianic prophecies \n fit together (1 Pet. 1:10-12).

\n
\n \n
    \n
  • Summary: The Person (see \n above) is Christ.
    • \n

(Mark 10:45)  \n For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to \n give His life as a ransom for many.

\n

(Luke 22:37) \n \"For I tell you, that this which is written must be fulfilled in Me, `AND \n HE WAS NUMBERED WITH TRANSGRESSORS'; for that which refers to Me has its \n fulfillment.\"

\n \n
\n

Jesus clearly understood that his \n substitutionary death was his main mission in the first coming. In the \n first advent, he comes to serve (as fulfillment of Anonymous Servant) \n to give his life as a ransom for the many. In the second advent, he comes \n as a king to be served.  Some believe that  Jesus was caught \n by surprise as a confused victim of some horrible circumstances. (See \n also Luke 22:37)

\n

Jesus did not go to the cross merely \n to be the perfect expression or example of sacrificial love. The cross \n does communicate this (1 Jn. 4:10; Phil. 2:3-8), \n but its primary purpose was to actually pay for true moral guilt. Paul \n insists that unless Jesus' death was atoning, it was needless (Gal. 2:21). \n

\n
\n \n
    \n
  • Summary: Jesus' substitutionary \n death was his main mission.
    • \n

Matthew 26:26-30 \n - Jesus teaches his disciples that his death is the ultimate fulfillment \n of the Passover meal, and that the blessings of the New Covenant are possible \n only because of his substitutionary death.

\n
\n

Matt 26:26-30 \n And while they were eating, Jesus took some bread, and after a blessing, \n He broke it and gave it to the disciples, and said, \"Take, eat; this \n is My body.\" 27 And when He had taken a cup and given thanks, He \n gave it to them, saying, \"Drink from it, all of you; 28 for this \n is My blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for forgiveness \n of sins. 29 \"But I say to you, I will not drink of this fruit of \n the vine from now on until that day when I drink it new with you in My \n Father's kingdom.\" 30 And after singing a hymn, they went out to \n the Mount of Olives.

\n

Luke \n 22:20 And in the same way He took the cup after they had eaten, \n saying, \"This cup which is poured out for you is the new covenant \n in My blood.
\n

\n

Note: Understanding \n the way the Passover was observed yields additional insight into Jesus' \n statements. The Synoptic authors seem to only mention the ways that Jesus \n diverged from the normal Passover liturgy. The head of the household normally \n passed the bread out in silence—but Jesus explained that it represented \n his body/physical death for them. They normally drank four cups—two \n before the meal and two afterwards. The third cup was called the \"cup \n of redemption,\" and Jesus explained its significance in vs. 28. \n The fourth cup was called the \"cup of consummation\" looking \n forward to God's future kingdom. In vs 29, Jesus refuses to drink this \n cup until he comes back. The Jews customarily ended this meal by singing \n Ps. 116-118. Note especially Ps. 118:6-9,17,18,22,23, which \n speak prophetically of Jesus' death and resurrection. See William L. Lane, \n The Gospel According to Mark (Grand Rapids: Eerdmans Publishing, \n 1974), pp. 504-509.

\n

Note: Jesus was \n not teaching transubstantiation here. First of all, he is already \n referring to a symbolic meal. Secondly, they knew where his body was (in \n front of them!) and would understand that he was speaking metaphorically \n (as he often did—\"I am the door;\" \"I am the true Vine\").. \n

\n \n

(Jer. 31:31-34) \n \"Behold, \n days are coming,\" declares the LORD, \"when I will make \n a new covenant with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah, (32) \n not like the covenant which I made with their fathers in the day I took \n them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt, My covenant which \n they broke, although I was a husband to them,\" declares the LORD. \n (33) \"But this is the covenant which I will make with the house of \n Israel after those days,\" declares the LORD, \"I will \n put My law within them, and on their heart I will write it; and \n I will be their God, and they shall be My people. (34) \"And they \n shall not teach again, each man his neighbor and each man his brother, \n saying, 'Know the LORD,' for they shall all know Me, \n from the least of them to the greatest of them,\" declares the LORD, \n \"for I will forgive their iniquity, and their sin I will \n remember no more.\"

\n \n
\n

This is an elaboration of the \n New Covenant blessings that had not yet been enacted (\"the days \n are coming\"): moral empowering through the Holy Spirit (\"my \n laws within them\"); personal relationship with God (\"they \n shall all know me\"); assurance of forgiveness (\"their sin \n I will remember no more\").Jesus is saying that his death is what \n makes it possible for God to grant us these blessings (see also Heb. 8:6-12). \n

\n

\n \n
    \n
  • Summary: The blessings \n of the New Covenant are made possible through Jesus' death.
