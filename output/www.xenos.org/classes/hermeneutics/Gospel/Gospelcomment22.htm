Structure studies in Matthew 15

\n

Matthew 15:21-28 The Syrophoenician woman

\n

Context: Why is this passage here?

\n
    \n
  • Preceding narrative: confrontation with Pharisees over “defilement”
    • \n
  • Following narrative: summary of healings, then 4000 fed
    • \n
  • Narrative directed both to the pagan woman (in a pagan setting) and to the disciples
    • \n
  • Mt. 15:21—28 extends the argument of the last interaction—that faith from the heart, not ritual cleansing from defilement, is what God seeks
    • \n
\n\n

Outline of narrative structure:

\n
    \n
  • First scene: request of woman to Jesus (15:21-22)—sets up the drama\n
      \n
    • Who? Where? When?
      • \n
    • Note the irony—Jesus leaves Israel after engaging Jerusalem’s religious elite over the nature of ritual cleanness, to pagan land. The Canaanite woman, unlike either the disciples or the religious authorities, recognizes Jesus’ true identity as “Son of David” (see also 9:27; 12:23—two prior uses of “Son of David”, both on lips of the unclean).
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Second scene: exchange with disciples (15:23-24)—establishes the significance of the deliverance.\n
      \n
    • Jesus’ silence is met by the disciples’ request to send her away.
      • \n
    • Jesus’ response is significant: “I was sent only to the lost sheep of Israel.”\n
        \n
      • The Son of David is Israel’s messiah in a unique way (see Jn. 4:22—26). But at the same time, Jesus has healed gentiles (8:5—13).
        • \n
      • These points contribute to the dramatic exchange between the woman and Jesus.
        • \n
      \n
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Third scene: exchange with the woman (15:25-28)—the sufficiency of faith in God’s mercy.\n
      \n
    • To the woman’s repeated plea Jesus says, “It’s not good to throw the children’s bread to the dogs.” I.e.—blessings go to Israel, not gentiles.
      • \n
    • Woman does not contradict Jesus, but extends the saying to include the needs of her daughter—a true act of reverent faith.
      • \n
    • Jesus, seeing her faith, grants her the request.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
\n

Structure summary: Context: challenge of internal vs. external cleanliness

\n
    \n
  • Supporting Point #1: Ironic identification by “unclean” woman that Jesus is “Son of David”\n
    • \n
  • Supporting Point #2: Messiah’s mission centers on the lost of Israel\n
    • \n
  • Supporting Point #3: Woman pleas for inclusion in blessings of God to Israel
    • \n
  • Main Point: God honors the greatness of woman’s faith (both persistence and content)—she is a strong antithesis to the rabbis’ tradition-based religion centering on purity and defilement \n
    • \n
\n

Matthew 15:1-20

\n

Context: Why is this passage here?

\n
    \n
  • Preceding passage: walking on the water—a challenge to Peter’s faith (compare with 9:27 where the emphasis is on Jesus’ unique authority)\n
    • \n
  • Following passage: delivering the Syrophoenician woman’s daughter and other healings—here is a woman who is certainly unclean\n Teaching is directed first to the Pharisees themselves (denunciation); then to the multitude; finally, to the disciples. Note that this is the only passage in this section in which all three audiences are involved.\n
    • \n
  • Mt. 15:1-20 reveals the heart of the conflict in Jesus’ ministry: hypocritical elevation of the authority of tradition (preoccupation with ritual cleanliness over revelation in Christ and OT scripture)\n
    • \n
\n

Outline of narrative structure:

\n

Introduction statement: Pharisees’ question (15:2): “Why do Jesus’ disciples violate traditions by eating with ritually unwashed hands?”

\n
    \n
  • Three scenes:\n
      \n
    • First scene (3-9)—addressing the Pharisees\n
        \n
      • Tradition invalidates the Law—rhetorical question v. 3\n
          \n
        • Example of corban and Law’s command to honor ones’ parents
          • \n
        \n
        • \n
      • Advocates of tradition are hypocrites\n
          \n
        • Indulging in heartless, vain worship (Isaiah 29:13)
          • \n
        \n
        • \n
      \n
      • \n
    • Second scene (10-11)—addressing the multitudes\n
        \n
      • Defilement is the result of what comes out of a person, not what goes in
        • \n
      \n
      • \n
    • Third scene (12-20)—addressing the disciples\n
        \n
      • Parable of the uprooted plant—directed against Pharisees
        • \n
      • Parable of the blind guides—directed against Pharisees
        • \n
      • What goes in does not defile, but what’s in the heart—summary of 11, 17-19, “these things that defile” of v. 20a
        • \n
      \n
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
\n

Conclusion statement: the heart, not unwashed hands, defiles the man (v. 20)

\n

Structure Summary: Passage in context of 14:22-33; 15:21-28 \n

\n
    \n
  • Introduction statement (15:2)—“why do Jesus’ disciples violate traditions by eating with ritually unwashed hands?”\n
      \n
    • Supporting Point #1: Pharisees’ question based on tradition that invalidates the Law—heresy \n \n
      • \n
    • Supporting Point #2: Pharisees’ focus on tradition indicates their hypocrisy \n
        \n
      • Heartless, vain worship described by Isaiah 29:13
        • \n
      \n
      • \n
    • Supporting Point #3: Defilement is the result of what comes out, not what goes in\n
        \n
      • Parables of denunciation directed to Pharisees with explanation to disciples
        • \n
      \n
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Conclusion statement (15:20) MAIN POINT—“[things of the heart] defile the man; but to eat with unwashed hands does not defile the man.”\n
