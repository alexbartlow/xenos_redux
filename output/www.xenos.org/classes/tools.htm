  • Hatch, E., and H.A. Redpath. A Concordance to the Septuagint.\n 2 vols. Oxford: Clarendon, 1897-1906 (reprinted by Baker, 1983)

    Not the kind of books you would buy for your personal library, but good for\n doing a word study on a NT word and it's background in the LXX.

    \n

    Moulton, W.F., A.J. Geden and H.F. Moulton. A Concordance to\n the Greek Testament. Edinburgh: T&T Clark, 1978

    \n

    A Greek concordance is a must-have if you are going to do word\n studies in the original language. This volume is good because the brief context is in the\n Greek, which is important if you are looking for particular constructions rather than a\n simple word.

    \n

    Wikgran, G. The New Englishman's Concordance of the New\n Testament. Grand Rapids: Zondervan

    \n

    If your Greek is not so good, this volume offers the brief context\n of the words in English. This would be a second-best alternative if you are doing word\n studies in the original language.

    \n

    Wigram, G.B. The Englishman's Hebrew and Chaldee Concordance\n of the Old Testament. Grand Rapids: Eerdmans, 1971.

    \n

    Similar to the Englishman's for the NT.

    \n

    Even-Shoshan, A. A New Concordance of the Old Testament.\n Grand Rapids: Baker, 1984.

    \n

    This concordance offers the brief context of each term in the\n Hebrew, which is important if you are looking for a particular construction instead of a\n simple word.
