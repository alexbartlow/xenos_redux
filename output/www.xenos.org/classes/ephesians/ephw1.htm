Introduction to Inductive Method:

\n \n

Art vs. Science

\n \n
\n

People want a cut and dry method to extract meaning from God’s word \n as though they were following a manual for building a deck. The problem is \n God’s word isn’t designed this way. There is no manual; there are \n only guidelines. This is because interpretation isn’t a science. There \n are methods we can follow to help us come to right conclusions but methods \n will only get us part of the way there. There is an art to interpreting the \n Bible. It requires skill, finesse, insight, and the power of the Holy Spirit. \n

\n
\n
\n

(2 Peter 1:20,21) But know this first of all, that no prophecy of Scripture \n is a matter of one's own interpretation, for no prophecy was ever made \n by an act of human will, but men moved by the Holy Spirit spoke from God.

\n

Class exercises are a dynamic process. I don’t have all the answers; \n rather, I gain insight from your approach as well. You may feel overwhelmed \n at the beginning; hang in there.

\n
\n \n

Approaching a book for study

\n \n

Preliminary assessment: What kind of book is this?

\n \n
\n

Biblical interpretation really begins with a thorough understanding of the \n Bible as a whole. But we gain that understanding bit by bit. No one starts \n out interpreting the Bible with a full-blown knowledge of all its parts. While \n it is true that the Bible builds upon itself and that later revelation often \n refers to what was written earlier, much of the New Testament was written \n to those who were not entirely biblically literate. Much of the NT is simplified \n and explained for us because it is largely written with a Gentile audience \n in mind.

\n

You have already answered a few questions as you begin to use this worksheet. \n You’ve decided that this book is a letter or an epistle. What’s \n the difference? An epistle is a kind of letter, but whereas a letter is usually \n written to a specific individual, such as Timothy, an epistle is written to \n a wider audience, such as the church in Galatia. But the most important thing \n to understand is that this kind of writing is occasional. By that we mean \n that there were specific circumstances which brought about the writing of \n this letter. It is written by an individual at a certain time and place usually \n in response to some event, question or occasion that has presented itself. \n For instance, the epistle to the Galatians is written in response to pressing \n concerns about harmful influences in the church. If we discern what situation \n occasioned the writing of the epistle we’re studying, we’ve gone \n a long way toward understanding the meaning of the book we’re studying.

\n

Because Ephesians is an epistle you’ve answered another question as \n to where it is situated within God’s redemptive plan. It is clearly after \n the crucifixion and resurrection of Christ and is written to help us understand \n more fully the meaning of those events and apply them to daily life.

\n Occasional: What's the occasion?
\n Audience Specific: It wasn’t written to us directly. That doesn’t \n mean that it doesn’t apply to us or that Paul didn’t have in mind \n the universal church when he wrote.

General Overview: Read through the book completely

\n
\n \n

The logic of beginning with overview:

\n \n
\n

The purpose of beginning by reading the entire book is that it gives you \n an understanding of where the author is heading. Once you know where the author \n is going, you’ll have a better understanding of how he builds his argument. \n Without getting a sense of the whole we tend to misuse the parts. We cannot \n really understand the parts of a text in isolation from each other. This is \n the error of proof-texting or wrenching a passage out of its context and applying \n it to a situation. (Example: James 2:14,24 \"What use is it, my brethren, \n if a man says he has faith, but he has not works? Can that faith save him? \n …You see that a man justified by works, and not by faith alone.\" \n Luther's response. Passage hinges on \"justification.\") If we have \n not taken the time to understand the context, we will be guilty of this error \n by default. However, if we have taken the time to attempt to understand the \n whole, our ability to understand the parts will be greatly enhanced.

\n
\n \n

Inductive-deductive Approach

\n \n
\n

In order to understand a specific passage of scripture \n we need to see the broader context. We call this the inductive-deductive approach \n to understand scripture. It is really just a reflection of how understanding \n occurs in general; that is, we tend to see the parts in terms of the whole. \n And we understand the whole as made up of various parts. When we approach \n the biblical interpretation in this way it allows us to have a system of checks \n and balances. The way this works is that we read a passage such as 1 John \n 3:6:

\n
\n
\n

\"No one who abides in Him sins; no one who sins has seen Him or knows \n Him.\"

\n
\n
\n

Those who want to prove that Christians no longer sin have used this passage. \n When we read this passage we may immediately question our salvation in Christ \n because our experience does not conform to this passage. We could waste a \n lot of time trying to interpret this passage out of its context. It is only \n when we are able to see the entire scope of John's epistle and attempt to \n harmonize this passage with the rest of his letter that we gain any clarity \n on what he is saying. So what has happened is that we have reasoned from the \n bottom up (what does this passage say) and from the top down (what does the \n rest of the book say: does our understanding of the theme fit the individual \n passages). We put together the whole in terms of the parts and the parts in \n terms of the whole.

\n
\n \n

Historical context: Author, audience, and third party

\n
\n

Read the book, noting every time the author is mentioned, every time the \n audience or readers are mentioned and every time anyone else is mentioned—we \n call them the third party. This third party is not who the letter is directed \n towards but they often figure prominently in why the letter was written. We \n identify parties in the letter in order to understand what historical circumstances \n motivated the author’s communication. Behind this method is an assumption \n about the nature of epistles which we’ve already mentioned, that is that \n epistles are occasional documents, they were written to address a specific \n situation on a specific occasion.

\n

Who makes the paragraphs? Unfortunately, paragraphs are not a part of the \n original manuscripts. The Greeks wrote without paragraph breaks, without sentence \n breaks and even without word breaks! Translators have added these divisions \n for us and because of that, the paragraph divisions and even the sentence \n divisions within your Bibles are not infallible.

Paragraph Study:

\n
\n

Carefully read the paragraph in an attempt to understand the following points. \n This part of the study is always done in reference to the larger understanding \n of the book.

\n
\n \n

Structure: Skeleton.

\n \n
\n

The goal here is to clarify the development of the author's thoughts or argument. \n The structure of an author’s writing functions like a body’s skeleton. \n It is unseen when you look at the surface. And yet, it provides the framework \n that in large part determines the shape and size of the body. Of course, it’s \n not the only determining factor in the way a person looks. Attached to the \n skeleton is muscle tissue—content—that drives the movement of the \n structure. Covering both the skeleton and muscular tissue is the skin—application—that \n gives final shape to the body. The skin is what gives final form to the biblical \n structure and content and it is what we see of a person day-to-day. But it \n is a reflection of the underlying content/musculature and skeletal structure. \n

\n

The structure of a book or a paragraph is generally unseen by the \n reader until he or she begins to dissect what is being said. The importance \n in this is that once you begin to recognize the structure, you will better \n understand how things fit together. How does the author organize what he wants \n to communicate? The way he organizes his statements will emphasize certain \n points while subordinating others. This is important for us because we want \n to emphasize what the writer emphasizes. This usually becomes clear as we \n see how an author puts together his thoughts.

\n
\n

A. Context: literary & theological

\n
\n

What are the ideas or themes communicated before and after your paragraph? \n Where is the author coming from and where is he going? How does this passage \n fit into the broader context and argument of the book you are studying?

\n


\n Where is the author going with the point that’s being made in the passage \n you are studying? Is there a break in thought between what occurred previously \n and what occurs afterward? If so, why? Becoming a good interpreter means being \n able to see the flow or logic of the text. To use the analogy of the structure \n of a passage as a skeletal framework, this means being able to see how the \n bones connect with in your passage and which bones they connect to before \n and after your passage.

\n
\n \n

B. Main Point: What is the central claim of this passage?

\n \n
\n

In order to understand a paragraph we need to know what is the central idea \n or theme of the paragraph. Ask What. What does the author want his \n readers to understand or what does the author want his readers to do?

\n
\n \n

Hints: \n

\n \n
\n

Conclusions are generally indicated by words like: thus, therefore, \n so, so that, hence, then, consequently. \"Therefore\" or \"for \n this reason\" often indicates the author’s main point. Circle these \n kinds of words that indicate that the author is drawing a conclusion.

\n
\n\n
\n

An imperative is a command. Often, but not always, in the epistles \n the main point of a passage is found in the imperative statements. The author \n is instructing his readers to do a certain activity or to think in a specific \n way. What is the author telling his audience to do?

\n
\n
    \n
  • Identify a controlling idea:
    • \n
\n
\n

Sometimes a passage doesn’t include an imperative or doesn’t have \n a conclusion indicator. Ask yourself what is the controlling idea of the passage. \n What universal truth or overarching idea accounts for all of the elements \n of the passage? What brings it all together?

\n
\n
    \n
  • Identify independent clauses:
    • \n
\n
\n

The main point will be contained in the part of the sentence that can stand \n on its own. It may not be a full sentence as it is written but it could be. \n A dependent clause is one that cannot stand alone as a sentence. It is by \n definition a modifying statement put there to give support or give clarification \n to the main clause. (Review the sentences in 1:14 to identify dependent versus \n independent clauses.)

\n
\n

C. Supporting points. How is the main point supported?

\n
\n

In other words, why is the claim true and why should the reader do what the \n author says?

\n

You’ve already discerned what the main point of the passage is, now \n you want to identify how the author backs up that point. What theological \n basis or principles are given by the author to support his claim? Or what \n illustrations does he use to support his point? In this section we are mostly \n asking questions about Why. Why does he want them to do what he tells \n them to do? Why do they need to understand this information? Why is it important \n to their Christian lives? This is a key question for which we need answers. \n

\n
\n

List out the supporting points.

\n \n

Hints: \n

\n \n
\n

Indicatives are statements that claim to be true. They are often the basis \n for an imperative.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Identify connective words:
    • \n
\n
\n

Connective words link a supporting idea to a main idea. They tell us the \n reason behind the central claim of a passage. Connective words are words such \n as: because, for, since, as, for this purpose, so that, in order that (1Pet4:6). \n These connective words connect to one of two kinds of statements: indicative \n statements or result clauses.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Identify result statements:
    • \n
\n
\n

These statements indicate the kind of results the author wants to see or \n wants to warn us about. It is not necessarily an indicative but it is the \n outcome sought or hoped for. In Galatians 3:24, as we have seen Paul concludes \n a section on the purpose of the law with \"Therefore the Law has become \n our tutor to lead us to Christ. . .\" He finishes this statement with \n the result that the Law is intended to bring about, namely, \"so that \n we may be justified by faith.\" Again, in Galatians 5:16-24 Paul tells \n his audience to \"walk by the Spirit,\" which is his main point. He \n backs up his claim by showing his readers the results of walking in the spirit \n as well as showing them the antithesis, which is walking according to the \n flesh. The desired outcome, or its antithesis, helps answer the question of \n why we should do what the author is telling us.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Identify Illustrations/Examples:
    • \n
\n
\n

Sometimes the author gives an illustration or example to support his main \n point. Paul does this in Galatians by using Abraham as an example of the principle \n that righteousness is by faith. In 3:6 he tells the Galatians, \"Even \n so Abraham believed God, and it was reckoned to him as righteousness.\" \n Paul uses Abraham as an example of the principle he’s trying to communicate. \n Whenever the author quotes Scriptures, he is citing an illustration or example. \n

\n
\n

Content/ Theology: Muscle

\n
\n

In this section we are not as concerned with how something is said or organized \n as we are with what is said. This is what we normally think of as interpretation. \n Think again of the passage as a body. The structure or the way the body is \n put together is the skeletal framework. The muscle that hangs on the skeleton \n is its content. As we’ve gone through identifying the structure of the \n passage, we’ve also done some of the work in examining the content. In \n identifying connective words and supporting ideas, we are examining the structure. \n When we ask what or to whom those ideas or words refer and what they mean, \n we are examining the content.

\n
\n

Hints: \n

    \n
  • Identify important words and phrases
    • \n
\n
\n

In this part of our study, we will want to gain clarity on exactly what is \n being said and what it means. One way that we do this is by clarifying important \n or unfamiliar words. It is easy to get bogged down in word studies because \n nearly every word seems interesting. Be selective in the words you choose \n to study, usually one to three words in any passage is sufficient.

\n
\n\n
\n

If you’re unfamiliar with a word, maybe you’ve never seen the word \n before or maybe you’ve just never heard a good definition for it, take \n the time to look it up in a good Bible dictionary. You may be surprised at \n the rewards you reap by clarifying unfamiliar words.
\n If you're studying Galatians chapter three you may wonder what exactly Paul \n means by the word \"blessed.\" This is a word that Paul draws from \n the Old Testament text but it is also a word that Jesus uses. With a relatively \n small amount of work you can discover that when Paul says \"So then those \n who are of faith are blessed with Abraham, the believer\" he is referring \n to much more than an emotional state. The word is used of the gift that would \n come to the Gentiles through Abraham's willingness to be used by God. It is \n a theologically loaded term that finds its fulfillment in Jesus Christ. Paul \n is saying that faith produces the righteousness of Abraham.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Strategically important words:
    • \n
\n
\n

One way to determine if a word is important is if it’s emphasized or \n repeated by the author. In studying the book of Romans the word justification \n appears numerous times and in many different forms. If we are to understand \n the meaning of the book of Romans, justification would be an important \n concept to understand.

\n

Another way that we decide the importance of a word is based on the role \n it plays in the author’s thought. Does the paragraph or sentence we’re \n studying somehow hinge on an understanding of this word?

\n

After you’ve studied the important or unfamiliar words, how does your \n new-found appreciation for this word contribute to your understanding of the \n passage. Eventually we’ll also want to ask how it affects our understanding \n of the book.

\n

    \n
  • Identify theological/historical contexts
    • \n
\n
\n

As you think about theological or historical contexts, think about the big \n picture. What other passages talk about these issues? What other passages \n might be relevant to the passage you are studying? Do other scriptures speak \n to the same issues that your passage addresses?

\n

Does the author quote from Scripture or refer to an historical event? If \n so, what purpose does he quote for? It is important to always look up the \n passage being quoted in its original context.

\n
\n
\n

.
\n

\n
\n
\n

This is the place to clarify or research historical or geographic references \n with which you are unfamiliar. A good Bible dictionary is helpful in this \n regard and will often provide insights into the text that you would otherwise \n miss.

\n

Look up quotations from the Old Testament that the author uses. What is the \n context of the original statement and how does the original context bear on \n the author’s meaning? Why does the author say what he does? If the author \n gives an imperative without providing a theological reason, it is important \n to ask what is the theological or cultural basis for the command. We have \n looked at how he says it and what he says, but it also important to ask why \n he says it. The \"why\" is often rooted in the nature of God.

Application: Skin

\n \n

How is the argument of the passage intended to affect the original audience \n in terms of actions, attitudes and convictions?

\n
\n

Let's recall the main point of the passage. If there is an imperative in \n the passage, what is author calling on his audience to do? The desired effects \n may be spelled out plainly by the author but it is important to pause and \n ask how the application relates to the historical situation of the audience. \n If the author is telling them to do something is it because they weren't doing \n it at the time? How would their circumstances relate to what they were or \n were not doing?

\n
\n \n

What similarities exist between the original audience and myself or my audience?

\n \n
\n

Before we can move to asking how this passage applies to my audience, or \n to myself, we need to examine the similarities between our situation and that \n of the original audience. (The analogy of skin is appropriate here because \n as circumstances and cultures change the application of a biblical principle \n will take on different manifestations. However, what does not change is the \n underlying structure of the author's argument--skeleton--and theological content--musculature. \n In fact, this underlying framework will determine the shape that our application \n takes in a given context. To carry this analogy further, what we want to avoid \n is introducing a layer of fat that distorts the theological shape and results \n in a culturally misappropriate application.)

\n
\n

Is the passage directly applicable to my audience because we are in the \n same situation as the original audience or is it only applicable in principle? \n

\n
\n

The reason we ask this question is because epistles are occasional documents, \n the writers are in the process of applying truths to specific life circumstances. \n Because our circumstances may differ from the original audience’s, we \n cannot necessarily make a one-to-one correlation. Epistles can be considered \n \"case studies\" which apply biblical principles to specific situations \n (Ericson, LMS, p 250); in as much as our current situation mirrors \n that of the original audience, we can apply the passage directly.

\n

But when our circumstances differ significantly, we often need to work from \n the principle being given in order to apply it to our situation.

\n \n

Examples: Eph 5:18 drunk with wine/head-covering

\n
\n
\n

Why is it that most women do not wear headcoverings in the church today even \n though Paul explicitly commands it? The reason has to do with cultural relevance. \n Paul was giving a practical expression of a spiritual truth, namely that we \n should—in this case women should—respect authority. The principle \n of authority (1 Cor. 11:3) is tied to a spiritual truth but the way that this \n respect is shown is in keeping with the cultural norms. 1Cor. 11:5,14, Paul \n is telling the church specifically not to go against cultural norms in expressing \n this principle of submission to authority. However, outward expression of \n this principle in dress or manner is no longer relevant today because it is \n not something our culture practices and yet the biblical principle remains \n true and should find expression in the attitudes and interactions between \n a husband and a wife.

\n
\n \n

How is the intent of this passage relevant to my audience or myself?

\n \n
\n

Recall what the main point of the passage is. It is important to apply what \n the text meant to apply; often this is found in an imperative statement.

\n

To what life issues does the passage speak? Think of specific situations \n in which this passage or its principle would apply in your life and be as \n specific as possible. We often limit our thinking to theological truths contained \n in the passage and then fail to apply the obvious imperatives contained in \n God’s word.

B. Main Point.

Theology

\n \n

Define:

\n
\n

Blessed—lit. \"good word\". Paul is not so much praising God \n as telling us that He is worthy of praise--which he elaborate upon in following \n verses.

\n

Predestined--to decide beforehand. Probably synonymous with vs. 3 \"chose\". \n The word will not yield a theological understanding, i.e., if God predestined \n us do we have free-will?

\n

Redemption—deliverance from bondage by payment of a price (or ransoming \n slaves, Ex 21). For us the price was high. It cost the life of our Lord Jesus \n Christ, \"through His blood\", because we were slaves to death. But \n God did not begrudge us this, His grace, \"He lavished upon us.\"

\n

Sealed--a seal denotes ownership, it is a sign which protects us. As Paul \n says elsewhere, \"If God is for us, who is against us?…Who shall \n separate us from the love of Christ?\"

\n
\n

Meaning:

\n \n
\n

This paragraph moves from eternity to eternity (O'Brien, p. 92).

\n

Everything given to us—and we have been given everything spiritually—has \n been given us in Christ. We are incredibly rich, in spiritual terms. We have \n been given these riches because Christ has been given these riches. We receive \n them as mediated through Christ. Everything we receive comes \"in Christ\" \n (11x).
\n

\n
\n

We receive them on three levels: past, present, and future.

\n

Past:

\n
\n

\"chosen for adoption\". To whom does this refer? Does this refer \n to individuals or the church? (Chosenness also requires a response vv. 4, \n 13. • Did God choose only those who would respond? • Did God choose \n without regard to our response? • Did God choose more generally (church: \n Jews & Gentiles) and our response decides whether we are included in that \n group? We don’t know the answers to these questions. We do know that \n we have been chosen in God somehow and yet it is also somehow dependent on \n our willingness to respond to the message.)
\n

\n
\n

Present:

\n
\n

Revelation, inheritance (:11) and redemption (:7).

\n
\n

Future:

\n
\n

Inheritance and redemption (:14)
\n

\n

God deserves praise and honor for what all he’s given us.

\n
\n \n

Application

\n \n

Them:

\n \n
\n

Praise God! (:6, 12, 14)

\n

Know what is true about you.

\n
\n \n

Us:

\n \n
\n

Meditating on all that God has given us, in specific detail, will produce \n gratitude and praise toward God. And a natural result of this would be to \n live our lives for His glory (but that’s getting ahead of our author.) \n Paul is content to simply spend some time praising God.

\n

Know what’s true of us has real-life value. Meditate on these things! \n

\n

God had us--He had you--in mind when he created the universe. He had determined \n to redeem a people to Himself before He ever laid the foundations of the world. \n And He knew that we would be a part of that.
\n He chose to adopt us. He chose too buy us out of slavery--not to sin, primarily--but \n to death, to redeem and forgive us. He didn't have to do this. He wasn't obligated \n to redeem a rebellious and obstinate and thoroughly hateful people. But He \n did.

\n

And beyond that He revealed to us His will. He made Himself known so that \n we wouldn't be in darkness, so that we wouldn't be worshipping the gods of \n the skies for rain or fertility. He revealed Himself so that we wouldn't be \n worshipping in futility, hoping that somehow we might appease Him--that we \n might figure out exactly what he wants so that we can avoid His anger.

\n

Beyond all of this, He's given us an inheritance in the spiritual realm. \n I recently came into an inheritance from my Grandmother, which was a nice \n surprise. I knew I'd get a few hundred dollars. As it turned out, it was much \n more than I expected. I was shocked. God's inheritance will be so much more. \n But it is there and it is waiting for me. And it is something I can be certain \n of because God has sealed me in Christ with His Holy Spirit. It is the Spirit \n of God that holds me firmly in place. As a young believer, I feared that I \n might let go of God. I failed to realize how strong is God's grip on me. It's \n not just me walking through this life, making my decisions, God's Spirit moves \n inside me and holds me close to Himself.
\n

\n

Praise God!

\n

How do you respond to \"Praise God\"? Get used to it, Rev 4. God \n is awesome. This is a worthy goal of a teaching. If you could do no more than \n inspire people to praise God for all he's done, I'd consider that successful. \n
