Homiletics \n Worksheet

\n

\nClarify the Thesis: \n

    \n
  • Central purpose of passage:
    • \n
\n \n

          What is \n the central point to be considered from the text?

\n
\n

Write down ideas until you mold an adequate and evocative thesis. All of \n the sub-points and illustrations should contribute to your central thesis.
\n State the thesis of the passage early and often in your teaching. Each sub-point \n must refer back to the main point.

\n
\n
    \n
  • Goal of teaching:
    \n Why is the teaching of this passage important: biblical clarification, \n correction or defending an attitude, belief or behavior. Goal is not always \n stated, but impacts how material is organized.
    \n
    • \n
\n

Examining the passage: \n

    \n
  • Summarize. What is the historical, theological or literary context \n as it relates to your thesis?
    \n What background does your audience need to have before they understand the \n passage?
    \n What extra information would make the passage come alive?
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Explanation/Support. What are the reasons given in the text that \n support the thesis?
    \n How does the author go about making his point?
    • \n
\n

          Clarify important words/theological \n concepts.

\n
\n

What is the passage saying or presupposing about key theological \n issues. Clarification should include illustration and antithesis where appropriate \n or relevant. This may also be a good place for group input.

\n

What did this passage mean to its original audience?

\n
\n
    \n
  • Application. Discussion questions to get at the practical importance \n of the study.
    • \n
\n
\n

What does it mean for us today in the local/universal church?

\n

As I was studying this passage, what occurred to me? How did it challenge \n me?

\n

How would my life be different if I acted on the basis of this truth? In \n what positive direction may God be calling on me to act?

Develop an Introduction \n \n
