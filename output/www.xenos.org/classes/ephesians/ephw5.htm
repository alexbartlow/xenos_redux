Theology

\n \n

Define:

\n \n
\n

\"Obey\" is a stronger word than submit. It is unconditional, usually \n applied to our response to the Lord, whereas submit implies a preceding dialogue.
\n

\n
\n
\n

\"Children\" deals with a relationship and not an age group.
\n

\n
\n
\n

Exod. 20:12; Deut. 5:16

\n
\n \n

Meaning:

\n \n
\n

This passage flows from the \"submit to one another\" verse in 5:21. \n It elaborates on what Christian relationships look like specifically within \n the family. The main thrust of the passage seems to be \"obey\" while \n the instruction to the fathers is almost an add-on for balance. Really, they \n are two sides to the same coin. Yet, obey flows most directly out of \"submit\" \n in 5:21 that acts as a header for this section.

\n
\n
\n

How is \"honor\" the first commandment? It is actually the fifth \n commandment in Exodus 20. It is the first commandment given that carries a \n promise with it.

\n
\n \n

Parallels:

\n \n
\n

Col. 3:20-21

Application

\n \n

Them:

\n

Us:

\n \n
\n

God promises to bless us as we obey our parents. We are promised long life \n and prosperity. God honors a respect for authority. It is part of the order \n of the universe and it is right. Don’t mouth off to your parents or dishonor \n them in a subtle way. This applies to us as adults just as well as when we’re \n children.

\n

Responsibility is never one way. Always within a relationship there are responsibilities \n for both parties, for children and parents, husbands and wives.

\n
\n
\n

We as parents, especially fathers, are commissioned to be involved in the \n lives of our children. It is not something that we can pawn off to our wives. \n Practically we can do this by spending time with our children, also by talking \n about the Lord with them—not just talking about ministry.

Theology

\n \n

Define:

\n \n

The repetition of the word \"stand\" echoes the repetition of the enemies \n assault which is unrelenting. (Pay attention to repetition it is usually there \n for a reason.)

\n
\n

\"Struggle\" palh, is a term common to \n wrestling in the first century. It highlights the intimate nature of the conflict. \n This war is not fought at a distance it is fought in our mind and in our circumstances. \n

\n
\n
\n

\"Gird your loins\" refers to God’s truth. The metaphor is to \n the girdle or jock strap upon which all of the soldier’s armor hung. \n While not being a piece of armor it is the crucial piece of armor.

\n

\"Breastplate\" refers to God’s righteousness secured through \n Christ’s death on our behalf.

\n

\"Shod feet\" refers to the Roman caliga a tool which gave \n their army significant advantage.
\n

\n

\"Preparation of gospel of peace\": Does it mean that we have prepared \n the gospel or that the gospel has prepared us? What does it have to do with \n our feet? The Greek is vague but the OT context helps. Is. 52:7

\n

\"How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those…\"

\n

It is a readiness to proclaim the message of peace, the message of good news. \n In its original context there is a horizontal element as well as a vertical \n element. Fittingly, there is a horizontal and vertical element in Ephesians \n as well.

\n

\"Shield\" refers to the qureoV or body \n shield. In Greek the word \"door\" is related to this word. It covered \n the entire person. The shield was made of wood and covered with metal straps \n and leather to extinguish flaming arrows. Refers to appropriating God’s \n promises on our behalf.

\n

\"Helmet\" was a bronze piece that covered the neck and extended \n over the eyes and cheeks. It refers to salvation, the fact that we are alive \n in and seated with Christ by grace.

\n

\"Sword\" refers to a short dagger for use in close combat situations. \n This sword is supplied by the Spirit and refers to the word, or rhma \n of God, being the word proclaimed.
\n We are to speak forth the message of peace—ironically—into \n the darkness in order to liberate those in bondage.

\n

Is. 11:4-5; 59:17 The armor we are instructed to put on is God’s own \n armor which both Yahweh and the Messiah are depicted as wearing in the OT.

\n
\n \n

Meaning:

\n \n
\n

The letter to the Ephesians comes to its conclusion in this section, 6:10-20. \n As with much of this letter, there is an already-not-yet tension in this material. \n While defeated, \"the prince of the power of the air\" still rules \n the day which is why Paul says \"the days are evil\" in 5:16. We need \n to appropriate the victory that has been won and stand firm in the Lord. We \n can overcome the devil because God has ultimately overcome him and because \n God provides the power, the same power that raised Christ from the dead.

\n
\n
\n

It is the \"rulers and authorities in the heavenly places\" which \n oppose the spread of the gospel and the \"worthy living\" to which \n we are called. The evil one opposes our walk with God. \"Put on\" \n the armor of God parallels the idea of \"putting on\" the new self \n created to be like God in holiness, righteousness and truth. \"Essentially, \n then, to ‘put on the new self’ is the same as donning the armour \n of God,\" (O’Brien, p 462).

\n
\n
\n

Because this warfare is spiritual it is fought with Spiritual weapons. It \n is fought primarily on the playing field of truth. Our position in Christ, \n the indicative truths which Paul has spent so much time elaborating on, is \n crucial to victory. It is crucial to our ability to stand firm in the battle. \n If we cannot stand firm or if we lose our footing in the battle, we’re \n cooked. Once we are able to stand firm, we can then fight a war that is waged \n offensively through biblical truth and prayer.

\n
\n
\n

The last and critical element in our struggle is prayer. This element is \n not subordinated to the pieces of armor but it is separate in order to give \n it prominence.

\n

Application

\n \n

Them:

\n \n

Us:

\n
\n

In order to stand firm there are a few things we need to do, namely we need \n to prepare ourselves with the armor God has provided.

\n

One of the ways in which the evil one would derail us is through temptation. \n Let’s relate these concepts back to chapter 4 where Paul tells us to \n put on the new self. Part of the wiles of the devil are to undermine our spiritual \n growth with God through temptation or opposition. Things that might derail \n our spiritual lives would be disunity, sensuality, greed, anger and unedifying \n speech.

\n

To stand firm against the wiles of the devil requires a life of prayer! (Consider \n how Paul has modeled this in his letter.)
\n

\n

Do you pray for all the saints? God calls on us as mature believers to stand \n in the gap.
\n

\n
\n
\n

We ought also to ask for prayer for ourselves as we approach the battle. \n Consider that Paul as he writes this is waiting to make his defense, with \n all boldness, before Caesar!

Election: What does it mean?

\n
\n

We have been chosen as God’s people- a new humanity in Him. The church \n is God’s elect in the same way that Israel was God’s elect. God \n didn’t choose specific individuals to be Israelites, nor did he choose \n us to be Christians. He chose the church and he predestined her to be one \n with Christ, a new humanity.
