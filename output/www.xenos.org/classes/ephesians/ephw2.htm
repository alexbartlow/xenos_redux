Theology

\n \n

Define:

\n \n
\n

\"Hope\" specifically dealing with our hope in Christ that we will \n be spared God's judgment, Rom 5:4-9. But more than that it includes the \"riches \n of the glory of His inheritance.\"

\n

\"Power\", the word in Greek is (                  \n ) which is where we get the word, dynamite. But this power of God's is not \n an explosive, quick-burning power. It is the power that sustains the universe. \n It is the power that raised Christ from the dead and it is the power by which \n he will eventually subdue the created order in Christ.

\n
\n \n

Meaning:

\n \n
\n

Father of glory is a title that basically means that God is the source of \n all glory-everything that is marvelous and powerful comes from God. Glory \n is often synonymous with power.

\n

Paul prays that they might understand the blessings that they’ve been \n given: hope, riches, and power. The hope Paul refers to begins with God’s \n calling a people to Himself and culminates with our resurrection in Christ. \n

\n

The inheritance Paul refers to is God’s inheritance, namely, us. It \n is in the church, made of both Jews and Gentiles that God will display to \n the universe His incredibly rich blessings. We will be trophies of grace. \n Think of this in terms of from where we’ve come.

\n

Last, all of God’s power at work in Christ’s resurrection is at \n work in us. There are no spiritual forces that can stand against God’s \n power. Christ reigns supreme over the church and over the universe.

\n
\n \n

Parallel:

\n \n
\n

Col 1:9 For this reason also, since the day we heard of it, we have \n not ceased to pray for you and to ask that you may be filled with the knowledge \n of His will in all spiritual wisdom and understanding…

Application:

\n \n

Them:

\n \n
\n

What is important for this church is that they grow, not in their knowledge \n of esoteric truths or biblical history, but in their knowing of God. Knowledge \n of God is not knowledge about God; it is not academic in the \n Greek sense of knowing—that is how we tend to use the word today. Knowledge \n of God is a relational knowing.

\n

This holds for us too. Doing God’s work, serving people and making disciples \n of Christ, can never be done at the expense of cultivating a relationship \n with God. And yet, our knowledge of God, our ability to know Him personally, \n is tied to our obedience. Therefore, if we want to grow we cannot do one or \n the other, contemplate God or serve Him, we must do both. There is always \n an active part of faith. To understand God intimately, we have to trust Him \n and that means to obey Him.

\n
\n \n

Us:

\n \n
\n

Do you pray this for those you are discipling/serving? I hope our goal in \n making mature followers of Christ is first and foremost that they come to \n know God in deeper ways. Out of this knowledge of God, if it is truly a \"knowing \n God\", out of this will flow sacrifice and service to others.

\n
\n
\n

The same power that was at work in the resurrection and glorification of \n Christ is the power at work in our lives.

\n
\n \n

Implications:

\n \n
\n

We can do God’s work with confidence, we can have confidence in God’s \n ability to change us. Christ has supreme authority over the universe and over \n us. To become cynical about our ability to see change occur in our lives is \n to demean God’s power. Do you struggle with being cynical that God can \n change you? You haven’t grasped the nature of God’s power.

Theology

\n \n

Define:

\n \n
\n

\"In the heavenly places\" is used 5 times in Ephesians and only \n in Ephesians (1:3, 20; 2:6; 3:10; 6:12). It denotes both the realm of God \n and the realm of God’s adversary in \"the principalities and powers.\" \n This expression says that something is true about us in the spiritual realm. \n Because we are \"in Chris\", what is true of Christ is true of us. \n

\n

\"In Christ\" denotes the idea of headship. We are in Christ’s \n lineage and what is true of Him is true of us. This idea of lineage has a \n greater communicative force in ancient times when one’s lineage said \n everything about the person. In our American thinking, lineage means next \n to nothing—here we have striven to erase such traditional concepts. As \n with Abraham and Isaac, it is the heir who receives the inheritance, as well \n as carries on the name or reputation of the family. So we both have an \n inheritance \"in the heavenly places\" and we carry on the family \n reputation here on earth, hence we are God’s workmanship, \n created for good works.

\n
\n

Meaning:

\n
\n

Even though we were formerly dead, God created us alive and exulted us with \n Christ and seated us in power in Christ. Contrary to Watchman Nee \"seated\" \n does not indicate a state of rest, rather one of power and conquest over one’s \n adversaries. (Paul will get to the implications of this in Chapter 6 when \n he deals with spiritual warfare.) What has taken place will result in incomprehensible \n outpouring of God’s incredible riches and kindness toward us—this \n is a result of nothing more than a gift (grace). Here for the first time in \n the letter, Paul indicates to us that our state of blessing carries with it \n certain obligations, noblesse oblige. God has created good works for \n us to take part in. Paul will begin to move in that direction. (But first \n he will remind us of from where we’ve come, a true rags to riches story.)

\n

That was the way of our life when we \n were dead. Yet now we have been shown mercy and grace. Shall we reject God’s \n mercy and live in the past?

\n
\n
\n

In contrast to our old way of life, God has created us for good works. These \n are what we ought to seek out.

\"peace\" Refers to a general well-being of a person and environment. \n It is a characteristic of the messianic kingdom, thus used for salvation in \n Luke (1:79; 2:14; 17:42). Christ is peace (Is. 57:19) Is. 9:6 Prince of peace… \n Micah 5:5 \"But as for you Bethlehem…And this One will be our peace. \n Is. 52:7 \"How lovely…are the feet of him who brings good news, who \n announces peace…\" who announces salvation. \n

The dividing wall is the law which is nullified through Christ's death, (\"which \n is law of commands\" see outline). The barrier is overcome by the blood \n of Christ. This law is symbolically portrayed in the temple structure itself \n (see Josephus, Jewish Wars 5.194).

There is a radical separation of Jew and Gentile in the 1st century \n because of the law. Jews and Gentiles could not eat together; they could not \n really touch or be personal with each other. This is by design and it also \n is symbolic of our separation from God, not just from His people.

Explain 1:15:

\n \n

\"Since the time I’ve heard about your faith in the Lord Jesus \n and your love for all the saints, have never stopped giving thanks for you…\" \n

\n
\n

It is likely that this letter was not written to the Ephesian church per \n se. Many original manuscripts do not have \"Ephesian\" in the address \n line. It was probably a circular letter intended for those living in the Lycus \n valley near Ephesus.

\n

Ephesians bears remarkable similarity to the book of Colossians. Many similar \n expressions are used. Tychicus is also the bearer of the letter to the Colossians, \n 4:7-8.

\n

There is one other interesting point worth noting. The early church heretic, \n Marcion, referred to this letter as the letter to the Laodiceans. Colossians \n refers to a letter to be read that was coming from Laodicea, 4:6. Some have \n concluded that this is that letter.

\n
\n \n

What do we know about them?

\n \n
\n

We know that they’re primarily Gentile from Paul’s references to \n the \"uncircumcised.\"

\n
\n

Situation: Why was this letter written?

\n
\n

The cult of Artemis was powerful in Ephesus and the surrounding areas. In \n the ancient world everyone worshipped some sort of God in order to bring fertility, \n rain or safety. In this kind of environment there was little assurance that \n your prayers would be heard or that you could appease the \"gods\". \n In Christ, we have confidence that we are at peace with God, that we are his \n heirs and that God has ultimate spiritual authority. One of the themes of \n Ephesians, not found elsewhere in NT, is one of \"the spiritual powers \n in the heavenly places.\" Paul seems to write in part to instruct his \n audience of the power and standing they have in Christ.

\n

Most likely this was a circular letter. It was carried by Tychicus and probably \n circulated around much of Asia Minor. For that reason, we don’t see personal \n greetings and we don’t see Paul addressing an issue specific to any one \n church, but rather issues that are of interest to a wider Gentile audience. \n Paul writes to shape their identities as Christians (O’Brien, p. 57), \n This is what it looks like to be a Christian. This is who you are.
