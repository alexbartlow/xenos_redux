Evangelism Workshop #1
\n Initiating\n

\n

Gary DeLashmutt and Jeff Gordon

\n 		\n \n \n \n \n \n \n
\n\n\n\n\n\n

To succeed in evangelism we must be bold in our faith.  Yet\n most of us struggle to some degree with fear of rejection when we think about\n sharing our faith.  This may be the biggest barrier that we face!  Timothy\n was a fearful, yet effective minister for Christ.  Paul wrote to Timothy to\n offer encouragement:

\n\n

2 Timothy 1:7,8  “For\n God has not given us a spirit of timidity, but of power and love and discipline.  Therefore\n do not be ashamed of the testimony of our Lord, or of me His prisoner; but\n join with me in suffering for\n the gospel according to the power of God . . .”

\n\n

Key points from\n this passage that you should discuss in your group include:

\n

God’s part: Paul directs\n Timothy to God’s part in evangelism (“power and love and discipline. . .\n according to the power of God. . .”).  To cultivate boldness, we must know\n and focus on what God promises to do in this area.  God’s power is manifest\n in evangelism in four ways:

\n

·He\n DRAWS people to seek God

\n

·He\n CONNECTS us with seekers

\n

·He\n EMPOWERS us as we witness

\n

·He\n CONVICTS people after we witness

\n

Share\n the scriptures that teach these truths.  Discuss how you have experienced\n God’s part in evangelism in your own life.

\n

Our part: Paul also calls on Timothy to do his\n part (“do not be ashamed of the testimony. . . join with me in suffering for\n the gospel.”).  We need to express our trust that God will do his part by actually\n stepping out in faith to be his witnesses!  

\n

Sometimes we feel\n that it will take a long time to regain our confidence in evangelism, but\n reaffirming God’s part and stepping out in faith will usually produce an\n immediate increase in confidence.  The following sections are meant to be\n covered in a cell group or workers’ meeting format.  You will probably need\n two weeks to cover this material.

\n\n

Questions\n that may initiate Spiritual Conversations

\n\n

Many people are willing to talk about spiritual matters,\n but only rarely will they initiate a spiritual conversation with you.  One\n way we can provide them with this opportunity is to ask spiritual questions.  Here\n are some interesting and thought-provoking questions that could help you start\n a spiritual conversation.  We are not suggesting a formula; each of these should\n be put in your own words so that you speak from your heart.  Remember that\n your goal is not necessarily to share the gospel in this conversation, but\n to make the person more comfortable discussing spiritual things—which will\n likely lead to further discussions.  Discuss these ideas in your cell group\n and consider other ways to pose the questions to get at the same spiritual\n issue.

\n\n

1.“Do you\n have any type of spiritual belief?”  OR “Do you have a spiritual\n category for that?”  Through this type of question we learn what they\n believe.  The most important thing to do is to listen carefully and\n ask follow-up questions.    Often you get \"I'm a Catholic, Methodist,\n etc\" response.   You could then ask them, “What was you’re your\n experience like growing up Catholic?” 
\n
\n

\n

2.\"What\n kind of spiritual background/church did you have growing up?\"  This\n may be an easier question to pursue with some people than #1.  Most people\n are willing to answer it because it simply asks for historical information\n and does not call on them to take a position on a system of belief.
\n
\n

\n

3.“If you\n died this week, where would you go?” OR “What do you think happens\n to people when they die?”   Discussions about the afterlife are\n often very helpful.  Consider following up this question with,  “Why\n do you think you would go there?” 
\n
\n

\n

4.“If what\n you believe is not true, would you want to know?”   Through\n this question we can find whether they are ready to take a new step.  Those\n who have experience asking this question have found that most people answer\n “Yes” and are open.  If the individual says, “No,” we need to carefully read\n their response and decide whether to ask them why, or simply move to a different\n subject. 

\n\n

5.“Would\n you mind if I shared something that someone told me?” OR “Is\n it ok if I tell you about my spiritual beliefs?”  If they don’t want\n to pursue this, let it go.  But if they are open, you can proceed with the\n gospel.  Always use scripture and if possible open your Bible and allow\n them to read the scripture you refer to.  The paper The Logic of Salvation (http://www.xenos.org/classes/papers/logic.htm) provides an excellent framework\n for presenting the Gospel.
\n
\n

\n

6.“Who\n do you think (believe) Jesus is(was)?”  If Jesus comes up in the discussion,\n then this type of question can be very helpful.  This is another way to get\n to the Gospel as you describe the unique person and mission of Jesus.
\n
\n

\n

7.\"What\n kind of evidence would it take for you to be willing to invite Jesus into\n your heart?\"People\n often don't know how to answer this question--but that in itself may help\n them.  It also gives the asker the opportunity to share how much evidence\n they had before they asked Christ--and what happened when they did.
\n
\n

\n

8.“Do you\n ever pray?” OR “Would you be willing to pray about what we’ve been\n discussing?”  If they say, “Yes,” encourage them to simply call out to\n God and ask that he reveal himself.  You might share John 7:17, “If any man\n is willing to do His will, he shall know of the teaching, whether it is of\n God, or whether I speak from Myself.”
\n
\n

\n

9.“Would\n it be ok with you if I pray for you right now?”  This will open the door\n for you to bring God into the situation and demonstrate a personal prayer.  In\n your prayer you can take their personal questions or concerns to God.  You\n will be amazed how often non-Christians are deeply moved by this type of\n personal prayer in their presence.  Consider asking them if they would like\n to pray aloud (or silently) with you. 
\n
\n

\n

10.“What do you want to accomplish\n in life?” OR “What is the purpose of life (or a specific part of your\n life)?”  These questions get at the issue of life purpose which is a\n hot topic today.  The fact the Rich Warren’s Purpose Driven Life is\n a New York Times Best Seller demonstrates that people are considering this\n issue.  You might consider suggesting that your friend read this book.
\n 

\n

Questions like these will prime the pump, but we must\n be good listeners to establish good conversations.  In James 1:19 we read,\n “This you know, my beloved brethren. But let everyone be quick to hear,\n slow to speak and slow to anger. . .”  Good listeners often find receptivity\n to their point of view since they show concern and respect for the other person.\n [See Watchman Nee. Character of God's Workman. Chapter on Listening].

\n

Exercise:
\n After discussing these questions in the group, have each person take about\n fifteen minutes to prayerfully consider the content in view of their non-Christian\n friends.   Then they should determine how they would put the content in their\n own words and write out a few questions.  They could share these with the\n group or you could break up into groups of three or four to share their questions.

\n


\n From Chit-chat to the Gospel

\n


\n Common conversation provides many opportunities to explore spiritual issues.  Some\n of us have the misconception that a deep personal relationship is needed to\n discuss spiritual things.  As a result, we have spiritual conversations with\n non-Christians only rarely—and little evangelistic fruit.  Many of us need\n to be more open about our spiritual interests in casual conversations so we\n have opportunities to find receptive people.  When it comes right down to it,\n most adults are so busy that it’s very difficult to build a close friendship\n outside the church.  If we wait to get close to someone before we mention spiritual\n issues, we won’t be discussing spiritual issues very often. 

\n\n

We need to be willing to talk as naturally and openly\n about our relationship with Christ as we would any other important aspect of\n our lives.  This paper provides a few suggestions for how we can apply this\n principle. 
\n
\n Review these in your cell group and with each discuss other ideas for the\n particular situation.  Encourage each member to think of how they could apply\n these ideas to their situation.

\n\n

Responses to common questions:

\n

Creative responses test for spiritual interest.  We\n need to be positive, open, and enthusiastic about our spiritual life.  Most\n of us fear that people will think we’re some kind of nut if we are enthusiastic\n about the Lord, but we need to put that fear behind us and not be so inhibited.  Here\n are a few examples of responses to common questions:

\n\n
    \n
  1. What have been up to lately?  What’s goin’ on?
    2. \n
      \n
    1. I’m learning some great things in this Bible study I’ve\n been attending.
      2. \n
    2. Discuss recent spiritual insights.  EG:
      3. \n
    \n
\n

·Our\n marriage is making some progress thanks to what I’m learning from the Bible.

\n

·I’ve\n had a major breakthrough in my life since I’ve learned about God’s forgiveness.

\n

·I’m\n getting some control over my anger thanks to God and a few good friends.

\n

·My\n wife and I have some really good friends that we’ve made through our church.  It’s\n made our family life a lot better.

\n
    \n
  1. How’s the family?  The patent answer is “Great. . .”  “John\n is playing soccer.  Beth got a scholarship to Harvard.  Albert is going to\n OSU.”  People put their best foot forward.  Where’s the reality of family\n life?
    2. \n
      \n
    1. We’re doing ok, but it’s been a real struggle for me\n trying to learn how to parent.  Why don’t they send an owner’s manual with\n these kids?
      2. \n
    2. We’re struggling in our marriage, but thanks to a few\n good friends and God’s help we’re making some progress.
      3. \n
    3. I’ve made some big mistakes financially and now we’re\n working hard to get out from under it.  I’ve been studying the Bible and\n getting God’s perspective on my money, so I believe there’s hope.
      4. \n
    \n
\n

Responses like these will\n disarm people and open many doors to spiritual conversation.

\n
    \n
  1. What are you doing this weekend? 
    \n Don’t be afraid to discuss the spiritual things we pursue?
    2. \n
      \n
    1. Sunday I’m going to our church.  I don’t like to miss\n that because I learn something about every week that’s helpful.
      2. \n
    2. We’re going camping with a group of friends from our\n church.  We’ve gotten to be really close over the years and it’s a great\n experience.
      3. \n

Initiating Natural Conversation about Spiritual\n Issues

\n

Besides responding to these basic questions as we’ve\n discussed above, we should also open and forthright about their relationship\n with God.  We need to be willing to discuss our relationship with God like\n we would other things in our life that we’re excited about.  For example, if\n you attended a great concert, you would naturally and enthusiastically tell\n your friends and co-workers about it.  In the same way we need to be open about\n the excitement that comes from faithfully following Christ.

\n\n

Consider the way God is\n personally affecting you and think how you could introduce the topic into\n conversation.  These may be areas that we find encouraging and exciting,\n but on the other hand challenging and perplexing issues can and sometimes\n should be discussed with our non-Christian friends.  Examples:

\n
    \n
  • I’ve been experiencing God’s peace when I used to be overwhelmed\n with anxiety.
    • \n
  • I just can’t get over how wonderful it is to have Christian\n friends who show me God’s love.
    • \n
  • Parenting has been very difficult lately and the hardships\n have made me wrestle with God’s faithfulness in my life.
    • \n
  • I’ve been perplexed as I’ve tried to figure out God’s will\n for my career. 
    • \n
  • My wife and I are realizing that we could relate a lot\n better if we prayed together more often.
    • \n
\n\n

Reading their response

\n

Reading responses in conversation takes a lot of experience\n and there’s no real formula to follow, but these guidelines will be helpful\n to consider:

\n

·If\n they express interest in spiritual things then feel free to share more, including\n the gospel and/or inviting them to an event or Bible study.  Consider inviting\n them to study the Bible with you one-on-one too.

\n

·If\n they respond negatively, this is a great opportunity to understand why\n they are negative about what you shared.  Ask questions to identify the source\n of their negativity: previous bad experiences, misconceptions, etc.  This will\n provide an opportunity for you to share the difference between biblical Christianity\n and their misconceptions. 

\n

·If\n they have no response, we should not push at this point, but remain confident\n that because God is at work our friend may be open to future spiritual conversations.  You\n will probably feel more confident to discuss spiritual issues in the future.
