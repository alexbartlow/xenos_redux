Week 5: Psalms of Praise [1] 

\n\n

Overview

\n\n

Psalms of praise (yadah - the verb meaning, 'to\npraise') are identified as such because of their form and subject matter. Praise psalms\nare often more general in their content and more focused on who God is rather than what he\nhas done. When God's actions are described, they are frequently used to detail aspects of\nGod's character, which are then praised by the psalmist. Inevitably, there is overlap\nbetween these genres, however, close reading of praise and thanksgiving literature does\nshow differences.  \n\n

\n\n
\n
    \n
  • A call to praise (directed toward self or others) 
    • \n
  • Reasons for the praise (e.g. attributes of God or deeds of\n God) 
    • \n
  • Conclusion - often a repetition of the opening call to\n praise (i.e. inclusio). 
    • \n
\n
\n\n
    \n
  • Types of praise psalms include: 
    • \n
\n\n
\n
    \n
  • Hymns[2]  - songs of praise 
    • \n
  • Covenant Songs - psalms directed toward the covenant\n relationships between God and others 
    • \n
  • Hymns of Zion/Temple - psalms focused on Jerusalem\n the place of the presence of God. 
    • \n
  • Royal Psalms - These focus on the human king of\n Israel or upon YHWH as king of Israel (47:7) 
    • \n
  • Messianic Psalms - these psalms attend to predictions\n concerning God's anointed representative who is yet to come or the kingdom he will rule.\n  
    • \n
\n
\n\n
    \n
  • Some psalms have elements of more than one genre.[3]

  • The importance of praise - many of the same things may be\n said of praise as of gratitude.

    Covenant Psalms

    \n\n

    God's covenant relationship with Israel is a key theme in\nthe Old Testament.[6] \nA covenant (berit) is an agreement, a pact or contract between two parties. Often,\ncovenants between God and others had the form of an imposed agreement by a conqueror upon\nthose who were conquered:[7] \n\n\n

      \n
    1. The parties are identified
      2. \n
    2. There's a recounting of the history of the relationship
      3. \n
    3. Rules controlling the future relationship are detailed
      4. \n
    4. Positive and negative consequences related to the rules of\n conduct are described
      5. \n
    5. Others are mentioned as witnesses to the treaty[8]  and
      6. \n
    6. The writing and disposition of two copies of the treaty are\n detailed.[9] 
      7. \n
    \n\n

    Format aside, the covenants between God and others\nemphasize the personal relationship he desires.[10]  There are several identifiable covenants in\nthe Old Testament: Adamic, Noaic, Abrahamic, Mosaic & Davidic. Psalmists usually focus\non the three associated with Israel, the Abrahamic,[11]  Mosaic[12]  and Davidic.[13]   \n\n

    Hymns of Zion/ Temple

    \n\n

    These psalms either praise Zion, the mountain upon which\nJerusalem is built or the temple of God in Jerusalem. They are related psalms. Zion is\npraised because the temple of God, representing God's presence amongst his people, is\nlocated there. God is the great creator-warrior king of Israel. Canaanite theology located\ntheir chief God, El, on Mount Zaphon and their other major deities upon other mountains.[14]  In a similar manner,\nZion theology places YHWH upon Mount Zion.[15]  According to Psalm 132:13, Zion is the\nplace God has chosen to manifest his presence in a special way to Israel.  \n\n

    Messianic Psalms

    \n\n

    The term messiah simply means, the anointed one.\nThis derives from the tradition of pouring olive oil on the head of a king, prophet or\npriest when they were commissioned - as a public testimony of their appointment. The term\ncame to have a specialized meaning in Hebrew literature as a particular individual whom\nGod would send at some future date to restore theocracy on earth and who was, in fact, God\nhimself, although this information was only slowly revealed. Early references do not\nspecify that they refer to a single character - the Messiah. Rather, we infer it from the\nclaims they do make and from their similarity to other definitive messianic passages.\n  \n\n
