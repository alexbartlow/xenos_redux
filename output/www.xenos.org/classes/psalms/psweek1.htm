Week 1: Overview, Authorship, Canonicity, Genre

\n\n

Overview

\n\n

The Hebrew Bible consists three parts: the Pentateuch,[1]  the Prophets[2]  and the Writings. [3]  This last part consists of\nfive books known as the Poetic Books. Psalms is one of those poetic books. The collection\nof 150 psalms spans 1,000 years[4] \nand is cited directly 116 times in the NT.

\n\n

This kind of poetry is not limited to the book of Psalms,\nhowever. Psalms are recorded during the times of the exodus (Ex.15:1-18, 21), judges\n(Jg.5:2-31), through the times of the kings & prophets (2Sam.1:19-27; 3:33-34;\n22:2-51; 23:1-7; 1Chr.29:10-13; Isa. 5:1-7, 23:16; 26:1-6; 27:2-5; Ez.19; Hos.6:1-3 &\nHab.3) and at least to the time of the exile (Ps.137).

\n\n

Efforts to collect this poetry may also have begun early\n(e.g. the book of Jashar is mentioned in Josh.10:13 & 2Sam.1:17-27).

\n\n

The material from the Qumran caves suggests that our\ncollection of psalms may only consist of a portion of the Psalms actually written.[5]  \n\n

    \n
  • Title of the book of Psalms
    • \n
\n\n
\n

The title used in the Hebrew Bible[6]  is Tehillim, meaning, \"Praise\n Songs.\" 

\n

Our title Psalms is taken from the Greek translation of a\n Hebrew word mizmor, meaning a song sung to instrumental accompaniment. 

\n

The LXX[7] \n translation of mizmor is the Greek word psalmoi meaning, \"songs to the\n accompaniment of a stringed instrument,\" (i.e. the Greek word, psallein means,\n \"to pluck a stringed instrument.\"). 

\n

Some authors will refer to the book as the Psalter because\n the Alexandrinus Greek manuscript of the OT called the book the Psalterion (i.e. a\n stringed instrument). 

\n
\n\n
    \n
  • Message - 'Honest to God' 
    • \n
\n\n
\n

Nearly all Psalms involve an honest communication with and\n response to God.[8] 

\n
\n\n
\n
\n

We should be as responsive to Him. Some of us are brutally\n honest with our doubts but unresponsive, even disloyal, to the truth. We don't tether our\n doubts onto God's ways.

\n

We should be as honest with Him. Some of us are too quick\n to stick truth into the holes that arise in our faith. It makes us too uncomfortable.[9]  Interestingly, the\n psalmists are as unguarded with their praise as they are with their doubts. I wonder if\n stifling one causes us to stifle the other? Perhaps we should be willing to appear\n undignified in order to be honest with God.[10]  

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

This honesty & responsiveness often produces a fruitful\n clash between God and man.

\n
\n\n
\n
\n

It's clear that God intends great spiritual growth to\n accompany and follow trials (Ja.1:2-5, 12; Heb.12:4-13; 2Cor.4:7-18). This spiritual\n growth includes increased confidence in and dependence on God's provision and increased\n commitment to God's goals. 

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

I hope you will use this five weeks to increasingly express\n uninhibited praise, gratitude, anger, anxiety, sadness, longing, confidence and joy to\n God. I think as you're open with Him you'll find he has a lot more elbow room to transform\n you into His own image.

Overview 

\n
\n\n
\n
\n

Psalms is divided into five books in the MT[11]  

\n

The first three books were probably compiled earlier than\n the last two books.[12] \n  

\n

There are duplicate Psalms (53 & 14; 70 & 40:13-17;\n 108 & 57:7-11/60:5-12). 

\n

By the time of the LXX the following Psalms were used at\n designated times: Sunday - Ps.24 (according to LXX); Monday - Ps.48 (according to LXX);\n Tuesday - Ps. 82; Wednesday - Ps.94 (according to LXX); Thursday - Ps.81; Friday - Ps.93\n (according to LXX) and Sabbath Day - Ps.92 (according to the Masoretic text). 

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n

Different MSS[13]  show different arrangements &\n combinations of Psalms. 

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • A Psalm may be split in one MS and combined in another.[14]  
    • \n
  • Superscripts/subscripts may differ between MSS.[15]  
    • \n
  • The LXX, Qumran & MT manuscripts seem all to derive\n their material from even earlier recognized canons.[16]

Authorship 

\n\n

Overview 

\n\n
\n

The Psalm text itself doesn't typically indicate\n authorship.[20] \n  

\n

34 Psalms have no authorship indicators in the MT but the\n LXX has titled all but the 1st two Psalms. 

\n

2/3 of the Psalms have been ascribed to David 

\n

If authorship is indicated it is contained in an associated\n title. 

\n
\n\n
\n
\n

Superscripts/Postscripts are very ancient.[21]  

\n

Concerning the term lamed: 

\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • The term lamed, most often, refers to authorship\n (e.g. Hab.3:1, Ps.18:1). 
    • \n
  • The NT seems to view lamed as a reference to\n authorship (e.g. Matt 22:41-45; Mark 12:35-37; Luke 20:41-44 (Psalm 110); Acts 1:16-17\n (Psalm 41); 2:25-34 (Psalms 16 and 110); Rom 4:6-8 (Psalm 32); Rom 11:9f (Psalm 69); Heb\n 4:7 (Psalm 95 following LXX). 
    • \n
  • However, lamed coupled with a personal name doesn't\n always suggest authorship (e.g. Ps.39:1) 
    • \n
\n
\n
\n\n
\n
\n

Some historical superscripts seem unsubstantiated by the\n content of the psalm that follows (e.g. 34). It's not clear whether this is because titles\n were speculative additions to the psalm, whether the psalms are generalized principles\n relevant to the historical antecedent or whether, as some argue, the superscripts are\n really subscripts for the previous psalm. 

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

For some time scholars tended to believe that many or most\n psalms were post-exilic for theoretical reasons.[22]  Recent analysis makes it pretty clear that\n the language and phraseology of the Psalms is much more ancient than found during the\n Maccabean period, however. [23] \n Except for 137 most of the Psalms are thought to be pre-exilic. 

\n

Davidic Psalms

\n\n
\n

Some have denied that that David wrote many of the Psalms\n ascribed to him but: 

\n
\n\n
\n
    \n
  • he wrote Ps.18 (see 2 Sam.22:2-51) 
    • \n
  • he was a poet (2Sam.23:2-51; 2Sam.1:19-27, 3:33-34, 23:2-7)\n  
    • \n
  • he was a musician (1Sam.16:18,23; 2Sam.6:5; Neh.12:36,46;\n Amos 6:5) and singer (2Sam.23:1) 
    • \n
  • he founded and organized the Temple music (1Chron.15:16-24;\n 16:7,31; 25:1; Ez.3:10; Neh.12:24, 46) 
    • \n
  • he founded Hebrew psalmody (Neh.12:24, 36,46)
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n

Overall, there's really no compelling reason to deny that\n David authored the Psalms ascribed to him when all the evidence is that he was quite\n capable as a poet, musician, singer and founder of temple musicology.[24]

Canonicity

\n\n

The collection of Psalms we possess is ancient but hard to\ndate. They were likely compiled at different times in Israel's history:  \n\n

    \n
  • During or at the end of David's life (c.f. 1Chr.23:2-6);\n  
    • \n
  • Possibly during the reign of Jehosahaphat (c.f. 2Chr.17:7-9;\n 20:19); 
    • \n
  • Possibly during the reign of Hezekiah (2Chr.29:25-30;\n Prov.25:1) and 
    • \n
  • Possibly during the times of Ezra & Nehemiah\n (Neh.12:27-30, 45-47)
    • \n
\n\n

Hebrew Poetry

\n\n

Ugaritic texts (Ras Shamra) are helpful in the elucidation\nof Hebrew words, phrases and concepts but no comparable collection of songs has been found\nin this literature. Hymns have been found in Egyptian, Sumero-Akkadian and Hittite\nliterature.

\n\n

What is common to Semitic poetry is the use of imagery and\nparallelism rather than meter and rhyme as is more typical of English poetry.

\n\n

Why use poetry at all? It's less precise. It's often less\ninformative. It's hard to read. All this is true but what's also true is that poetry slips\npast our guard (e.g. compare Exodus 14:26-31 with Exodus 15:1-5). As Longmann says,\n\"...poetry appeals more directly to the whole person than prose does. It stimulates\nour imaginations, arouses our emotions, feeds our intellects and addresses our\nwills...more than this, poetry is pleasurable.\"  \n\n

\n\n
\n

Imagery involves the use of simile, metaphor, mythological\n references or other literary devises that provide us with mental images. These images\n support the point the author is trying to make. The 1st Psalm is full of such images (e.g.\n sitting in the seat of mockers v.1; like a tree planted by the waters v. 3; like the chaff\n v.4). Psalm 23 is an extended metaphor that is as comforting to us as it would be to the\n sheep the psalmist is alluding to. 

\n

  • Parallelism (e.g. Ps.20:1)

This serves to tie\n one line with another.

Genre[27] 

\n\n

We don't have the time to study each Psalm[28]  in this course. Given\nthis, a useful approach to the Psalms might be to study types of Psalms and to take a\nclose look at examples of each type. Now this approach looks good on paper but the types\nor genres of psalms are not self-evident. If you read 5 scholars you'll get 7 opinions\nabout how to classify them. Some classify according to how the psalm was used,[29]  others classify\naccording to the content of the psalm\n[30]  and still others classify psalms according to both schemes. Each\nsystem seems like the proponent is trying to get a horse into a suitcase - a few things\nare left sticking out.

\n\n

The format I suggest isn't going to be any better. Some\npsalms will seem to fit into more than one category. Other psalms will not seem to fit\nwell into any category but here goes:  \n\n

\n\n
\n
    \n
  • Address to God (Ps. 13:1a) 
    • \n
  • The complaint (Ps. 13:1-2) 
    • \n
  • Expression of trust (Ps. 13:5) 
    • \n
  • The request (Ps. 13:3-4) 
    • \n
  • A vow to praise, sacrifice & instruct others (Ps. 13:6)\n  
    • \n
  • If the author gets resolution then he expresses joy (Ps.\n 6:8-10) 
    • \n
\n
\n\n
    \n
  • Psalms of Gratitude (Ps. 21, 23, 75, 135) 
    • \n
\n\n
\n
    \n
  • Announcement of the intention to express gratitude\n (Ps.116:1) 
    • \n
  • Delineation of God's deliverance (Ps.116:3-11) 
    • \n
  • Conclusion (Ps.116:12-19) 
    • \n
\n
\n\n
    \n
  • Wisdom Psalms (e.g. Ps.1 & 73)
    • \n
\n\n
\n

These psalms do not have a set form, except for the fact\n that they all recommend how the reader can live life skillfully before God. 

\n
\n\n
    \n
  • Psalms of Praise (e.g. Ps.95 & 117) 
    • \n
\n\n
\n
    \n
  • A call to praise God 
    • \n
  • A reason for the praise is given
    \n The reasons are usually directed toward God's attributes or actions in history/creation.
    • \n
  • A conclusion
    • \n
