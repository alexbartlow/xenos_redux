What is the Universal Church?

\n\n\n\n

Definition: The Universal Church derives its definition from the baptizing\nministry of the Holy Spirit. The key verse on this is 1 Cor. l2:l3,\"by one Spirit we\nare all baptized into one body.\" We see from this passage that the church is like the\nphysical manifestation of Christ, i.e., his body.

\n\n

Other passages which use the same imagery are Rom. l2:4-5; 1 Cor. l2:11,l8,27. The\npoint in all of these passages seems to be that anyone who has experienced this baptism is\nautomatically a member of the body of Christ.

\n\n

Words used for the church in the New Testament

\n\n
Church
\n
The word translated \"church\" in the English Bible is ekklesia.\n This word is the Greek words kaleo (to call), with the prefix ek (out).\n Thus, the word means \"the called out ones.\" However, the English word\n \"church\" does not come from ekklesia but from the word kuriakon,\n which means \"dedicated to the Lord.\" This word was commonly used to refer to a\n holy place or temple. By the time of Jerome's translation of the New Testament from Greek\n to Latin, it was customary to use a derivative of kuriakon to translate ekklesia.\n Therefore, the word church is a poor translation of the word ekklesia since it implies a\n sacred building, or temple. A more accurate translation would be \"assembly\"\n because the term ekklesia was used to refer to a group of people who had been called out\n to a meeting. It was also used as a synonym for the word synagogue, which also means to\n \"come together,\" i.e. a gathering. \"Body of Christ\" Since believers\n have been united with Christ through spiritual baptism, they are sometimes corporately\n referred to as the body of Christ. (Rom. l2:4-5; 1 Cor. l2:11,13,l8,27; Col. l:l8; Eph.\n 5:30) The idea seems to be that the group of Christians in the world constitute the\n physical representation of Christ on earth. It is also a metaphor which demonstrates the\n interdependence of members in the church, while at the same time demonstrating their\n diversity from one another. (Rom. 12:4; 1 Cor. 12:14-17)

What is the local church?
\n In this discussion exercise, ask the students to describe the scope or area encompassed by\n each of the following references. The point is that in each reference, the word\n \"church\" is in the singular. Since the scope of what is meant by each reference\n is different, we can draw conclusions about what constitutes a local church.

Question: What are some implications can we draw from these four passages\n concerning what size or structure a group must have to be considered a local church?
Answers: The word \"church\" is not a technical designation of a local\n group of any particular size or structure. Instead, it apparently described any extent of\n locality under discussion.
\n Therefore, in answer to the question, \"What constitutes a local church?\" the\n scriptural answer is that any part of the universal church which is somehow local can\n be said to be a local church. We would suggest this holds even down to the level where\n \". . . two or three have gathered together in my name. . . \" (Mt. l8:20) This\n seems to be Christ's version of what is necessary to have a local church.

\n A church of two or three may not be a very good church in that it is not able to fulfill\n all of the functions that are appropriate for a local church according to the New\n Testament, but this does not mean that it is not a church. A distinction must be made\n between that which determines the \"being\" of the church versus the\n \"well-being\" of the church.

\n
The local church in the New Testament.
\n While the definition of the local church is based upon our understanding of the universal\n church, the imperative passages about church life usually refer to the local church (i.e.,\n Rom. l2, 1 Cor. l2,l4; Eph. 4).
\n The significance of this is that if we try to apply principles like the inter-working of\n the members of the body as taught in 1 Cor. 12 to the universal church, we move away from\n the intention of the author to focus on the interaction of members on each other in\n Christian community.
\n Likewise, no structure or polity is given for the universal church except the unifying\n influence of the apostles who planted the local churches. There is also an example of a\n council of leaders from more than one local church meeting to resolve differences in Acts\n 15. We cannot say what the biblical pattern of extra-local church government was, since it\n is not given.
Optional discussion: It is customary in many theologies to construct a restrictive\n definition of what constitutes a local church. Sometimes several conditions, such as the\n proper observation of the sacraments, the presence of duly established clergy, a formal\n government, and ministry to all ages are given before a group can be called a church. What\n might be the motive for constructing such added conditions?
