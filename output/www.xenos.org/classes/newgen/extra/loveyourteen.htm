How to Really Love Your\nTeenager
\nby Ross Campbell, M.D.

\n\n\n

In this follow-up book to How to Really Love Your Child, Ross Campbell presents\nways in which parents can learn to communicate unconditional love to their teens.

\n\n

Chapter 1 -- Campbell makes the point that teens are not young adults, and that\ntheir emotional needs are still those of children. He claims that the anger and confusion\nof adolescence is due to a negative self concept resulting from not feeling genuinely\nloved and cared for.

\n\n

Chapter 2 -- The most important relationship in the home is the marriage. The\nsecurity of a teen is largely dependent on the quality of the marriage. The ability to\ncommunicate feelings, particularly unpleasant feelings, is critical in the marital\nrelationship. He warns against \"role reversal\"— where parents lean\non their teen as a counselor and confidant.

\n\n

Chapter 3 -- The basic foundation for a solid relationship with your teenager is\nunconditional love. If loved unconditionally, he will feel good about himself and be able\nto control his anxiety and behavior. If a parent overreacts emotionally, the lines\nof communication can be broken and the teen may turn to his peers for emotional nurture.\nThe more a parent loses self-control in front of their teen, the less respect the teen\nwill have for them.

\n\n

Chapter 4 -- The greatest need a teenager has is for focused attention.\nWithout it he feels insecure—and therefore more influenced by peer pressure.\nAdolescents need MORE time with family not less.

\n\n

Chapter 5 -- Appropriate and frequent eye and physical contact are two of the\nmost precious gifts you can give your teenager--touch their shoulder, massage a sore\nmuscle, or scratch their back.

\n\n

Chapter 6 – A terrific chapter on PARENTAL SELF-CONTROL. Emotional\noverreaction tends to cause an adolescent to respect the parent less. How can you expect\ngood self-control from your teenager if you do not have it yourself? Nothing severs\ncommunication with a teen worse than parental anger.

\n\n

Chapter 7 – The best chapter on TEENAGE ANGER! Young people tend to express\nanger immaturely until TRAINED to do otherwise. Campbell’s Anger Ladder illustrates\nthe different levels of maturity in expressing anger. Passive-aggressive behavior\nis the worst way to express anger. It causes most problems with today’s\nteenagers from poor grades to drugs and suicide.

\n\n

Chapter 8 -- From parent control to self-control. The goal is to control the\nrate by which the teen gains independence based on their level of maturity.

\n\n

Chapter 9 -- Teenage depression is difficult to identify because there are often\nno typical signs. In addition to changes in energy, sleep, appetite, and problems handling\nanger, they may have a decreased attention span, poor grades, boredom, physical\ncomplaints, or withdrawal. Boys often act out their depression through stealing/fighting,\nwhereas girls act out sexually.

\n\n

CRITIQUE: Campbell gives some excellent practical advice on how to effectively\ncommunicate unconditional love to teenagers. However, I feel he puts too much emphasis on\nfeelings and self-esteem. He seems to put forth self-esteem as a panacea for all teenage\nproblems: if we can make them feel loved and feel good about themselves, we can prevent\ndepression, substance abuse, or immorality. In addition to his often-simplistic solutions,\nhe also comes across uptight and rigid at times. All in all I would recommend this book,\nespecially the chapters on dealing with anger in our teens and in ourselves.
