Part 2: Conflict

\n
\n

A state of antagonism or opposition produced by competing wills,\n needs agendas or expectations.

\n \n \n
\n

1. Myths about conflict:

\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • Conflict is bad for the relationship
    • \n
\n \n
    \n
  • Conflict is a sign of a weak relationship
    • \n
\n \n

2. Conflict reality:

\n
    \n
  • It must be recognized and addressed
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • It is immensely useful and healthy for the relationship to grow and flourish!
    • \n
\n \n

3. Necessary, healthy conflict:

\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • Clarifies issues and principles
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Teaches and affirms values
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • Brings the parent and child together and avoids alienation
    • \n
\n \n

4. Unnecessary, unhealthy conflict: Caused by:

    \n
  • A competition of wills – an unhealthy need to \"win\" or to be in\n control\"
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • A lack of understanding of the child’s growing ability and development
    • \n
\n \n \n

5. Issues of Control:

\n \n \n
    \n
  • How we view parental responsibility
    • \n
\n
    \n
  • How we view personal autonomy
    • \n

4. The Role of the Will

\n
\n

In addition to self-awareness, imagination, and conscience, it is the fourth human\n endowment – independent will – that really makes effective self-management\n possible.

\n

It is the ability to make decisions and choices and to act in accordance with them.

\n

It is the ability to act rather than to be acted upon, to proactively carry out the\n program we have developed through the other three endowments.

\n

Empowerment comes from learning how to use this great endowment in the decisions we\n make every day.

\n

Stephen R. Covey
