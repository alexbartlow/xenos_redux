Theology

\n\n
\n

Define:

\n
\n

\"faith\" is defined as belief in Jesus Christ for\n salvation (justification) in contrast to good works.

\n

\"justified\" = dikaiow, this word is used\n five times in the passage. It is a legal term meaning the opposite of being guilty. It is\n to be declared innocent or righteous. It is to be imparted the standing of Christ’s\n perfect life; to obtain the standards of God or to live up to God’s character. We\n have been acquitted by God; but more than that, we have been given righteousness or the\n standard of the perfection of Christ’s life. (This is why the idea of our current\n actions somehow implicating Christ becomes important for Paul, v.17. See also v.20.)

\n
\n
\n\n
\n

Meaning:

\n
\n

Although it is hypocritical for our actions to contradict\n the gospel, it does not implicate Christ -- only ourselves. Paul defines what it means to\n die to the law: to be crucified with Christ. Paul is now made alive to God and lives in\n relation to God based on Christ's righteousness in him as opposed to his works. Right\n standing with God can never be through the Law because none measure up. Right standing\n with God is based on faith in the life of Christ.

Meaning:

\n
\n

Paul is arguing from the lesser to the greater. If it is\n true in human relations that one cannot change a covenant once it has been enacted, how\n much more is it true with God. Law stands in contrast to promise and while both are types\n of covenants a promise is clearly superior. The promise in this case was made to the\n singular \"seed\" which Paul concludes in Christ. God’s promise therefore was\n to Abraham through Christ. Christ then is Abraham’s heir and so are all who are in\n Christ and not necessarily those who are physical descendants.

4. Grace is a stumbling block (5:11)

\n \n
\n\n

Theology

\n\n
\n

Define:

\n
\n

\"severed\", to make ineffective or powerless; also\n to abolish or set aside.

\n
\n

Meaning:

\n
\n

Paul is not arguing here that circumcision results in loss\n of salvation, \"neither circumcision or uncircumcision means anything.\" Rather he\n is making the point that justification by works is just the opposite of justification by\n faith and it is for this reason that he exhorts the Galatians to stand firm. This\n imperative is followed by a series of indicative statements dealing with the issue of law\n and grace. Paul ends this series of indicatives by appealing to the Galatians in more\n direct terms to excise those who are oppressing them, thus \"it was for freedom that\n Christ set us free.\"

\n
\n
\n

Circumcision was symbolic of removing our calloused sin\n nature and exposing a vulnerable and sensitive heart before God (Deut. 30:6, Jer. 4:4).\n Paul turns this symbolism upside down in verse 4 when he states that under the new\n covenant, circumcision has just the opposite effect, instead of drawing us near to God the\n attempt to live out the law severs us from Christ. Paul's language is shockingly graphic\n but is used to a purpose, namely faith and law are not compatible in the Christian life.

3. Christians have crucified flesh (:24 Indicative)

\n
\n
\n\n

Theology

\n\n
\n

Define:

\n
\n

\"have crucified\", (3rd person pleural\n first aorist active indicative) probably means to have rendered the flesh powerless, used\n figuratively. The question surrounding this word has to do with the tense of the verb. Is\n this something that occurred at one point in time or something that had a distinct\n beginning and is ongoing.

\n
\n

Meaning:

\n
\n

Paul takes the idea of our freedom in Christ one step\n further. Not only are we freed from the demands of the Law but we are also freed from the\n tyranny of sin. Because we have crucified our flesh in Christ, we are no longer controlled\n by our fleshly impulses. Paul’s command to walk by the Spirit is only possible\n because our flesh no longer reigns supreme. Paul commands his audience from God to live by\n the Spirit. He supports this imperative by an illustration of the detrimental effects of\n the flesh in contrast to the life giving effects of the Spirit. He ends this section with\n the essential truth which enables the Galatians to live by the Spirit, that is, because\n they are no longer enslaved to their flesh.
