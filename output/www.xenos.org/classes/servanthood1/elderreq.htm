Qualifications \n for Elders
\n Paul emphasizes \n spiritual maturity and character over gifting. It is possible to be very \n gifted and knowledgeable, yet immature or carnal. Immature people often \n get into leadership, where they do the church much harm (see Diotrophes \n 3 Jn. 9). There is nothing wrong with the desire to be a Christian leader \n (1 Tim. 3:1), but it must be for the right reason.

\n

Since these qualities \n describe spiritual maturity, they are helpful in that they describe the \n character that the Holy Spirit is seeking to produce in all of our lives. \n Not surprisingly, most of these qualities are prescribed elsewhere in \n the New Testament for all Christians. If we allow the Holy Spirit to transform \n us into a man or woman of God, we can be sure that God will put us into \n the roles of leadership that he has prepared for us.

\n

Optional \n Discussion:
\n The Holy \n Spirit uses passages like this to turn up \"blind spots\" in areas \n so that we will allow him to change us over a period of time. As he convicts \n you, how should you respond?

\n

Answers \n include:
\n Acknowledge to him \n your lack, along with how you see this lack concretely manifesting itself \n in your life currently.

\n

Agree \n that you want him to change you in this area.

\n

Agree \n that you cannot change yourself, but that you believe that he can change \n you in this area, no matter how deeply entrenched it is.

\n

Ask \n him to give you practical steps of faith to take.

\n

Look \n for those who are strong in this area, observe how they exhibit this quality, \n and talk with them about how they developed it (Phil 4:9; 1 Cor. 11:1; \n Heb 13:7).

Above \n reproach
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:2; Titus 1:6,7)--anepilempton: unaccusable; anegkleton: \n unreprovable

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n is the summation of all other characteristics.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Not \n only the absence of disqualifying factors is in view, but positive \n things are evident

    • \n
  • \n

    A \n good reputation spiritually (Acts 6:3; 16:2).

Husband \n of one wife
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:2)--mais gunaikos andros: \"one-woman man\"

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n probably does not refer to polygamy (which was not common in the Roman \n empire), but rather that sexual morality is an established life-style. \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This \n qualification does not exclude divorcees; present life-style only \n is in view (as with all of the qualifications).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This \n includes flirting, porno habits, inappropriate \"counseling\" \n of the opposite sex, etc.

    \n
    • \n

Temperate \n
\n (1 Tim. 3:2)--nephalion: \n sober

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n is the opposite of being mentally and spiritually dense. It is linked \n with alertness in 1 Thess. 5:6 and 1 Pet. 5:8.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n person has a clear perspective on life, and a correct spiritual orientation. \n

    \n
    • \n
\n

Prudent \n
\n (1 Tim. 3:2; Titus \n 1:8)--sophrona: thoughtful, self-controlled, sane

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person is mentally healthy (Mk. 5:15; 2 Cor 5:13).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    He \n has an honest evaluation of himself which involves neither arrogance \n nor self-hate (Rom. 12:3).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n person evidences the ability to be reasonable, sensible, able to keep \n one's head (Titus 2:6; 1 Pet. 4:7).

    \n
    • \n
\n

Respectable
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:2)--kosmion: well-ordered

\n
    \n
  • \n

    A \n habit of orderliness and stability has been established (see 1 Tim. \n 2:9; 1 Pet. 3:4).

    \n
    • \n
\n

Hospitable
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:2; Titus 1:8)--philoxenon: \"lover of strangers\" \n

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person takes a genuine interest in new people. This would include \n both an outreach orientation and the willingness to open his home \n to others (Heb. 13:2).

    \n

Able \n to Teach
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:2)--didaktikon: skilled at teaching

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n elder must understand Scripture well enough to be able to effectively \n exhort in sound doctrine and refute those who contradict (Titus 1:9). \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This \n does not necessitate being a gifted large-group teacher.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    \"Grounded \n in the Word\" means that the elder can explain and apply biblical \n concepts in your his words, and discern error when he hears it.

Not \n Addicted to Wine
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:3; Titus 1:7)--me paroinon: \"not lingering over wine\" \n \n

    \n
  • \n

    The \n person has a demonstrated freedom from drunkenness, or substance abuse. \n There is no dependence on alcohol or other drugs.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Able \n to give up freedom to avoid stumbling a weaker brother (1 Cor. 8) \n

    • \n

Not \n self-willed
\n (Titus \n 1:7)--me authade: not a usurper

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n is linked with rebelliousness in 2 Pet. 2:10, and with usurpation \n of rightful authority in 1 Tim. 2:12.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    There \n should be a proven ability to defer to others.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Avoids \n a \"my way or the highway;\" attitude.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    To \n \"defer\" means that you actively get behind the others' way \n and help it to succeed.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Implies \n he is able to apologize

    • \n

Not \n quick-tempered
\n (Titus \n 1:7)--me orgilon: not inclined to anger

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person is not vengeful or violent, brooding or bitter (see Eph. 4:29,31), \n especially when he doesn't get his own way.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    When \n Moses struck the rock (Num. 20) he was refused entry into Canaan. \n When leaders misrepresent God by making him seem more angry than he \n really is, it's a serious thing (Jas. 1:19,20)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Leaders \n may get angry, but they should be slow to anger rather than having \n a short fuse.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n leader must be under control, avoiding violent outbursts

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Elders \n must be able to drop offenses, not hold onto them

    • \n

Not \n pugnacious
\n (Titus \n 1:7; 1 Tim. 3:3)--me plekten: not a striker

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person is not prone to physical or verbal abuse (i.e. slander, put-downs, \n etc.)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Not \n a fighter

Gentle
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:3)--epieike: gracious, forbearing

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person is not unduly rigorous or legalistic in his treatment of people. \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    He \n is kind, empathetic and patient with all people.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n opposite of quick-tempered, or pugnacious.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    People \n are fragile. We need to consider how our words and actions will affect \n them. See 2 Tim. 2:24,25; 1 Thess. 2:7; Gal. 6:1; Eph. 4:3; Col. 3:12,13; \n 1 Tim. 6:11; Gal. 5:22,23; Jas. 3:17.

Uncontentious
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:3)--amachon: peaceable

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n means not looking for ways to disagree or oppose; not loving to fight \n or quarrel.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n person possesses a positive and constructive point of view.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    This \n is the opposite of being self-willed.

Free \n From the Love of Money
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:3)--aphilagruron: not greedy

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n means the ability to be content with what one has materially (1 Tim. \n 6:8).

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n person is not motivated by financial considerations in ministry goals \n (see Acts 20:33)

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    True \n love for Christ and his work will become eclipsed by greed (see Mat. \n 6:24). Our day is replete with newsy examples of the error of money-love \n in the church.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    See \n 1 Tim. 6:6-11,17-19. Mature elders should give away much to others, \n and should live a simple life-style in order to curb temptation.

    \n

Manages \n own household well
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:4,5; Titus 1:6)--prohistemenon: to stand before; manage; to lead, \n used of an army commander standing before his men

\n
    \n
  • \n

    This \n is a demonstrated ability to lead spiritually and effectively in marriage \n and/or a rooming situation

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    The \n elder's family should respect him and voluntarily follow his leadership \n

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Examining \n one's family life tends to ensure that the person is spiritually authentic \n and not two-faced.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    Christian \n leader's first responsibility is to their own family. Prioritizing \n and practicing biblical principles with family and home is crucial \n in cooperating with God

\n (1 Tim. \n 3:6)--me neophuton: \"newly planted\"

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person has been a walking Christian long enough to be tested by God \n (see 1 Tim. 3:10)

    • \n
  • \n

    The \n person should have experienced success without becoming conceited \n

Having \n a good reputation with those outside
\n (1 Tim. \n 3:7)--exothen: used by Paul for non-Christians (Col. 4:5)

  • \n

    Non-Christians \n are unable to discredit the person.

\n
    \n
  • \n

    The \n person is fair and impartial in his dealings with people (1 Tim. 5:21). \n

    • \n
  • \n

    When \n favoritism and particular biases are adopted, the biblical concepts \n of righteousness and goodness fade, and with that, God's agenda and \n priorities.

    \n
    • \n
  • \n

    To \n gain victory in this area, one must be well aware of what his own \n bias tendencies are, and must resist those in favor of biblical truth \n

Devout
\n (Titus \n 1:8)--hosion: Practical seriousness and zeal for God's will

    \n

    A \n single-mindedness for God and His work.
