Worship in the New Testament \n

\n

Teaching Goals
\n To explain how worship \n in the New Testament is different from worship in the Old Testament.

\n

To motivate people \n toward a lifestyle characterized by New Testament worship.

\n

Introduction
\n It is common for churches to say that their first priority \n is the worship of God. This usually means that the corporate worship \n service on Sunday morning is the most important activity in which the \n church engages. Often, that worship service is liturgical--characterized \n by a set order of ritual, song, prayer, etc.

\n

Is this what the \n New Testament teaches about worship?

\n

A closer examination \n of the relevant biblical material affirms that worship is indeed the first \n priority of the church, but the New Testament defines worship in a very \n different way.

\n

A Change in Sacrifices
\n Read 1 Peter 2:5. This verse (along \n with vs. 9) teaches that all Christians are priests. Whereas in the Old \n Testament, only select Levites had this privilege, every Christian now \n has this privilege.

\n

What do priests \n do?
\n In the Old Testament period, their main function was to carry out \n the worship of God through the offering of sacrifices. There were essentially \n two different kinds of sacrifices: sin offerings, which were offered for \n moral offenses, and thank offerings, which were offered to express gratitude \n for God's goodness and blessing.

\n

The New Testament \n tells us that Jesus fulfilled the Old Testament sin offerings through \n his death (Hebrews 9:11-14; 10:1-14); therefore, we need never make such \n sacrifices to God again. But as Christians who have benefited from his \n sacrifice, we have the privilege to express our gratitude to God for Christ's \n work in many ways. Peter refers to this in 1 Peter 2:5 when he says that \n we \"offer up spiritual sacrifices to God.\" Peter does not specify \n here what these sacrifices are, except that they are spiritual, not physical. \n

\n

By studying other \n passages in the New Testament, however, we discover several different \n \"sacrifices\" by which the Christian may worship God. It is important \n to note that no one way is viewed as more spiritual than the others; all \n are important if we want to have full-orbed spiritual lives.
\n

\n

Ask the Group
\n Do you think we can go so far as to say that churches who practice \n a liturgy are wrong on that point?

\n

Different Ways to Worship
\n Offer God Your Whole Self:
\n \"I urge you therefore, brethren, by the mercies of God, to present \n your bodies a living and holy sacrifice, well-pleasing to God, which is \n your spiritual service of worship.\" (Romans 12:1)

\n

When an Israelite \n had received a blessing from God, he could have a priest offer up an animal \n as a \"whole burnt offering\" to show God his gratitude. God was \n pleased by this costly sacrifice, and expressed this by calling it a \"soothing \n aroma.\" (see Leviticus 3:5,16)

\n

Paul says that as \n Christians, we have received the mercies of God through Christ's work \n - a gift so fantastic that he spent the previous eleven chapters of this \n letter describing it. How can we say \"Thank you!\" to God for \n such a fantastic gift? Not by offering an animal, but by presenting to \n God something much more precious--our very selves. We can \"sign over \n the title deed\" of our lives and say \"God, I want the rest of \n my life and every part of my being to be one long expression of my gratitude \n for the gift that you have given me.\"

\n

We may think that \n this sacrifice is not very great because we have so many problems and \n faults, but God says that it is \"holy\" and \"well-pleasing\" \n to him! Notice that according to Paul, this sacrifice is our \"spiritual \n service of worship.\"

\n

The Greek word for \n \"service of worship\" is the word from which we get the term \n liturgy. Paul is saying that the elaborate worship service enacted by \n the Old Testament priests no longer has a place in Christian worship; \n it is now replaced by this very personal sacrifice.

\n

Ask the Group
\n What are some lines of thinking which oppose a person's movement toward \n offering his or her whole self to God? \n

\n
Offer God Your \n Praise:
\n \"Through Christ then, let us continually offer up a sacrifice \n of praise to God, that is, the fruit of the lips that give thanks to \n his name.\" (Hebrews 13:15)
\n
 
\n
Here is another \n spiritual sacrifice which pleases God--praising him and thanking him \n for all that he is and all that he does for us. The practice of thankfulness \n to God is stressed over and over again in the New Testament (see 1 Thessalonians \n 5:16-18; Colossians 3:15-17). Why is this? Does God need our gratitude \n so that he can feel good about himself?
\n
 
\n
Such a view obviously \n does not befit the God of the Bible--he is the only being in the universe \n who is completely self-existent and therefore needs nothing. We add \n nothing to God by praising and thanking him. God is indeed pleased by \n our gratitude, but the ones who benefit from this practice are us!
\n
 
\n
As we choose (often \n against our present feelings and circumstances) to recall God's blessings \n and then to thank him for these, we are keeping ourselves properly aligned \n with reality. Rather than buying into the lie that we are mistreated \n and unfortunate, we are by faith asserting the truth--that we are fantastically \n blessed beyond anything that we could ever deserve! In spite of our \n rebellion against God which deserves his wrath, he has forgiven us, \n adopted us into his family, guaranteed us eternal life, given us a significant \n role in his purpose, indwelt us with his Spirit, provided us with Christian \n friends-- and the list goes on and on.
\n
 
\n
The author's emphasis \n here is that we should worship God in this way \"continually.\" \n The idea that Christian worship takes place only (or especially) in \n a corporate worship meeting is utterly foreign to this verse. Because \n of Christ's payment for our sins, we have the privilege to draw near \n to God and communicate to him in this way at any time: in the morning \n when we wake up, on the way to work, during the busy day, when we are \n together with other Christians, alone in our room, etc.
\n
 
\n
It is wonderful \n to praise God with other Christians in song (Ephesians 5:19), but this \n should be only the \"tip of the iceberg\" of our thanks to God. \n
\n
 
\n
Ask the Group
\n What do you think erodes thankfulness in Christians? What do you \n think promotes thankfulness in Christians?
\n
 
\n
Offer God Your \n Material Resources:
\n \"And let us not neglect doing good and sharing, for with such \n sacrifices God is pleased.\" (Hebrews 13:16)
\n
 
\n
The author touches \n on two more ways in which we can worship God: doing good and sharing. \n \"Sharing\" probably refers to the generous giving of our material \n resources to God's people and God's work. This is explicitly identified \n by Paul as a sacrifice which pleases God: \"But I have received \n (your money gift) in full, and have an abundance; I am amply supplied, \n having received from Epaphroditus what you have sent, a fragrant aroma, \n an acceptable sacrifice, well-pleasing to God.\" (Phillipians 4:18) \n
\n
 
\n
Many Christians \n regard giving financially to God in the same way that they pay their \n taxes to the I.R.S. - they have to do it, and they look for ways to \n give as little as possible. Paul's view is very different from this. \n He says that giving is a privilege (2 Corinthians 8:4) and something \n that we should do generously (2 Corinthians 9:6), as an expression of \n our commitment to God (2 Corinthians 8:5).
\n
 
\n
When we give our \n money to God in this way by supporting our local church, other Christian \n workers and ministries, and helping the needy, God regards this as an \n expression of worship fully as spiritual as praising him. This is because \n giving of our money represents a giving of ourselves, since money represents \n the time and effort and creativity that we have invested in order to \n gain it. Such giving is also an expression of our trust in God's faithfulness \n to continue to meet our material needs--which Paul tells us God will \n fully supply (Phillipians 4:19).
\n
 
\n
Ask the Group
\n How can a Christian make the transition in his or her thinking from \n the \"I.R.S.\" outlook on giving to the \"privilege\" \n outlook on giving?
\n
Offer God Your \n Service to Others:
\n \"And let us not neglect doing good and sharing, for with such \n sacrifices God is pleased.\" (Hebrews 13:16)
\n
 
\n
The other sacrifice \n mentioned in this verse is \"doing good.\" This phrase refers \n to ministry--performing deeds of loving service to other people as representatives \n of Christ. When we relate to the people God brings into our lives with \n Christ-like, sacrificial love, God regards this as an expression of \n our worship to him. \" . . . walk in love, just as Christ loved \n you, and gave himself up for us, an offering and a sacrifice as a fragrant \n aroma.\"
\n
 
\n
God is pleased \n by this kind of life-style not only because he wants to love people \n through us, but also because this demonstrates that we are living with \n an attitude of trust in his love for us. We are motivated to love others \n because we understand and believe in the love that God has for us (1 \n John 4:16-19).
\n
 
\n
Every day, God \n gives us dozens of creative opportunities to say \"thank you!\" \n to him in this way-- serving our spouses, caring for our children, performing \n deeds of service for those in need, showing and sharing the love of \n Christ to our neighbors, those at work or school--the examples are endless. \n
\n
 
\n
We also have the \n special privilege of worshiping God through the exercise of our spiritual \n gifts. Paul speaks of his own apostolic ministry in this way: \" \n . . . because of the grace that was given to me from God, to be a minister \n of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles, ministering as a priest the gospel \n of God, that my offering of the Gentiles might become acceptable, sanctified \n by the Holy Spirit.\" (Romans 15:15,16)
\n
 
\n
After urging us \n to present our lives to God as an act of worship in Romans 12:1, Paul \n goes on to urge us to express that worship through the use of our spiritual \n gifts (vs. 6-8). As we discover our spiritual gifts and exercise them \n regularly in the service of others, and give God praise for the fruit \n of this ministry, we discover a form of worship that is uniquely satisfying! \n
\n

Discussion Questions
\n What if we emphasize one form of worship to the virtual exclusion \n of another? Consider the different mixes of exclusions, the possible motives \n behind each mix, and the possible outcomes of each.

\n

Prior to this study, \n which of these forms of worship did you understand the least and why? \n Which do you think is your strongest/weakest? Why?

\n

Conclusion
\n It should be clear from this study that worship in the New \n Testament is a lifestyle made up of many kinds of activity, not \n necessarily a corporate meeting.

\n

Why is this so \n important? When Christians view worship as the most important priority \n (which is correct) but have a superficial view of what worship is, the \n result is often a superficial and dichotomized Christian life. Such Christians \n are faithfully committed to attending the Sunday worship service, but \n because they view that as the essence of worship, fail to develop a lifestyle \n of whole- hearted commitment to God, thankfulness, financial stewardship \n and ministry. God is more pleased and we are more fulfilled when we develop \n lifestyles characterized by the full-orbed worship described in the New \n Testament.
