or Why Xenos will never be a model for other churches

\r\n

By Dennis McCallum

\r\n

Some observers have noted that Xenos is unusual. We agree. Our observations \r\n of other churches indicate that we are different than most churches in quite \r\n a few ways. Some are central, and some are fairly peripheral. Of course every \r\n group has its idiosyncrasies, but Xenos seems stranger than usual. Why do we \r\n feel the need to do things differently? That depends. Xenos didn't come out \r\n of an existing church, so we had no tradition to serve. And we realize that other churches have approaches that may be just as good or even better than ours. We have agreed we don't \r\n want to be different just to be different. But at the same time, we find that \r\n these differences are a good way to understand the thinking and values behind \r\n Xenos. Most of our odd features are the result of our own reasoning process \r\n and experience. For each feature, click to read a short explanation of what \r\n the difference is, and why we do it that way. Please note that we don't claim \r\n other churches should be like us. We learn from other churches all the time, \r\n and appreciate that there is more than one way to skin a cat!

\r\n

Explanations

\r\n

Home \r\n group leadership training standards higher than normal.

\r\n

How churches manage to train competent leaders during a one- to eight-week \r\n training program is a complete mystery to us at Xenos. Our training program \r\n takes several years of classroom and field training. We allow people to become leaders with relatively few qualifications. But if they want to remain leaders, they must complete their training over the next three years. Our typical leader has \r\n completed:

\r\n
  • 210 hours of classroom instruction with homework and graded exams; \r\n
  • Two to five years of personal mentoring from an older believer \r\n
  • Have either won non-Christians to Christ, or at least brought people \r\n who were converted\r\n
  • Have won one or more individuals into a personal discipleship relationship; \r\n
  • Have a proven character like that required \r\n for deacons in 1 Tim. 3.
    • \r\n

Why do we call for such a high level of training \r\n for leaders?

\r\n

First, Xenos home group leaders are responsible for leading \r\n and caring for groups that typically range from 15 to 60 people. These groups \r\n are \"home churches,\" not cell groups like in many churches. \r\n A home church a medium-sized group with a team of leaders. Since each home \r\n group takes care of its own leadership training, evangelism, pastoral work, \r\n teaching, worship, etc., we believe leaders need to be competent spiritual \r\n ministers (deacons) who are well-trained and capable of some sophistication. \r\n When you think about what leaders have to do, you see why:

\r\n
  • We expect our leaders to motivate their people biblically, which entails \r\n not just relying on group-think or sociological pressure, but actually persuading \r\n members that a biblical lifestyle is the way to live. \r\n
  • Such persuasion implies leaders know the Bible well enough to be persuasive \r\n in all major areas of Christian teaching. \r\n
  • Our leaders have to be prepared to answer questions about all areas of Christian \r\n teaching and thought. \r\n
  • They have to be prepared to lead their home church in waging \r\n spiritual warfare, so they must know about Satan and how to avoid aberrant \r\n teaching in this area. \r\n
  • They need to be competent to counsel people through typical \r\n non-clinical problems. \r\n
  • They have to conform to the character requirements of deacons \r\n as detailed in Scripture, which often entails some years of growth. \r\n
  • In most cases, they have to be mature enough to work on a leadership team without competing \r\n or fighting. \r\n
  • They have to be able to train their upcoming leaders in evangelism, \r\n follow-up, discipleship, pastoral work, Bible teaching, etc. This implies \r\n they know these areas themselves. \r\n
  • They have to serve as models of Christian living. In other words, their \r\n own lives must be stable and their relationships (including marriages) should \r\n be basically healthy.
    • \r\n

Considering what leaders need to be able to do, we don't understand how churches could develop good leaders in a few weeks or even a few months. \r\n In fact, we think one of the reasons churches are reluctant to fully \r\n delegate true responsibility to their lay leaders is their shallow level of \r\n training. They know intuitively they can't trust their under-trained leaders \r\n with sophisticated ministry because they are incompetent. But if this is true, \r\n who's fault is it?

Ministry Houses

\r\n

Xenos operates over 60 rooming houses dedicated to discipleship and evangelism. \r\n These houses typically have five to 12 men or women (but not both) living \r\n in them. The members usually subscribe to a ministry house covenant, which is an agreement \r\n to be accountable to their room mates for involvement and basic Christian \r\n living. During our history, ministry houses have been exceptionally effective \r\n at developing leaders in Xenos. They also cooperate with their respective \r\n home churches in outreach projects and parties. Single Christians in Xenos \r\n commonly live in a ministry house for several years. College home churches all operate \r\n two to five houses each, and the overwhelming majority of college members live \r\n in ministry houses. Adult home churches also have ministry houses for their \r\n singles.

\r\n

Ministry houses have proven to be a superb alternative to sending kids to \r\n Christian colleges. At Xenos, we have been very disappointed with the fruit \r\n born at Christian colleges, where students constantly surrounded with Christians \r\n seem to begin viewing Christianity as ho-hum. These students commonly come \r\n back spouting doctrinal trivia, but with no idea how to witness or relate \r\n to the real world.

\r\n

We also notice kids sent to secular universities without \r\n the support of a strong Christian community usually lose their spiritual vigor \r\n or worse. With ministry houses, kids get personal support and real community \r\n strong enough to counteract the powerful draw of our culture. At the same \r\n time, they venture into the secular world every day where they have to fight \r\n for their faith, and that leads to strength and realism in their walks.

No Designated \r\n Giving

\r\n

Most churches permit members to make special gifts to \r\n particular expenses in the church. At Xenos that's rarely allowed. If people \r\n want to make a donation to Xenos, they give their money to the general fund \r\n only. Our reason? We have observed that wealthy donors often attempt to influence \r\n the direction of the church through designated giving. In this way, the wealthy \r\n may end up having more influence than the poor, and according to James 2, \r\n that would be partiality. In the book of Acts, we see the believers laying \r\n their gifts at the feet of the apostles. In other words, those who are trusted \r\n with spiritual leadership should also direct the finances of the church.

\r\n

At Xenos, our financial decisions are made by the elders and by the Fiscal \r\n Support Team (FST). The FST is a group of more than 1,800 households who meet once a year for a weekend. There, they go over the whole financial \r\n picture for every area of ministry and make decisions about fiscal priorities. \r\n We think it would be wrong for people to overthrow the authority of the FST \r\n and the elders through designated giving.

\r\n

On several occasions wealthy members have protested this policy, even demanding \r\n that it be changed, or they would leave the church. We explained but did not \r\n change our position. A number of wealthy attendees have made good on their \r\n threat, which we were sorry to see, because we could have used their powerful \r\n financial help.

High Proportion of Converts

\r\n

According to several recent studies on churches in America, over 90% of growth in growing churches is the result of Christians transferring from other evangelical churches. Disturbed by these findings, Xenos leaders did a church-wide survey of our own membership in the spring of 2005. We were pleased and a bit surprised to find that 82% of Xenos members claim they were not involved in any church at the time they began coming to Xenos. Over 60% of our members reported that they met Christ at Xenos.

Elders Limited in Income \r\n and Assets

\r\n

Some of the elders at Xenos work full-time for\r\n the church, some part time, and others in secular careers. But they have\r\n all agreed to limit their incomes and assets, whether from the church or\r\n from other sources, including spouses' incomes. Our reason? First, we\r\n believe materialistic avarice is the curse of American society, often including\r\n the American church. As \r\n elders, we want to set an example for the church that says we can live\r\n at the need level, not at the greed level. Notice Paul did this by working\r\n hard and living simple in Thessalonica\"We did this, not because\r\n we do not have the right to such help, but in order to make ourselves\r\n a model for you to follow.\" (2 Thess. 3:9)

\r\n

The level to which elders' incomes\r\n are limited is very comfortable so this is hardly asceticism!

\r\n

We also observe that many churches are controlled by the wealthy, \r\n and are insensitive to the poor and even to students. When wealthy people\r\n get the church to commit to expensive options, the church ends up in debt\r\n and even more beholden to the wealthy.

\r\n

Wealthy church members often want\r\n to\r\n pay others for doing ministry they should be willing to do themselves.\r\n For a number of reasons, we feel it's good for elders to live at an income\r\n level similar to, or below most of our membership.

\r\n

Wealth can easily develop into an attraction that competes with the Lord \r\n for our attention. We want only elders who would gladly disown their wealth \r\n in order to have the opportunity to serve God. Those who refuse to divest \r\n themselves may be signaling a problem. We want our elders to have their attention \r\n focused on spiritual matters, not on the playthings of the wealthy.

\r\n

Not only elders' incomes, but also their assets, come under scrutiny. In \r\n American culture, it is not unusual for adults to receive an inheritance from \r\n a wealthy relative that could unbalance their lives. A man or woman who hardly \r\n thought about money before, may become fascinated by money after receiving \r\n a million dollar inheritance. Therefore, we call on elders to disclose their \r\n assets, and if they are excessive (beyond a home and some modest savings or \r\n retirement), we agree to divest ourselves of the excess.

\r\n

Although \r\n we feel our policy on this matter is somewhat soft, we think it is in general \r\n harmony with 2 Tim. 2:3,4, where Paul urges Timothy, \"Suffer hardship \r\n with me, as a good soldier of Christ Jesus. No soldier in active service entangles \r\n himself in the affairs of everyday life, so that he may please the one who \r\n enlisted him as a soldier.\"

\r\n

On more than one occasion, the elders' limited income and assets have \r\n served to dissuade would-be elders from joining the board on more than one \r\n occasion in the past.

No Worship Services

\r\n

Visitors to Xenos are always amazed (and often appalled) that we don't conduct \r\n worship services. This often leads to the commonly heard question, \"Why \r\n doesn't Xenos worship?\" Xenos leaders are never happy to hear this question, \r\n since it signals a misunderstanding. We certainly do worship the Lord! The \r\n problem is that the modern Western church has a very specific understanding \r\n of what worship is, and visitors do not find that particular form of worship \r\n at Xenos.

\r\n

Xenos leaders are not convinced the New Testament supports the modern American \r\n concept of the \"worship service.\" The early church had large meetings \r\n as well as home church meetings (Acts 2, 22). However, as we have studied \r\n these and related passages, we do not believe these large meetings were for \r\n the purpose of corporate worship, at least not as we see it in America today, \r\n with music, choirs, liturgy, etc. The descriptions of these large meetings \r\n never mention worship as their goal. Instead, they appear to be meetings for \r\n teaching and evangelism. Some forms of worship, including hymns, revelations, \r\n and tongues, were practiced at the home group meetings, according to 1 Cor. \r\n 14:26. This suggests to us that home \r\n groups are the best place for group worship in song.

\r\n

At a deeper level, we believe the modern church's idea of a worship service \r\n is based on the Old Testament concept of \r\n temple service, and in this sense is misleading. We believe the New \r\n Testament teaches that worship is something we do all \r\n the time, through at least five different modalities, of which singing \r\n is only one. Others specifically mentioned in the New Testament (using worship \r\n terminology) are:

\r\n
  • Financial giving (Heb. 13:16; Phil. 4:18)
    • \r\n
  • Evangelism (Rom. 15:15,16)
    • \r\n
  • Works of service (Heb. 13:16)
    • \r\n
  • Praise through prayer (Heb. 13:15)
    • \r\n
  • Devoting your whole life to Christ (Rom. 12:1).
    • \r\n

Xenos charges home churches with the mission of corporate worship. Our large \r\n meetings are for teaching and for outreach to non-Christians. Some home churches \r\n worship in song, and some just worship in prayer. Celebrating communion \r\n and baptisms are also handled by home churches. The approach is up to each \r\n group. See a central teaching outline \r\n or view a RealMedia presentation on \r\n this topic.

\r\n

Our unusual approach to this issue has had an interesting and unintended \r\n result. Christian visitors from other churches come to Xenos and are often \r\n dismayed to learn the worship services they are used to are not available \r\n in our church. Some also miss the cross, altar, and other forms of iconography \r\n in our auditorium. As a result, many of these visitors leave Xenos for a church \r\n more in line with their expectations. Over the years, Xenos leaders have become \r\n resigned to this pattern, because we feel called to reach those who don't \r\n like going to church anyway. As those who long for a church worship service \r\n move on, they open up space for those in our field of outreach who are delighted \r\n they don't have to sing Christian songs at our big meetings. Over the years, \r\n this filter has caused Xenos to have an extraordinarily \"un-churchy\" \r\n feel, according to most visitors.

Home group leaders \r\n handle all weddings, visitations and funerals

\r\n

In \r\n most churches, the staff handles functions such as these. In order to marry \r\n members, home church leaders have to be ordained by the church, and this is \r\n something most churches are reluctant to do. Churches don't want to proliferate \r\n ordained pastors who may not be well qualified. This, of course, leads to \r\n the conclusion mentioned earlier: that we must hold higher qualifications \r\n for our home church leaders.

\r\n

At Xenos, we not only allow home church leaders to carry out \r\n these functions with their members, we require it. If a couple approached \r\n one of the senior elders and asked to be married, we would point out that \r\n they need to ask their home church leaders to marry them. In a church where \r\n scores of marriages may occur every year, our top leaders would be unable \r\n to do much besides marry people every weekend if not for this policy. Visitation \r\n of the sick would require even more hours for the pastoral staff.

\r\n

But at Xenos, \r\n sick people are visited and counseled by people in their home churches. When \r\n visiting pastors ask how we find the time to disciple new leaders, write, \r\n study, travel, teach classes, etc., we point out that half the hours normal \r\n pastors would spend in basic pastoral functions are covered by our home group \r\n leaders, enabling us to focus more time on discipleship and equipping. Our \r\n elders consider this oddity one of the really important features of Xenos, \r\n opening the door to lay ministry in a number of ways.

All staff \r\n and eldership required to be in a home group

\r\n

This \r\n is certainly not unique, but in our experience, it is relatively unusual. \r\n Particularly important to us is that our top leadership is fully involved \r\n and actually lead regular home churches. When consulting with churches interested \r\n in building their home group networks, we often find that the senior pastor \r\n and others aren't in a home group for a variety of reasons, and have no intention \r\n of joining one. We find it unlikely that such churches will succeed in building \r\n high-caliber home-based body life.

\r\n

For one thing, if the top leadership isn't \r\n on board with the home fellowship agenda, how likely is it that the church \r\n will see this as a central issue? People also will quickly draw the conclusion \r\n that community of this kind must not be essential for spiritual health, because \r\n what's good for the goose is apparently not good for the gander.

\r\n

We think it is important that those on staff for pastoral counseling \r\n be leaders of home groups. This is because we have noticed a tendency in those \r\n involved in healing ministries to discount the importance of mission and leadership \r\n unless they are themselves vitally involved as leaders. Finally, top leaders \r\n who aren't involved weekly in personal discipleship and motivating a home \r\n group will not be drawing their illustrations and lessons from that experience \r\n set. We want our leaders to regularly relate what they are learning in their \r\n groups when teaching and speaking.

\r\n

We also believe the realities of group leadership and personal \r\n discipleship often bring to light the truth about Christian leaders' lives. \r\n We have seen repeatedly that when a Christian leader begins to develop a personal \r\n spiritual problem, it comes to light first in that person's home group. Likewise, \r\n leaders who are drifting into negative territory spiritually are usually the \r\n first to begin to downplay the importance of home fellowship ministry, discipleship \r\n and personal evangelism. Large-group preaching can be ego-enhancing, but personal \r\n discipleship is quiet and obscure background work. That's another reason we \r\n consider effective discipleship to be a prerequisite to public ministry.

\r\n

Finally, \r\n we want our paid staff to live the same struggle the rest of our leaders do: \r\n balancing career, family and volunteer ministry.

Home group \r\n leaders handle all church discipline

\r\n

Xenos home churches are \r\n like real churches. They carry out all the ministries a normal church would, \r\n including all the normal leadership functions. The only difference is that \r\n these leaders work under the oversight of our board of elders. Therefore, \r\n pastoral problems including those requiring discipline normally come up in \r\n the home church.

\r\n

We believe it falls to these groups to initiate discipline \r\n along the lines of Mat. 18. If a case reaches the extreme of exclusion from \r\n fellowship, the home church leaders are expected to recommend such action \r\n to the elders. The elders and oversight staff will review the case and must \r\n agree with the discipline proposed in advance, but the home church leaders \r\n will carry out the discipline.

\r\n

Normally, if the first several attempts to bring change have \r\n failed, a meeting is scheduled for committed members of the home group where \r\n the errant member can be confronted and admonished by the church. Group members \r\n have to be counseled and prepared for an experience so foreign to modern individualistic \r\n society. Leaders have to chair the meeting, making sure the tone is loving \r\n but firm. They have to make sure Xenos \r\n policy and biblical standards \r\n are followed.

\r\n

All of these functions require considerable maturity and good \r\n judgment on the part of home group leaders. No wonder most churches won't \r\n delegate this sensitive area to their home group leaders, especially if their \r\n requirements for leadership are low.

Staff hiring \r\n mostly limited to members

\r\n

At Xenos, we hire all our staff from within \r\n the church. We don't consider this to be a biblical mandate, and for that \r\n matter, we could hire from outside if the need was great, just as they did \r\n in Antioch when Barnabas went to get Paul (Acts 11:25ff). However, we feel \r\n we should prefer to hire within the church because of the message this conveys \r\n to the church. What will our people conclude if, every time we need a highly \r\n qualified leader, we look out to the job market rather than to our own people? \r\n Doesn't this suggest they are qualified to lead and serve unless it's a high-level \r\n position; but in that case, we have to hire a pro from outside? Isn't this \r\n a tacit affirmation of the clergy-laity distinction that has caused so much \r\n trouble in the history of the church, yet finds no legitimate basis in the \r\n Bible?

\r\n

Another reason for sticking with members is that people need \r\n to know those who become their leaders. If a person comes from outside, how \r\n do the \"sheep know his voice,\" to paraphrase Jesus' words. We find \r\n it hard to see how a community can feel confident about a person they have \r\n never known.

\r\n

We know many churches often hire from within, and some even \r\n hire most positions from within. But we feel we should hire all \r\n positions from within. If we feel some positions are so sophisticated that \r\n they require graduate degrees, why shouldn't we challenge members to get\r\n those degrees? If the church is charged with equipping the saints for the \r\n work of ministry, we believe we should not go outside the local church for \r\n ministers except in very unusual circumstances, probably involving extraordinary \r\n growth too rapid to allow internal equipping.

High level \r\n of biblical content

\r\n

Visitors at Xenos often comment on the content level \r\n of teachings. Apparently, we teach at a level considered too heavy for public \r\n meetings in most churches. Xenos leaders comment that they don't like the \r\n simplistic teaching level in some churches, and that they often have felt \r\n as though they were being treated like children when in church.

\r\n

We have observed an overall ignorance among the Christian public \r\n (as opposed to Christian leaders, who know plenty) that we find distasteful \r\n and want nothing to do with. The often-heard claim that the church today is \r\n too occupied with book knowledge and theology is confusing to Xenos leaders. \r\n We are aware of a handful of splinter groups where book knowledge might prevail, \r\n but the overwhelming majority of the church today seems incredibly ignorant, \r\n not only ignorant of contemporary scholarship in secular areas, but even ignorant \r\n of the Bible. We agree that even the relatively low level of Bible knowledge \r\n in some churches may exceed the level of actual practice of biblical teaching. \r\n But we don't believe that further reducing people's knowledge levels will \r\n help with their lack of action in obedience. In other words, if people are \r\n just studying the Bible but not practicing it, the solution is not to stop \r\n studying, but to start practicing. Paul was clear that our love should grow \r\n in real knowledge and all discernment (Col. 1:9,10).

\r\n

We also disagree that the general public is not responsive to \r\n sophisticated reasoning. Many Christian leaders seem to feel nonChristians \r\n will be bored if we teach too in-depth. But we find people enjoy being challenged \r\n in their thinking, and even if they don't understand parts of what they hear, \r\n they are still attracted to people who seem to know what they are talking \r\n about.

Three-level \r\n structure instead of two

\r\n

Most cell-based churches in America \r\n today have a two-level structure. The large meetings are the corporate worship \r\n meetings, and the small meetings are the cell groups. Cell groups, or small \r\n groups are usually six to 15 adults. Some churches have their small groups \r\n limited to believers only. Others welcome new people. But we have talked to \r\n a number of leaders who find themselves torn with this arrangement. Some pastors \r\n worry their believer-only small groups will turn inward and lose interest \r\n in outreach. The small groups could become Bible clubs for Christians, or \"holy huddles.\" Others worry that because their small groups are \r\n always geared toward new people, there are no meetings in the life of their \r\n church devoted to discipleship and deep learning. Churches have trouble raising \r\n up good leaders when no meetings offer deep learning and accountability.

\r\n

At Xenos, most groups have a three-level structure. We have our big meetings \r\n like other churches, although they are not worship services. \r\n We also have home churches, which are groups of 15 to 60 adults. Home churches \r\n are open to non-Christian guests, and are really small communities. Within \r\n each home church there are typically two or more cell \r\n groups. Our cell groups are usually four to 12 men or women, but not both. \r\n The men's or women's cell groups are for believers only, and usually have \r\n a fairly aggressive study schedule. They usually share and pray for each other \r\n as well. These are groups devoted to discipleship and spiritual growth. Thus, \r\n with our three-level structure, we have home group meetings devoted to outreach \r\n as well as some devoted to growth. For many groups, this means a third meeting \r\n each week. Other groups alternate the home church and the cell group meetings. \r\n We find that our three-level structure addresses all the needs in the church \r\n in a way no two-level structure is likely to do. We have experimented with \r\n two-level approaches on several occasions, but the groups always seem to go \r\n back to three-level arrangements after a time.

Home church planting is a prerequisite for high leadership roles

\r\n

Selecting good leaders is always difficult, and it's important, since the \r\n quality of any church is not likely to be higher than the quality of it's \r\n top leaders. We have observed many churches come under deficient leadership \r\n at times, and we want to avoid that. To select good leaders, we begin by limiting \r\n our search to our own church. This way, we know the person we are hiring \r\n and aren't dependent on secondhand reports from other groups. We try to get \r\n people with proven character as the top requirement. Then too, we find that \r\n for top staff—those directly involved in leading large sections of the \r\n church—we want those who are successful church planters.

\r\n

Planting home churches in Xenos is very difficult. Home church \r\n leaders not only have to take care of a group of 25 or more people, they have \r\n to promote evangelism, train new leaders and provide field experience for \r\n their new leaders. They have to develop, not just a leader, but a balanced \r\n team of leaders and a following to go with the new team. All of this takes \r\n some years of work and sacrifice. If the new church plant fails, they come \r\n right back to the planting church.

\r\n

We find the challenge of home church planting is so tough \r\n that only quality leaders with a fair amount of experience are able to pull \r\n it off. In Xenos, everyone respects successful church planters as those who \r\n know what they are talking about when it comes to ministry. That's why we \r\n select successful planters as home church consultants, course teachers, elders \r\n and other high-profile roles. It's great to be able to pick from those who \r\n have repeatedly accomplished a task we are all familiar with. We don't have \r\n to wonder how good such leaders are!

\r\n

One exception to this rule of thumb are leaders who have served \r\n in youth ministry. We have many of our best leaders serving as high school \r\n or junior high workers, and these often don't engage in home church planting \r\n as such. Yet, if they are producing disciples of high quality, they would \r\n be respected and hired just as readily as church planters.

Urban community development \r\n ministry uniquely emphasized

\r\n

Xenos is very heavily \r\n committed to Christian community development. We have not found other churches \r\n with a ministry analogous to our Urban Concern.

\r\n

Urban Concern is a $1.3 million-a-year effort to reach a one \r\n square-mile area of the inner city in Columbus, and to eventually transform \r\n that neighborhood through the love of Christ. The strategy is to focus on \r\n children, and stay with them over a 10-to 20-year period, helping their families \r\n bring them up in the nurture of the Lord. They will hopefully become leaders \r\n who will stay in the neighborhood and take over the process of transformation \r\n as indigenous leaders. \r\n

Entire teams formed, funded and sent to foreign fields

\r\n

According to missions experts like Tom Telford, Xenos' practice in the field of foreign missions is also very unusual. In our experience, most churches send missionaries from their own church either through their denomination, or through a variety of missions agencies. These agencies are usually chosen by the missionaries, based on their own criteria. The church will frequently have some contact with the agency, but most communication with the field is through the missionaries. Many independent churches will find themselves involved with several different agencies, and large churches may work through eight, 12 or more agencies.

\r\n

Xenos faced questions about how we wanted to do missions during the 1980s. We had been growing at a rate so rapid that it prevented us from sending missionaries earlier, in spite of a heartfelt desire to do our part in other cultures. Therefore, when our leadership considered where we wanted to go in world missions, we had the opportunity to write on a blank chalkboard rather than one cluttered with existing alliances and commitments.

\r\n

The Xenos leaders decided we wanted direct involvement with our missionaries and their ministries. We didn't want to simply have our people feel called to different fields and agencies and expect the church to give its blessing and support. After all, in the New Testament, the church commissioned missionaries and discerned the leading of the Spirit together. We realized Xenos had its own ministry ethos and emphases that were quite different from other churches. Xenos is very nontraditional and eager to avoid exporting a western-style church to new people groups where those forms would become a nuisance. We also felt a strong desire to keep our missionaries working together in teams on the field like they were used to doing in this country.

\r\n

We also wished we could find a mission agency that understood and appreciated our approach to ministry and our theological distinctives. Such an agency might be willing to place our people together in teams, perhaps with missionaries from other churches who feel compatible with our approach. Also, we felt that if we were willing to commit a good number of people to the field, an agency might be willing to include us in a partnering agreement, and share key decision-making responsibility with us.

\r\n

We set out to establish a partnership, first World Team, then also with Frontiers and OMF. These partnership agreements cover areas like

\r\n
  • who would go to what field, including screening procedures;
    • \r\n
  • field selection and research;
    • \r\n
  • missionary training and preparation;
    • \r\n
  • how teams would be composed and led;
    • \r\n
  • field supervision;
    • \r\n
  • financial responsibility and budget planning;
    • \r\n
  • visitation;
    • \r\n
  • home assignment arrangements.
    • \r\n

Xenos World Ministries and our partners have worked together under this agreement, with modifications from time to time, for 12 years. To date, we have accredited and sent nearly 40 career missionaries to six different fields under these agreements.
\r\n Some of the key benefits we see in these partnership arrangements are:

\r\n
  • Leaders of the church and agency building rewarding personal relationships over years. If we had been working with half a dozen agencies, we never would have been able to spend time and energy at the same level. The friendships we enjoy at the leadership level have been very beneficial at times of need or crisis on the field. Nothing serves better when resolving misunderstandings than trusting relationships.
    • \r\n
  • Xenos leaders have a visitation program for supporting our field operatives, and World Team, Frontiers, or OMF visit the same missionaries for regular reporting and oversight. This is very typical of course, but with the partnerships, there's a difference. We are able to work together both in pastoral issues and field strategy. Therefore, the issues one group of leaders sees when on the field or interacting via e-mail are regularly shared with the other. Nothing is more helpful than a well-informed \"heads up\" when visiting people we haven't seen for some time. Overall communication has been enhanced.
    • \r\n
\r\nPartnership has been the spirit desired by missions agencies for many years. Yet, for some reason, churches and agencies have not formed specific partnership agreements very often, that we are aware of. Lately, large churches are increasingly interested in these kind of agreements.\r\n \r\n

Leadership Teams in Xenos

\r\n

In the New Testament, eldership is always plural. The accountability of collective leadership is an important control that we believe was intended by God because of the fundamental untrustworthiness of human nature. By entrusting the local church to a group of leaders, the likelihood that one person will go bad or be misled and destroy the church is reduced. Satan's task is made more difficult—he must not only mislead and tempt an individual, but must win over a whole group of leaders to his ends. We believe the church would have done much better if it had stayed with plurality of leadership throughout its history.

\r\n

We know churches in the New Testament were organized with group elderships in each city. But we also know that there were often multiple home churches within a given city. How were the eldership teams related to the house churches in that city? Of this, we know little or nothing. Therefore, we conclude we are free to improvise in this area, as long as our structures result in the outworking of key principles of church life.

\r\n

In addition to eldership, New Testament churches had deacons, which means ministers, or servants. Nobody knows exactly what these ministers did, but judging from their qualifications, they were trusted servants of the church at a high level. While churches debate whether elders could include females, we think it is very clear that deacons could be male or female.

\r\n

In Xenos, we have a board of 8 elders overseeing the network of house churches, but our home churches are led by deacons. We don't believe the Bible calls for plurality of deacons when they lead groups, but we often prefer plurality, not only for elders but for deacons, especially if they are going to lead a sizable group. Here are a number of practical and theological considerations regarding plural leadership:

\r\n
  • With smaller group plants, forming a team would be a waste of manpower. We can't raise up enough leaders to form teams for even smaller group plants.
    • \r\n
  • A plurality of untrained, ignorant, and immature leaders is no more reliable than a single leader. Only if the leaders on a team are all trained and mature Christian workers can we assume that a group will be more reliable than an individual.
    • \r\n
  • We used to view our leaders as coequal within a team. Now we prefer to have a senior leader. Having a senior leader allows some insignificant decisions to be made without a meeting, which is easier for everyone. Also, recognizing a senior leader authorizes that person to take initiative in leading the group, and in leading the leadership team. This counters the paralysis that may result from \"leadership by committee.\" However, a majority of the team can overrule a senior leader, so accountability is preserved.
    • \r\n
  • Medium sized groups, like home churches, are large enough to have an identity as a group or a community, and may develop loyalty to their own home church, more than to the larger church. On the positive side, this makes home churches very hardy—nearly indestructible. On the other hand, many churches are reluctant to establish medium sized groups because of their history of divisiveness. We think plurality of leadership is the answer to this negative tendency found in some medium-sized groups. While plural leadership may decide to divide from the rest of the church, it seems much less likely that an entire well-trained team would decide to take this unrighteous direction. During over 30 years of ministry, Xenos has experienced very little divisiveness from home churches. On the other hand, sometimes churches' efforts to prevent division cause more harm than division itself. We would rather have one of two home churches leave if they want to than have all our groups suffer based on the fear of division.
    • \r\n
  • The size of the group suggests that teaching is more appropriate than mere sharing. Many churches worry this could lead to doctrinal aberrations. Again, plural leadership and good training is the best safeguard against doctrinal error. We find very suspicious the thought (sometimes openly expressed by pastors) that to prevent doctrinal error we should keep leaders ignorant and teach groups not to study the Bible together.
    • \r\n
  • Planting groups with plurality of leadership is slower than planting with single leaders. Another potential negative for leadership teams is disagreement within the leadership team. Corporate leadership requires a willingness to accept limitations on autonomy and decision making that the immature find irksome if not unacceptable. Leaders must develop skills of negotiation and patient communication in order to form a successful team. Certain self-willed and dominating individuals are weeded out by their inability to function as team players, and this is all to the good. Any individual who is too self-willed to work with colleagues on a team is not welcome to lead in our church.
    • \r\n
  • In Xenos, home church leaders are not empowered to remove other home church leaders from leadership. Only the elders can remove a home church leader. This prevents a majority from overrunning a minority in a team without outside confirmation. Sometimes, the lone dissenting leader is in the right!
    • \r\n
  • Since so many pastoral issues involve sensitive judgment calls, elders may find themselves wondering whether to trust the judgment of single leaders at times. But when a team of trained leaders concur in a judgment call, we have a good basis for trusting their view.
    • \r\n
  • Not only the elders' minds are eased by plurality, but home church leaders find their own minds eased by the opportunity to bounce ministry questions off other leaders who are actually involved in their ministry with them. A lot of potential leaders are more willing to consider being part of a leadership team who would not feel comfortable taking on leadership by themselves.
    • \r\n
  • Spouses who are not inclined to be leaders, either by gifting or temperament, find themselves willing to join in a leadership team with one or two other couples. We believe having couples lead together is healthy for marriages, and we strongly resist people leading without their spouses.
    • \r\n

Generate all our own children's curriculum

\r\n

We know we aren't the only ones to do this, but we do see it as unusual. We started doing this mainly because our leadership couldn't find curriculum that wasn't \"churchy.\" Xenos is a very non-churchy church, and we didn't want our kids subjected to a traditional church perspective.

\r\n

Another complaint was that much of the children's curriculum was too simplistic and didn't really teach the Bible. Our assessment was that kids were feeling patronized by oversimplified material that wasn't really challenging them.

\r\n

Finally, a lot of curriculum is legalistic. The focus seems to often be on good behavior, seeming to stress that being a good kid is what Christianity is all about. Our people were so frustrated they decided to write their own curriculum from preschool to fifth grade. The project took more than five years for a team of writers, all working at their own expense. Now we give the curriculum away for free to many churches. Come to think of it, the high price of children's curriculum is another gripe we had!

Over 50 percent of attendees involved in discipleship pairings

\r\n

Xenos is an indigenous church planting ministry. Our approach to ministry assumes qualified leaders constantly are rising up in our midst. We have to generate more than 50 leaders a year just to keep up, and that number continues to rise. When you consider how high our standards are for leadership, you realize this isn't an easy task. Lead pastor, Dennis McCallum has written a book on our philosophy of discipleship ministry, called Organic Disciplemaking.

\r\n

Today, around 1,000 adults and students at Xenos have someone they are discipling in private meetings (and many have several). This is in addition to our home churches, cell groups and classes. We find that these one-on-one, or three-way times are good for building friendships, and that many issues of application and personal character development can only come out in these meetings. Also, nothing is better than these small meetings for coaching in ministry development.

\r\n

We are careful to avoid any definition of discipleship that implies the discipler has control over the disciple, like in the so-called \"Shepherding Movement.\" We teach our people that discipling is a facilitating and helping role, not a controlling role. We are optimistic today that leaders will be ready when needed tomorrow because more than 2,000 of our people are actively engaged in personal discipleship.

No scripted ministry approach for lay leaders

\r\n

Most churches we have studied provide a curriculum or lesson plan for their home groups. These may be based on the sermon that week, or may be nationally published small group curriculum like Serendipity or Touch. We have looked at these options and experimented with some, but in the end prefer not to use any.

\r\n

Our leaders are equipped to develop their own lessons, mainly based on expository study of the Bible. By far the majority of our groups are engaged in expository Bible study of a New or Old Testament book at any one time. In addition, groups occasionally do topical series on subjects of interest, like marriage, finance, social ethics or theological topics. Our Study Center provides all the materials leaders need to do personal research, and groups also leave copies of series they have developed at the Center for other groups to use. In recent years, our leaders have also benefited greatly from our extensive Web site.

\r\n

We find that leaders who base their groups on prepared curriculum lose interest in teaching. The curriculum is often oversimplified and seems to do too much for the teacher. Teaching people the ways of God becomes something anyone can do and loses its challenge. People who are doing sophisticated work in their careers may come to lead their Bible study group and find themselves doing childishly simple work. No wonder they lose interest!

For instance, students learn their Bible better when they teach it, and they gain experience teaching and preaching. Later, when they take over their own groups, they will already have significant experience speaking in front of groups.

\r\n

No formal membership

\r\n

This certainly is not unique, but it is somewhat unusual, especially for large churches. Our refusal to establish a formal membership is the result of our theological understanding of church membership, and our efforts to emulate the early church. Our definition of the church is spiritual, (i.e. all true believers are part of the body of Christ whether they attend meetings or not) and therefore we feel it would be impossible for humans to accurately know who is a member and who is not. We believe efforts to establish membership can result in an institutional definition of the church rather than an organic or spiritual definition. Some that we define as members would be truly non-members, while others who we deny are members would in fact be members.

\r\n

We see this distinctive as a good thing, but at the same time, a minor thing. We don't feel strongly critical of churches that have formal memberships, and in fact, it would be difficult to say how much actual impact this feature has had on Xenos' development. Our leadership feel that refusing to define membership contributes to a proper view of the spiritual nature of the church. We tell people that we let God keep our membership rolls for us.

Strange name

\r\n

People are always asking about the name Xenos Christian Fellowship. Some of our members regret this name, because it can cause suspicion, like anything that is unusual. The name actually makes sense. Xenos is the Greek word for alien or sojourner, and the Bible says Christians are sojourners in this life; our citizenship is in heaven (Phil. 3:20). Also, the word means host, which we feel we are to non-Christian guests. But the reason we have the name goes back to the time of our incorporation as a church in 1982.

\r\n

By 1982, we had already existed for 12 years under the even stranger name, \"Fish House Fellowship.\" This name had never been chosen by us, but was rather a description of \"the fellowship meeting at the Fish House.\" Fish house was the name of the original house on The Ohio State University campus we used as a headquarters. By the 1980s, the house had long since ceased to exist, so calling our church by that name was absurd.

\r\n

The elders met at the time of our incorporation, realizing we would be stuck with any name we chose at that time. Some suggested a more conventional name, like some kind of community church. But the majority didn't want to be identified with the idea of a conventional church because our field of ministry is anti-church in attitude. We decided a name that raised questions, but didn't fit any known category (one that could be easily dismissed by cynical guests) would be better. At the time we were publishing a magazine called Xenos, so we decided to call it Xenos Christian Fellowship.

\r\n

We considered changing our name when we moved into our new facility in 1997, but decided not to. We were worried about becoming too conventional and being viewed like a normal church. Moreover, we already were known widely as Xenos, and felt it would be a major headache to change our name. We decided to keep our strange name and oddball image that comes with it. Xenos members jokingly refer to each other as \"Xenoids,\" often pronounced with a robot-like tone of voice.

\r\n

Unusual View of Confidentiality

\r\n

Xenos leaders take exception to the modern American view that counseling should necessarily be done in confidentiality, especially if confidentiality means counselors and pastors are not allowed to confer with other pastors and counselors. We find the notion of unqualified confidentiality may conflict with the biblical notion of corporate leadership and church discipline.

\r\n

How can leaders pastor collectively, if they are not free to share what they know from conversations with members? We feel the idea of collective or corporate leadership becomes a dead letter any time members can forbid leaders to discuss cases with each other.

\r\n

Likewise, today's view of confidentiality would seem to flatly contradict Jesus' call to \"tell it to the church\" when someone refuses to repent for sin. Sinful acts are usually those which clients are most eager to keep in confidence.

\r\n

As a result of these findings, clients seeking pastoral counseling from Xenos staff counselors must sign a waiver of confidentiality. The waiver makes clear that information will only be shared with pastoral staff except in cases of formal church discipline. If a client feels the need to counsel in complete confidence, we refer them to professional counselors outside the church.

\r\n

The same goes for informal counseling with Xenos leaders. We are on public record as rejecting the validity of unqualified confidentiality across the board, because as secrecy increases, community decreases.
